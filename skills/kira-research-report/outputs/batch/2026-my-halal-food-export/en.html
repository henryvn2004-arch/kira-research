Demand is structural, not cyclical

Section 04 · Macro Context
007 / 033

Macro indicators driving demand

A resilient growth path and a strong ringgit shape export economics in two directions: domestic supply capacity expands, while currency strength compresses the FOB price advantage.

Growth holds, inflation stays contained

The economy grew 5.2% in 2025 [World Bank 2025] and is set to expand around 4.4-4.9% in 2026 [IMF 2026]. Headline inflation averaged 1.4% in 2025 [World Bank 2025], keeping input-cost pressure on food processors modest by regional standards.

The currency cuts both ways

The ringgit appreciated 10.2% against the dollar in 2025 [IMF 2026], the region's strongest performer. For exporters priced in USD this compresses headline FOB competitiveness, but it lowers the cost of imported inputs and capital equipment — a margin offset for value-added processors over commodity shippers.

A growth base that supports scale

Stable growth and low inflation give exporters a predictable domestic platform from which to fund certification, segregated logistics, and destination-market entry — the three cost centres that gate export scale [Kira estimates].

Section 04 · Macro Context
008 / 033

Policy is the demand engine

Halal export in Malaysia is a stated national objective with a master plan, a target, dedicated parks, and a merged institution behind it. Policy clusters tightly in the 2024-2026 window.

A master plan with a hard number

The Halal Industry Master Plan 2030 targets a RM 231.1 bn industry contribution and RM 70 bn of halal exports by 2030, with a 10.8% share of GDP [MITI 2024]. Unlike aspirational framing, the plan is wired to incentives, parks, and agency mandates.

A merged trade-and-halal agency

In late 2024 the national trade-promotion agency merged with the halal development body [MATRADE 2025], pairing a network of roughly 49 overseas trade offices with halal-specific industry capability — a single institution for export market access.

Parks turn policy into capacity

HALMAS-accredited halal parks have drawn RM 16.7 bn of cumulative investment, targeting RM 25 bn by 2030 [HDC 2026]. Tenants access tax holidays of up to ten years and certification-cost support — the supply-side counterpart to the export target.

Section 05 · Sector Sizing
010 / 033

Market size & growth: halal food export, Malaysia

Total halal exports reached RM 68.5 bn in 2025, up 10.9% [MITI 2025]. Food and beverage is the lead category and the focus of this report; halal ingredients are the fastest-climbing adjacent layer.

A record base, broadening categories

From RM 53.7 bn in 2023, exports rose to RM 61.8 bn in 2024 and RM 68.5 bn in 2025 — a two-year gain of 27.5% [MITI 2025]. Food and beverage led at RM 36.9 bn (53.8%), with halal ingredients at RM 21.4 bn (31.2%) [MITI 2025].

Half-year momentum confirms the trend

First-half 2025 halal exports hit USD 7 bn, about 16.1% of total national exports [MATRADE 2025], evidence the category is now material to Malaysia's overall trade balance, not a niche.

Headroom against the policy target

The 2025 result already sits within touching distance of the RM 70 bn 2030 export target [MITI 2024], implying the binding constraint is no longer demand but value capture per unit shipped [Kira estimates].

The demand pool: where Malaysian halal lands

Global Muslim food spend approaches USD 2 trn, and OIC import demand grows faster still. Malaysia's destination mix tilts to ASEAN today, with the Gulf the higher-margin frontier.

A demand pool growing faster than GDP

Muslim food and beverage spend was USD 1.43 trn in 2023, heading to USD 1.94 trn by 2028 [SGIE 2024/25]. Intra-OIC import demand rises from USD 293.6 bn to USD 442.3 bn over the same window — 8.5% annual growth [SGIE 2024/25].

China is the largest single buyer

China took RM 6.25 bn of Malaysian halal exports, ranking second among destinations [MATRADE 2025]; ASEAN neighbours and the Gulf round out the top tier. The destination mix today is weighted toward proximity, not toward the highest-spend Gulf markets.

The Gulf is the value frontier

Saudi Arabia, Malaysia and the UAE rank among the five largest OIC halal-food importers [SGIE 2024/25]; the GCC imports roughly 85% of its food [Kira estimates]. Closing the gap between certification authority and Gulf shelf share is the central growth lever.

Section 06 · Segment Economics
013 / 033

Segment economics: the value-add ladder

Margin steps up sharply from commodity throughput to branded and ingredient formats. The export mix is migrating up the ladder, and that migration — not volume — is where value accrues.

Three rungs, widening margins

Processed F&B and ingredients carry materially higher gross margins than palm-derivative commodity flows — a step-up of roughly two to five times per rung from raw to specialty [Kira estimates]. Ingredients at RM 21.4 bn (31.2%) signal the mix is already climbing [MITI 2025].

Ingredients are the quiet engine

Halal-certified ingredients — flavours, enzymes, gelatine substitutes, emulsifiers — sell into other manufacturers and command recognition-premium pricing. They travel better than chilled meat and face lighter cold-chain cost, making them the most export-efficient rung [Kira estimates].

Ready-meal formats capture the convenience shift

Gulf demand is shifting toward frozen, ready-to-eat and meal-kit formats [Kira estimates]; these blend brand margin with the convenience premium urban Gulf households now pay, and reward exporters who invest in format over throughput.

Section 06 · Segment Economics
014 / 033

Sub-segment read: where to concentrate effort

Not every halal food sub-segment exports with equal efficiency. Cold-chain intensity, certification complexity, and destination fit sort the field into clear priorities.

Frozen & surimi: scale anchor

Frozen poultry, surimi and convenience seafood underpin Malaysia's export base, led by integrated producers with EU-grade plus halal dual certification [Kira estimates]. The constraint is cold-chain cost, not demand.

Snacks & bakery: shelf-stable advantage

Paratha, biscuits and shelf-stable snacks travel without cold chain and clear customs faster, giving them the lowest export-cost-to-value ratio of any sub-segment [Kira estimates]. They are the natural SME on-ramp to export.

Beverages & ingredients: premium tilt

RTD beverages and halal ingredients carry recognition-premium pricing and lighter logistics. They suit a value-capture strategy and align with the Gulf shift toward convenience and processed formats [Kira estimates].

Channel & distribution structure: two geographies

ASEAN absorbs the bulk of volume through proximate, relationship-led trade; the Gulf is the higher-value frontier reached via certification recognition and specialist importers. The mix should shift toward the Gulf over the plan period.

ASEAN: proximity and overland reach

Regional neighbours and China — the latter taking RM 6.25 bn [MATRADE 2025] — anchor today's volume. Short lead times, overland and short-sea routes, and established distributor relationships keep landed cost low and turn frequency high.

Middle East: recognition-gated, importer-led

Gulf entry runs through specialist importers and is gated by certification recognition under GSO 2055 [GSO 2026]. The GCC imports roughly 85% of its food [Kira estimates], and demand tilts to frozen, ready-to-eat and meal-kit formats — higher margin, longer chain.

The channel decision is the margin decision

ASEAN maximises throughput; the Gulf maximises value per unit. Exporters that build importer relationships and GSO-aligned documentation now capture the margin step before competitors crowd the recognised-origin position [Kira estimates].

Channel economics: the halal cold-chain cost wall

Halal logistics demands physical segregation, dedicated assets and end-to-end documentation. A single breach invalidates halal status — which makes the cold chain both the cost and the integrity guarantee.

Segregation is non-negotiable

Halal logistics requires segregation from non-halal goods at every stage — dedicated storage, equipment and transport — plus journey-tracking records [Kira estimates]. The fixed cost of dedicated lorries and warehousing is the single largest barrier to SME export scale.

The SME cost wall, and the pooling answer

With 89% of certified firms being SMEs [JAKIM 2024], few can fund segregated cold chain alone. Resource pooling among certified SMEs lowers per-unit logistics cost and is the structural unlock the master plan's parks are designed to enable [Kira estimates].

Multiplicity of regimes raises overhead

Exporters serving several markets must satisfy JAKIM, Indonesia's BPJPH, Singapore's MUIS and Gulf bodies simultaneously [BPJPH 2024]. Each adds documentation overhead; harmonised digital records are the cost-reduction lever.

Section 09 · Regulatory
025 / 033

Certification & regulatory landscape: recognition is the trade

JAKIM is the recognised gold standard, now fully digital. But every destination runs its own regime, and recognition between them is neither automatic nor symmetric — the regulatory map is the route map.

JAKIM: the gold standard, gone digital

JAKIM is the sole issuer of Malaysian halal certificates under MS1500, and from May 2025 all applications run through the MYeHALAL portal [Kira estimates]. Certificates are valid two years. JAKIM's mark is the most widely recognised globally [Kira estimates].

ASEAN: Indonesia's regime now bites

Indonesia's mandatory halal certification took effect October 2024, with imported-food enforcement extended to October 2026 [BPJPH 2024]. The largest single Muslim market is becoming the most documentation-intensive ASEAN destination.

Gulf: GSO 2055 governs entry

GCC entry runs through the GSO 2055 framework, with a draft update issued in early 2026 [GSO 2026]. Recognition is not automatic; foreign certificates require approval by local bodies such as the SFDA and GAC — the binding gate on Gulf shelf access.

Five-year outlook: scenarios to 2030

From a RM 68.5 bn 2025 base, the trajectory depends less on demand than on value capture and Gulf recognition. We frame three scenarios; the policy target sits between base and bull.

Base case: steady climb to the target

Halal exports reach roughly RM 88-92 bn by 2030 at a ~5-6% CAGR [Kira estimates], clearing the RM 70 bn policy target [MITI 2024] on continued F&B and ingredient momentum and gradual Gulf-share gains.

Bull case: recognition converts

If GSO 2055 recognition unlocks Gulf shelf at scale and ingredients keep climbing the value ladder, exports could approach RM 100 bn by 2030 [Kira estimates] — the value-capture, not volume, scenario.

Bear case: regime friction bites

Tightening ASEAN and Gulf documentation, a strong ringgit, and SME cost walls could hold exports near RM 80 bn by 2030 [Kira estimates]. The downside is execution and recognition, not demand.
