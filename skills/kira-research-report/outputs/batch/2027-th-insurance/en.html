The market state

Thai insurers wrote THB 941 bn in premiums in 2024, up 2.4% [TLAA-OIC 2024], with life at THB 654 bn (+3.2%) [TLAA 2024] and non-life at THB 286 bn (+0.5%) [TGIA 2024]. Headline growth is single-digit, but composition is moving fast: health and critical-illness premiums jumped 13.7% to THB 125 bn, now 19.1% of life premium [TLAA 2024].

Why now: demographics as demand engine

Thais aged 60+ reached 13.6 m, or 20% of the population, in 2024 [GISTDA 2025], outnumbering children for the first time. The country is on track to be super-aged — 28% over 60 — by 2035 [WHO 2024]. With out-of-pocket spending at 11.3% of health outlays [UHC Study 2024] and medical inflation running 8–10% [Bangkok Post 2024], the private protection gap widens each year the public scheme stays flat.

Five strategic implications for market participants

Five forces reshape competitive dynamics through 2032. Each is anchored to documented market data and our synthesis of Thailand's demographic and regulatory trajectory.

01 · DEMAND
Own the ageing customer's full lifecycle
With 60+ at 20% of population and rising to 28% by 2035 [GISTDA 2025] [WHO 2024], the winning carriers bundle health, annuity, and long-term care across one relationship rather than selling single products. Cross-sell depth, not new-logo count, drives 2027 value.
Anchor: 13.6 m Thais aged 60+ in 2024
02 · HEALTH GAP
The protection gap is the growth pool
Public cover reaches 74% of people [Frontiers 2024] but leaves out-of-pocket at 11.3% of spending [UHC Study 2024]. Health and CI premiums already grow 13.7% a year [TLAA 2024]. Closing the gap for the middle class is the single largest organic opportunity.
Anchor: 19.1% health share of life premium
03 · COST CONTROL
Copayment resets health economics
The OIC copayment clause, effective 19 March 2025 [OIC 2025], shares cost with policyholders to curb medical inflation of 8–10% [Bangkok Post 2024]. Carriers that price and communicate copay well protect loss ratios; laggards face anti-selection.
Anchor: 8–10% annual medical inflation
04 · CAPITAL
IFRS 17 and RBC favour scale balance sheets
Risk-based capital and IFRS 17 raise the cost of long-duration guarantees [OIC 2025]. Annuity-heavy books need disciplined ALM. Sub-scale insurers face a structural capital disadvantage versus the top three; expect selective consolidation.
Anchor: top 3 own ~53% of life premium
05 · DIGITAL
AI moves from pilot to underwriting core
AI underwriting, claims automation, and fraud models — which cut fraudulent claims ~30% in leading deployments [InsurTech 2025] — are table stakes by 2027. Bancassurance plus agent forces remain the trust channel for older buyers; digital augments, not replaces.
Anchor: ~30% fraud-claim reduction

Section 06 · The Demographic Transition
007 / 018

From aged to super-aged: the demand engine for cover

The share of Thais aged 60+ doubled from 10% in 2000 to 20% in 2020–24 [World Bank 2023] [GISTDA 2025], and is projected to reach 36% by 2050. Each percentage point shifts demand from accumulation products toward health, annuity, and long-term care.

An inverted age pyramid

In 2024 the 60+ population reached 13.6 m and overtook the 9.5 m children under 15 [GISTDA 2025]. A shrinking working-age base supports a growing dependent-elderly cohort — the classic ageing-society squeeze, arriving earlier in Thailand than in regional peers.

The super-aged threshold

The WHO projects Thailand becomes super-aged — 28% over 60 — by 2035 [WHO 2024]. By 2050 roughly 36% of the population will be 60+ [World Bank 2023]. Longevity raises the duration of health and care liabilities insurers must underwrite.

Regional divergence

The North, Central, and North-East regions already register the highest ageing indices [GISTDA 2025], driven by low fertility and out-migration of working-age adults. Provincial micro-markets mature at different speeds, shaping where rural distribution investment pays back.

Section 07 · The Health Protection Gap
008 / 018

Where universal coverage ends and private cover begins

Thailand's tax-financed Universal Coverage Scheme is a genuine achievement — but breadth is not the same as depth. As the population ages and medical costs outrun general inflation, the private health protection gap becomes the defining commercial opportunity.

Breadth without depth

The UCS covers about 74% of the population [Frontiers 2024], yet budgets lag the cost of care, and bypassing to private providers pushes out-of-pocket spending to 11.3% of health outlays [UHC Study 2024].

Why the gap widens

Health expenditure sat at 4.37% of GDP in 2023, rising only to ~4.49% by 2028 [World Bank 2023], against a Ministry framework capping total health spend at 5% of GDP. Yet long-term-care costs for dependent elderly are projected to reach THB 500 bn by 2040, with elderly healthcare costs set to triple by 2032 [NER 2024]. The fiscal envelope cannot absorb the ageing curve alone.

The commercial read

The middle class increasingly self-funds the difference between basic public care and the standard it expects — private rooms, faster access, chronic-disease management. That is exactly the demand health riders and standalone health policies capture, explaining the 13.7% premium growth in the segment [TLAA 2024]. The protection gap is not a market failure to lament; it is the addressable pool to underwrite responsibly.

Section 08 · Market Sizing & Product Mix
009 / 018

Premiums by line: health riders and annuities lead the mix

Life dominates the premium pool, but the growth is concentrated in protection and retirement products. Whole-life accumulation is mature; health riders, critical illness, and annuities carry the demographic tailwind into 2027.

Life leads, non-life steadies

Life premium of THB 654 bn (+3.2%) dwarfs non-life at THB 286 bn (+0.5%) [TLAA 2024] [TGIA 2024]. Within non-life, health rose a striking 24% in H1 2025 while motor crept up 2% [TGIA 2025] — the ageing signal is visible across both books.

Riders and annuities are the engine

Whole-life accounts for ~60% of life GWP, but supplementary riders hold 22.3% and grow faster [GlobalData 2025]. Health and CI riders are forecast at a 5.4% CAGR to 2028 [GlobalData 2025]. Annuities — paying to ages 90–99 — are the natural retirement-income answer for a super-aged society [Muang Thai 2025].

The USD frame

The total market is about USD 31 bn in 2024, projected to USD 38 bn by 2029 at a 4.1% CAGR [Statista 2025]; the life sector alone is forecast to reach USD 23 bn by 2028 [GlobalData 2025].

Market position

AIA is the clear leader of Thai life insurance, writing THB 167 bn and 25.5% of premium in 2024 [OIC 2024] — nearly double the next carrier. Its scale is built on the country's largest tied-agent force and a long-running emphasis on health and protection rather than pure savings products.

Why it wins the ageing customer

The leader's product depth across health riders, critical illness, and senior life maps directly onto the demographic shift. As health and CI premiums grow 13.7% a year industry-wide [TLAA 2024], AIA's protection tilt positions it to capture a disproportionate share of that pool.

Strategic posture

Group capital strength supports long-duration health and annuity guarantees under the tightening RBC and IFRS 17 regime [OIC 2025]. AIA pairs the trust of face-to-face agents — the channel older buyers prefer — with steady investment in digital underwriting and claims, positioning it to defend share as the market re-platforms.

Strengths & watchlist

  • Agent scale: largest face-to-face force, decisive for senior buyers
  • Protection tilt: health & CI focus rides the demographic tailwind
  • Capital depth: regional parent eases RBC / IFRS 17 burden
  • Watch: FWD's digital banca momentum; copayment communication risk

Section 11 · Regulation
014 / 018

A regime tuned for sustainability, not just growth

The OIC's 2025 agenda — copayment, risk-based capital, IFRS 17, and ESG — is explicitly about long-term solvency as the demographic and medical-cost curves steepen. Carriers that align pricing, capital, and disclosure early will out-earn those that resist.

Copayment: the demand-side brake

Effective for individual policies issued on or after 19 March 2025 [OIC 2025], the copayment clause shares claims cost with policyholders to control over-utilisation and stabilise premium rates. Built under the New Health Standard (OIC Orders 14–15/2564), it is a deliberate tool against medical inflation of 8–10% a year [Bangkok Post 2024].

RBC and IFRS 17

Risk-based capital and IFRS 17 sharpen the cost of long-duration guarantees [OIC 2025]. Annuity and whole-life books now carry transparent capital charges, advantaging insurers with disciplined asset-liability management and deep parental capital.

What it means for participants

The cost-control turn favours scale, actuarial sophistication, and clear customer communication. Carriers that explain copayment well retain trust and protect loss ratios; those that surprise policyholders risk lapse and anti-selection.

  • Winners: capital-strong, health-focused leaders with strong actuarial functions
  • Pressured: sub-scale insurers with thin capital under RBC / IFRS 17
  • Pivotal: distribution that can explain copayment without eroding trust
  • Emerging: standardised products easing comparison and price competition

The OIC's broader 2025 framework lists 13 mechanisms to strengthen the financial system against new global risks [OIC 2025] — a clear signal that resilience, not headline growth, defines the next cycle.

Section 12 · Technology in Distribution & Claims
015 / 018

Technology shifts from pilot to underwriting core

AI in underwriting, claims, and fraud detection has moved from differentiator to table stakes across the global industry [InsurTech 2025]. For Thai insurers serving an ageing book, the prize is faster onboarding, controlled loss ratios, and channel economics that keep trusted human advisers viable.

From experiment to infrastructure

Leading insurers and insurtechs now run machine-learning models across customer service, underwriting, claims, and fraud prediction [InsurTech 2025]. By 2026 these are no longer competitive advantages but baseline capability, and Thai carriers are integrating them into core workflows.

Fraud and loss-ratio control

AI fraud-detection has cut fraudulent claims by as much as ~30% in documented deployments [InsurTech 2025]. Paired with the copayment regime, this gives Thai health insurers a second lever against medical-cost escalation.

Channel: augment, don't replace

Older buyers still trust face-to-face advice and bancassurance. The realistic Thai pattern is AI-augmented agents and banca — digital tools speeding quotes, underwriting, and claims while the human relationship anchors the sale.

Six technology applications reshaping Thai insurers

Translation into operating practice will separate 2027 winners from laggards. These use cases span customer acquisition through claims control, each observable in Thailand or its closest regional benchmarks today.

01 · UNDERWRITING
Algorithmic health underwriting
ML models price health and CI risk for older applicants faster and more granularly, expanding insurable ages that historically faced blanket exclusions. Critical for capturing the 60+ pool profitably.
Benchmark: pan-Asian carriers
02 · CLAIMS
Straight-through claims automation
AI triages and settles routine health claims without manual touch, cutting cycle time and cost. Paired with copayment rules, it enforces consistent, auditable adjudication at scale.
Status: high maturity globally
03 · FRAUD
Claims fraud detection
Pattern models flag anomalous health claims, reducing fraudulent payouts by up to ~30% in leading deployments [InsurTech 2025]. A direct loss-ratio lever as medical costs rise.
Anchor: ~30% fraud reduction
04 · DISTRIBUTION
Agent & banca enablement
Recommendation engines surface the right annuity or health rider for each customer at the point of advice, lifting cross-sell across the ageing lifecycle while keeping the trusted human in the loop.
Observable: bancassurance leaders
05 · RETENTION
Lapse & persistency modelling
Predictive models identify policies at risk of lapse — heightened where copayment changes expectations — enabling proactive outreach that protects long-duration health and annuity books.
Tie-in: copayment communication
06 · CARE ANALYTICS
Long-term-care risk analytics
Emerging models project dependency and care-cost trajectories for the elderly — foundational for pricing LTC products against the THB 500 bn 2040 care-cost wave [NER 2024].
Stage: early, high strategic value

Section 13 · Outlook to 2032
017 / 018

Outlook to 2032: modest headline, decisive mix shift

We expect Thai premium growth to stay in the low-to-mid single digits, with the demographic transition driving above-trend expansion in health riders, annuities, and — late in the horizon — long-term care. The carriers that win are those that reposition toward the ageing customer now.

Base case

Industry premium compounds around 4% to 2029 [Statista 2025], with life reaching ~USD 23 bn by 2028 [GlobalData 2025]. Health and CI outpace the market at high single to low double digits [TLAA 2024] [GlobalData 2025] as the protection gap and medical inflation persist.

Upside & downside

Upside: faster middle-class uptake of private health, tax-incentivised annuities, and LTC product innovation. Downside: an economic slowdown compressing discretionary protection spend, or copayment friction depressing health-rider sales near term.

The strategic imperative

The demographic curve is the most reliable variable in this market. Reposition the book toward the 60+ lifecycle — health, annuity, LTC — and invest in capital strength and AI-augmented distribution. Sub-scale, savings-tilted insurers face the steepest adjustment.
