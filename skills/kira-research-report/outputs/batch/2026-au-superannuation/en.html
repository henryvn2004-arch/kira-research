The test is the consolidation engine

The Your Future, Your Super performance test scores a product against a benchmark built from its own asset allocation; miss by more than 0.50% and it fails, and two consecutive fails close the product to new members [APRA 2025]. That binary outcome has done more to thin the field than any merger incentive — members in failed products fell from ~1 million to 8,500 [APRA 2025], and weak funds now seek a stronger partner before the gate closes.

Scale has become the strategy

Nine funds above AUD 100 bn now hold 67.5% of sector assets, up from 63.1% a year earlier [Conexus 2026]. AustralianSuper passed AUD 410 bn and Australian Retirement Trust AUD 351 bn [AustralianSuper 2025; ART 2025]. Up to 21 funds with ~AUD 400 bn could merge in the near-to-medium term [Conexus 2026] — the next leg of concentration is already visible.

Five strategic implications for market participants

Where trustees, asset managers and service providers should focus capital and franchise effort over the next 24 months — ranked by the asymmetry of outcome as the test gate and the merger cycle interact.

01
Treat the test as a binary cliff, not a KPI
A miss beyond 0.50% fails, and a second consecutive fail closes the product to new members [APRA 2025]. Manage strategic asset allocation against the benchmark portfolio continuously — not annually. The cost of a single fail is reputational and a forced merger conversation, not a marketing footnote.

02
Sub-AUD 50 bn funds: choose the partner
Up to 21 funds with ~AUD 400 bn face credible merger pressure [Conexus 2026]. Funds below scale should select an acquirer from strength — clean book, passing test record — rather than negotiate after a fail. Member transfer terms and brand survival favour the early mover.

Internalise where scale pays back

Harden unlisted-asset valuation now

Section 05 · System Sizing

System sizing: from the pool to the mega-fund tier

The AUD 4.5 trn total splits into an APRA-regulated core of AUD 3.2 trn, within which nine mega-funds now hold the controlling share. Each step down the funnel is where the test and merger pressure bite hardest.

The total pool

Total superannuation assets reached AUD 4.5 trn at December 2025, up 7.9% year on year [APRA 2026]. The system remains in net inflow: the guarantee rate hit its legislated 12% ceiling in July 2025 [ATO 2025], and the working-age contribution base still exceeds drawdown.

The regulated core

Of the total, AUD 3.2 trn sits in APRA-regulated funds [APRA 2026] — the segment the performance test governs. The balance is mostly self-managed funds outside the test perimeter. The tested products covered 62% of regulated assets by value in 2025 [APRA 2025].

The mega-fund tier

Nine funds above AUD 100 bn hold 67.5% of sector assets [Conexus 2026]. The top two alone — AustralianSuper and Australian Retirement Trust — control roughly AUD 760 bn combined [Kira estimates].

Section 06 · Demand Drivers
008 / 019

Demand drivers: the 12% mandate meets an ageing base

Three forces set the system's trajectory — a now-maxed contribution rate, a member base tilting toward drawdown, and a covenant obliging funds to manage the retirement phase. Inflow growth is structural; the competitive fight is over who keeps members as they retire.

Contribution growth, now at its ceiling

The superannuation guarantee reached its legislated 12% rate on 1 July 2025, the final step of an increase begun at 9% in 2013-14 [ATO 2025]. No further rise is scheduled. Mandatory inflow therefore plateaus as a share of wages; future asset growth leans more on investment return and less on rate increases than the prior decade.

Concessional contribution caps held at AUD 30,000 for 2025-26, with a maximum contribution base near AUD 62,500 a quarter [ATO 2025]. Voluntary contributions and the wealth-channel top-up become the marginal growth lever as the headline rate stops climbing.

An ageing, drawdown-tilting base

The system is maturing: the leading edge of the membership has shifted from pure accumulation toward retirement-phase drawdown [Kira estimates]. Net inflow stays positive for now, but the decumulation cohort grows faster than the contributor cohort — the demographic that reshapes product demand.

The covenant reframes the contest

The 2022 Retirement Income Covenant requires trustees to formulate a strategy helping members balance income, risk and access in retirement [Treasury 2022]. It converts retirement from an exit event into a product line — income streams, longevity solutions and advice the performance test does not score.

  • Income products — account-based pensions plus emerging longevity-pooled options to manage the risk of outliving savings.
  • Advice at scale — digital and hybrid guidance to serve millions approaching drawdown without per-member adviser cost.
  • Member retention — retiring members are the highest-balance, most contestable cohort; scaled funds court them aggressively [Kira estimates].

Funds that win the accumulation test but neglect the covenant risk losing balances at the most valuable moment — the point of retirement, when switching is most considered.

Section 08 · Test Mechanics
010 / 019

Test mechanics and the failure history that thinned the field

A two-part test — benchmark-relative return plus an administration-fee check — with a hard 0.50% fail line. MySuper failures have collapsed to zero; the pressure has migrated to platform trustee-directed products, where weak legacy books concentrate.

How the test works

Each product is measured against a benchmark portfolio built from its own strategic asset allocation, plus an administration-fee comparison against the category median [APRA 2025]. Underperform the combined test by more than 0.50% and the product fails. A first fail compels member notification; a second consecutive fail closes the product to new members [APRA 2025].

MySuper: clean, but TDPs exposed

In 2025 all 52 MySuper products passed, and all 374 non-platform trustee-directed products passed; seven of 137 platform products failed [APRA 2025]. The 2024 round saw 37 platform products fail, concentrated in two trustees [APRA 2024]. The risk has migrated decisively to legacy platform books.

The cumulative effect

Members in products that did not pass fell from ~1 million to 8,500 since launch [APRA 2025] — the clearest evidence the gate works as a cleanup mechanism.

Position

AustralianSuper passed AUD 410 bn at December 2025 [AustralianSuper 2025], the clear scale leader with ~3.7 m members. Roughly half of assets are invested offshore, supported by investment teams across seven global offices including London, New York and Beijing [AustralianSuper 2025].

Strategy

The fund runs near 60% of assets in-house and targets 75% by 2030 [AustralianSuper 2025]. It expects to save over AUD 1.3 bn a year in fees by decade-end by building internal teams — the clearest expression of scale-as-strategy in the sector.

Watch items

  • Execution, not size — a new CIO inherits a ~AUD 410 bn book where the task is operating the scale, not adding it [Kira estimates].
  • Private-market governance — about 20% in private markets puts valuation discipline under regulator focus [ASIC 2025].
  • Operational resilience — the 2025 credential attacks tested member-service capacity at scale [HSF 2025].

Implication

The leader sets the cost benchmark every other fund is tested against. As internalisation matures, the fee gap to sub-scale rivals widens — reinforcing the case for mid-tier mergers.

Position

ART holds AUD 351 bn across ~2.4 m members [ART 2025], the clear number two. It was formed in February 2022 from the merger of QSuper and Sunsuper — still the largest fund merger in Australian history [ART 2025].

Strategy

Consolidation is the operating model, not a one-off. ART has positioned itself as a preferred merger partner for smaller funds seeking scale before the test gate, adding member books and assets through successor-fund transfers rather than organic growth alone [Kira estimates].

Watch items

  • Integration load — serial mergers demand administration and platform consolidation that can strain service quality [Kira estimates].
  • Private-market mix — roughly 20% in private markets carries the same valuation-governance burden as peers [ASIC 2025].
  • Brand dilution — absorbing varied member cultures tests retention at the retirement-phase margin.

Implication

ART is the natural home for many of the 21 merger-candidate funds [Conexus 2026]. Each successful transfer compounds its scale advantage and shortens the consolidation runway for the rest of the tail.

Aware Super ranks among Australia's top three funds by assets [SuperGuide 2025], rooted in the NSW public sector and grown through earlier mergers. It competes on cost and member outcomes rather than the scale-internalisation race the top two are running.

Private markets and the valuation question

Scale pushed funds into unlisted assets for return and diversification — the top two each hold about 20% in private markets. As the system grows, the regulator's focus has shifted from how much funds hold to how honestly they value it.

Why funds went unlisted

Mega-funds allocate heavily to infrastructure, property, private equity and credit — AustralianSuper and ART each near 20% in private markets [Conversation 2025]. Unlisted assets offer return premia, diversification and a smoother reported path than listed markets, and scale gives the largest funds direct access to deals smaller funds cannot reach.

But the same illiquidity that smooths returns also defers price discovery. Valuations rely on periodic models, not daily marks — a feature in calm markets, a fault line under stress.

The liquidity dimension

Regulators have warned on liquidity management where illiquid holdings are large and member switching is instant [Clayton Utz 2025]. A run-on-the-fund scenario meeting a stale valuation is the tail risk the prudential framework now targets directly.

The regulator turns to valuation

A December 2024 APRA review found 12 of 23 licensees needed material improvement in valuation governance, liquidity frameworks, or both, to meet Prudential Standard SPS 530 [APRA SPS530 2024]. ASIC's 2025 work (Reports 814 and 816) extended scrutiny to unlisted-asset valuation and audit quality across the sector [ASIC 2025].

  • Independent valuation — external specialists engaged more frequently; internal-only marks under challenge.
  • Audit cost — rising for funds with material illiquid books, a fixed cost that penalises sub-scale [Kira estimates].
  • 2026 priorities — ASIC has named unlisted-asset opacity a continuing enforcement focus [ASIC 2025].

Valuation governance is becoming a de facto scale test — another cost the largest funds absorb more easily than the tail.

AI in superannuation: efficiency upside against a sharper threat

AI sits at adoption stage two of three across fund operations — past pilots in member services, before deep integration in investment and valuation. The 2025 coordinated attacks made the security case as forcefully as the efficiency case.

The efficiency case

Scale funds serve millions; AI compresses the cost of advice, servicing and back-office processing as the decumulation cohort multiplies servicing demand [Kira estimates]. Internalising leaders extend the logic to investment operations — spreading, monitoring, valuation analytics.

The threat case

In 2025, coordinated credential attacks hit major funds; up to 600 stolen passwords were used in fraud and at least AUD 500,000 was taken from four accounts [HSF 2025]. The sector launched a joint cyber exercise, Operation Honey Bee II [APRA 2025b].

Outlook, scenarios and methodology

The pool keeps compounding; the open question is structure. Our base case sees mega-fund share past three-quarters by FY28 as the merger pipeline clears — with asset growth tied to return, not a rising contribution rate.

BASE CASE
~76% mega share FY28
Pool reaches ~AUD 5.4 trn by 2028 [Kira estimates]. Most of the 21 merger candidates pair off; the public-offer count drifts toward the mid-50s. The top three extend their fee and internalisation edge, and the tested perimeter stays clean at the MySuper level.
UPSIDE
Orderlyconsolidation
Strong returns and smooth integrations let scaled funds cut fees further and lead on decumulation. Valuation governance lifts sector-wide, defusing the unlisted-asset tail risk before it is tested by a drawdown event.
DOWNSIDE
Valuationshock
A market drawdown collides with stale unlisted marks and instant switching, forcing repricing and liquidity strain [Kira estimates]. Confidence and merger terms suffer; integration risk in serial roll-ups surfaces as service failures.
