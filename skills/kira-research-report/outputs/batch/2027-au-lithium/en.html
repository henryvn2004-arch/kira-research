The supply base

Australia produced 113,500 tonnes LCE in 2025 — roughly 33% of global output [OCE 2025] — from three large hard-rock spodumene operations in Western Australia. Greenbushes alone accounts for ~40% of global hard-rock supply [Geoscience AU]. The challenge has never been ore; it has been that 94% of Australian spodumene is exported as concentrate, almost entirely to China for processing [Kira estimates based on customs data].

The 2027 shift

Covalent Lithium's 50,000 tpa Kwinana refinery achieved first product in July 2025 and ramps toward nameplate by mid-2027. Policy support arrives simultaneously: the 10% Critical Minerals Production Tax Incentive triggers from 1 July 2027 [ATO 2025], effectively subsidising each tonne of domestic hydroxide output for 13 years. Korean buyers — locked in a structural hydroxide deficit and facing China exposure — are the natural offtake counterparty.

Korean buyers prefer floor-plus-market-share contracts. For Australian refiners, the optimal structure blends a hydroxide price floor with a spot-linkage upside: floor eliminates cashflow volatility below breakeven; upside participates in the 2027–2030 recovery [Kira estimates]. Volume commitments — not spot — are the currency Korean procurement teams negotiate hardest.

Diversify beyond China — 94% export concentration is a structural liability

Korea's NMC and NCA battery production requires lithium hydroxide — LFP's shift toward carbonate affects China-market batteries, not Korean-manufactured premium packs [Kira estimates]. Australia's hydroxide ramp lands on the right side of the cathode chemistry divide for its natural customer base.

Section 04 · Macro context
07 / 21

Macro indicators framing the 2027 base

Australia enters 2027 as a commodity exporter increasingly oriented toward strategic allies — and with a policy architecture that explicitly rewards domestic value-add over raw material export.

Critical minerals as declared national priority

The Critical Minerals Strategy 2023–2030 commits AUD 7 billion in production tax incentive support over the decade [DISR 2023]. The strategy explicitly targets the transition from raw-ore export to processed-chemical supply — lithium hydroxide from spodumene is the headline use case.

Korea and Japan trade architecture deepening

Australia–Korea critical minerals engagement has accelerated since 2022, with POSCO, LGES, and Samsung SDI all executing either equity investments or binding offtake deals. The AU–Korea FTA provides preferential tariff treatment, and the IRA's FTA-partner critical minerals requirement gives Australian-processed hydroxide a routing advantage into US battery supply chains via Korean cell manufacturers [Kira estimates].

AUD exchange rate as margin lever

Lithium is priced in USD; Australian producers' cost bases are largely AUD-denominated. A weaker AUD — trading around 0.63–0.65 USD through 2025 [RBA] — inflates AUD-equivalent revenue per tonne sold, meaningfully widening operating margins at any given USD spodumene price.

Section 05 · Market sizing
09 / 21

Production, export values & price trajectory

Australia's lithium complex is the largest globally by ore output — but value-chain position remains overwhelmingly upstream. The 2027 earnings recovery is price-driven, not yet refinery-driven.

Production heading to 127,000 tonnes LCE by 2027

Output reached 113,500 kt LCE in 2025, a marginal +1.8% against a year of extensive curtailments [OCE 2025]. The 2026 ramp is sharper: +6% to ~120,300 kt as Greenbushes' Chemical Grade Plant 2 expansion and Pilgangoora's P680 contribute fully. Through 2027, a 4.9% CAGR trajectory takes output toward ~127,000 kt LCE [OCE 2025].

Export earnings recovering from FY25 trough

Earnings collapsed from AUD 16.0 bn in FY23 to AUD 4.8 bn in FY25 as spodumene prices fell below USD 800/t [OCE 2025]. The OCE forecasts AUD 6.8 bn in FY27 [OCE 2025] — a meaningful recovery, though still less than half the FY23 peak. The gap closes as price, not volume, leads.

China concentration: 94% of spodumene — a structural risk

In H1 2025, China absorbed 1.86 mn of 1.98 mn total tonnes exported [Kira estimates based on customs data]. Indonesia emerged as the second-largest destination (+165% YoY to 66,678 t) but remains a rounding error by volume [Kira estimates]. This concentration means any Chinese processing policy shift compresses the entire Australian mining sector simultaneously.

Section 06 · Refining capacity build
11 / 21

Australia's hydroxide refining: from pilot to scale

The gap between Australia's position as the world's largest lithium miner and its near-zero presence in processed-chemical supply is closing — but more slowly than the policy rhetoric implied.

Covalent Kwinana — the live refinery

Covalent Lithium (Wesfarmers 50% / SQM 50%) achieved first lithium hydroxide product at its 50,000 tpa Kwinana refinery in July 2025 [Covalent 2025]. Intermittent odour engineering works delayed full production ramp; engineering completion was targeted for mid-2026. Nameplate capacity is expected by mid-2027 [Covalent 2025]. The feed comes from Mt Holland — a 308 million-tonne ore reserve [Covalent 2025] — which IMC began contract mining operations to accelerate from February 2026.

Kemerton — idled, creating commercial space

Albemarle's Kemerton refinery (linked to Greenbushes concentrate) was placed into care and maintenance after the company cancelled its second production train expansion [Albemarle AR 2025]. The idling removes a major competitor from the domestic hydroxide market — but also signals that lithium hydroxide refining in Australia is not yet commercially self-sustaining without price recovery and policy support.

Pipeline: What comes next

Several projects sit in pre-FID, contingent on the CMPTI trigger and price confirmation: POSCO's Korea-based processing of Australian spodumene (an intermediate pathway), and a range of domestic refinery concepts aligned with the federal government's downstream ambitions. The realistic domestic hydroxide capacity picture for 2027 is Kwinana at 50,000 tpa — not materially more [Kira estimates].

Section 07 · Korean offtake landscape
13 / 21

Korean battery makers' Australia supply architecture

Three distinct engagement models have emerged: equity investment plus proportional offtake (POSCO–MinRes), binding long-term hydroxide contracts tied to a specific new supply source (LGES–Liontown), and spot-plus-reserve hybrid deals for diversification without equity commitment.

MODEL A · EQUITY + PROPORTIONAL OFFTAKE
POSCO Holdings – MinRes (LithCo JV)

POSCO acquired a 30% stake in MinRes' operational lithium division for USD 765 mn (~AUD 1.2 bn) in November 2025 [MinRes ASX 2025]. The transaction gave POSCO indirect ~15% ownership in both Wodgina and Mt Marion, plus spodumene offtake proportional to its 30% interest. POSCO processes the Australian concentrate at its Korean hydroxide refineries, then supplies Korean and global battery makers — effectively integrating the Australian mine into the Korean battery supply chain without building Australian refining capacity.

Volume: proportional to 30% stake · Pricing: spot-linked, AUD-denominated supply cost
MODEL B · BINDING LONG-TERM OFFTAKE
LG Energy Solution – Liontown Resources

LGES holds a binding spodumene offtake agreement from Liontown's Kathleen Valley (alongside Tesla and Ford) [Liontown AR 2025]. Kathleen Valley produced over 300,000 wmt of spodumene concentrate in its first 11 months of commercial production [Liontown AR 2025]. The Liontown deal is spodumene-level — not hydroxide — with processing occurring outside Australia. It secures feedstock volume and price certainty for LGES' Korean hydroxide-to-cathode operations.

Volume: undisclosed; multi-year · Pricing: market-referenced with floor mechanism
Pricing mechanics: what Korean buyers accept
· Floor price: typically USD 700–900/t spodumene (2025 structure) [Kira estimates]
· Market linkage: spot or weighted-average quarterly reference price
· Upside sharing: buyer captures discount below market; seller retains upside above floor
· Volume commitment: 3–10 years at defined tonnage bands
· Hydroxide-level deals: rarer, require producer refinery certainty first

The structural driver is Korean cathode-maker exposure: China currently supplies 80% of South Korea's lithium hydroxide [Kira estimates based on Korea trade data]. Any supply-chain disruption propagates directly to NMC cathode output, which POSCO Chemical, Ecopro, and L&F supply to LGES, Samsung SDI, and SK On.

Policy architecture: CMPTI and the critical minerals strategy

The policy framework explicitly targets value-add processing — not ore export — and the CMPTI is the financial mechanism that converts that political intent into a bankable project cost structure.

The CMPTI: 10% refundable offset, 13 years

The Critical Minerals Production Tax Incentive provides a 10% refundable tax offset on the eligible cost of processing critical minerals in Australia [ATO 2025]. Eligibility commences 1 July 2027 and runs to 30 June 2040 — a maximum 10-year benefit window per operator (first production must start between 2027 and 2040). Lithium hydroxide produced from spodumene is explicitly within scope [DISR 2023].

Critical Minerals Strategy 2023–2030

The broader strategy commits AUD 7 bn in production tax credit support over the decade [DISR 2023]. It is designed to move Australia from raw-material exporter to processed-chemical supplier — and explicitly uses the CMPTI as the primary market incentive, supplemented by the National Reconstruction Fund and critical minerals offtake facilitation programs.

IRA FTA linkage: routing Australian hydroxide through Korea to the US

The US Inflation Reduction Act requires EV battery critical minerals to be sourced from FTA partners [IEA GCMO 2025]. Australia has an FTA with the US (AUSFTA). Australian spodumene processed in Korea — itself an FTA partner — creates an IRA-qualifying supply chain through the KORUS/AUSFTA nexus [Kira estimates]. This trade architecture is precisely what the POSCO–MinRes and LGES–Liontown structures are designed to exploit.

Section 10 · Demand drivers & risks
19 / 21

Demand is structural; risks are chemical and geopolitical

The EV cycle is now irreversible at the aggregate level — but the cathode chemistry split between LFP (carbonate) and NMC/NCA (hydroxide) creates differentiated demand trajectories for the two main lithium compounds.

EV + ESS demand: compound growth through 2030

Global EV sales are forecast at ~27 mn units in 2027, up from 20.3 mn in 2025 [IEA GCMO 2025]. Energy storage systems (ESS) add a second demand vector entirely decoupled from EV cycles. Total lithium demand in both compound forms is projected toward 1,100 kt LCE in 2027 [Kira estimates]. This demand trajectory survives any individual market's EV subsidy cycle — it is diversified across 30+ EV markets globally.

LFP vs NMC: the chemical bifurcation

LFP batteries (lithium carbonate chemistry) are dominant in China's passenger EV and ESS markets. NMC and NCA (lithium hydroxide chemistry) dominate Korean and Western battery output — higher energy density for performance applications [Kira estimates]. Korea's NMC orientation means growing Korean battery capacity creates growing hydroxide demand, and Australia's refinery build is precisely positioned for this customer.

China processing risk: the asymmetric exposure

China controls ~70% of global lithium refining capacity [IEA GCMO 2025]. A Chinese lithium export restriction — analogous to its 2023 graphite export controls — would simultaneously tighten global hydroxide supply while reducing the value of Australian spodumene to Chinese processors. Australian operators with domestic refining capacity or Korean JV partners are structurally hedged against this scenario.

Five-year outlook: forecast to 2030

The base case sees Australian lithium output reaching ~150,000 kt LCE by 2030, export earnings recovering toward AUD 9–11 bn as prices firm, and domestic hydroxide capacity scaling to 80–100 ktpa as the CMPTI draws new investment. Korea's share of Australian offtake rises from under 10% today toward 25–30% by 2030. The tail risk is a sustained LFP displacement of NMC in premium segments — a lower-probability scenario that nevertheless warrants structural hedging.
