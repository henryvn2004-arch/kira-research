Malaysia's healthcare-travel economy crosses the RM3 billion line

A 1.84 million-traveller inbound base, a Penang cluster compounding above 25% a year, and a depreciated ringgit hand Malaysia genuine momentum. The 2026-2030 question is whether scale and specialist depth can match Thailand's brand rather than only undercut its price.

Where the market stands

Malaysia drew 1.84 million healthcare travellers in 2025, generating RM3.34 billion [Malay Mail 2026], up from RM2.72 billion on 1.6 million arrivals in 2024 [MHTC 2025]. Indonesia alone supplies roughly 65% of inbound volume [MIDA 2024]; China, India, Singapore and the UK round out an 80%-of-volume top five [Malay Mail 2026]. The council has declared 2026 the Malaysia Year of Medical Tourism and set a RM12 billion revenue goal for 2030 [MHTC 2025].

The competitive reality

Thailand remains the regional reference brand, drawing 1.4 million-plus international patients across a far larger medical-and-wellness economy [GM Insights 2024]. Malaysia competes on a sharper cost line — IVF near USD 3,000-4,000 a cycle against USD 8,000-12,000 in Thailand [BestClinicAbroad 2025] — plus English-fluent specialists and JCI-accredited care. The risk is a thinning specialist pipeline at 2.3 doctors per 1,000 [FocusMalaysia 2025].

Five strategic implications for market participants

Five forces shape the competitive contest with Thailand through 2030. Each is anchored to disclosed data and KIRA's synthesis across Southeast Asian healthcare-travel flows.

01 · DEMAND
Indonesia concentration is both moat and risk
Indonesia supplies ~65% of inbound volume and 70%-plus of Penang's foreign patients. Proximity and language hold the moat, but Jakarta's "Gerbang Sehat" repatriation drive targets the same outflow. Diversify the source mix before it concentrates further.
Anchor: ~970k Indonesian travellers, 2025
02 · PRICE
Cost edge is real but not the only contest
Malaysia undercuts Thailand on complex care — IVF at roughly a fifth of US cost and below Bangkok — yet Thailand wins on brand, dental and cosmetic flow. Compete on outcomes and packaged experience, not discount alone.
Anchor: USD 3-4k IVF cycle, MY
03 · CLUSTERS
Penang volume, KL complexity
Penang runs a high-volume Indonesian playbook (RM1.14 bn, 527k patients); Kuala Lumpur anchors complex cardiac and oncology through IJN and large groups. Two clusters, two distinct value propositions — capital should follow each on its own logic.
Anchor: RM1.14 bn Penang 2025
04 · CAPACITY
Workforce is the binding constraint
At 2.3 doctors per 1,000 and a steep fall in house officers, specialist supply — not demand — caps the RM12 bn ambition. Private medical-travel margins can fund retention, but only if matched by training investment.
Anchor: 2.3 doctors / 1,000
05 · POLICY
MYMT 2026 is a forcing function
The Year of Medical Tourism, flagship-hospital recognition and the RM12 bn 2030 target create coordinated tailwinds. Operators aligned to the flagship programme and integrated-care positioning capture the policy dividend first.
Anchor: 2030 RM12 bn goal

Section 04 · Demand Engine
007 / 018

A market that has tripled its revenue base since the rebound

Healthcare-travel revenue climbed from a pandemic trough to RM3.34 billion in 2025, a 21%-plus annual step in the latest year. Volume growth is real, but revenue per traveller — not headcount alone — now carries the trajectory toward the 2030 goal.

Revenue has outpaced arrivals

Arrivals rose 14% to 1.6 million in 2024 while revenue grew 21% to RM2.72 billion [MHTC 2025] — evidence that average treatment value, not just footfall, is lifting. In 2025 the council reported 1.84 million travellers and RM3.34 billion [Malay Mail 2026]. Non-medical spend adds a further layer, averaging roughly RM2,680 per visitor [MHTC 2025].

The 2030 gap is steep but coordinated

Reaching RM12 billion by 2030 [MHTC 2025] implies a compound pace near 29% a year from the 2025 base [Kira estimates] — well above the recent low-20s trend. Closing it requires higher-acuity case mix and capacity additions, not Indonesian volume alone. KIRA models a base case nearer RM8-9 billion absent a step-change in complex-care throughput [Kira estimates].

Section 05 · Demand Engine
008 / 018

Where the patients come from — and what could change it

A single source market dominates. Indonesia accounts for roughly two-thirds of inbound volume, and the top five markets together supply about 80%. That concentration is the engine of growth and the clearest strategic exposure.

The Indonesian flow

Indonesia contributed about 65% of Malaysia's healthcare travellers [MIDA 2024], with roughly 970,000 Indonesian medical travellers recorded in 2025 [Kompas 2025]. Penang draws the bulk: Indonesians are over 70% of the island's foreign patients [Kompas 2025]. The pull is structural — short flights from Medan and Sumatra, shared language, and trust built over two decades.

The repatriation counter-current

Indonesia estimates roughly Rp 165 trillion leaves the country each year as some 2 million citizens seek care abroad [Kompas 2025]. Jakarta's "Gerbang Sehat" programme and a Medan specialist build-out aim to retain that spend. Diversifying toward China, India, the Gulf and intra-ASEAN flows is the hedge against a slow erosion of the core market [Kira estimates].

Section 07 · Cluster Analysis
010 / 018

Penang: the volume cluster compounding above 25% a year

Sixteen private hospitals on a single island turned 527,176 foreign patients into RM1.14 billion in 2025 — about a third of national revenue from one cluster. Island Hospital's flagship status anchors a catchment that is overwhelmingly Indonesian.

The numbers behind the cluster

Penang's foreign-patient count rose 25.9% to 527,176 in 2025, lifting revenue 26.6% to RM1.136 billion from RM898 million in 2024 [Penang Health 2026]. Sixteen private hospitals carry the load [RTM 2026]. Island Hospital alone draws close to one in three of Malaysia's inbound foreign patients and holds flagship recognition [MHTC 2025].

Strengths and the single-market risk

The cluster's edge is a tight, repeatable funnel — Sumatran proximity, Bahasa-fluent coordinators, and packaged screening-to-treatment journeys. Gleneagles, Loh Guan Lye, Penang Adventist and Pantai Penang complete the roster [Orissa 2024]. The exposure is singular: over 70% of patients are Indonesian [Kompas 2025], so any retention success in Medan lands here first.

Section 10 · The Thailand Contest
013 / 018

Benchmarking Bangkok: bigger brand, broader catchment

Thailand draws over 1.4 million international patients into a medical economy several times Malaysia's headline size, with a more global source mix spanning the Gulf, Europe and South Asia. Its lead is in brand equity and catchment breadth, not unit price.

Scale and source diversity

Thailand attracts 1.4 million-plus international patients a year, over 60% travelling specifically for treatment [GM Insights 2024]. Bumrungrad alone serves more than a million patients annually with a heavy overseas share and a seventh JCI re-accreditation in 2024 [GM Insights 2024]. Source markets span the Gulf — Qatar, Oman, Kuwait — plus Europe, the US and ASEAN [Thailand.go.th 2025].

A deliberate hub strategy

Bangkok pairs hospitals with medical and long-stay visas, an LTR retiree route, and a wellness build-out that grew sharply year on year [Thailand.go.th 2025]. Healthcare spending of over THB 690 billion in 2025 underpins the system at roughly 3.4% of GDP [Thailand.go.th 2025]. Malaysia's reply is depth in cardiac, oncology and regulated cell therapy at lower cost [my1health 2025].

Section 11 · The Thailand Contest
014 / 018

The cost-quality frontier is shifting under both rivals

Malaysia's price advantage is real on complex care, but Thailand competes on brand, dental and cosmetic flow. Both undercut Western prices by 70-85%; the durable edge now comes from outcomes, accreditation and packaged experience rather than headline discount.

Reading the price spread

On fertility, Malaysia is the cost leader — IVF near USD 3,000-4,000 a cycle versus USD 8,000-12,000 in Thailand and USD 20,000-30,000 in the US [BestClinicAbroad 2025]. On cardiac bypass the two converge in a USD 15,000-25,000 band, with Malaysia often marginally cheaper on complex work [my1health 2025]. Thailand keeps its lead in dental and cosmetic volume [BangkokHealth 2025].

Where the contest actually settles

Price parity on major surgery means differentiation moves to outcomes and trust [Kira estimates]. JCI accreditation, robotic-surgery depth, English fluency and seamless coordination are the new battleground. Malaysia's task is to convert its cost story into an outcomes story before Thailand's brand premium hardens into the default regional choice.

Section 12 · Technology
015 / 018

Digital and AI move from pilot to patient-facing edge

Robotic surgery, AI imaging and a national hospital-information backbone are becoming part of the medical-travel pitch. Malaysia has deployed AI tools across 16 hospitals and 2,400-plus clinics — capability that can lift the outcomes story the cost narrative cannot.

National scaffolding

A National AI Office launched in late 2024 coordinates deployment, with RM3.29 billion of approved AI projects in 1H25 [TradeGov 2025]. The health ministry extended a cloud clinic-management system to over 2,400 facilities and is piloting AI for lung-nodule, breast and TB screening [HealthTechAsia 2026].

At the bedside

Sunway has run 2,400-plus robotic procedures on Mako and ROSA platforms [Nucamp 2025]; Bangkok rivals counter with da Vinci centres of excellence [GM Insights 2024]. The race is now about visible, marketable capability.

Section 14 · Strategic Implications
017 / 018

What each stakeholder should do next

The competitive contest with Thailand rewards specific, differentiated moves. Five reads for the participants who shape the next phase of Malaysia's medical-travel economy.

01 · OPERATORS
Sell outcomes, not just price
With cardiac and IVF pricing near parity, lead with accreditation, robotic depth and published outcomes. Convert the cost story into a trust story before Thailand's brand premium hardens into the default.
Anchor: USD 15-25k bypass band
02 · INVESTORS
Back clusters on their own logic
Penang is a volume play on Indonesian throughput; KL is a complexity play on tertiary acuity. Underwrite each separately — bed expansion in Penang, specialist capability in the Klang Valley.
Anchor: RM1.14 bn Penang 2025
03 · POLICYMAKERS
Fund the workforce, not just promotion
At 2.3 doctors per 1,000, the RM12 bn target is supply-bound. Channel medical-travel margins into specialist training and retention, or the demand simply overflows to Bangkok and Singapore.
Anchor: 2.3 doctors / 1,000
04 · FACILITATORS
Diversify beyond Sumatra
A 65%-Indonesian book is fragile to repatriation. Build pipelines into China, India and the Gulf, and packaged journeys that match Thailand's experience layer [Kira estimates].
Anchor: ~65% Indonesia share
05 · TECH PARTNERS
Make AI patient-visible
National AI scaffolding is in place across 16 hospitals and 2,400-plus clinics. Translate it into marketable diagnostic speed and surgical precision that international patients can see and compare.
Anchor: 16 AI hospitals

Methodology & sources

How to read this report

This view triangulates two evidence streams. Where a figure carries a named source tag (e.g. [MHTC 2025], [Penang Health 2026]), the underlying data comes from that source; the full citation appears in the list opposite.

Where a figure carries [Kira estimates], KIRA analysts derived it from a blend of disclosed inputs — council figures, operator filings, cross-border price reads and channel checks — using the method noted in the relevant section.

Approximately 25% of quantitative claims are KIRA-derived; the remainder are externally cited. Cross-border counting bases differ between Malaysia (healthcare travellers) and Thailand (international patients), and are flagged where compared.

Research combined an English-language baseline with a Bahasa Malaysia and Bahasa Indonesia source pass to capture in-country reporting on Penang and Indonesian demand; local sources are cited via English aliases.
