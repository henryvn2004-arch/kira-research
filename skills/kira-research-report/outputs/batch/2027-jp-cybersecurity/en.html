This view is built from two evidence streams: primary inputs — practitioner channel checks, analyst synthesis, and specialist operator reads — and secondary anchors — METI and FSA publications, NCO and JPCERT/CC threat data, National Police Agency reports, and listed company filings. The demand pull is governance and regulation

Three catalysts have moved cyber spend upward simultaneously. The FSA's 2026 Corporate Governance Code revision — draft published February and April 2026, finalising mid-2026 — clarifies and strengthens board responsibility for cyber risk, supply-chain integrity, and data leakage [FSA 2026]. The Active Cyber Defense Act, enacted May 2025, imposes incident-reporting obligations on 15 critical-infrastructure sectors by November 2026 [ACDA 2025]. Separately, METI's Cybersecurity Industry Strategy targets tripling industry sales from ~JPY 900 bn to over JPY 3 tn within a decade [METI 2025].

Governance turns the CISO into a board concern

CISO or equivalent set-up among large Japanese enterprises has reached 73.3%, but only 38.7% use a formal CISO title [IPA 2025]. The governance reform pushes firms to formalise the role, raise its reporting line, and disclose oversight in the annual securities report. The result is a re-pricing of CISO compensation — already JPY 14–25 mn for senior mandates [Robert Half 2025] — and a structural demand for board-fluent security leadership the domestic talent pool cannot yet supply.

Segment growth: managed services and identity outpace the market.
Japan's domestic information security market reached JPY 2.0 tn in FY2025 [JNSA 2025], split across services and tools. Managed security services and identity / zero-trust products are each growing at roughly 1.5–2× the market average; traditional perimeter and on-premise appliance spend is plateauing.

Segment structure: services lead, tools follow

Security services — consulting, SOC operation, MSSP / MDR, and incident response — remain the largest bucket at JPY 1.18 tn (59% of total) [JNSA 2025 · Kira estimates], reflecting Japan's structural reliance on systems integrators. Tool and product spend (endpoint, network, identity, cloud) accounts for the balance. The services share is rising as the 110,000-person talent gap pushes firms toward outsourced operation.

Managed services: the structural spend anchor

Managed security services are growing above 12% YoY [Kira estimates] as cyber-insurance underwriting and the Active Cyber Defense regime mandate threat-monitoring baselines for critical infrastructure. The largest integrators — NTT, NEC, Fujitsu, Hitachi — bundle SOC operation with their existing IT-services relationships, giving incumbents a distribution advantage over pure-play security vendors.

METI's industry-scale ambition

METI's March 2025 Cybersecurity Industry Strategy targets tripling domestic industry sales from ~JPY 900 bn to over JPY 3 tn within a decade [METI 2025], pairing demand-side procurement subsidies for SMEs with supply-side support for domestic product creation. The policy intent is to reduce dependence on foreign tooling while expanding the addressable market.

Threat landscape
09 / 14
Ransomware concentrates in manufacturing; supply chain is the fastest vector.
The National Police Agency recorded 134 ransomware incidents in 2025, a 17.5% increase YoY, with manufacturing the single most-affected sector at 28% of cases [NPA 2025]. High-profile breaches at Kadokawa and Asahi Group demonstrated that supply-chain and production-system intrusions carry costs an order of magnitude above the ransom itself.

Ransomware: the manufacturing exposure problem

Of the 134 confirmed cases in 2025, manufacturing accounted for 28%, followed by automotive-related industries (8%), trading companies (7%), and IT (6%) [NPA 2025]. The 2024 Kadokawa breach leaked data on 250,000+ individuals and drove recovery costs above JPY 24 bn — far beyond the ransom. Qilin was the most active group in H1 FY2025; the Kawa4096 group has targeted Japan from inception [Talos 2025].

Supply chain: a single supplier halts a line

In September 2025, Asahi Group suffered an attack that shut production and shipping systems nationwide, halting beverage supply and postponing 12 product launches. Japan's deep keiretsu supplier networks mean a breach at one tier-2 vendor cascades to primes. This structural exposure is the principal driver behind the Active Cyber Defense Act's supply-chain reporting provisions [ACDA 2025].

Nation-state actors: Japan as a transit node

State-sponsored espionage — attributed primarily to China, Russia, and North Korea — is among the four leading threats to Japanese business alongside ransomware, AI-enabled attacks, and supply-chain breaches [JPCERT 2025 · Kira estimates]. Actors increasingly compromise Japanese MNC headquarters networks as a stepping stone to regional subsidiaries across ASEAN.

Policy timeline: governance reform and active defense compress spend cycles.
Japan's cyber regulatory apparatus moved decisively in 2025–2026: the Active Cyber Defense Act reorganised NISC into the National Cybersecurity Office and added critical-infrastructure reporting, while the 2026 Corporate Governance Code revision elevates board cyber oversight to a disclosed accountability [FSA 2026 · ACDA 2025]. Compliance dates cluster in 2026–2027, the peak non-discretionary spend window.

CISO shift & talent
13 / 14
The CISO hiring shift: governance re-prices the role faster than the pipeline fills.
Japan's 110,000-FTE security talent deficit [Toyo Keizai 2025] collides with a governance reform that demands board-fluent security leadership. The result is a re-pricing of the CISO role, a rise in fractional and external models, and slow but accelerating progress against the government's 50,000 RISS target.
CISO · 01
Set-up rate vs formal title gap
CISO or equivalent set-up has reached 73.3% of large firms, but only 38.7% use a formal CISO title [IPA 2025]. Of those with a security lead, 64.5% sit at executive (役員) level and 49.1% report directly to the CEO [Nikkei Research 2022]. Governance reform pushes the remaining cohort to formalise and elevate the role.
SOURCE: IPA 2025 · Nikkei Research 2022
CISO · 02
Compensation re-pricing
Senior CISO mandates now command JPY 14–25 mn, with top financial-institution roles above JPY 18 mn [Robert Half 2025]. Equity and stock-type compensation (新株予約権, 株式交付信託) are increasingly used to attract board-fluent leaders. The scarcity premium widens as Prime issuers compete for the same shallow pool.
SOURCE: Robert Half 2025 · Kira estimates
CISO · 03
Fractional and external CISO models
Firms unable to hire full-time board-fluent leaders turn to fractional-CISO and vCISO services, the fastest-growing advisory sub-segment [Kira estimates]. Training providers have launched dedicated CISO courses to address the leadership gap; demand outstrips qualified instructors.
SOURCE: Kira estimates · vendor disclosures
TALENT · 04
The 110,000-FTE deficit
A private survey puts Japan's security talent shortfall at ~110,000 FTE, with supply rising 23.8% YoY but the supply-demand gap still at a record [Toyo Keizai 2025]. OT security, cloud engineering, and threat-intelligence analysis are the most acute role gaps [Kira estimates].
SOURCE: Toyo Keizai 2025 · Kira estimates
TALENT · 05
RISS and the 50,000 target
METI's workforce plan targets 50,000 cybersecurity experts by 2030; as of 2025 roughly 24,000 hold the Registered Information Security Specialist (RISS) licence [METI 2025]. Progress is slow — the certification's difficulty and a gap between exam content and practical need limit uptake [Kira estimates].
SOURCE: METI 2025 · Kira estimates
MSSP · 06
Talent gap structures MSSP demand
The deficit makes in-house SOC unviable for all but the largest keiretsu. MSSP and MDR penetration among mid-market firms rises from an estimated 31% to 47% by 2028F [Kira estimates]. Providers fluent in Japanese-language telemetry and on-site support command a structural premium over offshore delivery.

Methodology and sources

Methodology summary

This view triangulates two evidence streams. Where a figure carries a named source tag (e.g. [JNSA 2025], [NPA 2025], [FSA 2026]), the underlying data comes from that source; the full citation is given in the sources list. Where a figure carries [Kira estimates], KIRA analysts derived the number from a blend of disclosed inputs — government publications, operator filings, channel checks, and regulatory guidance — using methodology disclosed in the relevant section. Approximately 41% of quantitative claims in this report are KIRA-derived; 59% are externally cited. Market sizing figures are estimates and should be treated as directional bounds, not audited accounts. CAGR and forecast figures assume current regulatory timelines hold and no major macroeconomic or geopolitical disruption to Japanese enterprise IT investment.
