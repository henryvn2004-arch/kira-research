A market growing into a margin squeeze

QSR grew 5.1% in 2025 [Nation Thailand 2025] inside a THB 657 bn restaurant-and-beverage market that expanded just 4.6% [Kasikorn Research 2025]. Demand is real, but a "value-for-money war" — co-pay stimulus, free-delivery promotions and fried-chicken price competition — is compressing per-ticket profit even as volume rises.

Two structural forces decide the winners

First, chain consolidation: chained outlets are compounding at roughly 8% a year against independents that still hold ~74% of foodservice [USDA FAS 2025]. Second, the aggregator toll: a LINE MAN–Grab duopoly now captures ~84% of delivery [Kira estimates], charging ~30% commission before VAT [Komchadluek 2025]. Scale and owned-channel mix are the only durable hedges.

005 / 028

Five implications for market participants

The 2026–30 window rewards operators who pair scale with channel discipline. Below: where to act, and the single number that justifies each move.

01 · CONSOLIDATION
Build scale or sell into a scaled group
Chained outlets compound at ~8% while independents hold ~74% of foodservice [USDA FAS 2025]. Sub-scale single-brand operators face structural cost-of-goods and rent disadvantages. Acquire density or become an acquisition target — the middle is the dangerous place.
Anchor: chains +8% CAGR vs market +4.6%
02 · CHANNEL
Treat the aggregator as a paid channel, not a partner
At ~30% commission before VAT [Komchadluek 2025], every delivery order surrenders roughly a third of menu value. Operators must hold a blended margin model — dine-in and owned app subsidising delivery, not the reverse.
Anchor: ~32% GP incl. VAT
03 · DUOPOLY
Price for a two-platform world
LINE MAN and Grab hold ~84% of delivery between them [Kira estimates]; ShopeeFood is the only live challenger at ~10% [The Standard 2025]. Multi-homing leverage has narrowed — menu surcharges and exclusivity deals now define the negotiation.
Anchor: LINE MAN 44% / Grab 40%
04 · FORMAT
Shift fixed cost into delivery-native footprints
41% of operators are adding delivery/takeaway zones and 34% plan takeaway-only branches [Nation Thailand 2025]. Cloud-kitchen and small-box formats cut rent and labour per order — the fastest route to delivery profitability.
Anchor: 34% plan takeaway-only sites
05 · POLICY
Position ahead of platform-fairness rules
ETDA is pursuing fee transparency under the Digital Platform Services regime but has no commission cap [ETDA 2026]. Operators that document true platform cost now gain leverage as soft-law guidance hardens through 2026–27.
Anchor: 2,056 platforms registered

Section 04 · Macro Context
007 / 028

A modest-growth economy carrying a recovering consumer

Thailand grew 2.4% in 2025 [NESDC 2025] with 2026 set in a 1.5–2.5% band [NESDC 2026]. Tourism and private consumption do the heavy lifting; exports face tariff headwinds.

Growth held up by services and tourism

NESDC reports 2.5% GDP growth in Q4 2025, up from 1.2% the prior quarter [NESDC 2025], with tourist arrivals projected to rise to 35 million in 2026 from 33 million [NESDC 2026]. Footfall recovery feeds mall-based and tourist-corridor QSR directly.

A consumer trading down

With household spending power soft, Thai diners are eating out less and chasing value [Bangkok Biz 2025]. Quick service — affordable, fast, branded — is the trade-down winner, which is why QSR outgrew the wider restaurant market in 2025.

Risk: export drag

NESDC flags US tariff exposure on rubber, gems, appliances and semiconductors as a 2026 risk [NESDC 2026]. Soft external demand keeps the domestic consumer cautious — reinforcing the value-tier tilt.

Section 04 · Macro Context
008 / 028

Co-pay stimulus reroutes spending through delivery

The 2025 "Khon La Khrueng Plus" co-pay programme extended to delivery for the first time, pulling stimulus spending onto aggregator platforms and amplifying their grip on demand.

Stimulus met the platform

When the half-pay subsidy was opened to food delivery, partner restaurants on the leading apps reported sales roughly tripling and orders above one million during the window [Brand Buffet 2025]. Diners placing two-plus orders a day rose ~2.5x [Brand Buffet 2025].

A policy lever with a side-effect

Stimulus revived demand — but routed it through channels charging ~30% commission [Komchadluek 2025]. Government cash effectively flows partly to platforms, a dynamic now central to the fee-fairness debate covered in Section 09.

What it signals for 2026

Co-pay programmes are now a structural demand variable, not a one-off. Operators that capture stimulus on owned channels — not only aggregators — protect both margin and customer data.

Section 05 · Sector Overview
010 / 028

QSR inside a THB 657 bn foodservice market

Thailand's restaurant-and-beverage market reached THB 657 bn in 2025 [Kasikorn Research 2025]. Quick service is ~THB 47.7 bn of it [Marketeer 2025] but grows faster and is far more chain-led.

The shape of the market

Total foodservice is valued near USD 35.4 bn in 2025 rising toward USD 38.1 bn in 2026 [USDA FAS 2025]. QSR holds the single largest format share — over half of foodservice revenue by some operator reads — and grew 5.1% in 2025 against a 4.6% market [Nation Thailand 2025] [Kasikorn Research 2025].

Why QSR outperforms

Affordability, speed and brand trust make QSR the trade-down format in a value-conscious cycle. Urban density and delivery reach extend each outlet's catchment well beyond its four walls.

The chain advantage

Independents still hold ~74% of foodservice [USDA FAS 2025], but chained outlets compound at ~8% — capturing share through standardised operations, financing and brand recall.

Section 05 · Sector Overview
011 / 028

Chains take share as independents hold the base

The consolidation story is a rate-of-change story: independents remain the majority, but chains add outlets and revenue faster, and the gap widens every year.

The 8% wedge

Chained outlets are growing at roughly 8% a year [USDA FAS 2025] — almost double the headline market. Compounded over five years, that wedge shifts several points of share from independents to groups such as CRG, Minor Food and the QSR franchisees.

Format expansion drives it

Growth is led by QSR-format and chain expansion specifically [USDA FAS 2025]. Groups open small-box, standalone and cloud-kitchen sites at a cadence independents cannot finance.

Where independents still win

Street food and single-location operators retain price and authenticity advantages. The contested ground is mid-market branded fast food, where chains convert independents' customers via delivery reach and loyalty programmes.

Section 06 · Segment Economics
012 / 028

Fried chicken is the centre of gravity

Within QSR, fried chicken is THB 27.6 bn — 58% of the market [Marketeer 2025], growing 8–10% a year and now drawing a wave of new entrants competing on sauce and format.

One segment dominates

Fried chicken's THB 27.6 bn dwarfs burgers and pizza [Marketeer 2025]. KFC alone runs over 1,200 outlets across three franchisees [Bangkok Today 2025], anchoring the segment while Korean and US challengers — Pelicana, Wingstop — enter in 2025.

Beverage-led formats grow alongside

Premium ice cream and coffee-club formats add ticket and frequency; Minor's Swensen's holds over 75% of premium ice cream [Minor Food 2025]. These formats carry higher gross margin and lower delivery dependence than core fast food.

The economics diverge by segment

Fried chicken is volume-and-price-competitive; beverage and dessert formats defend margin. Operators increasingly run a portfolio to blend the two profiles across one supply chain.

02 · BURGERS
Burgers — international brands, local price war

03 · PIZZA
Pizza — delivery-native, commission-exposed
The Pizza Company (Minor) dominates a ~THB 6 bn segment built for delivery [Kira estimates]. Owned-app penetration is the key margin lever against aggregator commission.

Beverage & dessert — the margin formats

Section 08 · Demand & Channels
021 / 028

Dine-in anchors, delivery sets the margin

Dine-in is still the largest service type at roughly two-thirds of foodservice occasions [USDA FAS 2025], but delivery is compounding fastest — and at ~30% commission it dictates per-order economics.

The channel split

Dine-in holds ~67% of service occasions [USDA FAS 2025]; takeaway and delivery take the rest and grow double-digit. 41% of operators are adding delivery/takeaway zones; 34% plan takeaway-only branches [Nation Thailand 2025].

Why the channel mix matters

A delivery order surrenders ~30% before VAT to the platform [Komchadluek 2025]. Operators run a blended model — dine-in and owned-app orders subsidising the lower-margin aggregator channel.

Owned channels as the hedge

Brand apps, loyalty and dine-in promotions reclaim margin and customer data. The most profitable operators treat aggregators as incremental reach, not the core P&L.

Section 08 · Demand & Channels
022 / 028

The aggregator order, decomposed

On a typical THB 200 delivery order, the operator keeps roughly two-thirds after commission and VAT — before food, packaging and labour cost. This is why delivery is volume-positive, margin-negative without scale.

Where the money goes

Platforms take ~30% commission plus 7% VAT on that fee [Komchadluek 2025] — effectively ~32% of menu value. The consumer pays a separate delivery fee to the rider/platform, so the operator’s loss is the commission, not the logistics.

The promotion tax

"Free delivery" and discount codes are part-funded by the GP operators already pay [Grab TH]. In a value-for-money war, promotional intensity is the hidden second commission.

The scale answer

Groups negotiate lower effective GP, run cloud kitchens to cut occupancy, and push owned apps. Independents without that leverage absorb full commission — the structural engine of consolidation.

Section 09 · Regulatory & Policy
023 / 028

Platform regulation moves on fairness, not fee caps

The ETDA Digital Platform Services regime governs 2,056 registered platforms [ETDA 2026]. For 2026 it pursues fee transparency and shared responsibility — but stops short of a commission cap.

The current framework

Under the Royal Decree on Digital Platform Service Business (B.E. 2565), ETDA regulates platform conduct around three principles for 2026 — "practicable, verifiable, shared responsibility" — covering product standards, fair competition and fraud prevention [ETDA 2026].

The GP question

A commission-fairness "restart" directs ETDA to unpack every fee layer [Bangkok Biz 2025]. But a legal gap remains: the Internal Trade Department oversees retail prices while ETDA cannot set fee caps — so it relies on soft-law consultation [ETDA 2026].

Gig-worker pressure

Rider piece-rate cuts have triggered protests; ETDA is reviewing insurance, leasing and contract terms with the Land Transport Department and Bank of Thailand [ILO 2025].

AI in QSR: from kitchen display to dynamic pricing

The global AI-in-QSR market is small but compounding fast — roughly USD 0.9 bn in 2024 toward USD 12 bn by 2034 at ~29% CAGR [Market.us 2024]. In Thailand, the aggregators are the earliest and deepest adopters.

Aggregators lead adoption

Operators follow into the kitchen

74% of Thai QSR operators already use technology to improve efficiency [Nation Thailand 2025]; AI demand-forecasting feeds kitchen display systems that adjust prep to the next 30 minutes of predicted orders.

The economics

Where commission compresses margin, AI-driven waste, labour and promo optimisation is the offsetting lever — not a moonshot, but a margin point.

Six AI applications reshaping Thai quick service

AI in Thai QSR is an operating layer, not a pilot. The aggregators run on it today; operators win the next margin point by bringing it into the kitchen and the owned channel.

01 · DEMAND
Demand forecasting into the kitchen
AI predicts the next 30 minutes of orders and adjusts prep in the kitchen display system, cutting waste and wait. The single highest-ROI use case for high-volume fried-chicken and burger lines.
Pilot: KDS auto-prep adjustment
02 · PRICING
Dynamic menu & delivery pricing
Aggregators already vary delivery fees and surface personalised promotions by demand and weather. Operators can mirror this on owned apps to defend margin on peak orders.
Observable: Grab/LINE MAN AI pricing
03 · ROUTING
Rider dispatch & batching
LINE MAN and Grab use AI to batch and route riders, lifting orders-per-hour and trimming delivery cost — the efficiency edge behind 2025 profitability.
Benchmark: LINE MAN profitable 2025
04 · PROMO
Promotion-ROI optimisation
Most promo spend in a value-for-money war is unaccountable. AI attributes lift to spend, cutting the "second commission" of blanket discounting.
Anchor: promo = hidden 2nd cost
05 · CRM
Personalised reorder & loyalty
Wongnai’s review-and-order data lets the leader personalise reorder prompts — a discovery-and-retention moat operators chase via owned loyalty.
Observable: Wongnai data moat
06 · OPS
Cloud-kitchen orchestration
AI sequences multi-brand orders across one cloud kitchen, raising throughput without new real estate — the format CRG is scaling.
Benchmark: CRG cloud-kitchen rollout

5-year outlook: volume grows, the margin fight intensifies

KIRA models the QSR market reaching ~THB 71 bn by 2030 in the base case [Kira estimates] — a ~7% CAGR — with the spread between scenarios set by consolidation pace and platform commission.

Base case · ~THB 71 bn by 2030

Volume compounds ~7% on urbanisation, delivery reach and chain expansion [Kira estimates]. Commission holds near 30%; chains keep taking share from independents.

Bull · faster, if owned channels scale

If operators shift mix to owned apps and cloud kitchens and co-pay stimulus recurs, value growth accelerates and margin stabilises — the upside hinges on channel control, not demand.

Bear · margin erosion bites

A weak consumer plus rising effective commission and promo intensity could hold growth to low single digits and trigger independent closures — accelerating consolidation but shrinking the profit pool.
