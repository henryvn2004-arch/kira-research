Growth has gone overseas

Japan's anime market reached a record ¥3.84 trn in 2024, up 14.8% [AJA 2025], with overseas sales surpassing the home market for a second year at ¥2.17 trn — 56.5% of the total, growing 26% against 2.8% at home. Anime is now structurally an export industry: foreign demand, not domestic, sets the growth rate, and streaming licensing plus global merchandise are the two engines.

The value split is the story

Merchandising is the single largest segment at ¥749 bn and streaming has overtaken TV, film, and home video combined [AJA 2025]. But the production studios that create the work capture an estimated ~6% of overseas sales and ~16% domestically [Kira estimates based on METI papers]; production committees and IP holders retain the licensing and merchandise upside. Where you sit in the chain decides whether the boom is a windfall or a squeeze.

02 · Strategic Implications 05 / 15
Five strategic implications for market participants

Derived from the structural trends in this report — relevant to IP holders, production studios, streaming distributors, merchandise licensees, and overseas partners operating in the Japanese anime value chain.

01 · POSITION
Own IP, or own a window — not just the work
Value accrues to rights holders and licensors, not to production capacity. With studios capturing ~6% of overseas sales [Kira estimates], operators that only make episodes face a structural margin ceiling. Acquire or co-own IP, or secure an equity seat on the committee, before scaling headcount.
Anchor: ~6% studio share of overseas sales [Kira estimates]
02 · LICENSE
Treat streaming as an annuity, not a one-off sale
Streaming reached ¥266 bn and now exceeds TV, film, and video combined [AJA 2025]. The shift from flat license fees to co-production and minimum-guarantee deals rewards holders who retain renewal optionality. Structure rights to recapture value as platforms compete for simulcast exclusivity.
Anchor: ¥266 bn streaming, > TV+film+video [AJA 2025]
03 · MERCHANDISE
Build the merchandise flywheel around durable IP
Merchandising is the largest segment at ¥749 bn [AJA 2025], and a single franchise can clear USD 1.6 bn a year across toys, games, and licensing [Bandai Namco FY26]. Anchor merchandise to multi-decade IP with cross-generational reach; novelty titles rarely sustain the cadence.
Anchor: ¥749 bn merchandising, top segment [AJA 2025]
04 · EXPORT
Unify overseas rights to recapture leakage
Overseas grew 26% to ¥2.17 trn [AJA 2025]. Holders that centralize re-licensing through a single window lift royalty recovery and curb value leakage to intermediaries. Fragmented territory-by-territory deals cede margin; consolidate distribution and merchandise rights regionally.
Anchor: +26% overseas YoY [AJA 2025]
05 · SUSTAIN
Price in the labor and capacity constraint
A record market sits atop a fragile production base: roughly 60% of studios ran losses in 2025 and labor is scarce [METI 2025]. Reform of working conditions raises unit cost. Partners that under-fund the studio tier risk supply disruption; price sustainability into long-term output deals.
Anchor: ~60% of studios loss-making 2025 [METI 2025]

Two markets in one number

The ¥3.84 trn figure is the broad industry market — all anime-related commerce, from broadcast to merchandise, events, and overseas licensing [AJA 2025]. The narrower production market of studio revenue is ¥466 bn, up 9.1% [AJA 2025]. The gap between the two — roughly eight to one — is the central fact of the industry's economics: most of the money is made downstream of the people who draw the frames.

Within the domestic market, merchandising is the largest single segment at ¥749 bn, ahead of streaming, music, and live events [AJA 2025]. Streaming reached ¥266 bn and now exceeds the combined total of television, theatrical film, and home video — a structural reordering of how anime is distributed and monetized.

The overseas inversion

Overseas sales hit ¥2.17 trn, 56.5% of the total, growing 26% while the domestic market grew 2.8% [AJA 2025]. For a second consecutive year, foreign demand outweighs home demand — anime is, by revenue, an export sector.

04 · Streaming Licensing 08 / 15
Streaming licensing: from flat fees to co-production stakes

Global platforms now compete for simulcast exclusivity, and the deal structure is shifting from one-off license fees toward minimum guarantees, co-financing, and equity in production. That shift hands renewal optionality — and a larger share of the upside — back to IP holders who hold their rights.

The platform contest

Anime streaming is led by two operators: a generalist platform earning an estimated USD 2.07 bn from anime and a specialist earning ~USD 1.16 bn [Streaming reads 2025]. The specialist surpassed 21 million paying subscribers in 2025, up from 17 million, lifting anime to one of its parent's fastest-growing entertainment lines [Sony FY25].

Competition has collapsed the old release-window advantage. In 2025 the generalist expanded weekly simulcasting for marquee titles [Streaming reads 2025], matching the speed model that once defined the specialist — pushing both toward exclusivity and co-production to differentiate.

Why the structure matters

The distributor parent acquired the specialist platform for ~USD 1.175 bn in 2021 and, in 2025, stood up a joint production label to make platform-exclusive anime, later adding a studio outright [Sony FY25]. Vertical integration converts a licensing cost into owned supply — and tilts negotiating power toward whoever controls both the IP and the pipe.

Global merchandise franchises: where the lifetime value sits

Japanese IP dominates the all-time franchise rankings, and the revenue is powered by merchandising more than by box office or streaming. The durable, cross-generational franchise — not the breakout season — is the asset that compounds across toys, games, and licensing for decades.

Merchandise, not box office

Pokémon remains the highest-grossing media franchise of all time at an estimated USD 288 bn lifetime, ahead of Hello Kitty at USD 88.5 bn and Mario at USD 57.3 bn [Franchise reads 2025]. The common thread is merchandising: toys, trading cards, and licensing — not films — drive the bulk of franchise value.

The annual cadence is large too. One IP holder's flagship franchise generated ~USD 1.62 bn in a single year across games, toys, anime, and licensing, overtaking its stablemates for the first time [Bandai Namco FY26]. Two further franchises cleared ¥190 bn and ¥140 bn the same year.

The export multiplier

Franchise merchandise travels. The leading IP holder's Americas sales grew 53.7% year on year [Bandai Namco FY26], evidence that the overseas pull in the headline market figures is concentrated in proven, globally recognized properties rather than spread evenly across the catalog.

07 · Committee Economics 13 / 15
Production-committee economics: who captures the export boom

The production-committee (seisaku iinkai) system pools financing across broadcasters, publishers, and merchandisers — and concentrates licensing and merchandise profit with the committee, not the studio. As global platforms join as investors, the negotiating structure is beginning to shift.

The structural squeeze

Committees set production budgets against the broadcaster sale price while retaining licensing and merchandise profits. Studios are estimated to capture only ~6% of overseas sales and ~16% of domestic sales [Kira estimates based on METI papers], which is why a record ¥3.84 trn market coincides with a loss-making studio tier.

The consequence is visible in the production base: roughly 60% of anime studios ran losses in 2025 amid a chronic labor shortage [METI 2025]. Reduced overtime under working-hours reform has squeezed margins further even as overseas demand rises — a record top line sitting on a fragile cost base.

What is changing

Global OTT platforms entering committees as capital partners shifts bargaining power, and some studios are moving beyond production-only models toward owning a rights stake [METI 2025]. IP holders are also unifying overseas re-licensing through single windows to lift royalty recovery.

Policy response

  • Working-conditions body (2025) — government to establish a third-party mediator on studio contracts, wages, and hours [METI 2025].
  • Content-export target — overseas content revenue (anime, manga, games) targeted at ¥20 trn by 2033 [METI 2025].
  • Committee transparency — policy councils reviewing the committee model's effect on creator compensation [METI 2025].
  • Distribution support — logistical and financing aid for studios expanding into North America, Europe, and Southeast Asia [METI 2025].

Implication

The policy direction and the deal-structure shift point the same way: value migrates to whoever holds rights and renewal optionality. Partners that fund only production capacity, without an equity or rights position, face a structural margin ceiling even in a record market.

08 · Outlook 14 / 15
Five-year outlook: overseas-led growth, contested at the studio tier

A record, overseas-led market with durable franchise economics and a fragile production base. The base case is continued mid-to-high single-digit growth; upside and downside hinge on streaming-licensing depth, merchandise franchise cadence, and whether the studio tier is funded to sustain output.

Base case — overseas keeps pulling

The market reaches roughly ¥6 trn by 2030 [Kira estimates], on mid-to-high single-digit growth led by overseas streaming and merchandise. This tracks the global anime forecast of ~USD 60 bn by 2030 at a ~9–10% CAGR [Industry forecasts 2025], with merchandise the largest share and streaming the fastest-growing.

Upside — franchises travel further

If IP holders deepen streaming co-production and disciplined overseas merchandise rollouts, and policy hits its ¥20 trn content-export ambition [METI 2025], the market can run materially above base — driven by exports rather than the domestic base.

Downside — the studio tier breaks

If the loss-making production base is under-funded and labor scarcity bites, supply constrains output and a weaker yen dents reported growth, holding the market nearer ¥5 trn by 2030 [Kira estimates].

Approximately 25% of quantitative claims are KIRA-derived; 75% are externally cited. All USD figures are period-average conversions; reported revenue in USD is sensitive to JPY movement, and franchise lifetime figures span all media over multiple decades.
