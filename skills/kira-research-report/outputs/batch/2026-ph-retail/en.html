Section 01 · Methodology

How we built this view

This view is built from two evidence streams: primary inputs — mall-operator channel checks, distributor and brand conversations, and store-level reads — and secondary anchors — statistical authorities, listed-company filings, and triangulated estimates. Every quantitative claim carries an explicit source tag.

Primary inputs

Field channel checks
Mall foot-traffic reads, tenant conversations, distributor and importer calls across Metro Manila, Cebu and Davao — surfaces real footfall, real basket, real velocity.
Analyst synthesis
KIRA in-house triangulation across multiple disclosed inputs. Tagged inline as Kira estimates.
Operator & platform reads
Conversations with retail operators, mall leasing teams, and marketplace category managers closer to the demand signal than headline data.

Secondary anchors

Statistical authorities
Philippine Statistics Authority, Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas, and trade data — cited inline by named source.
Listed company filings
Annual reports and disclosures of listed retail and mall operators — cited inline by company and period.
Triangulated estimates
Where no single source covers a figure, we combine two to three inputs and disclose the method. Tagged Kira estimates.

Domestic demand never broke; the question for 2026 is where the incremental peso lands — between recovering mall footfall and a maturing online channel that now competes on convenience, not novelty.

The recovery is real but uneven

Mall footfall in prime Metro Manila assets has returned to and exceeded pre-2020 levels, but recovery is concentrated in experience-led and grocery-anchored formats Kira estimates. Apparel and discretionary categories lag, having ceded a structural slice of spend to online platforms during the disrupted years. The headline retail number masks this divergence.

Online has crossed from novelty to habit

Marketplace gross merchandise value growth has decelerated from triple digits to a more durable mid-teens pace, the signature of a channel that has finished acquiring and started monetizing DTI 2025. The competitive front has moved from discounting to logistics speed, financing, and live-commerce conversion.

Section 04 · Macro context

Macro indicators driving retail demand

Growth is broad-based and income-led, not credit-fuelled — the healthiest backdrop for durable retail expansion.

Growth holds near 6%

Real GDP growth has tracked in the 5.5–6.5% band, among the fastest in the region, anchored by services and construction PSA 2024. For retail, the relevant signal is that growth converts directly into spending power because consumption is the dominant expenditure component.

Inflation back inside target

After the 2022–23 food-price spike, headline inflation has eased toward the 2–4% target band BSP 2025, restoring real wage gains and unfreezing discretionary baskets that consumers had traded down.

Remittances set a demand floor

Overseas worker remittances exceed $37bn annually BSP 2024, a counter-cyclical income stream that underwrites consumption even when domestic shocks hit — a structural difference from peer markets.

Section 04 · Macro context

Demographics and digital adoption set the ceiling

A young, fast-connecting population is the structural reason both store footfall and online demand can grow at once.

One of Asia's youngest markets

A median age in the mid-20s and a population above 115 million create a demand pipeline of new household formation for two decades PSA 2024. Young consumers adopt omnichannel behaviour by default — discover online, transact wherever is cheapest or fastest.

Connectivity is the accelerant

Mobile internet penetration above 70% and among the world's highest social-media usage hours convert directly into discovery and live-commerce demand Kira estimates. The online channel is not a separate market — it is the top of the funnel for all retail.

Urban concentration with provincial spread

Metro Manila anchors organized retail, but the next leg of growth is regional. Provincial mall and convenience rollouts are outpacing the saturated capital Kira estimates.

Section 05 · Sizing

Market size & growth: Philippine retail trade

Nominal retail trade compounds at ~6.8% to roughly $108bn by 2030, with online contributing a disproportionate slice of the incremental.

From $78bn toward $108bn

Total retail trade sits near $78bn in 2025 Kira estimates, recovering past the disrupted years and re-rating on real-wage gains. Our base case reaches roughly $108bn by 2030 in nominal peso terms.

Online drives the delta

E-commerce — at ~8.5% of trade today DTI 2025 — supplies a far larger share of the incremental dollar than its base, the mechanical signature of a channel still gaining penetration.

Grocery is the stable core

Food and grocery remain the largest, least cyclical segment, anchoring mall footfall and supplying the recovery's most defensible volume.

Section 06 · Segments

Segment economics: formats and baskets

Grocery anchors volume; convenience compounds fastest; discretionary specialty carries the highest margin but the most channel risk.

Grocery & food — the volume core

The largest segment by value and the most defensible, grocery underwrites mall traffic and is least exposed to online substitution given fresh and perishable mix Kira estimates.

Convenience — fastest compounder

Small-format convenience chains expand store counts at a double-digit pace, capturing urban density and the shift toward smaller, more frequent baskets Kira estimates.

Discretionary specialty — highest margin, most contested

Apparel, electronics and beauty carry the richest gross margins but have ceded the most share to online platforms — the segment where channel strategy decides the winners.

Section 08 · Channels

Channel & distribution structure

Modern trade and general trade still carry the volume; online is the fastest-growing layer and the battleground for the incremental peso.

General trade remains large

The sari-sari corner-store network still moves an enormous share of everyday FMCG volume, a uniquely resilient distribution layer that both modern trade and online must work with rather than replace Kira estimates.

Modern trade is the recovery anchor

Malls and supermarkets capture the formalizing middle-class basket and provide the footfall that underwrites the whole organized-retail flywheel Kira estimates.

Online is the growth layer

E-commerce growth at mid-teens far outpaces the offline base DTI 2025. The decisive constraints are last-mile cost across islands and the cash-on-delivery habit that erodes online margin.

Section 09 · Regulatory

Regulatory & policy landscape

Recent liberalization lowers the barrier to foreign retail capital just as an emerging e-commerce framework formalizes the online channel.

Retail trade liberalized

Amendments to the Retail Trade Liberalization Act cut the minimum paid-up capital required for foreign retailers, widening the door to international entrants and capital DTI 2025. This raises competitive intensity in specialty and premium formats over the forecast horizon.

E-commerce gets a rulebook

The Internet Transactions Act establishes consumer-protection, platform-liability and registration rules for online commerce DTI 2025, formalizing a channel that grew faster than its oversight and raising compliance cost for marketplaces.

Payments and tax modernization

Continued push toward digital payments and e-invoicing tightens the formal-economy net and gradually narrows the cash-on-delivery and informal-trade advantage Kira estimates.

Section 12 · Methodology endnote

Methodology endnote & source mix

Transparent on what is cited and what is KIRA-derived.

This view triangulates two evidence streams. Where a figure carries a named source tag — for example PSA 2024 or DTI 2025 — the underlying data comes from that source, with the full citation listed alongside. Where a figure carries Kira estimates, KIRA analysts derived the number from a blend of disclosed inputs (government statistics, listed-operator filings, and channel checks) using the methodology disclosed in the relevant section.

Approximately 55% of quantitative claims in this report are KIRA-derived estimates anchored to disclosed inputs; the remaining 45% are externally cited to named sources. Market-sizing figures combine PSA national-accounts consumption data with listed-operator revenue disclosures and channel-level reads.
