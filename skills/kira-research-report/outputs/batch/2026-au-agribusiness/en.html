Methodology

How this report was researched, structured, and quality-checked. Source tags appear inline throughout; the source key at the bottom of each page resolves aliases to full citations.

Primary & secondary sources

Our research team drew on Australian government statistical bodies — ABARES (Australian Bureau of Agricultural and Resource Economics and Sciences), DAFF (Department of Agriculture, Fisheries and Forestry), and DFAT (Department of Foreign Affairs and Trade) — as primary anchors for production volumes, export values, and trade-agreement terms.

Industry bodies consulted include Meat & Livestock Australia (MLA), Grain Producers Australia, and Austrade. Operator-level data was sourced from public company filings and investor materials for JBS, Teys Australia, NH Foods, and Thomas Foods International.

Market intelligence was supplemented by USDA FAS Japan and Korea commodity reports, Japan Customs, and Korea Customs Service trade statistics.

Scope and limitations

This report covers calendar year 2025 actuals and 2026–2031 forecasts. Export-value estimates in AUD and USD are stated in nominal terms unless noted. Tariff schedules reflect JAEPA and KAFTA as of January 2026. Currency assumes AUD/USD 0.63 (2026 baseline [Kira estimates]).

Record production + structural Asian demand

Australia's agribusiness sector enters 2026 at historically peak capacity. The national cattle herd remains above 30 million head [MLA 2026], driving forecast beef production of 2.9 million tonnes CWE — the highest volume on record. Simultaneous bumper winter crops yield 37 million tonnes of wheat and a record 15.5 million tonnes of barley [ABARES Mar 2026]. Japan and South Korea collectively absorbed 479,000 tonnes of Australian beef in 2025, up 6.7% year-on-year, while wheat and barley flows reinforce Australia's position as the dominant grain supplier to both markets.

FTA tariff convergence: the competitive risk

Australia's longstanding tariff advantage in Japan under JAEPA is being eroded. Under CPTPP, Canadian and New Zealand beef now faces the same staged tariff schedule as Australian product, with rates tracking toward 9% by 2033 [Kira estimates]. Korea's KAFTA beef tariff reaches zero in January 2028 [DFAT KAFTA], but the KAFTA safeguard can reimpose up to 40% on volumes above trigger levels, as Korea did in Q4 2025. The net effect: volume access is structurally assured; premium pricing and chilled-product positioning are the differentiating levers.

Section 04 · Macro Context 007 / 020

Macro indicators driving Australia's agribusiness export cycle

Three structural forces converge in 2025–26: completion of the cattle herd rebuilding cycle, back-to-back above-average winter grain crops, and persistent US beef supply shortfall redirecting Northeast Asian demand toward Australia.

2025–26 is a record year across every major metric. Gross production value reaches AUD 101.4 bn [ABARES Mar 2026], driven by high cattle turnoff and the second-largest wheat crop on record. The AUD is expected to trade at approximately USD 0.63 in 2026 [Kira estimates], providing a modest currency tailwind for exporters relative to the 2021–22 peak of USD 0.75+.

Key macro drivers

Cattle herd cycle at peak. The national herd reached 30.78 million head in 2026, having recovered from drought lows through an extended rebuilding phase [MLA 2026]. Slaughter is forecast at 9.45 million head — the highest since 1978. This peak-cycle dynamic means high export volumes now, but a structural decline in herd size will suppress volumes from 2027 onward as producers retain heifers.

Grain production record. The 2025–26 winter crop delivered wheat at 37 mt (+34% above 10-year average) and barley at a record 15.5 mt (+32% above 10-year average) [ABARES Mar 2026]. Ample domestic grain supply reduces feedlot input costs and supports grain-fed beef program expansion.

US supply shortfall redirects Asian demand. The US cattle herd is in a prolonged rebuilding phase, constraining beef export availability. Japan and Korea have both shifted sourcing toward Australia to replace US grain-fed supply, lifting Australian market share in both destinations in 2025 [MLA 2025].

2026–27 normalisation ahead. ABARES forecasts a 6% fall in gross production value to AUD 98.3 bn in 2026–27 as drier seasonal conditions reduce crop volumes and livestock prices ease from cyclical highs [ABARES Mar 2026].

Chilled premium: the structural differentiator

Japan's beef import structure skews heavily toward chilled product in value terms — approximately 61% of Australian export value is chilled [Kira estimates]. Wagyu-cross and high-marble-score chilled product commands 3–4x the price of frozen grassfed cuts. The chilled shelf-life constraint (roughly 90 days at -1°C) creates a natural barrier to entry for distant competitors; Australia's 10–14 day sea freight transit to Japan is well within this window, while South American competitors face 28+ days.

Section 08 · Demand Drivers — Japan 014 / 020

Japan: structurally import-dependent, near-term demand softness, JAEPA utilization deepening

Japan is Australia's largest beef export market by value — AUD 734 mn in 2025 [MLA 2025]. A weak yen compressed import volumes in 1H 2025, but structural domestic supply contraction and the JAEPA tariff trajectory sustain Australia's long-run advantage.

Demand dynamics

Australia holds 47% market share of Japan's beef imports by volume as of 1H 2025 [Japan Customs 2025], with the US at 37%. The 13-percentage-point advantage reflects Australia's superior cold-chain proximity and the JAEPA-CPTPP frozen beef tariff, now at approximately 25.8% and tracking to 19.5% by the end of JAEPA's schedule [DFAT JAEPA].

Japan's 1H 2025 total beef imports fell 13% year-on-year as a weak yen (JPY/USD ~155 average) inflated import prices in yen terms, squeezing mid-tier supermarket beef budgets [Japan Customs 2025]. This represents a cyclical — not structural — compression; yen recovery toward JPY 140 levels would restore import affordability.

Chilled beef dominates value. Approximately 61% of Australian beef export value to Japan is chilled product [Kira estimates], concentrated in the supermarket and premium restaurant segments. The chilled advantage is irreproducible by South American competitors given transit time — Brazilian beef takes 28+ days to Japan, well beyond the 90-day chilled window at -1°C.

FTA utilization: JAEPA

JAEPA overall preference utilization exceeds 95% for agricultural products [DFAT JAEPA]. Beef tariff progression: frozen at 25.8% (2026) → 19.5% (2033); chilled at comparable staged reductions. Under CPTPP, Canadian and NZ beef faces an identical staged schedule, eliminating Australia's pre-2019 bilateral advantage. Differentiation must come from product quality, not tariff arbitrage.

Japan channel structure

Supermarket retail (~45% of import volume): Ito-Yokado, AEON, and Ito-Yokado specialty chains are the dominant buyers. Chilled Australian beef (chuck, round, striploin) commands premium placement. Wagyu-cross graded product occupies the premium tier at JPY 2,500–5,000/100g [Kira estimates].

Foodservice / yakiniku (~35% of import volume): Japan's ~15,000 yakiniku restaurants are the largest channel for mid-grade Australian beef. Short ribs (kalbi) and thick-cut loin cuts are favoured. NH Foods and JBS both have dedicated foodservice supply programs targeting this segment.

Gyudon / fast-casual chains (~20% of import volume): Yoshinoya, Sukiya, and Matsuya collectively process millions of frozen Australian beef portions daily. This segment is highly price-sensitive; the frozen grassfed AUD 5–7/kg bracket dominates. US competition is strongest here; Hanwoo supply is irrelevant at this price point.

Section 08 · Demand Drivers — Korea 015 / 020

Korea: KAFTA tariff convergence, Hanwoo supply contraction, safeguard risk

South Korea absorbed 221,350 tonnes of Australian beef in 2025, up 10% year-on-year — a record [MLA 2025]. The combination of declining domestic Hanwoo supply and US supply constraints created exceptional demand pull. The 2028 KAFTA zero-tariff completion and concurrent safeguard expiry are the critical inflection points.

Demand fundamentals

Domestic Hanwoo supply is declining. Korea's 2025 domestic beef production fell 3% to approximately 358,000 mt [USDA FAS Korea 2025], driven by herd contraction as producers responded to years of losses from high feed costs. This structural supply gap supports continued import demand growth — Korea's total beef imports in 2025 reached approximately 581,000 mt [USDA FAS Korea 2025], up marginally on 2024.

Australia holds ~46% import volume share in Korea in late 2025, competing closely with the US at ~48% [MLA Korea Snapshot Jan 2025]. The competition splits on product type: US dominates grain-fed short ribs (galbi) and thick-cut loin for grilling — Korean consumers' highest-preference format. Australia is competitive in chuck, round, and thinner-sliced cuts for retail.

Government demand stimulus added a cyclical boost. Korea's July 2025 "livelihood recovery consumption coupon" program provided household food-spend subsidies that partially offset weak consumer sentiment and drove a spike in restaurant beef demand [Korea Consumer Agency 2025].

KAFTA safeguard: the structural constraint

Korea applied its KAFTA agricultural safeguard on Australian beef in Q4 2025, reimposing elevated tariffs on volumes above the safeguard trigger [MLA Korea Snapshot Jan 2025]. The safeguard mechanism is scheduled to expire on December 31, 2028 — coinciding with the zero-tariff completion. The 2026–2027 window is therefore the last period where Australian exporters can accumulate volume before both the tariff floor and safeguard mechanism change simultaneously.

Korea channel structure

Retail (supermarket & butcher) (~50% of import volume): E-Mart, Lotte Mart, and HomePlus are the dominant retailers. Australian grassfed and grain-fed beef occupies mid-tier shelf space at KRW 18,000–35,000/100g, below Hanwoo (KRW 60,000–120,000/100g) [Kira estimates].

HoReCa / grilling restaurants (~35% of import volume): Korea's galbi and bulgogi restaurant culture drives sustained demand for Australian short ribs (LA-cut kalbi), brisket, and thin-sliced chuck. Australian product competes on price-to-quality ratio versus US in this channel.

Food manufacturing (~15%): Frozen processed meat products for convenience-food manufacturers. Australian frozen grassfed dominates on cost.

Section 10 · Technology Impact 018 / 020

Technology in Australia's agribusiness export pipeline: six use cases

Technology adoption in Australian agribusiness sits between stages two and three on our maturity curve — past pilot, before full integration. Six use cases collectively address the quality yield, cost efficiency, and market access dimensions of the Japan/Korea export corridor.

Adoption context

Australia's agribusiness technology uptake is driven by three forces: labour cost pressure (AU agricultural wages among the highest globally), the Japan/Korea premium market requirement for consistent grading and traceability, and government R&D co-investment through MLA's Producer Levy-funded programs. Digital ear-tag systems now cover an estimated 40–50% of the national cattle herd [Kira estimates based on MLA digital adoption surveys].

Market sizing

The Australian agri-tech market is estimated at approximately AUD 1.2–1.8 bn in annual revenue across precision agriculture, livestock management, and grain analytics [Kira estimates]. The sub-segment most directly applicable to the Japan/Korea export corridor — export-grade quality assurance and traceability technology — is approximately AUD 200–300 mn [Kira estimates].

Key forecast sensitivities

Cattle herd trajectory is the single largest volume variable. MLA projects herd easing from 30.78 mn to approximately 28 mn head by 2028 [MLA 2026]. Each 1% herd reduction reduces slaughter by approximately 90,000 head, translating to roughly 20,000 tonnes CWE less production annually [Kira estimates].

Yen/AUD rate is the single largest value variable. A 10% depreciation in JPY against AUD (e.g. JPY/AUD 105 → 115) reduces the AUD repatriation value of Japan beef exports by approximately AUD 70–80 mn annually [Kira estimates].

KAFTA safeguard reinstatement risk in 2026–2027 could suppress Korean volume by 10–15% in any year it is applied above trigger thresholds [Kira estimates based on DFAT KAFTA trigger levels].

China reactivation as a major AU beef buyer following any resumption of suspended trade would redirect a portion of export supply away from Japan/Korea, tightening available volumes and supporting price for existing buyers [Kira estimates].
