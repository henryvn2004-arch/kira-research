Macro indicators driving logistics demand

Thailand's GDP grew 2.4% in 2025 [NESDC 2026], with 2026 projected at 2.3% and 2027 at 1.8% [BoT 2026]. Soft headline growth masks the real logistics engine: China+1 manufacturing relocation and record investment applications concentrated in the Eastern seaboard.

Growth is soft, but investment is not

Real GDP expanded 2.4% in 2025 [NESDC 2026]; NESDC's 2026 range is 1.5–2.5% and the Bank of Thailand sees 2.3% in 2026, easing to 1.8% in 2027 [BoT 2026]. Exports and technology-cycle private investment carry the expansion; households and SMEs stay under pressure. For logistics, the trade and investment channels matter more than consumption.

China+1 is the freight tailwind

BOI investment applications reached THB 1.37 trn (~USD 42 bn) in Jan–Sep 2025, up 94% YoY [BOI 2025]. China accounted for close to 40% of approved applications. Chinese firms in industrial estates rose from 875 (2023) to 1,584 (2025) [IEAT 2025]. Roughly one-third of approved capital (~USD 2.08 bn) landed in the EEC [BOI 2025].

Gateway position

Thailand sits as ASEAN's principal multimodal land bridge — ranked 34th on the World Bank LPI 2023 [WB LPI 2023] — with sea, road, and now rail routes into China and mainland Southeast Asia converging on the Bangkok–Eastern seaboard axis.

Infrastructure timeline anchoring the next decade

The 2025–2030 window is the operational ramp for a decade of committed spend. Laem Chabang Phase 3, the three-airports rail, U-Tapao, and the Bangkok–Nong Khai rail shift from procurement into construction and first operations — each one a re-pricing of the lanes around it.

The megaproject pipeline

The three-airports high-speed rail carries a THB 271.8 bn investment across 220 km and nine stations, targeting 2030 operations [EECO 2025]. U-Tapao and the Eastern Aviation City exceed THB 290 bn, with a cargo hub designed for 1 m+ tonnes a year [EECO 2025]. Laem Chabang Phase 3 lifts container capacity to 18 m TEU and 3 m vehicles [EECO 2025].

Execution risk is the variable

Laem Chabang Phase 3 Terminal F1 handover slipped from Nov 2025 over reclamation-contract disputes; the private partner now builds the superstructure toward 2027 operations [Nation 2025]. The pattern — committed capital, contested execution — means operators should position around timing, not announcements.

Rail is the structural cost lever

The Ministry of Transport targets logistics cost at 9.5% of GDP within five years [MOT 2025], leaning on double-track and new rail to shift freight off road. The Bangkok–Nong Khai high-speed line's Phase 2 was approved Feb 2025 [SRT 2025].

Section 05 · Sector Overview & Sizing
010 / 026

Market size & growth: Thailand logistics

Thailand's freight & logistics market is sized at USD 53.4 bn in 2025, reaching USD 56.6 bn in 2026 and ~USD 71 bn by 2030 at a ~5.8% CAGR [Kira estimates]. Freight transport is 61% of the market; courier, express & parcel (CEP) is the fastest-growing function.

The headline anchor

The freight & logistics envelope reads USD 53.4 bn in 2025 rising to USD 56.6 bn in 2026 [Kira estimates]. By function, freight transport commands ~61% of value, with sea, road, and air splitting the balance. State-led infrastructure and China+1 inflows underpin a steady, if unspectacular, mid-single-digit expansion.

Where the growth concentrates

The aggregate masks segment heterogeneity. CEP/parcel compounds at ~7.2% and cold chain at ~8% [Kira estimates]; 3PL at ~7% on a USD 4.8 bn base [Kira estimates]. Road freight — the volume base — grows with the headline as double-track rail slowly captures share.

Fragmentation persists in road

The long tail of road freight remains structurally fragmented — thousands of owner-operators — while parcel and 3PL concentrate around a handful of scaled players. That split defines where consolidation value sits.

Section 05 · Segment Economics
011 / 026

Segment economics — growth, margin, capital intensity

Sub-segment economics diverge sharply. Cold chain and 3PL pair defendable margin with double-digit-adjacent growth; parcel grows volume but has only just clawed back to profit after a brutal price war. Road freight moves the tonnage but compresses margin under fuel and labour.

Cold chain — highest margin, highest capex

Cold-chain operators run the richest margins in the sector on a USD 1.2 bn base growing ~8% [Kira estimates]. Refrigerated storage is ~41% of the segment [Kira estimates]. The catch is capex intensity: temperature-controlled bays and reefer fleets carry payback periods that only clear if food-export and grocery-e-commerce demand holds.

Parcel — scale or exit

The CEP market (~USD 2.9 bn, ~7.2% CAGR [Kira estimates]) handles 10–12 m parcels a day [Bangkok Post 2025] at THB 25–30 per item. After years of losses, Flash returned to profit in 2025 [Flash 2025]; the price floor now rewards density and platform lock-in over reach.

3PL — quietly compounding

The 3PL market sits at USD 4.8 bn, on track to USD 7.4 bn by 2032 [Kira estimates], with road ~67% of mode mix. FDI-driven contract logistics is the most defensible pocket — SCGJWD, WHA, and global forwarders all expanding.

Section 06 · EEC Corridor Activation
013 / 026

The megaproject stack: port, rail, air

The EEC spans Chonburi, Rayong, and Chachoengsao with a ~THB 1.7 trn (USD 49.9 bn) five-year commitment [EECO 2025]. Three assets dominate the logistics picture: a deepwater container port, a 250 km/h rail spine, and a cargo-capable third international airport.

Laem Chabang Phase 3 — the container anchor

Phase 3 adds five berths and two rail yards, lifting the port toward 18 m TEU and 3 m vehicles per year [EECO 2025]. Terminal F1 land reclamation is complete and under testing; the private partner builds the superstructure toward 2027 operations [Nation 2025], after a handover slip over reclamation-contract terms.

Three-airports high-speed rail

The THB 271.8 bn line links Don Mueang, Suvarnabhumi, and U-Tapao across 220 km, nine stations, and five provinces at 250 km/h, targeting 2030 operations [EECO 2025]. It reshapes passenger flow but, more importantly for freight, anchors U-Tapao as an integrated cargo node.

U-Tapao & Eastern Aviation City

The THB 290 bn+ U-Tapao program covers 6,500 rai with a cargo hub built for 1 m+ tonnes a year [EECO 2025], plus an MRO center — positioning the Eastern seaboard as an air-cargo gateway to complement the sea and rail routes.

Section 06 · EEC Corridor Activation (cont.)
014 / 026

From port to platform: multimodal & warehousing

The EEC's value to logistics operators is not the port alone but the rail-to-quay integration and the warehouse belt behind it. The Single Rail Transfer Operator yard and a wave of multi-temperature warehouse supply turn Laem Chabang from a sea gateway into a distribution platform.

The SRTO rail-transfer unlock

Phase 3 pairs new berths with a Single Rail Transfer Operator (SRTO) yard [EECO 2025], moving containers between ship and train without a road leg. This is the structural cost lever behind the 9.5%-of-GDP logistics-cost target [MOT 2025]: every box shifted from truck to rail compresses lane cost and emissions.

Warehouse demand tracks the corridor

Ready-built warehouse stock stands at ~6.4 m sqm with 86–88% occupancy (2024) [Colliers 2025]. E-commerce, cold chain, and specialized occupiers now drive most new take-up, with EEC industrial towns the demand core. Supply growth may nudge occupancy toward the mid-80s by 2027 [Colliers 2025].

Multi-temperature capacity arrives

New supply is deliberately cold-capable: MOL's OMEGA 1 Bang Na adds multi-temperature bays across 100,000 sqm by 2027 [Colliers 2025], while WHA Daiwa Logistics Property and SCGJWD expand temperature-controlled footprints near the EEC and Bangkok's south.

Section 07 · ASEAN Cross-border Trucking
016 / 026

Road and rail on the China–Laos–Thailand corridor

Four GMS road routes — R3A, R9, R12, R3B [ADB GMS 2024] — carry Thailand's overland trade with China and mainland ASEAN. Since 2022, the China–Laos railway has added a faster, competing spine, and the two modes now split cargo by value, urgency, and perishability.

The R3A road corridor

R3A runs Bangkok–Chiang Khong–Houayxay–Boten–Kunming, crossing the 4th Thai–Lao Friendship Bridge. Trade over the route topped CNY 52 bn in 2022 [DITP 2025]. It remains congested, and border friction — single-stop inspection gaps, Lao toll and China customs mark-ups — is the persistent cost, not the road itself.

Rail changes the perishables game

Since direct China–Laos–Thailand rail launched in July 2022, 451 freight trains had run by Feb 2025, moving 230,200 tonnes, as daily routes expanded from 2 to 23 [China-Laos Rail 2025]. For durian and chilled fruit, rail cut transit from ~2 days by road to under 15 hours [DITP 2025] — a decisive shift for time-sensitive exports.

Nong Khai becomes the inland gateway

A freight link now connects Vientiane's Thanaleng Dry Port with Nong Khai [China-Laos Rail 2025]. Thailand's Bangkok–Nong Khai high-speed Phase 2 was approved Feb 2025 [SRT 2025], and a second Nong Khai–Vientiane rail bridge starts in Q3 2026 for 2029 opening [MOT 2025] — closing the last-mile rail gap.

Demand drivers & channel structure

Three demand engines pull logistics infrastructure with them: China+1 export manufacturing, e-commerce and social commerce feeding the parcel base, and cold-chain retail plus food export feeding temperature-controlled demand. Each targets a different asset.

China+1: the freight pull

Chinese manufacturers relocating under tariff pressure lifted Thailand's BOI applications to THB 1.37 trn in Jan–Sep 2025 (+94% YoY) [BOI 2025], concentrated in electronics, EVs, and metals. Computer and electronics output ran ~68% above pre-pandemic levels [BOI 2025]. Each new plant is a fresh freight and warehousing endpoint on the Eastern seaboard.

E-commerce: the parcel pull

Social commerce — the "shoppertainment" model on TikTok and Lazada — drives parcel demand to 10–12 m items a day [Bangkok Post 2025]. Platform–courier tie-ups now route that volume, making marketplace relationships the decisive channel variable.

Cold chain: the temperature pull

Frozen and chilled grocery e-commerce, tourism-driven food service, and food export for durian and seafood pull cold-chain demand. The Eastern region grows fastest on EEC incentives, and multi-temperature warehouse supply is arriving to meet it [Colliers 2025].

Regulatory & policy landscape

Policy is the tailwind; execution is the variable. The logistics-cost reduction target (9.5% of GDP), the EEC Act incentives, and GMS cross-border transport agreements shape the operating environment — but reclamation disputes, single-stop-inspection gaps, and PPP timing decide the real pace.

Headline: the cost-reduction mandate

The Ministry of Transport targets logistics cost at 9.5% of GDP within five years [MOT 2025], from 12.8% in 2025 [NESDC Logistics 2025]. The lever is modal shift — double-track rail, the SRTO yard, and the Bangkok–Nong Khai high-speed line (Phase 2 approved Feb 2025 [SRT 2025]) — moving freight off congested road.

EEC Act incentives

The EEC framework offers land-use rights, tax privileges, and one-stop approvals that anchor the ~THB 1.7 trn investment pipeline [EECO 2025]. BOI promotion is running at record levels [BOI 2025], with roughly one-third of approved capital directed into the corridor.

Cross-border: the friction frontier

GMS agreements underpin R3A/R9/R12 road transport [ADB GMS 2024], but single-stop inspection remains incomplete and Lao toll plus China customs mark-ups add cost. Harmonizing rail–road documentation at Nong Khai is the next unlock.

AI in Thailand logistics — sizing & use cases

Thailand ranks 2nd in ASEAN and 14th globally for robot installations, with 24% of the region's robot market in 2024 [ARR 2025]. The bigger story is a labour-gap-driven pivot to autonomous trucking and warehouse automation off a USD 4.3 bn base [Kira estimates].

Adoption trajectory

Thailand held the largest share (24%) of Southeast Asia's robot market in 2024 [ARR 2025], anchored by the EEC's automotive and electronics base. Industrial-robot density is second only to Singapore in ASEAN.

The labour-gap forcing function

A structural driver shortage makes automation strategic. The autonomous-trucks market is modelled to grow from USD 4.3 bn (2025) toward USD 14.7 bn by 2031 at ~23% CAGR [Kira estimates] — fleet operators adopting to cover capacity, not just cut cost.

The skills bottleneck

High upfront cost, integration complexity, and an AI-talent shortage are the brakes. Programs such as the pledge to train 100,000 Thais in AI by 2026 [Nation 2025] target the gap, but capability lags capital.
