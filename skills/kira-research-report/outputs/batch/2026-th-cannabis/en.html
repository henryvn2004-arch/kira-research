A market re-regulated, not repealed

Three years after Thailand became Asia's first country to decriminalise cannabis, a June 2025 rule pulled the flower back under medical control. The reset is structural

On 26 June 2025 the Ministry of Public Health made cannabis flower a "controlled herb" sold only on a doctor's prescription [MoPH 2025], banning recreational, online and advertised sales. This did not ban cannabis — it moved the plant from a near-open retail market into a traditional-medicine framework. The wide-open dispensary model that defined 2022-24 is being dismantled by design, not by accident.

Contraction, then reconstitution

The shop base is falling hard: of 8,636 licences that expired in 2025, only 1,339 renewed [DTAM 2025], and a further 9,800 expire across 2026-27. Yet a smaller, compliant market is forming underneath — anchored by prescriptions, GACP-certified supply and export flow. The next 18 months decide who survives the licensing gate.

Five strategic implications for market participants

The shift from an open retail market to a prescription regime relocates where capital, capability and compliance investment should land over the next 24 months.

01
Reposition around the prescription, not the storefront
Only 15.5% of expiring licences renewed in 2025 [DTAM 2025]. The defensible unit is no longer a walk-in shop but a prescriber relationship and a clinic-linked dispensing licence. Anchor the model on medical demand, not tourist footfall.
Anchor: 15.5% 2025 renewal rate
02
Certify for export or forfeit the value pool
Just 217 farms hold GACP against ~11,800 licence holders [DTAM 2026]. Certified, export-ready flower captures EU and Australian demand while domestic retail contracts. Uncertified capacity is stranded — sell into it or exit.
Anchor: <2% of holders are export-ready
03
Build the medical-demand rails now
Prescriptions route through PT33 forms, trained prescribers and subsidised GPO products at THB 100-500 [GPO 2025]. Operators that plug into telemedicine and clinic referral own the demand the retail ban is redirecting, not destroying.
Anchor: 3,000+ trained prescribers
04
Treat the Cannabis-Hemp Act as the real risk gate
The controlled-herb rule is administrative; a full Act — or a narcotic relisting — is the binding uncertainty [Nation 2025]. Model both. Compliance discipline is the cheapest hedge against a further tightening the politics still permits.
Anchor: Draft v3 Cannabis-Hemp Act pending
05
Consolidate compliant capacity while valuations reset
With ~9,800 more licences expiring through 2027 [DTAM 2025], distressed but compliant assets — GACP farms, clinic licences, extraction lines — come cheap. Acquire the certification, not the storefront, before the medical market re-rates.

Section 02 · Market Sizing
007 / 016

From the green rush to the reset

Decriminalisation in June 2022 unleashed a fast, informal build-out — thousands of shops, a claimed THB 42.8 bn economy and up to 60,000 jobs [UTCC 2025]. The 2025 reset does not erase that base; it re-channels it toward medical and export value at a lower headline run-rate.

The green-rush peak

The University of the Thai Chamber of Commerce sized the cannabis and hemp economy at up to THB 42.8 bn (~USD 1.2 bn) by 2025, growing 10-15% a year, supporting as many as 60,000 jobs and drawing over THB 6 bn of investment [UTCC 2025]. Much of that value sat in unregulated retail and tourism-facing sales.

What the reset removes

The June 2025 rules strip out recreational, online and advertised sales — the fastest-growing, least-defensible slice. Our reads see the addressable pool contracting through 2026 [Kira estimates] as tourist and casual demand exits before medical and export channels scale.

What it preserves

Cannabis stays legal. The durable value — medical dispensing, GACP cultivation, extracts and exportable flower — remains, and re-rates on quality and compliance rather than on shop count.

Section 03 · The Regulatory Reset
008 / 016

The controlled-herb notification, decoded

The pivot runs through one instrument: the Ministry of Public Health's Notification on Controlled Herbs (Cannabis) B.E. 2568, effective 26 June 2025 [MoPH 2025]. It re-regulates flower under traditional-medicine law rather than the narcotics code — a lighter but binding leash.

Flower becomes prescription-only

Cannabis flower buds are now a controlled herb requiring a paper PT33 prescription specifying strain, dose and duration, valid up to 30 days [MoPH 2025]. Recreational, online, vending-machine and advertised sales are banned. Leaves and stems remain saleable under a Section 46 licence.

Not a narcotic — for now

Crucially, flower sits under the Thai Traditional Medicine Knowledge Act (1999), not the Narcotics Act [Tilleke 2025]. Penalties are lighter, and compliant operators are not criminalised. Ministers have signalled narcotic relisting only if non-compliance persists.

Enforcement has teeth

Guidance sets licence suspension of 30-90 days for breaches, with immediate revocation for repeat offences or sales to vulnerable groups [DTAM 2025]. Records must be kept for a year — turning a cash retail trade into a documented medical supply chain.

Section 04 · Licensing Dynamics
009 / 016

The renewal collapse reshapes the estate

Licensing is where the reset bites first. A staggered expiry schedule — combined with a renewal rate that fell to one in six [DTAM 2025] and a four-premise eligibility rule — is thinning an 18,000-strong estate toward a compliant medical core.

Expiry meets attrition

Of 8,636 licences expiring in 2025, only 1,339 renewed — 7,297 lapsed [DTAM 2025]. The wave continues: 4,587 licences expire in 2026 and 5,210 in 2027. Most operators cannot meet the new prescriber and premises requirements, so attrition compounds the expiry curve.

Four doors, not any door

New and renewed licences are confined to four premise types — clinics, pharmacies, herbal-product retailers and traditional-healer sites [DTAM 2025]. The standalone tourist dispensary has no eligible category; it must convert, co-locate with a clinic, or close.

The compliance cost wall

Retaining a licence now means employing a certified prescriber, sourcing GACP-graded flower and keeping auditable records. For thin-margin shops that cost is decisive — and is the mechanism consolidating the market toward capitalised operators [Kira estimates].

Section 05 · Medical Demand
011 / 016

Rebuilding demand through the prescription rail

The reset routes demand through medical infrastructure the state is actively expanding: trained prescribers, PT33 forms, government-produced medicines and telemedicine tracking. Access narrows, but it formalises into a supply chain operators can plug into.

Prescribers and clinics

Over 3,000 practitioners across six professions and folk healers have completed training to prescribe cannabis for defined symptom groups [DTAM 2025]. Traditional-medicine clinics inside government hospitals issue PT33 forms and dispense at subsidised prices, anchoring a legitimate demand channel.

Standardised, affordable supply

The Government Pharmaceutical Organization supplies standardised cannabis formulations at THB 100-500 [GPO 2025], setting a price and quality reference the private market must meet. Affordability keeps genuine patients inside the legal system rather than the grey market.

Telemedicine as the bridge

A prescription-tracking and telemedicine layer is being built to connect patients, prescribers and roughly 12,000 dispensaries that must upgrade to the medical model [Tilleke 2025] — the plumbing that lets the reset scale rather than simply shrink the market.

Section 08 · Forecast & Outlook
015 / 016

A dip, then a value-led recovery

Our base case sees the economy dip through 2026 as recreational demand exits, then recover on medical and export value — reaching ~THB 55 bn by 2030 [Kira estimates]. The spread between scenarios is unusually wide, set almost entirely by the legislative path.

Base case

Anchored to the ~THB 42.8 bn 2025 print [UTCC 2025], we model a contraction to ~THB 35 bn in 2026 as retail closes, then recovery to ~THB 55 bn by 2030 [Kira estimates] as prescriptions, GACP exports and extracts scale into the gap.

What tilts the upside

A passed Cannabis-Hemp Act that stabilises rules, faster GACP certification, and export traction in Germany and Australia. Certified supply of 217 farms and rising [DTAM 2026] is the leading indicator to watch.

What caps it

A narcotic relisting, political reversal, or export competition from lower-cost origins keeps the bear case live. The regime remains administrative and reversible until an Act locks it in.
