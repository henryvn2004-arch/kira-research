How we built this view

This view is built from two evidence streams: primary inputs — operator and enterprise channel reads on compute capacity, LLM deployment timelines, and governance posture — and secondary anchors — IMDA, EDB, and MAS publications, hyperscaler investment disclosures, and triangulated market estimates.

Primary inputs

Operator & enterprise reads
Reads on GPU compute capacity build-out, enterprise LLM deployment timelines and managed-service adoption drawn from hyperscaler investment releases, enterprise software filings, and sector practitioner commentary from finance, logistics, and government verticals.
Analyst synthesis
KIRA in-house triangulation across substantive English-language research queries, pattern-matching against regional digital-infrastructure and AI-services studies. Tagged [Kira estimates] throughout.
Governance reads
Analysis of IMDA and PDPC publications including Model AI Governance Framework v2, the National AI Strategy 2.0, and MAS guidance on responsible financial-sector AI. Calibrated against implementation timelines.

Secondary anchors

Statistical authorities
Infocomm Media Development Authority (IMDA), Economic Development Board (EDB), Ministry of Finance (MOF), and Singapore Department of Statistics. Source of digital-economy, investment-approval, AI-budget, and employment anchors, cited inline.
Hyperscaler filings
AWS, Google Cloud, and Microsoft Azure Singapore investment announcements and region-expansion disclosures. NVIDIA DGX Cloud partnership terms and NUS/NTU research infrastructure commitments used as capital proxy metrics.
Triangulated estimates
Enterprise adoption rates and AI-services spend splits where single-source disclosure is thin. We combine 2–3 inputs and disclose method. Tagged [Kira estimates] with anchor metric noted inline. See endnote.

Five strategic implications

Where value accrues through 2029, and what market participants should do about it. Governance compliance is the new enterprise procurement gate; compute access is table stakes but utilisation is the differentiator.

01
Governance compliance as enterprise moat
Model AI Governance Framework v2 has moved from voluntary to expected in regulated sectors. Vendors with pre-built compliance tooling — audit trails, bias documentation, human-oversight gates — close deals faster in finance and government. Ungoverned models will not clear procurement.
ANCHOR · MAS 2026 Notice on AI risk applies to all licensed banks
02
Finance is the anchor vertical
DBS, OCBC, UOB, and GXS collectively deploy over 800 internal models in active production [Kira estimates]. Credit decisioning, document-intelligence, and client-communication automation dominate. Smaller fintechs adopt via API; incumbents build proprietary fine-tuned stacks.
ANCHOR · finance = ~34% of enterprise AI spend
03
Power is the compute ceiling
Singapore's power grid cannot absorb unconstrained GPU build-out; the post-moratorium data-centre framework caps new capacity additions and prioritises AI workloads over general compute [EMA 2026]. Operators with existing power allocation own the scarcest input.
ANCHOR · DC moratorium lifted with capacity caps
04
Research-to-commercial pipeline is a real differentiator
NUS and NTU jointly published over 2,400 AI papers in 2024 [NRF 2025], and spin-out count grew 31% YoY. The A*STAR AI accelerator and AISG grants funnel applied talent toward enterprise use-cases. Competitors without this pipeline face 18–24 month talent acquisition lags.
ANCHOR · NUS/NTU ranked top-5 APAC AI research institutions
05
Regional hub thesis is real but contested
Malaysia, Indonesia, and Thailand are all landing hyperscaler regions at lower power-cost. Singapore's advantages — rule-of-law, talent, connectivity, financial-centre co-location — are durable for high-sensitivity, high-complexity workloads only. Commodity GPU inference may migrate offshore within 3 years.
ANCHOR · SG power cost 3–4× regional peers

Section 08 · Governance Framework
010 / 014

Model AI Governance Framework v2 and sectoral rules

Singapore's regulatory posture is principles-based, not prescriptive — the Model AI Governance Framework v2 (MAIGF v2) sets expectations around transparency, accountability, and human oversight. MAS and MOH layer sector-specific requirements on top.

MAIGF v2: the baseline

Updated in 2020 and supplemented by the AI Verify testing toolkit (2022, updated 2024), the framework covers explainability standards, data governance, human-in-the-loop requirements, and operational risk management for AI systems [PDPC 2024]. Adoption is voluntary but de-facto mandatory for MAS-regulated entities and government-procurement contracts above SGD 2 m.

MAS AI Notice: binding on finance

The Monetary Authority's Notice MO&S 2026 requires all MAS-licensed banks and insurers to document model risk for any model affecting a customer outcome, maintain human override capability on credit decisions, and conduct annual AI risk assessments [MAS 2026]. Non-compliance triggers supervisory review. DBS, OCBC, and UOB have each published public AI Governance Statements in response.

Procurement gate: AI Verify certification

Government agencies procuring AI products above SGD 2 m must require vendors to pass AI Verify v2 evaluation across 11 ethical-principle dimensions [GovTech 2026]. This creates a de-facto certification moat for vendors — those without certification face 6–9 month procurement delays or exclusion from public-sector contracts.

Section 09 · Talent & Regional Positioning
011 / 014

Talent pipeline and Singapore's regional position

Singapore's talent advantage is real but under pressure. 15,000 AI practitioners target by 2030 [IMDA 2024], an active Tech.Pass visa framework, and world-ranked university AI labs. Against that: a regional cost differential that threatens to shift commodity inference workloads offshore.

Talent production: structured and fast

NUS School of Computing and NTU jointly produce approximately 3,200 AI-relevant graduates annually [Kira estimates], supplemented by SkillsFuture AI reskilling streams targeting mid-career professionals. The AI Apprenticeship Programme (AIAP) at AISG has placed over 600 researchers into industry roles since 2019 [AISG 2025]. A*STAR employs ~500 active AI researchers across 4 institutes.

Tech.Pass: structured global intake

The Tech.Pass scheme (launched 2021, enhanced 2024) allows established AI practitioners earning above SGD 22,500/month to relocate without an employer sponsor [MOM 2025]. Approximately 2,800 Tech.Pass holders were active as of Q4 2026 [MOM 2026], adding senior ML engineering, LLM research, and AI product expertise that the domestic university pipeline cannot supply fast enough.

Regional competition: real and sharpening

Malaysia (Johor), Indonesia, and Thailand are all landing hyperscaler GPU infrastructure at power costs 3–4× lower than Singapore [Kira estimates]. For commodity inference workloads — batch prediction, fine-tuning at scale — the economics will shift regional. Singapore defends in high-trust, latency-sensitive, regulatory-compliant workloads: private-banking model inference, government workloads requiring sovereign residency, and R&D that benefits from proximity to a deep talent pool.

Section 10 · Forecast 2027–2031
012 / 014

Forecast 2027–2031: three scenarios

Our base case holds ~28% CAGR through 2029, moderating to ~18% in 2030–2031 as the market matures. The bull case assumes Singapore successfully anchors ASEAN headquarters AI functions; the bear case reflects power-capacity constraints and regional compute arbitrage compressing local spend.

Base case (probability 55%): disciplined growth

Enterprise adoption deepens in finance and logistics; government AI spend ramps through 2029 per Budget commitments. Power-capacity allocation remains tight but does not collapse. AI-services market reaches USD 14.5 b by 2030 [Kira estimates]. Singapore retains hub status for high-trust workloads; commodity inference moves partially to Malaysia and Indonesia by 2029.

Bull case (probability 25%): ASEAN HQ magnet

Singapore successfully positions as the ASEAN AI headquarters destination for multinationals preferring regulatory clarity and talent depth over cost. Tech.Pass intake accelerates; NUS/NTU spin-out exits attract APAC AI talent. Market reaches USD 19 b by 2030 [Kira estimates], with Singapore commanding a disproportionate share of ASEAN enterprise AI spend.

Bear case (probability 20%): cost pressure erodes volume

Power costs and land constraints push 3–4 GPU-intensive workloads offshore per year from 2028 onward. Governance complexity adds friction for mid-market enterprise adoption. Market growth slows to ~15% from 2028; total market reaches only USD 10 b by 2030 [Kira estimates]. Singapore retains financial-sector AI spend but loses logistics, manufacturing, and BPO AI workloads to MY and ID.
