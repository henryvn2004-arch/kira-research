Vietnam's healthcare market is propelled by three durable forces: a rapidly ageing population, a middle class that reached 26% of the population by 2026 and demands higher-quality services, and a public hospital system that cannot expand fast enough to absorb the load. Private capital — domestic conglomerates and regional healthcare groups — is filling the gap, but penetration remains early-stage.

Demand is demographic, not cyclical.

Vietnam's population aged 60+ rose from 11.9% in 2019 to 13.9% by 2023 WHO 2024, compressing the timeline to a super-aged society. Chronic disease burden — cardiovascular, diabetes, respiratory — expands proportionally, and these conditions require multi-visit, capital-intensive care. Even a 2027 economic softening would compress discretionary health spending at the margin, not the structural demand floor.

Public capacity cannot absorb the load alone.

Vietnam's hospital bed stock stands at approximately 3.4 beds per 1,000 population World Bank 2024 — against WHO's 5-bed guidance and below the ASEAN-4 median. National targets call for 33 beds per 10,000 by 2025, a figure requiring sustained capital outlay from both public budgets and private operators. Central-level hospitals in Hanoi and Ho Chi Minh City routinely run at 120–140% of stated capacity.

Private sector: early-stage consolidation underway.

Approximately 400 private hospitals operate as of 2025 MOH Vietnam 2025, concentrated in Hanoi, Ho Chi Minh City, and second-tier cities. Foreign-backed transactions — Thomson Medical's USD 381.4 mn acquisition of FV Hospital, Raffles Medical's expansion, and Korean/Japanese FDI inflows — signal that regional healthcare groups have moved from observer to active acquirer. Profitability among private operators is highly dispersed; scale and brand determine margin.

Market sizing: USD 18–20 bn by 2027, hospital segment at USD 10+ bn

Total healthcare expenditure

Vietnam's total healthcare spending reached approximately USD 17 bn in 2024 World Bank 2024, rising from USD 14.5 bn in 2022 as rising incomes and expanded social insurance coverage drove utilisation. Our analysts project the range to reach USD 18–20 bn in 2027 Kira estimates, implying an 8–10% CAGR — above nominal GDP growth, consistent with the global pattern of healthcare spending outpacing the broader economy as populations age.

Hospital segment

Hospital-sector revenues — the investable and analytically tractable subset — were valued at USD 8.3 bn in 2024, forecast to reach USD 11.6 bn by 2030 at a 5.9% CAGR TechSci Research 2024. This more conservative rate reflects the dominance of public-sector pricing in the base; as private capacity expands at premium price points, realised CAGR may trend above consensus.

Pharmaceutical market

The pharmaceutical segment, sized at USD 6.7 bn in 2022, is forecast to reach USD 8.9 bn by 2026 at a 7.3% CAGR Vietnam Briefing 2025. Vietnam's total pharmaceutical import value reached USD 4.3 bn in 2025, underscoring that domestic manufacturing captures volumes but foreign products command most of the value pool — a structural margin opportunity for import-distribution operators.

Analyst note

GDP per capita is projected to reach USD 7,500 by 2030 World Bank 2025. Historical Southeast Asian patterns suggest healthcare spending accelerates non-linearly at the USD 4,000–8,000 per-capita income band — Vietnam is entering that zone now, making 2026–2030 the most dynamic window for private sector entry.

Hospital infrastructure: structurally under-bedded, policy-targeted for private expansion

Bed ratio: below regional and WHO benchmarks.

Vietnam had 3.38 hospital beds per 1,000 population in 2023 World Bank 2024. National targets set 33 beds per 10,000 by 2025 and 35 by 2030, with a long-term vision of 45 per 10,000 by 2050 — implying a persistent investment requirement of 2,000–4,000 new beds per year to stay on trajectory. The ASEAN-4 comparators sit at 3.1–4.5 beds per 1,000; Vietnam's absolute deficit is structural, not cyclical.

Public hospital overload: 120–140% occupancy in tier-1 cities.

Central hospitals in Hanoi and Ho Chi Minh City routinely operate at 120–140% of stated capacity Kira estimates based on MOH utilisation reports. Ho Chi Minh City is adding three regional general hospitals in 2025–2026 — Thu Duc (1,000 beds), Hoc Mon (700 beds), and Cu Chi (1,000 beds) MOH Vietnam 2025 — which will relieve near-term pressure in the south but will not address structural deficit in Hanoi or secondary cities.

Private sector: growing share but starting from a low base.

Private hospitals account for approximately 14% of hospital count and a policy-targeted 10% of total beds MOH Vietnam 2025. Government Decision 201/QD-TTg mandates the private share of beds reach 15% by 2030 — doubling from the current base over four years. This is a de-facto policy licence for private capital deployment at scale, backed by a regulatory framework that increasingly allows 100% foreign-owned hospital operations via WTO CPC 9311 provisions.

BHYT: from near-universal coverage to free basic hospital services by 2030

Coverage scale: USD 12 bn market with structural tailwinds.

The Vietnam health insurance penetration market was valued at USD 12.1 bn in 2025 GlobalRiskCommunity 2026, forecast to reach USD 22.6 bn by 2034 at a 7.2% CAGR. BHYT social insurance is the dominant engine: a mandatory, largely employment-funded scheme covering the formal sector, with government subsidy extending coverage to poor, near-poor, and rural households. The non-BHYT voluntary private health insurance market remains small but is growing as the urban middle class purchases supplemental coverage.

2026 reform package: reimbursement rates and coverage extensions.

Vietnam's National Assembly passed amendments to the Health Insurance Law effective January 2026 that carry three significant implications. First, reimbursement rates for cross-referral (tuyến trên) care are increasing, partially addressing the long-standing disincentive that caused patients to self-refer to central hospitals at lower coverage rates. Second, two groups — persons aged 75+ receiving social retirement benefits and near-poor households — receive 100% BHYT coverage from January 2026 Vietnam.vn 2025. Third, the law includes a roadmap toward free basic hospital services from January 2030, funded through progressive BHYT premium expansion.

Implications for private hospitals entering the BHYT network.

Private hospitals that achieve BHYT accreditation gain access to an enrolled population of 90+ mn — a near-captive demand pool with guaranteed reimbursement. The trade-off: BHYT fee schedules compress inpatient margins, particularly for low-complexity procedures where public hospitals compete directly. The rational private-sector strategy is to offer BHYT access in specialist wards (oncology, cardiovascular, orthopaedics) where complexity premiums are defensible, while maintaining premium out-of-pocket wards at arm's length from BHYT pricing.

Medtech and pharmaceutical import dynamics

Medtech: USD 1.77 bn in 2025, 90%+ imported; pharma approaches USD 9 bn

Medical devices: structurally import-dependent.

Vietnam's medical device market reached USD 1.77 bn in revenue in 2025 Vietnam Briefing 2025, growing at an 8.6% CAGR toward USD 2.47 bn by 2029. More than 90% of medical equipment is imported, driven by low import duties (zero for many critical items), absence of domestic production quotas, and limited local manufacturing capability. The government actively encourages imports where local supply cannot meet clinical demand — a policy stance unlikely to change materially before 2030.

Key supplier countries and FDI flows.

In the first half of 2024 alone, medtech and medical device projects attracted over USD 200 mn in new FDI, primarily from South Korea, Singapore, Japan, and China Vietnam Briefing 2025. Total registered healthcare FDI capital reached approximately USD 1.8 bn across 160 active projects by mid-2024. Korean and Japanese groups are active across diagnostics, imaging, and surgical instruments; Singapore-based groups concentrate in hospital services and distribution.

Pharmaceutical market: domestic volume, foreign value.

The pharma market is approaching USD 8.9 bn by 2026 at a 7.3% CAGR from a USD 6.7 bn base in 2022 Vietnam Briefing 2025. Total pharmaceutical imports reached USD 4.3 bn in 2025. Domestic production covers approximately 60% of volume but only 46% of value — meaning imported products, though fewer in number, command the high-value patented and specialty drug segment. More than 90% of active pharmaceutical ingredients (APIs) are still imported, creating a structural dependency that the 2025 Pharmacy Law revision is beginning to address by expanding FIE rights in API import and local distribution.
