How we built this view

This view reads two connected mechanics: the January 2025 recalibration of the S13O/13U/13D tax incentives and the asset-class allocation that recalibration, and the wider cycle, is steering. Built from regulator primary sources and bank family office survey data, triangulated to a Singapore lens. Every quantitative claim carries a source tag. Data horizon: 2020–2029E.

01
Primary regulatory sources
Our analysts drew directly from MAS published statistics and circulars — the Singapore Asset Management Survey 2024, the fund tax incentive circular and its 2025 updates (S13O / S13U / S13D), and the 5 July 2023 revision to application guidelines. Threshold, local-spend, and Designated-Investments conditions are quoted from the circular text, not paraphrased.
02
Allocation and flow evidence
Asset-class allocation anchored on bank family office survey data — the UBS Global Family Office Report 2025 and 2026, read for global baselines and the Asia-Pacific and Southern-Asia cut — cross-checked against MAS survey strategy-mix and inflow tables. Where a Singapore-specific figure is not separately disclosed, we triangulate from the regional aggregate and tag [Kira estimates].
03
Flow and sizing model
Annual 13O/13U application flow is not published office-by-office; our analysts model it from the incentive-base trajectory, cohort composition, and structuring-firm intake, calibrated to the base crossing 2,000 in 2025. Forward allocation and formation paths are scenario-based. Figures beyond declared 2024 survey data are tagged [Kira estimates].

The incentive flow moved to substance

From January 2025, S13O and S13U measure AUM against Designated Investments rather than total assets, add tiered local business spend, and hold both schemes to a 10% or SGD 10 mn local-investment floor [MAS Tax 2024]. The 5 July 2023 tightening had already reset the application bar. The net effect is a flow filter: fewer, larger, better-papered applications, which we size at roughly 200–240 a year versus a ~400 peak [Kira estimates].

Allocation is re-pointing at the same time

Family office books are mid-recalibration: 60% intend a strategic allocation change in 2026, up from 35% [UBS GFO 2026]. Private credit doubled its share in a year [UBS GFO 2025]; private equity and real estate are being trimmed; gold and digital assets sit at the margin. The floor and the allocation cycle pull the same way — toward substance held in, and deployed through, Singapore.

02 · Strategic Implications 05 / 14
Five strategic implications for market participants

Derived from the flow shift and allocation trends in this view — relevant to single-family offices, private banks, fund administrators, private-credit and PE managers, and the legal and tax advisers structuring 13O/13U applications in Singapore.

01 · STRUCTURE
Design the book to Designated Investments, not gross AUM
From January 2025 the AUM test runs on Designated Investments, and on cost not NAV [MAS Tax 2024]. Cash-heavy or non-qualifying holdings no longer count toward the SGD 20 mn / SGD 50 mn thresholds. Applications built on the old gross-asset assumption fail the entry test; re-paper before filing.
Anchor: SGD 20 / 50 mn DI-basis AUM floor [MAS Tax 2024]
02 · FLOW
Price for fewer, larger, slower-to-file applications
The quality filter cut the flow to an estimated 200–240 a year from a ~400 peak [Kira estimates], while ticket size rose. Service firms should staff for depth per mandate — substance builds, IP hires, local-spend plans — not for volume throughput.
Anchor: ~210 apps 2024 vs ~400 peak [Kira estimates]
03 · ALLOCATION
Build the private-credit shelf before the reallocation lands
Private credit doubled its family office share in a year and is the most-sought class for new capital [UBS GFO 2025], while Singapore's private-credit AUM grew 21% [MAS AMS 2024]. Managers with asset-backed and senior-lending shelves capture the reallocation; latecomers meet a crowded book.
Anchor: +21% SG private-credit AUM 2024 [MAS AMS 2024]
04 · LOCAL SPEND
Turn the local-investment floor into deployed strategy
The 10% or SGD 10 mn qualifying-local floor [MAS Tax 2024] is a mandated allocation, not a compliance line. Direct it toward SGX-listed equities, VCC-wrapped local funds, or SG-licensed private credit so the floor earns rather than parks.
Anchor: 10% / SGD 10 mn local floor [MAS Tax 2024]
05 · TIMING
File into the three-month window with a complete pack
From July 2025 MAS targets a three-month approval for complete applications [MAS Tax 2024]. The speed accrues only to files whole on submission — DI-basis AUM, two-or-three IPs, tiered local-spend plan, source-of-wealth. Incomplete filings forfeit the window.
Anchor: 3-month target for complete files [MAS Tax 2024]

Two rounds of tightening

The 5 July 2023 revision reset the entry bar: funds had to meet the SGD 20 mn (13O) or SGD 50 mn (13U) minimum at application and throughout, be invested wholly in Designated Investments at the point of application rather than held in cash, and staff at least one non-family investment professional [MAS Tax 2023]. The January 2025 round went further on measurement — AUM tested against Designated Investments and on cost, with tiered local spend and the local-investment floor [MAS Tax 2024].

The application flow narrowed in response. Our analysts size gross filings at roughly 200–240 a year against a ~400 peak [Kira estimates] — a deliberate quality-over-quantity filter, not a loss of interest. The base still crossed 2,000 in 2025 [MAS SFO 2025] because approved offices are larger and stickier.

What the filter selects for

The regime now rewards economic substance: professionals on the ground, spend in the local economy, and assets held in qualifying form. Marginal applicants — thin books, cash parked pending deployment, single-family-member teams — no longer clear the gate. That reshapes the intake toward mid-size and large principals with real operating substance.

Speed for the prepared

MAS paired the higher bar with a three-month approval target for complete applications from July 2025 [MAS Tax 2024]. The trade is explicit: more to prove, but a faster answer for files that arrive whole. Incomplete submissions — missing IP hires, no tiered local-spend plan, unresolved source-of-wealth — fall out of the window and back into open-ended review.

The two-scheme ladder

Section 13O requires a minimum SGD 20 mn AUM and two investment professionals, at least one a non-family member; Section 13U requires SGD 50 mn and three professionals [MAS Tax 2024]. From January 2025 that AUM is measured against Designated Investments and on cost, not NAV — a stricter test than the pre-2023 gross-asset basis [MAS Tax 2024].

Local spend scales with size

Tiered local business spend now applies: SGD 200k a year below SGD 250 mn, SGD 300k to SGD 2 bn, and SGD 500k above [MAS Tax 2024]. The design forces economic contribution proportional to the book — advisory, audit, tax, IP compensation, and operating cost incurred in Singapore.

The floor on top

Both schemes must also hold 10% of AUM or SGD 10 mn in qualifying local investments, whichever is lower [MAS Tax 2024] — the allocation channel we read on page 12. Together, the ladder converts the incentive from a paper wrapper into a substance commitment.

Nearly half in private markets

The global family office book runs 30% equities, 21% private equity, 18% fixed income, and 11% real estate, with private debt and hedge funds at 4% each and gold at 2% [UBS GFO 2025]. Private equity, private debt, hedge funds and real estate together approach half the portfolio — a private-markets tilt far heavier than a typical institutional balance.

The Asia-Pacific cut

Southern-Asian family offices hold 69% in traditional assets — 31% equities and 27% fixed income — and 31% in alternatives, with private equity 11% and private debt 6% [UBS GFO 2025]. Singapore-domiciled books sit close to this regional shape, with a heavier alternatives skew among the larger 13U cohort [Kira estimates].

Why this matters for Singapore

Alternatives grew 14% and private credit 21% in the 2024 survey [MAS AMS 2024]. Family offices are over-indexed to exactly this segment, lifting their share of fee economics above their share of headline AUM.

07 · Local Floor 12 / 14
The local-investment floor as an allocation channel

The 10% or SGD 10 mn qualifying-local floor [MAS Tax 2024] is where policy and portfolio meet: a mandated slice of every 13O/13U book that must be held in Singapore. Read as strategy, not compliance, it is a recurring inflow into a defined set of local assets.

What qualifies

The floor is satisfied by qualifying local investments — among them SGX-listed equities, qualifying debt securities, funds distributed in Singapore, and eligible private-market vehicles [MAS Tax 2024]. For a mid-size 13U book, 10% on a cost basis is a material, standing commitment; for the largest books, the SGD 10 mn lower-bound caps the absolute figure.

Because the test is on cost, not NAV [MAS Tax 2024], the floor is a stable planning input rather than a moving target — an office can size it once at deployment and hold it through the cycle.

Where the floor points capital

  • SGX-listed equities and REITs — the most liquid route to satisfy the floor, and a natural home for the trimmed-property allocation seeking listed real-asset exposure.
  • SG-domiciled private credit — pairs the reallocation magnet with the local requirement; asset-backed and senior-lending shelves distributed in Singapore qualify.
  • VCC-wrapped local funds — sub-fund structures that book qualifying local exposure inside the same chassis as the main portfolio.

The aggregate read

Across a base past 2,000 offices [MAS SFO 2025], even the lower-bound floor aggregates into a durable, policy-mandated bid for Singapore assets — modest per office, material in sum, and by design counter-cyclical to redemption behaviour elsewhere [Kira estimates].

Base case to 2029

Our analysts project managed AUM toward SGD 9–10 trn by 2029 [Kira estimates], a ~10% CAGR off the 2024 SGD 6.07 trn [MAS AMS 2024], assuming the tax regime stays stable and the flow filter holds. The family office base extends past 3,000 offices on fewer but larger annual approvals [Kira estimates].

Scenarios

  • Bull: private credit and infrastructure demand accelerates; 13U-scale applications rise and the local floor deepens the SGX bid [Kira estimates].
  • Base: steady filtered formation; allocation re-points as surveyed, floor holds.
  • Bear: a source-of-wealth enforcement shock or a further threshold tightening slows the flow; AUM growth reverts toward 6–7% [Kira estimates].

The binding variable

Not demand — substance capacity. Local-spend, IP-hire, and source-of-wealth throughput cap how fast quality applications clear. That, not headline appetite, sets the ceiling.
