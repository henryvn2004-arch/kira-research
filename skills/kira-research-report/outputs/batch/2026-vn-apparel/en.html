How we built this view
This view draws from two evidence streams: primary inputs — analyst synthesis and channel checks across garment exporters, factory operators and sourcing intermediaries in HCMC, Binh Duong, Dong Nai and northern clusters — and secondary anchors — association export statistics, customs trade data, listed-company filings, and US and EU import authority data. Every quantitative claim carries an explicit source tag.

Primary inputs

Factory and sourcing checks
Conversations with garment manufacturers, sourcing agents, industrial-park operators and global brand procurement contacts across HCMC's garment district, Binh Duong and Dong Nai clusters — surfacing real CMT-to-OEM transition timelines, buyer concentration, and margin erosion dynamics not visible in headline export numbers.
Analyst synthesis
KIRA in-house triangulation across customs trade series, listed-group quarterly filings and brand-order-book data to derive unit-value trends, ODM share estimates and vertical integration economics. Tagged inline as [Kira estimates].
Buyer and brand reads
Sourcing-shift signals from US and EU brand procurement disclosures; US CBP import statistics by HTS chapter; EU Eurostat apparel import data — cross-checked against VN customs export series.

Secondary anchors

Association and statistical authorities
Vietnam Textile & Apparel Association (VITAS) export and capacity data; General Statistics Office output and FDI series; General Department of Vietnam Customs garment and fabric trade flows — cited inline by named source and period.
Listed-company filings
Annual reports and quarterly results of TNG, Thanh Cong (TCM), Song Hong Garment (SHG), Garmex Saigon and Vinatex subsidiaries — revenue, margins, buyer mix and capex plans cited by company and period.
Triangulated estimates
Where no single source covers a figure — particularly OEM/ODM share and vertical integration economics — we combine 2–3 inputs and disclose the method. Tagged [Kira estimates]. Approximately 35% of quantitative claims are KIRA-derived; 65% are externally cited.

US position held through tariff absorption

Vietnam absorbed ~20% MFN tariffs on most garment categories [USTR 2025] and retained its #1 US apparel supplier rank by leveraging speed-to-market, buyer relationships and logistics depth [OTEXA 2025]. No comparable sourcing base — in cost, capacity and delivery — has displaced it for core US fast-fashion and sportswear buyers [Kira estimates].

The vertical integration bet

CMT's share of the export floor has fallen from ~70% in 2021 to ~55% in 2025 [Kira estimates]. OEM accounts for ~35% and ODM ~10% — small but growing, as listed groups like TNG and Thanh Cong invest in fabric-sourcing and design capacity to own more of the value chain [Kira estimates].

Five strategic implications for market participants.
Derived from the structural findings in this report. Each implication is anchored to a quantitative data point that market participants should treat as a directional signal, not a point forecast.
01 ·
Accelerate the CMT-to-OEM transition
CMT exporters average ~3–5% EBITDA margins; OEM operators achieve ~8–12% by owning fabric sourcing and QC [Kira estimates]. The transition requires 18–36 months of buyer qualification, but the margin delta justifies the investment for any exporter shipping USD 50 m+ to a single buyer.
ANCHOR: 3–5% CMT vs 8–12% OEM margins [Kira estimates]
02 ·
Maximize EVFTA preference before the 2027 review
EVFTA's fabric-forward rule delivers 0–12% EU duty savings [MOIT 2025]. With utilization still at ~48% [Eurostat 2025], the unrealized preference is significant. Exporters who secure EU-qualifying fabric supply chains now will have a structural cost advantage over non-EVFTA competitors by 2027.
ANCHOR: ~48% EVFTA utilization 2025 [Eurostat 2025]
03 ·
Diversify beyond the top-3 US buyer concentration
The top 3 US apparel brands account for an estimated ~35–40% of Vietnam's US garment exports [Kira estimates]. Exporter revenue resilience requires adding 4–6 qualified buyers per factory; EU, Japanese and Korean buyer pipelines reduce single-country demand risk.
ANCHOR: ~38% top-3 US buyer concentration [Kira estimates]
04 ·
Build fabric-sourcing before the capacity arrives
Korean and domestic mills are adding upstream capacity, but commissioning takes 24–36 months [Kira estimates]. Exporters who lock in offtake agreements now — before capacity tightens — will qualify for EVFTA cumulation and reduce the ~60% fabric import bill [VITAS 2025] from FY2027 onward.
ANCHOR: ~60% of fabric currently imported [VITAS 2025]
05 ·
Invest in automation to defend the wage gap
Vietnam garment wages of ~USD 300/month [Talentnet 2025] are rising ~10%/yr. Automation and AI-assisted quality control maintain cost competitiveness while freeing labour for ODM design functions. Operators who automate CMT lines protect margins as the China wage gap narrows to ~USD 275/month by 2028 [Kira estimates].
ANCHOR: ~USD 300/mo VN garment wage [Talentnet 2025]

US export resilience
07 / 14
America's #1 apparel supplier — sticky despite tariffs, vulnerable to concentration.
Vietnam shipped ~USD 14.5 bn to the US in 2025, holding its #1 supplier position at ~38% of US apparel imports by value [OTEXA 2025]. The position is structural: no alternative market offers Vietnam's speed, capacity and buyer-relationship depth at comparable cost. But buyer concentration is the embedded risk — three US mega-brands drive a disproportionate share of order volume.

How tariffs were absorbed

The ~20% MFN blended rate [USTR 2025] was absorbed three ways: (i) factories accepted ~3–4 pp margin compression to retain volume; (ii) US brands accepted ~5–7 pp of FOB price increase passed through to retail; and (iii) lead-time and logistics advantages offset the tariff premium versus alternative origins [Kira estimates]. Bangladesh and India gained share in mid-range woven, but not in key US activewear and casual categories [OTEXA 2025].

Buyer concentration is the structural vulnerability

An estimated ~38% of Vietnam's US garment exports flow to the top 3 US buyers [Kira estimates]. Any order pullback, sourcing-review or brand restructuring creates concentrated volume risk. The exporters best insulated are those with 6+ qualified buyer relationships spread across US, EU and Japanese markets [Kira estimates].

China substitution incomplete

China's US apparel share fell from ~35% in 2018 to ~20% in 2025 [OTEXA 2025]. Vietnam captured approximately half of that shift, with Bangladesh and Cambodia absorbing most of the remainder [OTEXA 2025]. Further China substitution is incremental from here [Kira estimates].

EU market dynamics
08 / 14
EVFTA preference half-utilized — a ~USD 600 m duty saving left on the table.
Vietnam exported ~USD 5.1 bn of garments to the EU in 2025 [Eurostat 2025], but EVFTA preference utilization stands at only ~48% [VCCI 2025], leaving an estimated ~USD 600 m of annual duty savings unrealized [Kira estimates]. The barrier is the fabric-forward rule: most Vietnamese garment makers still source fabric from China rather than from EVFTA-qualifying origins.

EVFTA is under-used at the critical stage

EVFTA's two-stage fabric-forward rule requires fabric woven in Vietnam or the EU (with Korea cumulation available) [MOIT 2025]. Of the ~USD 5.1 bn EU garment export, only ~48% qualifies under EVFTA due to Chinese fabric in the supply chain [VCCI 2025]. The remaining 52% enters under EU standard MFN duty of 10–12% for key garment chapters [Eurostat 2025].

The Korea cumulation bridge

The EVFTA Korea-cumulation clause lets fabric of Korean origin qualify as Vietnamese origin for EU preference [MOIT 2025]. As Korean mills relocate to Vietnam, this pathway broadens — exporters co-located near Korean fabric mills will be the first to lift EVFTA utilization above 60–65% [Kira estimates].

EU due-diligence rules add sourcing complexity

The EU Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CS3D) and ESG disclosure requirements will require garment exporters to document full supply-chain traceability by 2027 [EU 2025]. Exporters with vertically integrated or directly contracted fabric sources are better positioned for compliance [Kira estimates].

Vertical integration
10 / 14
CMT scale is necessary — OEM/ODM economics is where margin lives.
CMT margins average ~3–5% EBITDA [Kira estimates]; OEM operators — who own fabric sourcing, QC and logistics management — achieve ~8–12%. The integration shift is two-stage: first, fabric-sourcing ownership (CMT to OEM); then design capability (OEM to ODM). Listed Vietnamese groups are at various stages of each transition.

The CMT ceiling

CMT's ~55% share of export revenue reflects Vietnam's legacy cut-sew base [Kira estimates]. Revenue scales with volume, but margin does not — fabric cost, shipping variability and buyer price pressure all compress EBITDA. Factories at CMT often operate with net profit below 2% after exchange-rate moves [TNG 2025].

OEM: the inflection most exporters are pursuing

OEM requires the exporter to source, quality-test and manage fabric independently before cutting. The margin premium is 3–7 pp over CMT [Kira estimates]. TNG generates ~78% of revenue from OEM/ODM [TNG 2025]; Thanh Cong (TCM) is at ~65% [TCM 2025], each investing in fabric-sourcing desks and vendor-qualification systems.

ODM — design ownership — is early but real

ODM accounted for an estimated ~10% of Vietnam's garment exports in 2025 [Kira estimates], concentrated in sportswear and workwear niches where Vietnamese designers have developed buyer-qualified patterns. The ODM share is expected to reach ~15% by 2028 as brand relationships and design talent deepen [Kira estimates].

Risk register
12 / 14
Three structural risks shape the 2026–2028 export outlook.
The garment sector's resilience has been demonstrated through tariff cycles and demand slowdowns — but three structural risks have the potential to compress margins and disrupt market positions if they materialize simultaneously [Kira estimates].
Risk Severity Horizon Mechanism Mitigation available
US tariff escalation High 2026–2027 A further 10–15 pp MFN tariff increase on apparel chapters would erase most remaining CMT margin. Vietnam has no bilateral FTA with the US; MFN rate absorption capacity is near its limit at ~20% [USTR 2025]. Bangladesh and India's lower cost base becomes structurally attractive for basic categories [Kira estimates]. Move volume to OEM/ODM faster (margin buffer); diversify buyer base to EU and Japan (where EVFTA and VJEPA reduce tariff exposure) [Kira estimates].
Order concentration unwinding High 2026–2028 Top-3 US buyers represent ~38% of Vietnam's US exports [Kira estimates]. Any major brand's sourcing diversification to South Asia, nearshore Mexico or Ethiopia creates concentrated revenue risk for the Vietnamese factories dependent on that order book. Garmex Saigon's 2024–2025 capacity underutilization illustrates the downside [Garmex 2025]. Qualify 6+ buyers per factory; secure EU/Japanese buyer relationships; build ODM design capability to reduce substitutability [Kira estimates].
Labour cost and turnover Med Structural Garment wages rising ~10%/year [Talentnet 2025] with ~20% annual turnover [VN News 2025] compress CMT margins and raise training costs. The wage gap versus Bangladesh (~USD 95/mo) and India (~USD 145/mo) narrows each year [Kira estimates]. Skills for ODM design roles are the binding constraint on the model transition. Automate CMT lines; invest in QC and design skill-building; shift to higher-complexity categories (activewear, technical garments) with higher per-unit value [Kira estimates].

Methodology endnote and source mix

How to read the numbers

This view triangulates two evidence streams. Where a figure carries a named source tag — for example [OTEXA 2025], [Eurostat 2025], [VITAS 2025] — the underlying data comes from that source, with the full citation listed opposite. Where a figure carries [Kira estimates], our analysts derived it from a blend of disclosed inputs — customs trade series, listed-company filings, buyer-concentration analysis and channel checks — using the method noted in the relevant section.

Approximately 35% of quantitative claims are KIRA-derived; 65% are externally cited. Export figures are on an association-reported or customs-data basis. OEM/ODM mix percentages are KIRA analyst estimates derived from listed-company revenue disclosures and supply-chain channel checks — directional, not audited. Forecast scenarios are KIRA constructions anchored to the VITAS 2030 target trajectory.

Currency: figures are USD unless marked. Vietnam Dong (VND) figures for listed companies are stated at ~25,000 VND/USD average conversion for illustrative purposes.
