A production base, not a demand story

Thailand is Southeast Asia's largest vehicle producer — 1.456 million units in 2025, of which roughly two-thirds shipped for export [FTI 2025]. The 2026 thesis is structural, not cyclical: the question is not whether Thailand makes cars, but which powertrains and which suppliers the production base keeps. EV sales reached an 19.4% share at 120,301 units in 2025 [FTI 2025].

The repositioning is asymmetric

Japanese OEM share fell to 69.3% in 2025 from 76.7% a year earlier [Nikkei 2025]. Suzuki and Subaru have exited local assembly; Honda halved capacity to 270,000 units [Just Auto 2025]. Chinese entrants — led by BYD, MG and GWM — localized to roughly 22.6% of the market while supplying ~85% of pure-EV volume [Proliance 2025].

Five strategic implications for market participants

Five forces reorder the production base through 2031. Each is anchored to documented data and KIRA's synthesis across the Thai automotive cluster.

01 · LOCALIZE
Local content becomes the gate, not the option
The 40% BEV local-content rule plus a 2:1 production offset in 2026 rising to 3:1 in 2027 [BOI 2025] turns import-and-sell into a penalty-repayment risk. Imported battery cells stop counting toward local content after June 2026 [Just Auto 2025]. Localize cells and modules or forfeit incentives.
Anchor: 3:1 offset ratio by 2027
02 · CONSOLIDATE
Japanese capacity rationalizes around hybrids
Suzuki and Subaru exited local assembly; Honda halved capacity to 270,000 units [Just Auto 2025]. Survivors defend pickups and lean into HEV, where 2025 output rose 12.3% to 214,317 units [FTI 2025]. Hybrid is the Japanese bridge — not a full BEV pivot.
Anchor: −7.4 pp Japanese share in one year
03 · DISRUPT
Chinese entrants rewire the supplier base
Chinese OEMs reached ~85% of pure-EV volume [Proliance 2025], importing a vertically integrated supply model that bypasses local Tier-2/3 firms. 816 component companies and ~326,400 workers sit in the exposure zone [ISEAS 2025].
Anchor: 326k workers in transition exposure
04 · AI/DIGITAL
Smart-factory adoption defends cost position
Over 40% of large Thai manufacturers have adopted at least one Industry 4.0 technology [MOI 2025]. Predictive maintenance and vision-based quality control offset the cost gap against Chinese vertically integrated lines; laggards cede unit economics.
Anchor: 40%+ Industry 4.0 adoption
05 · EXPORT
Exports anchor the base through the 2027 cliff
Exports of 935,750 units in 2025 [FTI 2025] keep lines running as domestic demand softens; pickups led Middle East shipments at 63% [FTI 2025]. Policy now lets exported EVs count toward offset obligations — exports become the localization release valve.
Anchor: ~64% of output exported

Section 04 · Macro Context
007 / 024

Macro indicators driving automotive demand

A 2.4% 2025 print slowing toward ~2.0% in 2026 [NESDC 2026] frames a demand-constrained year. Tourism and government capex carry the economy; household credit and exports stay the swing factors for vehicle sales.

Growth decelerates into 2026

The economy grew 2.4% in 2025, ahead of forecasts, but momentum cools [Nation 2025]. NESDC holds a 1.5–2.5% range for 2026 with a 2.0% midpoint [NESDC 2026]; the World Bank reads it lower at 1.3% [World Bank 2025]. Auto demand sits at the soft end of that band.

Inflation diverges, credit stays tight

Bank of Thailand projects headline inflation near 2.9% in 2026 on energy pass-through, easing to 1.5% in 2027 [BOT 2026]. Tight household credit and high auto-loan rejection continue to cap pickup and passenger-car affordability — the structural drag on domestic sales.

Capex and tourism offset

Private consumption, a tourism recovery, and stepped-up government capital expenditure sustain 2026 momentum [NESDC 2026]. None of these pull vehicle demand directly; the auto base leans on exports and policy-driven EV adoption instead.

Section 04 · Macro Context
008 / 024

Policy and trade moves shaping the auto base

The macro lever for automotive is industrial policy, not monetary policy. EV 3.5 incentives, tightening local-content rules, and US tariff exposure set the 2026 operating envelope more than GDP does.

Industrial policy leads

EV 3.5 (2024–2027) underwrites the BEV build-out with excise cut to 2% and consumer subsidies tapering to THB 50,000 in 2026 [EY 2024]. From 2026 importers must assemble two vehicles locally per one imported [BOI 2025] — the policy converts demand support into a localization mandate.

Trade exposure rises

Export growth is expected to slow in 2026 on US tariff measures and global trade friction [NESDC 2026]. Middle East shipments — Thailand's third-largest auto export market at 200,001 units in 2025 [FTI 2025] — already dipped on Strait of Hormuz disruption, exposing the export-led model's fragility.

Four flagged risks

NESDC flags external demand, household debt, geopolitical trade, and structural competitiveness as 2026 risk signals [NESDC 2026]. For autos, all four route through the same channel: can the production base hold export volume while it re-tools for EVs?

Section 05 · Sector Sizing
010 / 024

Production volume: a low base recovering toward 1.5 million

Output fell 0.9% to 1.456 million units in 2025 [FTI 2025] — near a five-year low — before a forecast 3.1% rebound in 2026. The recovery is export-led and EV-mix-driven, not a domestic demand surge.

2025: a trough, not a collapse

Production reached 1,455,569 units in 2025, down 0.9% but above the annual target [FTI 2025]. Exports fell 8.2% to 935,750 units valued at THB 622 bn [FTI 2025]. Pickup output was resilient at 712,579 units (−3.0%); ICE passenger cars bore the brunt at −29.1%.

2026: an export-anchored rebound

FTI targets 1.50 million units in 2026 (+3.1%) — 950,000 for export and 500,000 domestic [FTI 2025]. January–April 2026 output rose 4% to 473,545 units, with 66.9% built for export [FTI 2026], confirming the export anchor as the base's stabilizer.

Capacity overhang

Installed capacity is ~2.55 million units, projected near 2.98 million by 2028 [Intellify 2025]. At 1.5 million output, utilization runs near 55–60% [Kira estimates] — the structural gap that forces Japanese consolidation and rewards whoever fills lines with EV volume.

011 / 024

Market value: domestic sales stabilize, EV share climbs

The domestic passenger-car market is valued near USD 17.8 bn in 2025 [Statista 2025], growing low-single-digit as EV penetration deepens. Value migrates toward electrified powertrains faster than total volume moves.

A low-growth value pool

Thailand's passenger-car market is sized at USD 17.8 bn in 2025, projected to ~USD 19.8 bn by 2029 at ~2.7% CAGR [Statista 2025]. Total new-vehicle sales reached 592,608 units in 2025 [Intellify 2025] — a soft domestic pool that magnifies the powertrain-mix shift.

EV value share accelerates

EVs took a 19.4% sales share at 120,301 units in 2025 [FTI 2025]. Industry bodies project the EV share rising to 20–25% in 2026 [Thansettakij 2026]. Because BEV transaction prices sit above mass ICE cars, the EV value share runs ahead of its unit share.

Pickups hold the value floor

One-ton pickups remain the value and export backbone — Thailand's structural advantage and the segment Japanese OEMs defend hardest. The 2026 value question is whether pickup electrification (Isuzu, Toyota) protects the floor as passenger value tilts to Chinese BEVs.

Section 06 · Segment Economics
012 / 024

Powertrain economics: ICE erodes, hybrids bridge, BEV scales

Three powertrains, three trajectories. ICE passenger output fell 29.1%, hybrids rose 12.3%, and BEV jumped 632% in 2025 [FTI 2025] — and each carries a different supplier base and margin profile.

ICE: managed decline

ICE passenger production dropped to 247,929 units (−29.1%) in 2025 [FTI 2025]. ICE retains the deepest local supplier base — an engine-and-transmission ecosystem that BEVs render largely obsolete. Pickups (−3.0%) keep diesel-ICE relevant, slowing the decline at the value end.

Hybrids: the Japanese bridge

HEV output rose 12.3% to 214,317 units and PHEV surged 116.7% to 17,296 [FTI 2025]. Hybrids let Japanese OEMs reuse much of the ICE supplier base while electrifying — the highest-margin defensible position as the BEV price war compresses pure-EV economics.

BEV: scale, thin margin

BEV production reached 70,914 units, up 632% [FTI 2025]. Chinese-led, vertically integrated, and priced into a margin-eroding war [Just Auto 2025]. Volume is real; profit per unit is not — which is precisely why localization economics, not headline EV share, decide who survives.

Section 06 · Segment Economics
013 / 024

The supplier base is where the transition bites

An ICE vehicle uses ~30,000 parts; a BEV uses ~3,000 [ISEAS 2025]. Entire component categories disappear — and Thailand's deep Tier-2/3 base is the asset most exposed to the powertrain shift.

The parts-count cliff

The 10x reduction in part count rendering engines, transmissions and exhaust systems obsolete is the core risk: 816 component companies and ~326,400 workers sit directly in the transition's exposure zone [ISEAS 2025]. This is the single largest socio-industrial cost of Thailand's EV pivot.

Tier-1 adapts, Tier-2/3 squeezed

Tier-1 suppliers can co-develop new EV technology and largely withstand the shift; Tier-2 and Tier-3 firms — engine-component specialists with thin balance sheets — face consolidation or exit [Thai News 2025]. The Chinese "vertical-integration import" model bypasses these locals, deepening the squeeze.

The assembly-base risk

If Chinese OEMs source domestically only to clear local-content thresholds — with minimal technology transfer — Thailand risks becoming a simple assembly base rather than a value-added hub [ISEAS 2025]. The localization rules are designed precisely to force deeper sourcing before the 2027 cliff.

Strategic position

Toyota anchors Thailand as its largest Southeast Asian production base. The November 2025 ninth-generation Hilux unveils a multi-pathway strategy — ICE and BEV variants on the IMV-successor platform — with phased global rollout from FY2026 [Toyota Asia 2025].

The Hilux BEV pairs a 59.2 kWh battery, 300km+ range and dual-motor AWD [InsideEVs 2025], electrifying the pickup franchise rather than abandoning it. Thailand–Australia engineering co-development signals the export base stays central.

Strategic position

BYD's Rayong factory — its first outside China — spans ~960,000 sq m with 150,000-unit annual capacity and over THB 35 bn invested [Nation 2025]. It reached 100,000 cumulative Thai deliveries by October 2025 [CnEVPost 2025], the fastest local ramp of any entrant.

Strengths

021 / 024

Demand structure: export-led, pickup-anchored

With 935,750 units exported in 2025 [FTI 2025] against ~500,000 sold domestically, Thailand is an export platform first. The Middle East, Australia and ASEAN absorb the pickup-led volume that keeps lines running through soft local demand.

The export channel dominates

Exports of 935,750 units (−8.2%) worth THB 622 bn in 2025 [FTI 2025] outweigh domestic sales roughly two to one. Through April 2026, 66.9% of output was export-bound [FTI 2026] — the channel that decouples the production base from Thailand's weak domestic cycle.

The Middle East and Australia lead

The Middle East was the #3 export market at 200,001 units (21.2% of exports) in 2025, with pickups at 63.4% of the mix [FTI 2025]. Australia is a top destination for passenger and pickup units worth ~USD 2.9 bn [Trading Economics 2024]. EV exports are nascent but emerging.

Domestic channel reorders

Domestically, Chinese EV entrants are building direct and brand-store channels that bypass the traditional Japanese-aligned dealer network. The showroom model is fragmenting just as the powertrain mix shifts — a double disruption to incumbent distribution economics.

Section 09 · Regulatory & Policy
022 / 024

Regulatory landscape: localization rules tighten toward a 2027 cliff

EV 3.5 is the dominant policy lever. Its local-content thresholds, production-offset ratios, and battery-cell sourcing deadlines — not consumer subsidies — now determine which OEMs keep their incentive economics.

From subsidy to localization mandate

EV 3.5 (2024–2027) cut excise to 2% and taper subsidies to THB 50,000 in 2026 [EY 2024]. The binding terms are structural: 40% local content for BEVs, 45% for PHEVs, plus a 2:1 production offset in 2026 rising to 3:1 in 2027 [BOI 2025].

The battery-cell screw

The share of imported battery cells counting toward the 40% rule falls from 15% to 10% in January 2026 — and to zero after June 2026 [Just Auto 2025]. Manufacturers must file domestic-sourcing plans. Yet Thailand still has no domestic cell manufacturer [ASEAN Briefing 2025] — the policy's central tension.

The 2027 cliff and the rescue debate

Cumulative EV-chain investment hit THB 140 bn by October 2025 across 43 manufacturers [BOI 2025]. With EV 3.5 support ending in 2027, auto groups urge a successor scheme before carmakers revert to tariff-free imports — the "production cliff" that would hollow the supplier base [Nation 2026].

Section 11 · Five-Year Outlook
025 / 024

Five-year outlook: production recovers as the EV mix passes 30%

Total output rebuilds slowly toward the ~2 million-unit range by 2030 [Kira estimates] while EV share of sales climbs past 30% [Intellify 2025]. The base survives — but its powertrain and supplier composition is remade.

Base case: managed transition

Production climbs from 1.50 million units in 2026 toward ~1.9–2.0 million by 2030 [Kira estimates], as EV sales reach 31.3% of the market and EV production targets approach 750,000 units a year by 2030 [Intellify 2025]. Exports remain the anchor; pickups hold the value floor.

Bull case: localization sticks

If a post-2027 incentive scheme lands and domestic battery-cell capacity materializes, Thailand consolidates as ASEAN's EV export hub — output exceeds 2.1 million units and the supplier base re-skills into EV components rather than hollowing out.

Bear case: the 2027 cliff

Without a successor to EV 3.5, carmakers revert to tariff-free imports and the local supplier base erodes [Nation 2026]. Output stalls near 1.5 million; Thailand drifts toward a thin assembly base as value migrates to integrated Chinese supply chains.
