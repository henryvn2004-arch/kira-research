How we built this view
This view is built from two evidence streams: primary inputs — analyst synthesis, supplier and industrial-park channel checks across the northern and central automotive clusters — and secondary anchors — government statistics, customs trade data, listed-group filings, and ministry policy texts. Every quantitative claim carries an explicit source tag.

Primary inputs

Field and cluster checks
Conversations with Tier-1 and Tier-2 suppliers, industrial-park developers, and procurement contacts across Vinh Phuc, Hai Duong, Bac Ninh, Hung Yen and Chu Lai — surfaces real spec-in cadence, OEM qualification timelines, and local-content economics not visible in headline data.
Analyst synthesis
KIRA in-house triangulation across customs trade series, FDI registrations, and supplier filings to reconcile a components market sized differently across sources. Tagged inline as [Kira estimates].
Operator and specialist reads
Input from component makers, JV operators, and supporting-industry associations closer to the OEM-qualification and localization-rate economics than aggregate trade tables.

Secondary anchors

Statistical and customs authorities
General Statistics Office output and FDI series; General Department of Vietnam Customs trade data for component import and export value — cited inline by named source and period.
Ministry and group filings
Ministry of Industry & Trade supporting-industry policy and decree texts; listed-group and JV disclosures on plant investment, supplier counts, and capacity — cited by named source and period.
Triangulated estimates
Where no single source covers a figure, we combine 2–3 inputs and disclose the method. Tagged [Kira estimates]. Approximately 30% of quantitative claims are KIRA-derived; 70% are externally cited.

Executive summary
04 / 14
Export muscle, local-content gap — the value chain deepens from the Tier-2 floor up.
Vietnam runs a paradox: it is the world's third-largest exporter of automotive wiring harnesses [UNIDO 2025], yet the localization rate on passenger vehicles still sits near 7–15% [Dan Viet 2024]. The 2026 inflection is policy-led — Decree 205 of 2025 resets supporting-industry incentives just as Japanese OEM anchors push qualification deeper into the Tier-2 base.

An export story already, a domestic-content story not yet

Vietnam exports components at scale — global majors place wiring, seats, tyres, plastics and precision-mechanics plants here for shipment to the US, Europe and ASEAN [Vietnambiz 2025]. Yet the chain is shallow at home: makers produce mostly simple, bulky, low-tech, labour-intensive parts [Dan Viet 2024], so a domestically assembled car still carries roughly only 20% local content [Vietnam Briefing 2025] and the import bill climbs.

Policy resets the Tier-2 economics

Decree 205 of 2025, effective 1 September 2025, lets prioritized supporting-industry firms recover up to 50% of machinery, R&D, training and technology-transfer cost [VietnamPlus 2025]. Paired with the standing 0% import duty on components for domestic assembly through 2027 [Vietnam Briefing 2025], it is aimed squarely at moving Vietnamese SMEs into the OEM-qualified Tier-2 tier [Kira estimates].

Market sizing
07 / 14
A USD 4.5 bn market that exports more than it localizes.
The components market was sized near USD 4.5 bn in 2023, on a path toward USD 13.1 bn by 2032 at roughly 12.6% CAGR [R&M 2024]. The defining feature is trade: a USD 5.93 bn component import bill in 2025, +21.5% YoY [Bao Dau Tu 2025] runs alongside a fast-growing export base that turned a roughly USD 1 bn parts surplus [Doanh Nhan SG 2025].

Imports fund the missing middle

Vietnam spends USD ~5.93 bn a year importing components [Bao Dau Tu 2025] — chiefly from Korea, China, Thailand, Japan and India [Dan Viet 2024]. These are the engine, transmission, electronics and precision parts the home chain does not yet make at OEM grade, so domestic assembly running at record capacity pulls imports up in step [Vietnambiz 2025].

Exports are the new growth pillar

Component and parts exports reached over USD 3.9 bn in Q1 2025 alone [Doanh Nghiep TT 2025]. Japan is the largest export market at USD ~788 m in Q1, up 10% YoY; the US is second near USD 749 m [Doanh Nghiep TT 2025]. Wiring harness is the single largest export line and ranks #3 worldwide [UNIDO 2025].

The surplus turn matters

The parts trade tipped to roughly a USD 1 bn surplus [Doanh Nhan SG 2025] — a structural signal that Vietnam's edge is export-grade labour-intensive components, not yet import-substituting domestic content [Kira estimates].

Localization gap
08 / 14
The localization rate, between reality and ambition.
Passenger-vehicle localization sits near 7–15% in practice [Dan Viet 2024], far below the policy target of 40–45% by 2025 and 50–55% by 2030 [Vietnam.vn 2025]. The old volume-based ratio was abolished in favour of the ASEAN value-added formula, but the structural gap — a thin Tier-2 base — is the same.

Target and reality have not converged

Government policy set 40–45% by 2025 and 50–55% by 2030 [Vietnam.vn 2025]; the working reality on most passenger lines is 10–15%, with the average near 7–10% [Dan Viet 2024]. Foreign carmakers present for 30 years still trail the 40% commitment, because the supplier ecosystem to feed them never fully formed [Dan Viet 2024].

The measure changed, not the gap

Vietnam abolished the legacy localization-ratio method and moved to the ASEAN value-added formula [Bao Chinh Phu 2022]. On that basis the Toyota Vios reaches ~43% [Vietnamnet 2025] — but the headline-vehicle average stays low because precision and electronic content is imported.

EV pulls a parallel curve

VinFast reports EV localization near ~60% ex-battery — body, motor, roof, suspension [Dan Viet 2024]. A domestic OEM scaling EV volume is the one force bending the localization curve upward faster than the legacy ICE supplier base [Kira estimates].

Cluster structure
10 / 14
Two clusters carry the build-out; the Tier-2 floor is the bottleneck.
Demand concentrates in the northern Red River Delta — Vinh Phuc, Bac Ninh, Hai Duong, Hung Yen, Hai Phong — anchored by Toyota, Honda and Ford, and in the central Chu Lai complex built by THACO [Vietnam Briefing 2025]. The structural gap is depth: of 377 auto-related manufacturers, only the foreign-invested 46% reliably supply OEM-grade parts [VIA 2024].

The northern OEM cluster

Toyota's Vinh Phuc plant (1995) localizes through 60 suppliers, 13 Vietnamese, with 1,000+ localized items [Automotive Logistics 2025]; Ford's Hai Duong plant (1997) carries over USD 208 m invested [Automotive Logistics 2025]. Japanese-developed parks — Sumitomo's Thang Long system at 1,100+ hectares [Sumitomo 2025] — house the Tier-2 ecosystem in Vinh Phuc, Hai Duong and Hung Yen.

The central Chu Lai hub

THACO Industries' Chu Lai complex carries USD ~550 m of investment [Komaspec 2025]; in early 2025 it opened auto-body, premium-glass and electrical-equipment plants worth USD ~75 m across 50,000 m² [THACO 2025]. It supplies wire harness (200,000 sets/yr), seats and precision parts to KIA, Mazda, Peugeot, Hyundai, Toyota and Isuzu [THACO 2025].

Climbing the supplier ladder

Tier-2 entry is a multi-year qualification: a Hung Yen mechanical SME invested in CNC and an ISO/TS 16949 quality system over nearly 10 years to become a Tier-2 supplier to Toyota Vietnam [Vietnamnet 2025]. Quality discipline, not capital alone, gates the cluster [Kira estimates].

AI and operations
12 / 14
AI lands first at the quality gate that decides Tier-2 qualification.
For a Vietnamese components maker, the binding constraint is consistent OEM-grade quality, not unit cost. That makes the first AI payback operational — computer-vision defect detection, predictive maintenance, and demand sensing — the same capabilities that close the gap to a Japanese Tier-2 audit [Kira estimates].

Quality is the qualification gate

Tier-2 entry hinges on defect rates and process control an OEM audit will accept [Vietnamnet 2025]. Vision-based defect detection at QC stations replaces sampling inspection and is the highest-return early use case for a supplier chasing qualification [Kira estimates].

Maintenance protects the contract

Once-installed OEM contracts run 5–10 years; a line stoppage risks the relationship. Predictive maintenance now flags failures 2–4 weeks ahead [Oxmaint 2025], and the wider AI-in-manufacturing market scales from USD 8.6 bn (2025) toward USD 230 bn by 2034 [Standard Bots 2025], with agentic adoption forecast to rise from 6% to 24% by 2026 [Deloitte via RTInsights 2025].

Methodology endnote and source mix

How to read the numbers

This view triangulates two evidence streams. Where a figure carries a named source tag — for example [Bao Dau Tu 2025], [VIA 2024], [R&M 2024] — the underlying data comes from that source, with the full citation listed opposite. Where a figure carries [Kira estimates], our analysts derived it from a blend of disclosed inputs — customs trade series, FDI registrations, supplier and group filings, and cluster channel checks — using the method noted in the relevant section.

Approximately 30% of quantitative claims are KIRA-derived; 70% are externally cited. The components market is reported on a market-value basis per the primary anchor; trade figures are on a customs-value basis. Localization rates blend legacy and ASEAN value-added bases and are flagged where the method matters. Cluster and supplier-mix shares are directional KIRA reads, not audited.

Currency: figures are USD unless marked. Forecast scenarios are KIRA constructions anchored to the published ~12.6% CAGR trajectory and the 50–55% 2030 localization target.
