How we built this view
This view is built from two evidence streams: primary inputs — field channel checks, analyst synthesis, and specialist practitioner reads — and secondary anchors — CSA publications, MAS circulars, GovTech releases, INTERPOL ASEAN threat assessments, and listed company filings. Every quantitative claim carries an explicit source tag.

Primary inputs

Practitioner channel checks

Analyst synthesis

Specialist and operator reads
Direct input from managed security service providers (MSSPs), OT/ICS security integrators, and threat intelligence platform vendors operating in the SG-APAC corridor.

Secondary anchors

Regulatory and government sources

Listed company filings

Triangulated estimates
Where no single source covers a figure, we combine 2–3 inputs and disclose the method. Tagged [Kira estimates]. Approximately 38% of quantitative claims are KIRA-derived; 62% are externally cited.

The demand pull is regulatory and structural

Three simultaneous catalysts have compressed enterprise security budgets upward. MAS TRM Guidelines updated in 2024 introduced mandatory third-party risk and cloud security controls with compliance deadlines falling 2025–2026 [MAS 2024]. CSA's Critical Information Infrastructure (CII) sector-specific cybersecurity codes of practice now carry audit obligations for 11 sectors [CSA 2025]. Separately, the SG government's digital infrastructure expansion — SGD 50 bn committed over five years [GovTech 2025] — expands the attack surface and the addressable market simultaneously.

Zero trust: from pilot to procurement wave

Enterprise zero trust adoption has accelerated from 23% in 2022 to 41% in 2025 [Kira estimates], but deployment maturity remains shallow. Most adopters are at Stage 2 (identity-centric, perimeter-diminishing) with fewer than 14% having reached Stage 3 (continuous verification across all workloads) [Kira estimates]. The gap between Stage 2 and Stage 3 drives sustained vendor spend — particularly in micro-segmentation, identity governance, and SASE architecture — over the 2026–2028 window.

Five strategic implications for market participants.
Derived from the structural findings in this report. Each implication is anchored to a quantitative data point that market participants should treat as a directional signal, not a point forecast.
01 ·
Position ahead of the MAS TRM compliance wave
MAS TRM deadline economics compress FSI cybersecurity budgets into 2025–2026. Banks and insurers account for 34% of SG enterprise cyber spend [Kira estimates]. Vendors without MAS-aligned product certification are structurally disadvantaged in procurement cycles beginning now.
ANCHOR: 34% of SG enterprise spend is FSI-sourced [Kira estimates]
02 ·
Zero trust maturity gap is the 3-year product opportunity
Only 14% of SG enterprises have reached full zero trust Stage 3 [Kira estimates]. The 27pp gap between adoption and maturity drives micro-segmentation, identity governance, and SASE spending. Vendors building Stage 2→3 migration tooling are best positioned through 2028.
ANCHOR: 14% at Stage 3 maturity vs 41% adoption [Kira estimates]
03 ·
OT/ICS security is the fastest-growing sub-segment
CII sector code mandates and the SG port/energy expansion create an OT security addressable market growing at 22% CAGR through 2028F [Kira estimates], nearly double the overall market rate. IT/OT convergence specialists command 40–60% margin premiums over generalist vendors.
ANCHOR: 22% CAGR for OT/ICS security 2025–2028F [Kira estimates]
04 ·
Talent deficit structures the MSSP opportunity
A 5,400 FTE cybersecurity talent shortfall [IMDA 2025] makes in-house SOC unsustainable for all but the largest enterprises. MSSP penetration among mid-market firms is rising from 28% to an estimated 44% by 2027F [Kira estimates]. Domestic MSSPs with CSA-accredited operations command a pricing premium over offshore delivery models.
ANCHOR: 5,400 FTE deficit · MSSP penetration 28→44% [IMDA 2025 · Kira estimates]
05 ·
SG's SOC hub ambition creates regional revenue upside
CSA's ASEAN Cybersecurity Strategy positions SG as the region's intelligence-sharing and SOC hub. Cross-border MSSP and threat intelligence revenue from ASEAN clients already represents 18% of SG-headquartered vendors' revenue [Kira estimates], growing to an estimated 26% by 2028F as Thai, Philippine, and Indonesian CISOs seek SG-licensed providers.
ANCHOR: 18% cross-border revenue share, rising [Kira estimates]
