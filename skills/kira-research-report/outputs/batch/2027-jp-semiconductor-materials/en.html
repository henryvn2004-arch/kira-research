The setup

Japan is the gravitational centre of the semiconductor materials layer — roughly 48% of global materials supply [Fuji Keizai] in a market that hit a record USD 67.5 bn in 2024 and USD 73.2 bn in 2025 [SEMI]. The chip-making world consumes materials in Taiwan, Korea and China, but it sources them disproportionately from Japan.

The shift

The 2027 move is about the leading edge. AI accelerators pull the process to 2nm gate-all-around and High-NA EUV, multiplying patterning steps and resist consumption [secondary]. EUV photoresist alone scales from ~USD 296 mn toward ~USD 1.4 bn by 2031 at ~25% CAGR [Valuates] — and Japanese firms hold the bulk of it.

Five strategic implications for 2027

Where the Japanese materials base must act. Each implication is anchored to the section that develops the evidence.

POSITIONING
Defend the EUV resist chokepoint

The most defensible asset is not volume but the EUV photoresist near-monopoly [secondary]. Qualification cycles run years; once a resist is locked into a 2nm flow, it is sticky. The 2027 imperative is to keep that lead as High-NA ramps.

See Competitive landscape · p.011
VALUE MIX
Ride steps-per-wafer, not wafer starts

Advanced nodes multiply patterning, deposition and CMP steps [secondary]. Materials consumption per wafer rises faster than wafer count — the growth lever is content per chip, where Japanese resist, slurry and gas all gain.

See Segment economics · p.009
CUSTOMER RISK
Localise next to the fab, hedge geography

Leading-edge demand sits in Taiwan, Korea and the new US/Japan fabs [secondary]. Suppliers that co-locate resist and slurry plants beside customers — including domestic Rapidus and TSMC Kumamoto — protect share against localisation pressure.

See Demand & supply chain · p.016
POLICY
Bank the security premium

Economic-security policy now treats materials as critical supply, channelling subsidy and demand toward domestic capacity [METI]. The 2027 task is to convert that political tailwind into qualified leading-edge capacity, not just commodity tonnage.

See Policy landscape · p.018
AI & PACKAGING
Follow HBM and packaging into the materials it needs

Packaging materials grew faster than wafer-fab materials in 2024–25 [SEMI], pulled by HBM stacking and 2.5D/3D integration. Dielectrics, RDL resists and bonding films are the next content tier — where Japanese suppliers should over-index capacity in 2027.

SECTION 04

Macro context: Japan 2027

A low-growth, normalising-rate economy where a weak-then-firming yen has flattered export earnings and where semiconductors have become a declared strategic priority. The constraint is not demand — it is talent, power, and the pace of leading-edge localisation. Materials are now national-security infrastructure.

Section 04 · Macro context
07 / 23

Macro indicators driving the 2027 base

Modest growth and a normalising policy rate frame the backdrop; the swing variable for materials exporters is the yen, which weakened sharply before firming — directly shaping reported margins on dollar-priced sales.

Growth is slow but stable

The Bank of Japan guides real growth around 0.5–0.8% for fiscal 2026 [BOJ], a low-growth backdrop where any high-growth export cluster — semiconductors and their materials — carries outsized macro weight.

Rates normalising off zero

The policy rate sits at ~0.75% with the central bank guiding further hikes and core inflation revised up toward ~2.8% [BOJ]. The end of ultra-loose policy lifts the cost of the capacity-heavy investments materials suppliers are making.

The yen does the heavy lifting

A yen near ~155–162/USD [BOJ] inflates the yen value of dollar-priced materials exports — flattering reported revenue, but raising the cost of imported tooling and feedstock [Kira estimates].

Section 05 · Sector sizing
09 / 23

Market size & growth: semiconductor materials

The materials market set records on the AI-chip recovery. Front-end (wafer-fab) materials dominate value; back-end (packaging) materials are the faster-growing slice — and Japan over-indexes in both.

A USD 67.5 bn record, growing

Global materials revenue rose 3.8% to USD 67.5 bn in 2024 and 6.8% to USD 73.2 bn in 2025 [SEMI]. Fuji Keizai projects front-end materials reaching ~USD 56 bn and back-end ~USD 14 bn by 2030 [Fuji Keizai].

Front-end leads, back-end accelerates

Wafer-fab materials were USD 42.9 bn and packaging materials USD 24.6 bn in 2024; packaging grew faster (+4.7% vs +3.3%) [SEMI] — a signal of where AI/HBM demand is pulling content.

Japan's near-half share

Japanese suppliers hold roughly 48% of global materials [Fuji Keizai], and far more in the leading-edge chemistries — a structural position built on decades of qualification depth [Kira estimates].

Segment economics: where Japan owns the chokepoints

The materials layer is not one market but a dozen specialty chemistries. Japan's share is highest — often near-monopoly — in the most process-critical ones: photoresist, silicon wafer, photomask blanks, and high-purity gases.

Photoresist — the crown jewel

Five firms supply ~90% of advanced photoresist [Fuji Keizai]; in EUV the concentration is even tighter [secondary]. Years-long qualification makes share sticky and margins structurally high [Kira estimates].

Wafers & blanks — duopolies

Shin-Etsu and SUMCO together exceed half of global 300mm wafers [secondary]; photomask blanks are a Shin-Etsu/AGC duopoly near ~50% [Fuji Keizai]. Capital-intensive, slow to displace.

CMP & gases — contested but strong

CMP slurry sits near ~40% with strong Japanese players [Fuji Keizai]; high-purity gases and sputtering targets remain Japan-led but face more competition [Kira estimates].

Section 08 · Demand & supply chain
17 / 23

Demand drivers & the EUV supply chain

The materials cycle is now a leading-edge story. AI accelerators and HBM concentrate demand at the most advanced nodes, where each generation multiplies the patterning, deposition and polishing steps that consume Japanese materials.

Consumers vs suppliers

Taiwan was the largest materials consumer at USD 20.1 bn in 2024, ahead of China (USD 13.5 bn) and Korea (USD 10.5 bn) [SEMI]. Demand sits offshore; supply of the critical chemistries stays disproportionately Japanese [Kira estimates].

Steps-per-wafer multiplier

Multi-patterning, GAA transistors and High-NA EUV raise the number of critical patterning steps per wafer [secondary] — so resist and ancillary consumption climbs faster than wafer starts.

Localisation pressure

New fabs in the US, Japan (TSMC Kumamoto, Rapidus) and Europe pull suppliers to co-locate [secondary] — a logistics and qualification challenge, but also a way to defend share.

Section 09 · Policy landscape
19 / 23

Policy: the revival strategy & economic security

Policy now treats advanced chips and their materials as strategic infrastructure. The state is funding leading-edge logic at scale and designating materials supply chains as critical — a structural tailwind for domestic capacity.

A multi-trillion-yen commitment

The revival strategy builds on the 2021 and 2024 plans to rebuild leading-edge logic and advanced packaging onshore [METI]. Government support for Rapidus alone is capped at ~¥1.7 tn [METI].

Rapidus as anchor demand

Rapidus targets 2nm logic with ~¥676 bn for front-end and ~¥127 bn for back-end approved for fiscal 2025 [METI] — a domestic, leading-edge customer for Japanese materials [Kira estimates].

Materials as critical supply

Economic-security policy designates semiconductor supply chains — including materials — as critical, prioritising support for high-dependency, hard-to-substitute inputs [METI].
