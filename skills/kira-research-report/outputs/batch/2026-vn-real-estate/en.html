How we built this view

Twelve research queries across English and Vietnamese-language sources, three independent secondary anchors per major data point, and KIRA in-house estimates wherever park-level disclosure falls short. Every quantitative claim carries an explicit source tag; estimate methodologies are disclosed in the endnote.

Primary inputs

Developer disclosures
Annual reports and quarterly filings of Becamex IDC (BCM), Kinh Bac (KBC), IDICO (IDC), and Viglacera (VGC) — the four listed developers covering ~25% of the active IP land bank by area.
Analyst synthesis
Triangulation across twelve substantive queries (eight English, four Vietnamese) plus pattern-matching against our prior Vietnam manufacturing studies. Tagged [Kira estimates].
Channel reads
Conversations with FDI advisory boutiques, RBF/RBW brokers in Bac Ninh and Long An, and freight-forwarder distribution leads. Used directionally to triangulate rental trajectory and absorption velocity.

Secondary anchors

Statutory authorities
Ministry of Planning & Investment (FDI registration), General Statistics Office (GSO), Ministry of Construction industrial-park inventory, NAPAS / SBV macro data, World Bank Logistics Performance Index. Source of macro, FDI, occupancy and rental anchors.
Brokerage research
Cushman & Wakefield Vietnam, JLL Vietnam North/South market dynamics quarterlies, Savills industrial briefings, Vietdata industrial-park census. Cross-referenced with broker advertised quotes for board-level rental anchors.
Triangulated estimates
Sub-segment sizing where park-level public disclosure is thin (e.g. RBW absorption by sub-region, blended take-rate by tenant tier). Tagged [Kira estimates] with anchor metric disclosed inline. See endnote for full methodology.

FDI is the demand engine, not the variable

Disbursed FDI hit a five-year high at USD 27.6 bn in 2025 [MPI 2026], with manufacturing alone taking USD 22.9 bn — 82.8% of the total. Singapore, China, Hong Kong, Japan, Sweden, Taiwan and South Korea collectively accounted for ~85% of newly registered capital [MPI 2026]. This isn't a tourist FDI cycle — it's a multi-decade China+1 reallocation that is still mid-pipe.

Supply is structurally constrained

478 operational parks as of end-2025 [MoC 2025], national occupancy 80-85%, southern hotspots at 90%+. The 2030 plan adds 35,000 ha across 221 newly planned and 76 expanded parks [Govt 2024] — but licensing-to-handover sits at 3-5 years. Sub-segment squeeze is in ready-built factories (RBF national occupancy 88%) where 54% of new manufacturing FDI now lands [C&W 2025].

Section 04 · Macro Context
007 / 029

Growth, FDI, and the demand engine

GDP grew 8.02% in 2025 [GSO 2026] — the fastest pace since 2007. The World Bank forecasts 6.8% in 2026 [WB 2025]. Disbursed FDI USD 27.6 bn, the five-year high [MPI 2026]; manufacturing took 82.8%. This is the macro setup industrial-park developers compound through: deep tenant demand, ample foreign capital, and a government setting an ambitious 10% growth target for 2026 [MPI 2025].

FDI is the structural anchor

USD 38.42 bn registered + USD 27.6 bn disbursed in 2025 [MPI 2026]. Manufacturing & processing alone: USD 9.8 bn newly registered (56.5% share) and USD 22.9 bn disbursed (82.8% share). Combined newly-registered + adjusted in manufacturing reached USD 18.6 bn (59.2% of total). This is the cleanest macro signal for industrial-park demand.

Source-country diversification

Singapore led with USD 4.84 bn (27.9% of new capital) — mostly RHQ-routed Asian capital. China USD 3.64 bn (21.0%), Hong Kong USD 1.73 bn (10.0%), Japan USD 1.62 bn (9.4%), Sweden USD 1 bn (5.8%), Taiwan USD 966 M (5.6%), South Korea USD 896 M (5.2%) [MPI 2026]. No single source dominates — that's the China+1 thesis crystallizing.

Macro outlook holds

Government plans 10% GDP growth target for 2026 [MPI 2025]; World Bank base 6.8%, bull 7.1% in 2027 [WB 2025]. IMF more cautious at 6.5% citing tariff drag [IMF 2025]. The gap between government ambition and multilateral base case is informative — even the bear scenario keeps Vietnam in the SEA growth top-three.

Infrastructure delivery and the tariff overhang

Long Thanh airport commercial start: mid-2026 [VnPlus 2026]. Bien Hoa-Vung Tau expressway substantially complete by May 2026 [Govt 2026]. HCMC Ring Road 3 + Ben Luc-Long Thanh expressway anchor the southern logistics spine. Counter-current: the US 20% headline tariff + 40% transshipment penalty [USTR 2025] hits in the same window the infrastructure unlocks demand.

Long Thanh anchors the south

Phase 1 capacity: 25 million passengers + 1.2 million tonnes air cargo annually [VnPlus 2026]. Connecting expressways — HCMC-Long Thanh-Dau Giay (widening), Bien Hoa-Vung Tau (75% complete) [Govt 2026], Ben Luc-Long Thanh, HCMC Ring Road 3 — substantially complete end-2026. Result: an integrated air-cargo / seaport (Cai Mep-Thi Vai) / IP corridor that didn't exist before.

Logistics cost compression is a real lever

Vietnam logistics cost currently 16.8-17% of GDP [Govt 2025] vs world average 10.6%. National strategy targets 12-15% by 2035 [Govt 2025]. LPI rank 43rd globally [WB 2024]. Every 1pp compression releases ~USD 5 bn of corporate cost — most of it captured by IP-tenant manufacturers via lower outbound freight.

Tariff is the asymmetric drag

Trump-administered 20% Vietnam export tariff + 40% transshipment penalty for Chinese-content goods (effective July 2025) [USTR 2025]. PwC survey: 86% of businesses concerned, 44% began relocation or production dispersion [PwC 2025]. IMF revised 2026 growth to 5.2% in a tariff-stress scenario [IMF 2025]. The macro signal is real; the IP-tenant signal is more nuanced (electronics + EV component tenants are partly insulated by US-side capacity tightness).

Sub-segment trajectory — 2026

Four sub-segments matter for the strategic read. Raw land lease anchors revenue; RBF wins new FDI tenants; RBW rides e-commerce; data centres are the optionality bet. Concentration, growth, and margin profile diverge sharply across the four.

Raw land lease — USD 2.2 bn revenue pool

~52% of developer-side revenue pool [Kira estimates]. Land rent USD 132/m² north, USD 177/m² south for full lease term (~45-50 years remaining) [JLL/C&W 2025]. Northern rent +5.6% YoY, south +3.5%. Take-rate to developer ~70% gross of land acquisition cost. The base-economic engine.

RBF (ready-built factory) — USD 1.0 bn

~25% of pool. Supply 11.6 M m² nationwide (south 6.5, north 5.1) [JLL 2025]. National occupancy 88%; south 92%. Monthly rental USD 4-6/m² in north (Bac Ninh, Hai Phong) and USD 5-7 in south key parks. RBF captures 54% of new FDI manufacturing licenses [C&W 2025] — the fastest-growing sub-segment.

RBW (ready-built warehouse) — USD 0.6 bn

~13% of pool. Southern supply 2.4 M m² (+11.2% YoY); northern absorption hit 236,000 m² (+13% YoY) [JLL 2025]. Demand driven by USD 25 bn e-commerce GMV growing +20% annually [Govt 2024] — not FDI manufacturing. CAGR 11.2% to 2035 per market sizing [Expert Market 2025].

Data centres — USD 0.4 bn rising

Capacity 525 MW 2025 heading to 1,000 MW by 2030 [B-Company 2025]. AI data-centre commitments now exceed USD 7 bn aggregate [ERP.today 2025]. Power, cooling and 100kW+ rack density requirements mean the asset class straddles industrial real estate and specialist infrastructure — growth is high but operator base narrow.

Section 06 · Segment Economics
011 / 029

Segment economics — rental, occupancy, payback

Where the money actually accrues. Rental rate, occupancy velocity, and developer-side margin vary by an order of magnitude across the four sub-segments — and the gap is widening as the channel consolidates around ready-built product.

Land lease — capital-heavy, long-tail margin

Sticker rent USD 132-200/m² for ~45-50 year lease term [JLL/C&W 2025] — revenue is recognised over the lease but cash often collected upfront. Developer gross margin 40-55% after land acquisition + infrastructure capex. Payback 5-8 years on a typical 200-400 ha greenfield park, accelerating to 3-5 years in northern hotspots.

RBF — the fastest payback

Monthly rent USD 4-6/m² north, USD 5-7 south on 10-15 year tenancy [Cafeland/KTG 2025]. Construction USD 350-450/m². Stabilised yield 9-11%. Time-to-lease 6-9 months — the headline metric for the asset class. RBF is the highest IRR sub-segment when occupancy holds 85%+.

RBW — the e-commerce play

Monthly rent USD 3.5-5/m² typically [Cafeland 2025], 7-10 year tenancies (shorter than RBF). Construction USD 280-360/m². Stabilised yield 8-10%. Demand pulled by e-commerce + 3PL, not manufacturing — different tenant covenant (logistics operators vs FDI manufacturers).

012 / 029

Where margin actually compounds

A 200-ha greenfield park acquired today pays back in 5-8 years on land lease alone. Layer in ready-built factory development on 20-25% of the area, and developer-IRR rises 400-700 bps with limited incremental land cost. This is the structural reason listed developers are pivoting toward built-form product.

The mix math

Pure land-lease park (200 ha, north): blended developer IRR ~14-16% [Kira estimates]. Mixed park (160 ha lease + 40 ha RBF on retained land): ~18-22% IRR. The capital efficiency is in the RBF layer on land already acquired — not in chasing new greenfield in the same district. Becamex and Kinh Bac have both confirmed this trajectory in 2025 filings.

Rental escalation is real, not nominal

Northern industrial land rent +5.6% YoY 2025, south +3.5% [JLL 2025]. RBF monthly rent +6-8% in Bac Ninh and Hai Phong over the same period. Inflation runs ~4.5% — real rental escalation is 1-4pp. This is the rental-pricing-power signal: occupancy at 88-92% means landlords set the price, not tenants.

Tenant covenant is the credit lever

Tenant mix shifted toward listed multinationals: Samsung, LG, Foxconn cluster takes 1.2 M m² of factory space across HCMC + Hanoi [Vietnam.vn 2026]. Lease-loss provision falls below 0.5% on tier-1 FDI tenants. Sub-scale developers stuck with domestic SME tenants face 2-4% lease-loss + slower payment cycles — the structural reason for consolidation.

Strategic position

The southern hub anchor. Becamex's structural advantage is the township + industrial-park combination — My Phuoc, Bau Bang, plus the VSIP joint-venture series with Sembcorp. After-tax profit reached VND 3,516 bn (+46.8% YoY) on consolidated revenue VND 6,972 bn (+31.2%) [BCM AR 2025]. Q3 2025 alone: VND 422 bn PAT (+16.2%); 9M 2025 profit nearly tripled to VND 2.27 trillion [The Investor 2025].

Recent moves

  • 2024-2025: Expanded VSIP-series JVs into Binh Phuoc and Quang Tri to widen the southern footprint.
  • 2025: Township-residential cross-sell to industrial-park tenants — worker housing + service ecosystem.
  • 2025-2026: Eco-industrial-park transition pilots at Bau Bang to qualify for Decree 35/2022 incentives.

What we're watching

Three vectors. (1) Township unit-sale velocity — the recurring-vs-one-time revenue mix in 2026. (2) HCMC merger overhang — Binh Duong's absorption into greater HCMC reshuffles provincial-government relationships. (3) Tariff exposure — Becamex's tenant mix is heavier in electronics + furniture exporters, where the 20% US tariff bites first.

Strategic implication

For market entrants: direct competition in southern key economic zone is closed. Viable angles are (a) niche provincial markets where Becamex hasn't built yet, (b) RBW-focused plays where Becamex is under-indexed, or (c) eco-IP / data-centre formats where the asset playbook is different from Becamex's land-lease + township DNA.

020 / 029

Channel and lead origination

FDI tenants reach Vietnamese developers through four distinct channels — each with different conversion velocity and counter-party economics. Embassy + investment-promotion-agency channels still dominate large-tenant origination [Kira estimates]; broker channels handle most of the mid-market RBF/RBW flow.

Channel mix has hardened

Tier-1 multinationals (Samsung-scale): ~70% reach via direct embassy / provincial investment-promotion office [Kira estimates]. Korean Investment Promotion Agency (KOTRA), JETRO, Singapore EDB are the most active inbound conduits. Lead-to-lease cycle 12-18 months.

Mid-market goes broker

USD 5-30M FDI cohort routes ~60% via international brokerages (JLL, Cushman & Wakefield, Savills, Knight Frank) with the balance via local industrial brokers. Lead-to-lease 4-9 months on RBF; 2-5 months on RBW. Broker fees 2-4% of multi-year contract value — the developer-side cost has stabilised.

Domestic + Asian SME

The third channel: Asian-SME tenants (Taiwan, Korea, Japan) that operate through their own diaspora networks. ~15% of new tenant volume but disproportionately fills smaller RBF units (1,000-5,000 m²). Direct walk-up to provincial-IP authorities is still meaningful for this cohort.

Section 09 · Regulatory Landscape
021 / 029

Regulatory & policy landscape

Three policy vectors define the regulatory inflection. Decree 35/2022 (effective Jul-2022) restructured IP management and codified eco-industrial-park criteria. Decision 21/2025/QD-TTg defined the green-classification list. Personal Data Protection Law (effective Jan-2026) creates a hard data-localisation floor for data-centre demand. The Ministry of Planning & Investment + Ministry of Construction are the first-class actors.

Decree 35/2022 — IP management baseline

Replacing Decree 82/2018, effective Jul 2022 [Govt 2022]. Codified eco-industrial-park criteria with 25%+ green-space allocation, set requirements for cleaner production + industrial symbiosis. Cleaner-production firms qualify for the 10% preferential CIT rate for 15 years + 4-year tax holiday + 9-year 50% reduction [SCP 2026]. Provincial-level licensing delegation accelerated park approvals in 2024-2025.

Decision 21/2025 — green list

Defined the list of green-classified projects [QD 21/2025]. Critical for tenants accessing green-credit lines + tax incentives. Only 15-20 of 478 parks currently meet eco-IP criteria [SCP 2025] — below 5% of total. The runway for compliance + premium-rent uplift is multi-year.

Personal Data Protection Law — data-centre catalyst

Effective Jan 1, 2026 [Govt 2025]. Requires domestic storage of Vietnamese user data with penalties up to 5% of annual revenue for cross-border transfer violations. Direct implication: data-centre demand from Vietnamese-operating digital platforms hardens. Combined with the AI capacity wave (USD 7 bn+ commitments [ERP.today 2025]), data-centre is now the highest-CAGR sub-segment.

AI in Vietnam industrial RE — sizing & trajectory

Data-centre capacity rises from 525 MW in 2025 to ~1,000 MW by 2030 [B-Company 2025] — nearly doubling. Where the demand sits

Smart-factory adoption in IP tenants is still early: only 18% of Vietnamese enterprises use AI today vs 25% in Thailand and 40% in Singapore [RMIT 2025] — the runway for tenant-facing AI services is multi-year.

5-year outlook: base, bull, bear

Our base case sees the developer-side revenue pool reach USD 7.6 bn by 2031 at 13% CAGR. Bull case (tariff resolution + accelerated infrastructure delivery): USD 9.4 bn. Bear case (tariff bites harder + FDI dispersion accelerates): USD 5.9 bn. The range reflects tariff and channel uncertainty, not headline-FDI risk — the structural China+1 reallocation continues even in bear.

Base case — the central scenario

13% CAGR through 2031. Land lease grows 10%; RBF 15%; RBW 18%; data-centre 24% [Kira estimates]. Area expansion of +24% by 2030 [Govt 2024] holds the volume floor; rental escalation 5-7% blended holds the price floor. Long Thanh + Bien Hoa-Vung Tau open the southern logistics corridor for 2027-2031 absorption.

Bull case — what would it take

+ Tariff resolution within 2026-2027 (US 20% headline becomes negotiated lower band).
+ Long Thanh + connector expressways meet end-2026 deadline.
+ Data-centre commitments materialise on planned schedule (USD 7 bn+ deployed by 2028).
+ Eco-IP transition accelerates — premium-rent uplift on green-classified parks.
Result: 17% CAGR to USD 9.4 bn by 2031.

Bear case — the asymmetric risk

+ US tariff escalates or transshipment enforcement broadens.
+ PwC-surveyed 44% FDI relocation [PwC 2025] becomes 60%+.
+ Infrastructure timelines slip 12-18 months.
+ Rate-cycle bites consumer credit + slows e-commerce growth that pulls RBW.
Result: 7% CAGR to USD 5.9 bn by 2031.
