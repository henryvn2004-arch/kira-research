Indonesia's data center market — a 2027 landing test

A USD 2.8 bn market in 2025 sits beneath a multi-gigawatt announced pipeline, yet only about 420 MW is live nationally. Power supply from PLN, not demand, sets the pace at which Jakarta and Batam campuses actually energize.

The setup

Indonesia's data center market reached roughly USD 2.8 bn in 2025, on a path toward USD 6.1 bn by 2031 at a ~13.7% CAGR [DC Portfolio 2026]. Live IT capacity stood near 420 MW in mid-2025 [Kominfo 2025]. The ambition dwarfs that base: a single Batam campus is contracted for 450 MW [Bisnis 2026], and a Cikarang campus targets up to 500 MW scaling to 1 GW [DCD 2025].

The constraint

The bottleneck is physical. Operators have signed PLN supply deals totalling 1.2 GW — the largest such commitment on record [Bisnis 2026] — but that power lands in phases through 2026–2027. The RUPTL 2025–2034 plan funds 69.5 GW of new generation and 47,758 ckm of transmission [PLN RUPTL 2025], yet campus energization tracks grid-connection windows, not announcements.

Five strategic implications for market participants

Five forces govern who captures Indonesia's data center build-out through 2027. Each is anchored to documented data and KIRA synthesis.

01 · POWER
PLN supply is the binding constraint
RUPTL 2025–2034 funds 69.5 GW of new generation and USD 183 bn of investment [PLN RUPTL 2025]. But campuses energize on grid-connection windows; 1.2 GW of signed supply lands in phases. Power readiness, not demand, sets the 2027 ceiling.
Anchor: 1.2 GW contracted
02 · TWO CLUSTERS
Jakarta scale, Batam compliance edge
Jakarta-Cikarang holds ~57% of capacity and the interconnection [Kira estimates]; Batam absorbs Singapore overflow with sub-2 ms latency and SEZ ownership relief [DCD 2025]. The two clusters serve different buyers, not the same one.
Anchor: <2 ms Batam–SG
03 · FOREIGN CAPITAL
Hyperscalers anchor the pipeline
Microsoft opened its Indonesia Central cloud region in May 2025 on a USD 1.7 bn pledge; AWS commits ~USD 6 bn [Microsoft 2025]. Global cloud demand underwrites wholesale colocation, but lands through local power and land partners.
Anchor: USD 1.7 bn Microsoft
04 · REGULATION
Localization manufactures demand
GR 71/2019 forces public-sector and strategic data in-country; UU PDP No. 27/2022 adds penalties up to 2% of revenue, enforced since Oct 2024 [ITIF 2025]. Compliance demand is structural — a demand floor beneath colocation.
Anchor: 2% revenue penalty
05 · CLEAN POWER
Green supply is a landing prerequisite
RUPTL targets 76% renewables in new capacity [PLN RUPTL 2025]; operators pair deals with on-site solar, e.g. a 200 MWp PLTS for hyperscale campuses [Bisnis 2026]. Clean-energy access now gates global cloud tenants with carbon targets.
Anchor: 200 MWp PLTS planned

Section 04 · Market Sizing
007 / 017

Capacity roughly quadruples, revenue more than doubles by 2031

Two clocks run at different speeds. Installed capacity climbs from ~420 MW toward 1.5–1.8 GW; market value compounds as AI-grade racks and colocation premiums lift revenue per MW.

Capacity curve

Live IT capacity was about 420 MW in mid-2025, with the minister projecting growth toward ~900 MW on land-clearing data [Kominfo 2025]. Over five to seven years, total installed capacity is widely seen rising to 1.5–1.8 GW [DC Portfolio 2026], with one dataset modelling IT load from 1.44 GW in 2025 to 3.56 GW by 2030 on the most aggressive read.

Revenue curve

Market value stood near USD 2.8 bn in 2025, on track for USD 6.08 bn by 2031 (~13.7% CAGR) [DC Portfolio 2026]. Colocation compounds fastest — from ~USD 460 mn in 2024 toward USD 1.15 bn by 2030 [DC Portfolio 2026].

Footprint

The market spans 88 existing facilities, 25 upcoming, across 17 cities [DC Portfolio 2026]. Jakarta holds ~57% of capacity [Kira estimates], but Batam is the fastest-growing cluster on a ~21.7% CAGR to 2031 [DC Portfolio 2026].

Section 04 · Demand Drivers
008 / 017

Three demand engines: AI compute, cloud regions, and localization

Demand is the structurally easy side. An AI-investment wave, hyperscaler cloud-region landings, and a hard data-residency regime each pull capacity in — and they reinforce each other.

AI compute

The AI-optimised data center market reached USD 0.66 bn in 2025, headed for USD 1.44 bn by 2030 at a 16.9% CAGR [AI DC 2025]. Indosat markets NVIDIA H100 clusters as “GPU Merdeka”, BDx launched a sovereign AI facility on NVIDIA silicon in Dec 2024, and an AI Center of Excellence with NVIDIA and Cisco followed in 2025 [Microsoft 2025]. AI racks draw far more power per cabinet, amplifying the grid question on page 010.

Cloud regions

Microsoft opened its Indonesia Central cloud region in May 2025 with in-country data residency; AWS, Google Cloud and Microsoft now run multi-AZ regions guaranteeing sub-20 ms domestic latency [Microsoft 2025]. That underwrites a wholesale colocation pipeline exceeding 250 MW across Jakarta and Batam [DC Portfolio 2026].

Localization mandate

GR 71/2019 keeps public-sector and strategic data in-country, and UU PDP No. 27/2022 hardens the regime with revenue-based penalties [ITIF 2025]. This converts compliance into durable colocation demand independent of the AI cycle.

Section 05 · Power & the Grid
010 / 017

Power supply is the gate, transmission is the lock

RUPTL 2025–2034 commits to a large generation build, but the binding question for campuses is where and when firm load reaches the meter — and how much of it is clean.

The plan

The RUPTL 2025–2034 (issued May 2025) targets 69.5 GW of new generation by 2034, 76% renewable, with USD 183 bn of investment and 47,758 ckm of new transmission [PLN RUPTL 2025]. The first five years front-load 27.9 GW, including 12.2 GW of renewables [PLN RUPTL 2025].

The commitment

PLN has signed data-center supply deals totalling 1.2 GW — the largest such commitment on record [Bisnis 2026]. The headline PJBTL for DayOne in Batam alone reserves 511 MVA (~450 MW), phased across 2026–2027 [Bisnis 2026].

The clean-power fix

Sustainability is now a campus-design input: PLN–operator deals pair load with a 200 MWp solar (PLTS) build to meet investor carbon standards [Bisnis 2026]. Clean siting, not just raw megawatts, increasingly decides which hyperscale tenants commit.

Section 05 · The Operational Gap
011 / 017

The gap between announced and energized

The pipeline reads in gigawatts of intent; the meter reads in hundreds of megawatts of delivery. Closing that gap by 2027 is a power-and-construction problem, not a demand one.

What is live

National live IT capacity was about 420 MW in mid-2025 at 65–75% utilization [Kominfo 2025], spread across 88 facilities in 17 cities, with Jakarta dominant [DC Portfolio 2026]. The base is real but thin against the announced pipeline.

What is announced

The Batam pipeline alone is large: DayOne is contracted for 450 MW [Bisnis 2026], BW Digital's NDP1 is 120 MW (Q4 2025), and the Nongsa campus reaches ~540 MW contracted by Q4 2027 [DCD 2025]. In Cikarang-Bekasi, Digital Edge plans up to 500 MW (USD 4.5 bn), PDG's JC4 240 MW, and Edgnex 144 MW (USD 2.3 bn, online Dec 2026) [DCD 2025].

What lands by 2027

Most flagship campuses target 2026–2027 phased energization, tied to PLN connection windows. [Kira estimates] put realized 2027 live capacity near 1.0–1.1 GW — a large step up, but a fraction of the headline multi-gigawatt pipeline.

Section 05 · Connectivity & Regulation
012 / 017

Batam's cable edge; data rules harden the demand floor

Two enablers complete the constraint picture: cross-strait bandwidth that makes Batam a Singapore-overflow hub, and a data-residency regime that guarantees baseline local demand.

Connectivity

The 50 km Nongsa-Changi cable (Telin & BW Digital), ready Q4 2025, runs a minimum 24 fiber pairs to East Singapore with under 2 ms DC-to-DC latency [DCD 2025]. Batam has access to over 15 operational and planned subsea systems — the backbone of its overflow bid.

The Singapore overflow

Singapore's power-driven moratorium redirects capacity to Johor and south to Batam [DCD 2025]. Batam pairs sub-Singapore latency with SEZ relief — 100% foreign ownership and tax incentives in zones like Nongsa — positioning it as a compliance-and-latency hub distinct from Jakarta scale [ITIF 2025].

Regulation

GR 71/2019 mandates in-country storage for public-sector and strategic data; private operators may store offshore subject to access and supervision rules [ITIF 2025]. UU PDP No. 27/2022 adds penalties up to 2% of annual revenue, enforced since Oct 2024 — anchoring colocation demand.

Methodology & sources

How the numbers were derived, and the full citation for every named source used in this view.

Methodology

This view triangulates two evidence streams. Where a figure carries a named source tag (e.g. [PLN RUPTL 2025], [Bisnis 2026]), the underlying data comes from that source; the full citation appears at right. Where a figure carries [Kira estimates], KIRA analysts derived it from a blend of disclosed inputs — operator filings, PLN supply agreements, government plans — using the method disclosed in the relevant section.

Capacity figures reconcile published market datasets against operator and PLN disclosure; the live-versus-pipeline gap and the 2027 scenario fan are KIRA constructions. Approximately 25% of quantitative claims are KIRA-derived; the remainder are externally cited.
