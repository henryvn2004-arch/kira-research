A guaranteed market the public purse can no longer fully fund

Korea's eldercare demand is locked in by demographics — the country turned super-aged in 2024. The 2027 question is not size but who pays and who staffs it: the long-term care fund slides toward deficit and the sector is short some 100,000 workers by 2027.

Demand is locked in; funding and labour are the constraints

The 65-plus cohort reached 10.8 million in 2025 — 21.2% of the population [Statistics Korea 2025] — and rises toward 30.9% by 2036 [Statistics Korea 2025]. Long-term care benefit spend has nearly doubled in five years to ₩14.8 trillion [NHIS 2025], while the LTCI fund is projected to run a ₩3.8 trillion deficit by 2030 [NABO 2025]. Demand is guaranteed; the binding scarcities are the fund and the workforce.

The 2027 reform tilts toward cost-sharing and productivity

With reserves thinning, the policy lever shifts from expansion to premium increases, integrated home-and-medical care, and care-tech subsidy [MOHW 2025]. Operators cannot price their way out of a nationally set fee schedule; they must lift output per caregiver. Care robotics and digital monitoring are the scaled responses — and a private silver-economy layer is forming beside the public system.

Section 05 · Strategic Implications
005 / 014

Five moves as a funding wall meets a labour cliff

The 2027 reform rewards output per caregiver and private revenue lines the public fund cannot cap. Winners convert subsidy and demographic certainty into recurring, non-LTCI revenue and build the workforce pipeline early.

01 · PRODUCTIVITY
Bank care-tech subsidy as staff-hour savings
Municipal pilots fund radar sensors, repositioning beds and exoskeletons in nursing homes [Seoul Welfare 2026]. Operators that redesign night and transfer routines around them free real care hours; those that bolt them on free almost none.
Anchor: 6 Seoul facilities in 2026 digital pilot
02 · PRIVATE LAYER
Build the silver economy beside the public fund
The senior market reaches ₩168 tn by 2030 [KHIDI 2024] while LTCI fees stay nationally capped. Senior housing, monitoring and private top-up services earn revenue the reform cannot throttle.
Anchor: ₩168 tn vs ₩72.8 tn (2020)
03 · WORKFORCE
Solve the pipeline before the 2027 shortfall bites
The sector is short ~100k workers by 2027 [MOHW 2025] and ~66% of caregivers are already 60+ [MOHW 2025]. Foreign-caregiver tracks and degree programmes are opening; early movers lock in supply.
Anchor: 24 university caregiver tracks (2026)
04 · HOUSING
Capture the middle tier of senior housing
Senior housing sits at under 1% penetration [Colliers 2024], split between luxury silver towns and public rentals. The "middle-class gap" between them is the largest unserved opening for operators and financiers.
Anchor: <1% senior-housing penetration
05 · POLICY-WINDOW
Position for premium and integration reform
The LTCI premium rate rose to 13.14% of health contributions for 2026 [KTE 2025], with integrated home-and-medical care expanding to 250 centres by 2027 [MOHW 2025]. Align service design to the integration model now.
Anchor: 250 integrated-care centres by 2027

Section 06 · Demand Drivers
007 / 014

Super-aged by 2024, 30% old by 2036

Korea crossed the 20% threshold in 2024 — faster than any peer. What changes through 2027 is velocity: the share of over-65s climbs steeply, and a private silver economy scales beside the public care system.

The fastest ageing on record

The population aged 65+ reached 10.8 million in 2025, 21.2% of the total [Statistics Korea 2025]. The share is projected to pass 30.9% by 2036 and 40% by 2050 [Statistics Korea 2025] — a transition that took other advanced economies far longer.

A silver economy doubling to 2030

The market for elderly-oriented goods and services is forecast to reach ₩168 trillion (≈ USD 128 bn) by 2030, up from ₩72.8 trillion in 2020 [KHIDI 2024]. Senior healthcare alone is projected to climb from USD 55 bn to USD 128 bn over the decade [KHIDI 2024].

Uneven regional strain

The over-65 share is 18.8% in the capital region but 23.7% outside it [Statistics Korea 2025]. Rural provinces age first and hardest — where care infrastructure and staffing are already thinnest [Kira estimates].

Section 06 · The Public Envelope
008 / 014

A fund the 2027 reform is built to keep solvent

Long-term care spend has nearly doubled in five years while the LTCI fund slides toward deficit. The 2027 reform shifts the lever from expansion to premium increases, integration and productivity.

The spend escalator

LTC benefit outlays rose from ₩7.7 trillion in 2019 to ₩13.2 trillion in 2023 and ₩14.8 trillion in 2024 — about USD 10.3 bn [NHIS 2025]. Expenditure has outpaced recipient growth, the structural signature of an ageing risk pool.

The deficit ahead

Reserves are projected to thin sharply, with the fund running a ₩3.8 trillion deficit by 2030, widening to ₩23.2 trillion by 2040 [NABO 2025]. The LTCI premium — still only ~0.7% of income versus Japan's 1.5% [PMC 2024] — rose to 13.14% of health contributions for 2026 [KTE 2025] and has clear room to climb.

What 2027 changes

The reform path expands integrated home-and-medical care to ~250 centres by 2027, strengthens coverage and care-worker conditions, and leans on care-tech subsidy [MOHW 2025] — containing cost growth without cutting eligibility outright.

Section 09 · Capacity & Workforce
010 / 014

The labour cliff: an ageing workforce short ~100k by 2027

Care capacity is large on paper but fragile in practice — the workforce is itself ageing fast, and senior housing is barely built out. Both gaps widen the case for automation, foreign labour and private build-out.

The workforce is ageing into the gap

Some 657,000 people worked in care in 2024, but about 66% were 60 or older [MOHW 2025], and foreign caregivers numbered just 6,644. The government projects a shortfall near 100,000 by 2027 and ~116,000 by 2028 [MOHW 2025], rising to 380,000–710,000 by 2032.

Capacity is broad but thin

The system runs ~22,100 home-care providers and ~6,300 institutional providers [NHIS 2024], with over 1 million LTCI beneficiaries [NHIS 2024]. Coverage breadth is real, but bed and staffing density lag the speed of demand.

Senior housing is the underbuilt frontier

Dedicated senior housing sits at under 1% penetration [Colliers 2024], with 77% of over-65s in older general housing. The unmet "middle tier" between luxury silver towns and public rentals is the largest greenfield opening [Sedaily 2026].

Section 12 · Digital Health & Robotics
012 / 014

Digital care: the payoff is staff-hours returned

With the workforce shrinking, the credible technology case is operational — fewer night checks, fewer transfers by hand, earlier risk flags. Korea is moving from companion robots to subsidised, sensor-led nursing-home deployments.

Monitoring & safety first

Seoul's 2026 digital-transformation pilot puts radar fall-sensors, repositioning beds, lifts and exoskeletons into six nursing homes [Seoul Welfare 2026], targeting transfer and fall-risk tasks that are hardest on staff.

Companion to clinical

Over 12,000 Hyodol companion robots are deployed to seniors living alone via public programmes [RestofWorld 2025]; the next phase is purpose-built physical AI for care settings from 2028 [Sedaily 2026].

A device pipeline forming

MFDS senior-care device approvals rose from 108 in 2024 to 157 in 2025 [KHIDI 2024] — a 2.5x lift in two years that signals a maturing supply base.
