How we built this view
This view is built from two evidence streams: primary inputs — analyst synthesis and channel checks across franchise, delivery, and discount-retail formats — and secondary anchors — fair-trade and statistics-agency disclosures, listed and registered franchise filings, and triangulated estimates. The squeeze is structural, not cyclical

The market is the world's third largest [FTC 2025], yet franchisee profit is thinning. Per-3.3㎡ sales at the value leader fell 15.9% over four years [Asiae 2025], while the delivery duopoly — over 90% combined share [PSPD 2025] — takes 23–26% of each order. Headquarters earn; the storefront absorbs the platform, the avian-influenza input shock, and a 50% oil-price rise [Sedaily 2026].

Growth migrates to premium and overseas

With delivered prices brushing KRW 30,000 [Korea Times 2026], the home market has hit a pricing ceiling and a saturation wall. The leaders are responding by pushing overseas — BBQ now runs ~700 stores in 57 countries [Korea Herald 2025] — and by opening premium flagship and value-concept formats to defend a polarising shelf.

Market sizing
07 / 14
A saturated pool where revenue grows on price, not stores.
Franchise chicken revenue reached KRW 8.78 trn in 2024, up 7.3% [FTC 2025], the world's third-largest market behind the US and China. But the gain is nominal — driven by price increases, not new demand — across a base of 31,397 stores and 647 brands [Statistics Korea 2025] where franchises account for roughly 70% of all chicken eateries [Korea Herald 2024].

The store base has hit saturation

Store count crossed 31,000 in 2024, up 5.3% [Statistics Korea 2025], but density is now extreme: 647 registered brands fight for the same dense urban catchments [FTC 2025]. New-store growth no longer creates net demand — it cannibalises, and per-store sales at value brands fell 15.9% over four years [Asiae 2025].

Premium is the upper tier

Listed franchisors have pushed signature combos toward the KRW 30,000 delivered threshold [Korea Times 2026], driven by oil and poultry input shocks. Premium is now a deliberate tier: better-for-you oven-roast lines and flagship formats target consumers who will pay for occasion rather than trade down.

Discount retail reframes value

The floor moved: Emart sold fried chicken at KRW 3,480 and Lotte Mart a bucket at KRW 5,000, shifting ~100,000 units in one event [Korea Times 2025]. Hypermarket counters now anchor consumer price expectations and pull the value-seeking segment out of franchise delivery entirely.

Demand & pricing
08 / 14
The 30,000-won wall meets an input shock and a value trade-down.
Demand is mature and price-sensitive. Signature combos climbed toward KRW 30,000 delivered [Korea Times 2026] as a 2025–2026 avian-influenza cull exceeded 400,000 birds and soybean oil rose nearly 50% [Sedaily 2026]. The result is a consumer revolt: shoppers anchor to discount-retail counters, and the mid-tier franchise loses the value-seeking household.

Input shocks force the price up

Kyochon raised its signature Honey Combo to KRW 23,000 from 20,000 [Korea Times 2026], pushing delivered totals near KRW 30,000. The driver is structural cost: highly pathogenic avian influenza culled over 400,000 birds per category and oil prices jumped ~50% [Sedaily 2026], compressing a category that runs on a thin franchisee margin.

The consumer hits a ceiling

The KRW 30,000 chicken has become a national affordability flashpoint, with franchises under public and political pressure to freeze prices [Korea Times 2026]. Demand is increasingly bifurcated: occasion-led premium buyers absorb the hike, while price-led households trade down to retail and ultra-value concepts.

Value trade-down is structural

Discount retailers met the moment — Emart at KRW 3,480, Lotte Mart selling ~100,000 buckets at KRW 5,000 in a single event [Korea Times 2025]. Franchises responded with value formats such as a KRW 9,900 roasted line [Korea Times 2025], conceding that the mid-tier price point no longer clears in a cost-conscious market.

Delivery dependency
10 / 14
A two-app duopoly takes the marginal won.
Delivery is the decisive channel for chicken, and it is a duopoly. Baemin and Coupang Eats hold over 90% combined share [PSPD 2025], inside a food-delivery market worth roughly KRW 27 trn in 2024 [Statistics Korea 2025]. Combined commissions and fees reach 23–26% of order value and exceed 30% on low-ticket items [PSPD 2025] — the largest single drag on franchisee economics.

The duopoly is entrenched

Baemin leads with about 60% share and 21.69 m monthly active users; Coupang Eats follows at 10.89 m [PSPD 2025]. Together they exceed 90% of the market [PSPD 2025], leaving franchisees no realistic exit channel. Delivery is the dominant chicken order mode, so platform terms set the storefront's true margin.

The take is 23–26% and rising on small tickets

A 2025 civic-group analysis put Baemin's combined commission and fees at 23–26% per order, exceeding 30% on cheaper items [PSPD 2025]. On a KRW 20,000 order, the platform layer can absorb most of a franchisee's gross margin before rent, oil, and labour — explaining why headquarters earn while storefronts thin.

Fee reform did not fix the math

Under political pressure, Baemin cut the headline rate from 9.8% to a 2–7.8% sliding scale by sales volume [Korea Herald 2025], but per-order delivery fees rose KRW 200–500 at the same time [Korea Herald 2025]. Net savings for many small operators are marginal; the structural dependency is unchanged.

AI and operations
12 / 14
Automation lands where labour, oil, and delivery volatility collide.
A thin-margin category facing high rents, a shrinking labour pool, and a ~50% oil-price rise [Sedaily 2026] is turning to automation. Frying robots already prepare 100 chickens in two hours versus five staff [Techxplore 2025]; the near-term AI payback is operational — kitchen automation, demand sensing for lumpy delivery orders, and waste control — not consumer novelty.

Frying robots attack the labour line

A robotic arm can fry 100 birds in two hours — work that needs multiple fryers and at least five people [Techxplore 2025]. Robot-kitchen operators run dozens of locations, and majors including a global QSR chain have piloted Hyundai-built frying robots in Korea [Thespoon 2025], targeting the highest-cost, highest-turnover task.

Demand sensing tames delivery volatility

Order flow is lumpy and platform-driven. AI demand forecasting at store-hour granularity is the highest-return software use case, cutting prep waste and stock-outs in a category where oil and poultry inputs are both volatile and expensive [Kira estimates].

5-year outlook
13 / 14
A domestic plateau; the growth runs overseas.
Our base case sees domestic franchise revenue grind from KRW 8.78 trn (2024) [FTC 2025] toward roughly KRW 10.4 trn by 2030 [Kira estimates] — low-single-digit, price-led growth as the store base saturates. The real variance sits in three levers: delivery-commission regulation, the input-cost cycle, and how fast overseas franchise compounds off a low base.

Methodology endnote and source mix

How to read the numbers

This view triangulates two evidence streams. Where a figure carries a named source tag — for example [FTC 2025], [PSPD 2025], [Korea Times 2026] — the underlying data comes from that source, with the full citation listed opposite. Where a figure carries [Kira estimates], our analysts derived it from a blend of disclosed inputs — franchise-disclosure filings, platform commission data, store-count census, and channel checks — using the method noted in the relevant section.

Approximately 28% of quantitative claims are KIRA-derived; 72% are externally cited. Franchise revenue and operating-profit figures are reported on a headquarters (franchisor) basis per Fair Trade Commission disclosure; store counts are on a registered-franchise basis. Delivery-commission, price-tier, and use-case adoption reads are KIRA triangulations and are directional, not audited.

Currency: figures are KRW for the domestic market and company results unless marked USD for global context. Forecast scenarios are KIRA constructions anchored to the disclosed revenue trajectory, store-saturation reads, and overseas-franchise plans.
