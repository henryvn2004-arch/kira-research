The target is met; the next leg is harder

Malaysia reached 31% installed RE capacity by December 2025 [PETRA 2026], clearing the NETR's 2025 marker, on a base that rose from 9.5 GW in 2024 [NETR 2023]. The roadmap now asks for 40% (~18 GW) by 2035 and 70% by 2050 [NETR 2023] — a ~1 percentage-point-a-year climb that the LSS PETRA pipeline of 6.0 GW approved solar [EC 2025] only partly funds.

Corporate demand is the swing variable

The Corporate Renewable Energy Supply Scheme lets large users contract developers directly for a grid access charge [PETRA 2024]. With data-centre power demand projected to rise from 9 TWh to 68 TWh by 2030 [Ember 2025], CRESS converts hyperscaler appetite into bankable offtake — the binding input on whether new solar gets financed.

Five strategic implications for market participants

Where capital, project effort, and policy engagement should concentrate over the next 18-24 months — ranked by the asymmetry of return given the corporate-tariff opening and the grid-integration build.

01
Sell offtake before you bid the next LSS round
CRESS lets corporates contract developers directly for a grid access charge [PETRA 2024]. With Bridge Data Centres alone contracting 400 MW of green supply [w.media 2025], a signed corporate PPA de-risks a project more than a marginal LSS tariff win. Lead with offtake, not auction volume.
ANCHOR · 400 MW single CRESS contract signed
02
Underwrite firming, not just nameplate solar
The NETR calls for 500 MW of battery storage by 2030 [NETR 2023] and MyBeST is procuring 400 MW/1,600 MWh for 2027 COD [PETRA 2025]. As solar share rises, intermittency makes BESS and grid-forming capability the scarce asset. Bundle storage into the bid or lose dispatchability value.
ANCHOR · 400 MW MyBeST storage for 2027
03
Treat the grid as the real bottleneck
TNB's RP4 framework permits RM42.8 bn of capex over 2025-2027 [Kenanga 2024], with a contingent tranche tied to data-centre-driven grid expansion. Projects in grid-constrained corridors stall regardless of tariff. Site to where evacuation capacity and substation headroom actually exist.
ANCHOR · RM42.8 bn TNB RP4 capex 2025-27
04
Position for the regional grid, not just the domestic one
LTMS-PIP now trades multidirectionally, doubling capacity to 200 MW and adding Malaysia-to-Singapore flow [FMT 2025]. Singapore targets 6 GW of low-carbon imports by 2035 [EMA 2025]. Malaysia is positioned as both corridor and exporter — an offtake market beyond the domestic grid.
ANCHOR · 6 GW Singapore import target 2035
05
Pace entry to the 18 GW gap, expect compression
The 40%-by-2035 target implies ~18 GW [NETR 2023] from ~13 GW in 2025. LSS tariffs have fallen to 13-16 sen/kWh [theEdge 2025], squeezing EPCC margins. Our base case clears ~16-17 GW by 2035 [Kira estimates] — real delivery, thinner returns. Compete on cost of capital.

Section 04 · Macro context
007 / 029

Demand growth is the structural tailwind

Malaysia grew 5.4% YoY in Q1 2026 [DOSM 2026], with construction up 7.7% and manufacturing 5.9%. Bank Negara guides 4-5% for the year [BNM 2026], while a data-centre wave lifts electricity demand far above the historical trend — the demand case for new clean capacity is not in question.

Growth holding above 5%

Q1 2026 GDP rose 5.4% YoY [DOSM 2026], beating the 5.3% advance estimate, led by services (+5.4%), construction (+7.7%) and manufacturing (+5.9%). Bank Negara projects full-year 2026 growth of 4-5% [BNM 2026]; the IMF pencils 4.7% for 2026 and 4.3% for 2027 [IMF 2026].

Electricity demand re-rates upward

The marginal driver is no longer just households and industry. Data-centre electricity demand alone is projected to climb from 9 TWh in 2024 to 68 TWh by 2030 [Ember 2025] — a step-change that adds firm baseload pull and pushes peak grid stress, where solar plus storage must respond.

The transition is capital-hungry

Meeting the NETR path needs more than USD 26 bn of investment to 2030 for solar, EV charging and renewable direct-use alone [IRENA 2025]; long-run green-growth targets lift the sector's GDP contribution toward RM220 bn by 2050 [NETR 2023]. The capital gap, not demand, is the binding macro constraint.

Section 04 · Macro context (cont.)
008 / 029

Coal still anchors the grid — that is the transition task

Despite a 31% installed RE share, coal and gas still supply the bulk of generation, with coal slated for phase-out only by 2044 [PETRA 2026]. The NETR reframes the task: not whether to add renewables, but whether the grid and firming can absorb them fast enough as data-centre load compounds.

Installed share leads generation share

The 31% figure is installed capacity [PETRA 2026]; renewable generation share runs lower because solar's capacity factor sits near 15-18% [Kira estimates]. Coal remains the generation workhorse, with thermal still dominant on energy delivered — the gap between nameplate and dispatch is the real story.

The phase-out path is long

Coal is scheduled to phase out by 2044 [PETRA 2026], and the NETR front-loads enabling infrastructure — grid, storage, interconnection — before the bulk of fossil retirement. RP4 sequences TNB grid capex into 2025-2027 [Kenanga 2024], deliberately ahead of the steep renewable ramp.

NETR frames the finance

The NETR's flagship projects target RM25 bn of investment and 23,000 jobs in the near term [NETR 2023], scaling the sector's GDP contribution toward RM220 bn by 2050. Disbursement pace and bankable-project supply — not the headline pledge — will decide the build rate.

Section 05 · Sector overview & sizing
010 / 029

The solar build is sized by the auction calendar

Cumulative solar reached ~2.3 GW by end-2024 [pv-magazine 2025], and LSS PETRA has approved 6.0 GW across six rounds to 117 companies [EC 2025]. The forward build runs through the auction pipeline plus a fast-growing corporate-PPA stream — the two together set the 2027-2032 trajectory.

Auctions set the floor

LSS5 (LSS PETRA) offered 2 GW and awarded in December 2024; LSS5+ approved 1,975 MW from 4,073 MW of bids [pv-magazine 2025], with projects coming online from 2027. Cumulative LSS approvals reached 6,028 MW across six rounds [EC 2025].

Corporate demand adds a second stream

On top of auctions, CGPP allocated a virtual-PPA quota and CRESS opened direct corporate supply [PETRA 2024]. CRESS alone is projected to attract over RM10 bn of investment and lift RE capacity toward 40% by 2035 [PETRA 2024]. The corporate channel is structurally additive to LSS.

Capacity to 18 GW by 2035

From ~13 GW of RE capacity in 2025 [PETRA 2026], the NETR targets ~18 GW by 2035 and 55 GW by 2050 [NETR 2023]. Solar carries most of the near-term build; hydro and storage anchor the firming layer.

Section 05 · Sector overview & sizing (cont.)
011 / 029

Falling tariffs compress the EPCC value pool

LSS5 winning bids reached 13-16 sen/kWh, the lowest since the programme began in 2016 [theEdge 2025]. Cheaper panels enlarge installed volume but thin per-MW margins — the value pool is shifting from EPCC build to O&M, asset ownership, storage and the corporate-PPA premium.

Tariffs at record lows

LSS5 cleared at 13-16 sen/kWh [theEdge 2025], driven by the cheapest panel prices on record. LSS5+ bids ran broadly in line at 14-16 sen [pv-magazine 2025]. Lower tariffs expand the addressable build but squeeze contractor returns — scale and cost of capital now decide who clears.

The contract pipeline is large

LSS5+ alone is estimated to unlock ~RM18 bn in EPCC and equipment contracts [SolarQuarter 2025]. The order book is real, but it accrues disproportionately to integrated players that can finance assets and capture recurring O&M, not pure EPCC bidders.

Value migrates downstream

As build margins compress, the durable returns sit in asset ownership, firming (BESS) and the corporate-PPA premium that CRESS unlocks. Developers with balance sheets to hold assets and offtake to underwrite them out-earn those selling EPCC at auction-clearing prices [Kira estimates].

Section 06 · Segment economics
012 / 029

Four segments, four different margin logics

Solar dominates new capacity but earns auction-thin tariffs; hydro is large but slow-moving; BESS is scarce and rising in value; biomass and biogas stay niche. The firming layer — storage — is where scarcity value concentrates as solar penetration climbs.

Solar — volume, thin margin

Utility-scale solar carries the build but clears at 13-16 sen/kWh [theEdge 2025] with ~15-18% capacity factors [Kira estimates]. Returns depend on cost of capital and O&M capture, not tariff. C&I rooftop under CRESS earns a better unit economics through the corporate premium.

Hydro — large, firm, slow

Hydro supplies roughly 10% of generation [Ember 2025], concentrated in Sarawak's large dams. It is firm and low-marginal-cost but capacity additions are lumpy and politically gated. Sarawak's surplus is increasingly framed as an export and grid-integration asset.

Storage — the scarce firming asset

The NETR targets 500 MW of BESS by 2030 [NETR 2023]; MyBeST procures 400 MW/1,600 MWh for 2027 [PETRA 2025], and TNB's 100 MW/400 MWh Santong project is the first grid-connected BESS [Energy-Storage.News 2026]. As solar share rises, firming value re-rates upward.

013 / 029

Storage economics decide how much solar the grid can take

Solar's value is capped by what the grid can absorb at midday. Without firming, additional nameplate gets curtailed. BESS converts curtailed solar into dispatchable evening supply — the economics of storage, not panels, now set the ceiling on solar's growth.

The duck-curve constraint is here

As solar approaches double-digit generation share, midday oversupply and evening ramp force either curtailment or firming. Ember frames solar-plus-flexibility as critical to affordability and security [Ember 2025] — the system needs storage to keep adding solar economically.

BESS is moving from pilot to programme

MyBeST's 400 MW/1,600 MWh for 2027 [PETRA 2025] and the Santong 100 MW/400 MWh grid-forming asset [Energy-Storage.News 2026] mark the shift from demonstration to procurement. RP4 carries a contingent capex tranche specifically for BESS and grid expansion [Kenanga 2024].

Where the returns sit

Firming earns through capacity payments, arbitrage and ancillary services rather than energy tariff. As renewable penetration rises, these revenue stacks deepen — making integrated solar-plus-storage developers the structural winners over pure-play EPCC [Kira estimates].

TNB is the system's structural anchor: it owns the transmission and distribution grid, runs LSS procurement, and increasingly holds renewable assets directly. Under RP4's incentive-based regulation it is permitted RM42.8 bn of capex over 2025-2027 at a regulated return near 7.3% [Kenanga 2024].

The corporate green tariff turns AI load into offtake

Data-centre power demand is set to rise from 9 TWh to 68 TWh by 2030 [Ember 2025], concentrated in Johor. CRESS and CGPP convert that hyperscaler appetite into direct corporate PPAs — the bankability anchor that decides which renewable projects reach financial close.

Johor is the demand epicentre

Johor's data-centre supply is heading toward 3.6 GW by 2030 on some reads [ISEAS 2025], with Sedenak, Kulai and Nusajaya already near 1.6 GW. Johor now requires green energy for new data centres — turning a siting rule into structural renewable demand.

CRESS is the bankability bridge

CRESS lets corporates contract developers directly for a grid access charge [PETRA 2024]. Bridge Data Centres signed a 400 MW green-supply contract via CRESS [w.media 2025], and TotalEnergies-Google inked a 21-year PPA for Citra Energies solar to power Google data centres [TotalEnergies 2025].

Why this changes the build

A signed corporate PPA underwrites project finance more durably than a marginal LSS tariff win. As the KWTBB levy is waived for GET, CRESS and CREAM [PETRA 2024], the corporate channel's economics improve — pulling additional, additive capacity into the pipeline.

The ASEAN grid makes Malaysia a corridor and an exporter

LTMS-PIP, ASEAN's first multilateral power trade, is now fully multidirectional — doubling capacity to 200 MW and adding Malaysia-to-Singapore flow [FMT 2025]. With Singapore targeting 6 GW of low-carbon imports by 2035 [EMA 2025], the regional grid is a second offtake market beyond the domestic system.

From transit to two-way trade

Launched in 2022, LTMS-PIP first wheeled Lao hydropower to Singapore via Thailand and Malaysia. In 2025 it became multidirectional, doubling tradable capacity to 200 MW and enabling direct Malaysia-to-Singapore export [FMT 2025] — the cornerstone proof-of-concept for the wider ASEAN Power Grid.

Singapore is the anchor buyer

Singapore raised its low-carbon import target to ~6 GW by 2035 [EMA 2025], and SGEI was established in 2025 to develop cross-border interconnections. Malaysian and Sarawak renewables — plus the Johor-Singapore corridor — are natural supply into that demand.

Infrastructure is the gate

A 300 kV HVDC Thailand-Malaysia link and Malaysia's onward grid carry the flows [FMT 2025]. Scaling beyond pilot volumes needs new interconnection capacity — making transmission, not generation, the binding constraint on regional trade.

Regulatory & procurement landscape

The policy stack is unusually coherent: NETR sets the destination, LSS PETRA runs the auctions, CRESS and CGPP open corporate offtake, MyBeST procures storage, and RP4 funds the grid. The friction points are TKDN local content and grid-evacuation capacity, not policy ambition.

CORPORATE OFFTAKE
CRESS & CGPP
CRESS (effective Sep-2024, opened to existing users Mar-2025) lets corporates buy renewable power directly for a grid access charge; CGPP is the earlier virtual-PPA route. CRESS targets RM10 bn+ investment and ~14,000 jobs, lifting RE toward 40% by 2035. KWTBB levy waived for GET/CRESS/CREAM.
SRC: PETRA 2024, ENGIE 2025
AUCTION PROGRAMME
LSS PETRA (LSS5 / LSS5+)
The Energy Commission's flagship competitive solar auction has cleared 6.0 GW across six rounds to 117 firms; LSS5+ approved 1,975 MW from 4,073 MW of bids, online from 2027. Winning tariffs fell to 13-16 sen/kWh — record lows that reward scale and cheap capital.
SRC: EC 2025, pv-magazine 2025
STORAGE & GRID
MyBeST & RP4
MyBeST (Nov-2024) procures 400 MW/1,600 MWh of BESS for 2027 COD; four winners shortlisted from 28 applicants. TNB's RP4 IBR framework permits RM42.8 bn capex over 2025-2027, with a contingent tranche for data-centre-driven grid and storage expansion.
SRC: PETRA 2025, Kenanga 2024
FRICTION POINTS
TKDN & evacuation capacity
Local-content (TKDN-style) requirements and module-origin rules can slow procurement, while grid-evacuation headroom is the hard physical gate in fast-growing corridors. Projects clearing tariff still stall without substation access — the binding constraint shifts from price to infrastructure.

AI in Malaysian renewables: forecasting is the near-term prize

As solar share climbs, forecast error becomes a direct cost — in curtailment, balancing, and trading. Machine-learning forecasting has cut solar error by 15-20% against conventional models [arXiv 2025], and that accuracy gain translates straight into grid value and storage-dispatch efficiency.

Forecasting cuts the cost of intermittency

ML solar forecasting reduces MAPE by 15-20% versus statistical baselines [arXiv 2025], with advanced methods claiming up to 50% error reduction in major markets [Meteomatics 2025]. For a grid adding solar fast, every point of forecast accuracy lowers balancing cost and curtailment.

Grid & storage optimisation

AI-driven load and generation forecasting supports real-time dispatch and BESS arbitrage [REW 2025]. As MyBeST storage comes online in 2027, model-driven dispatch is what converts a battery's nameplate into realised revenue stack.

The double-edged demand

The same AI workloads driving data-centre load to 68 TWh by 2030 [Ember 2025] also supply the tools to manage the resulting grid. The net effect on the system depends on whether efficiency gains outrun consumption growth — an open question.
