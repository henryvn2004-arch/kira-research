How we built this view

This view is built from two evidence streams: primary inputs — school-fee channel checks, operator and admissions reads, and analyst synthesis — and secondary anchors — statistical authorities, ministry data, and international-school survey datasets. Every quantitative claim carries an explicit source tag.

Primary inputs

→ Fee & admissions channel checks
Published tuition schedules, reservation-fee structures, and admissions-cycle reads across HCMC and Hanoi bilingual and international schools.
→ Analyst synthesis
KIRA in-house triangulation across disclosed enrolment, capacity, and FDI inputs. Tagged inline as [Kira estimates].
→ Operator & institution reads
Conversations with school-group operators, transnational-program coordinators, and edtech founders closer to the demand signal than headline data.

Secondary anchors

→ Statistical & ministry authorities
General Statistics Office, Ministry of Education and Training, and Foreign Investment Agency data — cited inline by named source.
→ International-school datasets
Sector census of school counts, enrolment, and staffing for the international and bilingual segment — cited by dataset and year.
→ Triangulated estimates
Where no single source covers a figure, we combine two to three inputs and disclose the method. Tagged [Kira estimates].

Demand is structural, not cyclical

The middle class is set to roughly double from 13% of the population in 2023 toward 26% by 2026 [World Data Lab 2025], adding an estimated 23-36 million people by 2030 [World Data Lab 2025]. Private schools still hold under 10% of total enrolment [MOET 2024], leaving a multi-year pull that an income slowdown would compress, not erase.

Bilingual is the wedge

Bilingual schools — local roots plus an international track — price at roughly 60% of premium international fees [Kira estimates] and are absorbing the bulk of new private demand. International and bilingual enrolment rose 35% to 153,236 in five years [ISC Research 2025], with Vietnam among the top five fastest-growing markets globally [ISC Research 2024].

Section 05 · Demand Drivers
007 / 017

What pulls Vietnamese families into private education

Three reinforcing forces — income formation, urban concentration, and an English-proficiency premium — are widening the addressable base for bilingual and international schooling well beyond the expatriate core.

Income formation

Average monthly income reached VND 5.4m in 2024, up 9.1% year on year, and VND 6.9m in urban areas [GSO 2024]. The middle class is set to move from 13% of the population toward 26% by 2026 [World Data Lab 2025], with GDP per capita targeted near USD 7,500 by 2030 [GSO 2024].

Urban concentration

The urban share rises from 37% in 2020 toward 44% by 2030 [GSO 2024], concentrating demand in Hanoi and Ho Chi Minh City, where private supply is densest. Roughly 10 million people join the urban base over the decade [GSO 2024].

The English premium

English proficiency is the recurring purchase driver. Private tutoring already reaches 42% of children aged 6-18 and 77% of upper-secondary students [World Bank 2024]; bilingual schooling captures that spend inside a single, structured pathway.

Section 06 · Market Sizing
008 / 017

Sizing the private-education value pools

Vietnam's private-education spend splits across K-12 fees, transnational tertiary, and supplementary learning. Bilingual K-12 is the largest and fastest-compounding pool; supplementary and edtech ride alongside it.

Where the money sits

Private K-12 fees anchor the sector: a mid-tier bilingual seat runs VND 350-550m per year (~USD 14-22k), with premium international reaching VND 1.2bn-plus [Intl Schools DB 2025]. Across 153,236 international and bilingual students [ISC Research 2025], fee value already runs into the low billions of USD [Kira estimates].

Tertiary and supplementary

Transnational tertiary adds a structurally distinct pool — ~430 joint programs and five foreign-invested universities [FIA 2025] [MOET 2025]. Supplementary learning is vast: private tutoring reaches most upper-secondary students [World Bank 2024], increasingly intermediated by digital platforms.

Trajectory

We see private-education value compounding in the high single to low double digits through 2027 [Kira estimates], led by bilingual K-12 seat growth and tertiary partnership expansion rather than fee inflation alone.

Section 07 · Bilingual K-12
010 / 017

Bilingual K-12: the fastest-growing private segment

Bilingual schools blend the national curriculum with an international track at roughly 60% of premium international fees — the price point where Vietnamese middle-class families convert from public to private.

Why bilingual wins

Families want international outcomes without severing the Vietnamese curriculum or paying top-tier fees. Bilingual delivers both, pricing near 60% of premium international [Kira estimates] and capturing the bulk of net-new private enrolment as the segment scaled to 153,236 students [ISC Research 2025].

Fee architecture

HCMC mid-tier bilingual runs VND 350-550m a year, with premium international reaching VND 750m-1.2bn-plus [Intl Schools DB 2025]. One-time building funds of VND 20-100m and reservation fees add a recurring cash-flow layer for operators [Intl Schools DB 2025].

Capacity is the constraint

Seat supply, not demand, now gates growth in tier-1 districts. Staffing scaled 38% to 15,083 teachers in five years [ISC Research 2025], yet qualified bilingual-teacher supply remains the binding limit on credible expansion.

Section 09 · Tertiary Partnerships
013 / 017

Transnational education: the second growth axis

Foreign partnerships now span franchised and joint degrees, articulation pathways, and a small set of foreign-invested universities — anchored by ~430 joint programs at 65 institutions [FIA 2025].

The partnership stack

Vietnam hosts ~430 joint training programs across 65 higher-education institutions [FIA 2025], plus five fully foreign-invested universities carrying 11,977 students as of mid-2024 [MOET 2025]. RMIT alone exceeds 12,000 students as the only fully established branch campus [RMIT 2025].

Capital is flowing in

Education FDI reached ~USD 4.6bn across ~707 projects by early 2025 [FIA 2025]. A 2025 UK-Vietnam network drew 14 institutions into a structured TNE framework [British Council 2025], signalling that partnership volume, not just capital, is scaling.

Where it heads

Demand is anchored by ~30,000 Vietnamese students abroad in a single destination market [British Council 2025]; transnational programs let families capture foreign credentials onshore at lower cost. We expect joint-program count to keep compounding through 2027 [Kira estimates].

Section 10 · Regulatory Framework
014 / 017

A regulatory frame that is opening and tightening at once

Recent rules widen the path for joint programs and online delivery while raising the bar for branch campuses — a deliberate quality filter that favours well-structured, reputable entrants.

K-12: the local-intake cap

Decree 86/2018 governs foreign-invested schools, capping Vietnamese-student share at 50% in preschool and 20% at primary and secondary [Decree 86 2018]. The cap protects national-curriculum priorities and is the single biggest reason bilingual — which sidesteps it — is the volume engine.

Tertiary: the top-500 gate

Decree 124/2024, effective 20 November 2024, requires a foreign branch campus to sit in the global top 500 in at least one of the prior three years, while simplifying 14 of 21 application forms and moving submissions online [Decree 124 2024].

Joint programs: the new mix rules

Circular 07/2025, effective 5 May 2025, fixes delivery: online programs may run >50% online (undergraduate only), hybrid 30-50%, with a minimum 25% of teaching by foreign-partner faculty [MOET Circular 07 2025].

Section 11 · AI & Edtech Impact
015 / 017

Where technology reshapes private education

Digital and AI-assisted learning is not a side-market — it is becoming the English-proficiency layer underneath both K-12 and tertiary, and a defensible bundle for school operators.

English is the killer app

English-language learning dominates Vietnamese edtech demand, with AI-driven personalization the fastest-moving sub-segment [Kira estimates]. Roughly 8.8 million people already use online learning services [Kira estimates].

Capital is arriving

Investment has flowed in — on the order of USD 400m-plus across ~70 funds into Vietnamese edtech over recent years, with Singapore the most active backer [Kira estimates]. M&A and strategic tie-ups are accelerating into 2025.

The school bundle

The strategic move is integration: school groups that bundle AI language tools into the bilingual offer defend fee premiums and lift retention rather than ceding the layer to pure-play apps.
