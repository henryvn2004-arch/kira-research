Section 04 · Executive Summary
004 / 018

A care system run by demographics, paid by a fixed envelope

Japan's eldercare market is no longer growing on demand — demand is guaranteed. It is now gated by two ceilings: a 570,000-worker shortfall by 2040 and a long-term-care budget the 2027 reform is built to contain. Both push the same answer: do more care with fewer hands.

Demand is locked in; supply is the constraint

The 65-plus cohort reaches 36.6 million in 2025 and peaks near 38.8 million in 2042 [MHLW 2025]. In 2025 every post-war baby-boomer turned 75 — the "2025 problem" — so certified-care demand now accelerates structurally. Yet the workforce need climbs to 2.72 million by FY2040 against 2.15 million today [MHLW 9th Plan 2024], with a job-to-applicant ratio already at 3.9x [JILPT 2025]. The binding scarcity is hands, not patients.

2027 reform tilts the field toward productivity

The Reiwa-9 (2027) long-term-care revision moves toward a wider 20% copayment and asset-based eligibility [MRI 2025], holding the public envelope flat in real terms. Operators cannot price their way out; they must lift output per caregiver. Care robotics and home-care subscriptions are the two scaled responses — one inside the facility, one in the home.

Section 05 · Strategic Implications
005 / 018

Five moves as the labour cliff meets a flat budget

The 2027 reform rewards output per caregiver, not headcount. The winners convert subsidy capital into permanent staff-hour savings and bundle home services into recurring revenue.

01 · PRODUCTIVITY
Bank the subsidy as staff-hour savings, not capex relief
Prefectural programmes fund 75–80% of monitoring and transfer-assist kit [MHLW Care-Tech 2025]. Operators that redesign night shifts around it free ~10% of care hours; those that bolt it on free almost none.
Anchor: 1.8 staff /home/month freed at scale
02 · SUBSCRIPTION
Move home care from visit-billing to recurring plans
Monitoring subscriptions run ¥0–5,000/month [Otera 2025] beside ¥37k/month average home care [FranceBed 2025]. The recurring layer lowers churn and creates a non-LTCI revenue line the reform cannot cap.
Anchor: ¥37k vs ¥125k home-vs-facility cost
03 · CONSOLIDATION
Scale buys the data and the subsidy-application muscle
The top operators — Nichii, SOMPO Care, Benesse — already run integrated tech models [Nikkei 2025]. Sub-scale homes lack the back-office to claim subsidies or absorb device downtime, accelerating roll-up.
Anchor: top-3 ≈ ¥530 bn combined revenue
04 · DEPLOYMENT
Treat robotics as workflow change, not gadget purchase
A ¥33m monitoring AI is called "unusable" where care flow is unchanged; penetration stalls near 40% even with funding [Seodoa 2025]. Value accrues to vendors who ship integration and training, not hardware alone.
Anchor: ~40% adoption ceiling without redesign
05 · POLICY-WINDOW
Position for asset-tested eligibility before 2027
The reform widens the 20% copay and adds asset checks [MRI 2025]. Higher out-of-pocket lifts demand for cost-transparent home options and private top-up services — a structural opening for subscription operators.
Anchor: 20% copay band set to widen

Section 06 · Demand Drivers
007 / 018

Peak-old: the demand curve runs to 2042, not 2027

Japan crossed into a super-aged structure years ago. What changes now is velocity — the post-war boomers entering the high-dependency 75-plus band all at once.

The 75-plus surge

In 2025 the population aged 65+ reaches 36.6 million, more than 29% of the total, and rises to a peak near 38.8 million by 2042 [MHLW 2025]. The sharper signal sits inside that total: roughly 8 million boomers born 1947–49 are now 75 or older, about 17.5% of the population [Kira estimates].

Why 75 is the threshold that matters

Care-certification and benefit use rise steeply after 75. With the whole boomer cohort now across that line, the count of certified users accelerates rather than plateaus — the demand engine for both facility and home care through the late 2030s.

Regional concentration

The strain is uneven. By FY2040 the projected caregiver shortfall is heaviest in Tokyo (~76k), Kanagawa (~53k), Saitama (~45k) and Osaka (~41k) [Joint 2024] — the metros where land and wage costs make staffing hardest.

Section 06 · The Public Envelope
008 / 018

A ¥14 trillion bill the 2027 reform is built to contain

Long-term-care spend has quadrupled since the system began. With demand still climbing, the 2027 revision shifts the lever from expansion to cost-sharing.

The spend escalator

LTC outlays rose from ¥3.6 trillion in 2000 to ¥11.7 trillion in 2019 and stand near ¥14.3 trillion in the FY2025 budget — about USD 91 billion [MHLW 2025]. This is now a fiscal-structure number, not a niche health line.

What the 2027 revision changes

The Reiwa-9 reform advances a wider 20% copayment, monthly burden caps, and asset-based eligibility verified via the MyNumber system [MRI 2025]. A planned care-plan fee was deferred into the same 2027 cycle after operator pushback [Monolith 2024].

The strategic read

Public reimbursement growth is being throttled deliberately. Operators face flat real fees against rising wage and input costs — the economic case for automation and for non-LTCI private revenue strengthens with every reform step.

Section 09 · Institutional Robotics
010 / 018

Care robotics: a small market scaling on a subsidy spine

Institutional care robotics is still early in absolute terms, but the policy machine behind it — prefectural subsidies of 75–80% — is what makes the 2027 trajectory credible.

Market size today

Japan's care-robot market was roughly ¥18 billion on ~9,000 units shipped in 2022 [SVP 2025]. The narrower medical-and-nursing-care robot segment runs ¥14.2 billion in FY2021, forecast to ¥37.6 billion by FY2027 [Statista 2024] — a ~2.6x lift in six years.

The subsidy spine

Prefectural Care Technology programmes reimburse 75–80% of qualifying kit, with package caps of ¥4–10 million per site [MHLW Care-Tech 2025]. Transfer-assist, mobility, toileting, monitoring and bathing devices all qualify.

The adoption gap

Funding is not the binding constraint — workflow is. Monitoring penetration stalls near 40% even where ¥10m subsidies exist [Seodoa 2025], because devices bolted onto unchanged routines do not free staff time.

Section 09 · Home-Care Subscriptions
011 / 018

Subscription monitoring: the home becomes the unit of care

Home care costs roughly a third of facility care, and a layer of low-priced monitoring subscriptions is forming on top of it — a recurring-revenue line that sits largely outside the LTCI fee schedule.

The cost arbitrage

Average home care runs about ¥37,000/month against ¥125,000/month for facility care [FranceBed 2025]. As copayments widen under the 2027 reform, this gap pulls marginal demand toward the home.

The subscription layer

Monitoring services now span sensor, camera, dispatch and visit types at ¥0–5,000/month [Otera 2025]. A representative offer pairs a ¥33,000 device with ¥1,980/month service [Chikaku 2025]; municipalities such as Yokohama subsidise up to ¥1,000/month [Yokohama 2025].

Why operators want it

The recurring layer lowers acquisition cost on the core home-visit business, raises retention, and earns revenue the reform cannot cap. It also generates the in-home data that underpins AI intervention planning (Section 15).

Section 15 · AI Impact
016 / 018

AI's payoff is staff-hours returned, measured at the home

The credible AI case in Japanese eldercare is operational: fewer night checks, less paperwork, earlier risk flags. The benchmark deployment shows what "good" looks like.

Monitoring & safety

Privacy-preserving sensors and fall-detection now anchor demand [OpenPR 2025]. Fujitsu's mmWave system flags falls without cameras [MobiHealth 2025], cutting night-round labour.

Record & plan automation

Voice-entry care records, dispatch optimisation and AI care-plan support are the high-ROI workflow layer; FY2025–26 deployment support reaches ~¥30 billion [MHLW Care-Tech 2025].

The proof point

SOMPO's "future care" model, rolled to all its homes, frees ~1.8 staff per home per month — about 10% of care headcount [Nikkei 2025].
