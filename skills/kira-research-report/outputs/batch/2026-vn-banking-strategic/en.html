How we built this view

This view is built from two evidence streams: primary inputs — analyst synthesis, operator reads, and channel checks with bankers and corporate treasurers — and secondary anchors — the central bank and statistical authorities, listed-bank filings, and triangulated estimates. Triangulated estimates. Where no single source covers a figure, we combine two to three inputs and disclose the method. Tagged [Kira estimates].

Growth normalises, not stalls

System credit rose ~18% in 2025, the fastest pace in five years, lifting outstanding credit past VND 18.4 quadrillion — about USD 670 bn [SBV 2025]. The central bank steers ~15% for 2026 [VietnamPlus 2026]. Sector pre-tax profit grew ~15% in 2025 and is projected up ~16% in 2026 [FiinRatings 2026]. The story is normalisation, not contraction — with profit dispersing sharply across the field.

Margin and funding squeeze

NIM fell to ~2.9% in 2025 from 3.5% a year earlier and stays below 3% in 2026 on higher funding cost [FiinRatings 2026]. Banks with deep low-cost deposit franchises — MB at 37% CASA, Techcombank at ~35% [Techcombank 9M25] — defend margin best; the rest absorb the cost-of-funds drag.

Section 05 · Sector size, credit and profit
007 / 017

A USD 670 bn credit book, growing into a tighter margin

System credit expanded ~18% in 2025; the central bank guides ~15% for 2026 to balance growth against liquidity and funding-cost pressure.

Scale and trajectory

Outstanding credit passed VND 18.4 quadrillion — about USD 670 bn — by end-2025, up ~18% [SBV 2025]. The central bank's ~15% 2026 target reflects a deliberate slowing to protect system safety as banks lean on costlier medium- and long-term funding [VietnamPlus 2026].

Profit dispersion sets in

Aggregate pre-tax profit at the 15 largest lenders rose ~15% in 2025 on a ~17.6% sector ROE [FiinRatings 2026]. Sector PBT is forecast up ~16% in 2026, but the field diverges sharply — well-capitalised banks with NPL control pull ahead; weaker-asset-quality names stay under pressure [FiinRatings 2026].

The funding-cost watch

Higher deposit competition lifts cost of funds across the system, the principal reason NIM holds below 3% into 2026 [FiinRatings 2026].

Section 07 · Japanese strategic capital
010 / 017

The USD 6 bn stake map: Japan anchored in the top tier

Japan's three megabanks each chose a top-tier Vietnamese bank; regional lenders and consumer-finance arms extend the footprint. Capital follows the FDI corridor.

The strategic logic

Japanese banks anchor the capital flows of Japanese corporates into Vietnam — trade finance, M&A advisory, supply-chain financing and infrastructure funding [VIR 2025]. The stakes are entry points to a manufacturing-and-FDI corridor, not financial bets on Vietnamese bank earnings alone.

Three megabanks, three Big-tier banks

MUFG holds ~19.7% of VietinBank; Mizuho ~15% of Vietcombank; SMBC ~15.01% of VPBank, the latter a USD 1.5 bn 2023 deal — one of the largest banking M&A transactions in Vietnam's history [SMBC 2023]. Cumulative Japanese deployment across banks and finance arms approaches USD 6 bn [Kira estimates].

Beyond the megabanks

Aozora took ~15% of OCB; SMBC's consumer-finance arm sits inside FE Credit; further positions span finance, securities and insurance affiliates [theinvestor 2025].

Section 10 · Cashless scale and funding
013 / 017

Cashless scale becomes a funding and acquisition engine

NAPAS processed 13 bn+ transactions in 2025, up ~40%; mobile-payment value rose 70-75%. Digital franchises convert this into CASA and low-cost deposits.

The cashless step-change

The national switch handled 13 bn+ transactions in 2025, up ~40% [NAPAS 2025]. Non-cash payment value reached ~25x GDP in the first seven months [SBV 2025], while ATM use fell over 20% — a decisive shift to mobile rails [SBV 2025].

From transactions to funding

Banks that own the daily transaction relationship capture demand deposits. MB leads at 37.2% CASA, Techcombank ~34.7% [Techcombank 9M25] — the cheapest funding in the system, and the clearest defence against sub-3% NIM.

The inclusion wedge

Account ownership reached ~87% by end-2025 [SBV 2025]; core journeys — onboarding, cards, small unsecured loans — are near-100% automated at leading banks [SBV 2025], lowering cost-to-serve for thin-file customers.

AI is moving from pilot to production across origination, risk and service — concentrated at the digitally-advanced private banks.

AI scoring on alternative data automates approval and prices risk for first-time borrowers, cutting loan decisions from days to hours [Kira estimates]. Central to closing the SME gap.

eKYC and biometrics
Face, fingerprint and OCR-based eKYC compress account opening and card issuance. VietinBank FaceID kiosks cut processing ~30% [Kira estimates]; TPBank evaluates 128 facial criteria.

Fraud and transaction monitoring
Models screen millions of transactions daily for anomalies. VPBank, VietinBank and Vietcombank run deep-learning fraud and card-risk detection at scale [Kira estimates].

Personalised next-best product
On large installed bases, AI drives cross-sell. Techcombank built ~100 m personalised data points, sending 52 m+ recommendations to 4 m+ customers [Kira estimates].

Section 12 · Regulatory frame and ownership
015 / 017

The regulatory frame: ownership headroom, with a digital opening

Three 2024-25 measures reshape foreign capital and digital entry — the central bank holds the rule on each.

Foreign ownership cap

The general cap on foreign ownership in commercial banks holds at 30% [S&P Global 2025]. Decree 69/2025, effective 19 May 2025, lifts it to 49% for banks undertaking mandatory restructuring of weak lenders — provided they are not state-majority [Decree 69/2025]. MB, HDBank and VPBank are the principal beneficiaries.

The new Law on Credit Institutions

LOCI 2024 (No. 32/2024/QH15), effective 1 July 2024, recognises digital banks and, for the first time, permits an SBV-supervised sandbox [LOCI 2024].

The fintech sandbox

Decree 94/2025, effective 1 July 2025, formalises the sandbox for three solutions — credit scoring, open-API data sharing and peer-to-peer lending — open to banks, foreign-bank branches and fintechs [Decree 94/2025].
