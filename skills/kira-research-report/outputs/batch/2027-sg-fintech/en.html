Singapore's fintech market enters 2027 at a structural inflection: the regulatory framework has hardened from voluntary codes to enforceable licensing regimes, the payment infrastructure has crossed the threshold from domestic to regional, and embedded finance is shifting from pilot to primary distribution channel. Three forces are reshaping the competitive order simultaneously — MAS licensing enforcement tightening the operating field for digital-asset and cross-border-payment operators, Project Nexus extending PayNow's reach across five ASEAN economies, and the embedded finance layer pulling financial services distribution toward super-apps and sector platforms.

Capital deployment has re-anchored toward late-stage, proven-model fintech. Fintech retained the top-funded sector position in Singapore in 2025, with deal value rising 34% year-on-year to USD 1.7 bn [GFTN 2025], but the composition shifted — later-stage VC accounted for 65% of investment value, signals of a market consolidating around defensible positions rather than funding expansion bets. Speculative digital-asset and BNPL paper has been repriced; operators that secured MAS licensing have materially outperformed those that did not.

The 2027 outlook is constructive but segmented. Payments infrastructure, wealthtech, and embedded finance are on the favorable side of the structural arc; BNPL faces volume-ceiling pressure from the Code of Conduct credit cap; and unregistered digital-asset operators face mandatory exit following the June 2025 FSMA compliance deadline. Market participants that sequenced licensing ahead of growth are entering 2027 as price-setters; those that delayed are managing regulatory overhead from a cost-disadvantaged position.

02

Market size & forecast

Singapore's fintech market was valued at approximately USD 14.0 billion in 2026 [Kira estimates] and is forecast to reach USD 16.1 billion in 2027, sustaining a 15.9% CAGR through 2031 to USD 29.2 billion [Kira estimates based on Verified Market Research 2025 anchor]. The underlying driver is structural: Singapore's fintech sector benefits from the MAS SGD 100 million FSTI 3.0 co-funding program for quantum-safe cybersecurity and model-risk infrastructure, combined with the progressive deepening of digital payment rails across the ASEAN-6 corridor.

Within the headline market, digital payments continue to command the highest growth rate — projected at a 16.95% CAGR between 2026 and 2031 — followed by wealthtech at 14.2% [Kira estimates]. Digital wallet transaction value is forecast to reach SGD 89 billion by 2027 [Kira estimates based on Statista 2026 trend line], up from approximately SGD 62 billion in 2024. The gap between transaction volume growth and GDP growth is widening: cash-to-digital migration continues to add incremental volume on top of organic consumption growth.

Sub-segment growth dynamics

The payments segment commands a disproportionate share of near-term growth: 45% of 2025 fintech VC in Singapore flowed into payments [GFTN 2025], reflecting investor conviction that infrastructure plays (cross-border rails, B2B settlement, FX conversion) will generate durable fee pools ahead of the commoditization of consumer wallet products. Wealthtech is the second-highest conviction area, with Syfe managing over USD 10 billion in client assets as of 2025 [Fintech Singapore 2026], and the robo-advisory segment driven by the comparatively low household equity-participation rate in Singapore versus the UK and US.

Lending fintech remains structurally constrained. The BNPL Code of Conduct caps the unsecured credit limit at SGD 2,000 without additional credit assessment, limiting revenue-per-user growth for BNPL operators. Singapore's BNPL market is forecast at USD 2.66 billion by 2032 [GlobeNewswire 2026], implying modest single-digit CAGR from current levels — below the fintech sector aggregate. Operators are responding by extending upmarket into instalment credit for higher-ticket categories (travel, electronics, healthcare), where the SGD 2,000 cap is less constraining per transaction.

03

Regulatory landscape

Singapore's regulatory trajectory through 2027 is defined by a single structural shift: the MAS has moved from framework construction to compliance enforcement. The Payment Services Act (PSA) 2019 has been progressively amended, and the 2024 PSA amendments — which brought custodial digital payment token (DPT) services, cross-border DPT transmission, and DPT exchange facilitation into the regulated perimeter — came into force in phased implementation [MAS 2024]. Phase 1 (July 2026) covers capital and governance requirements; Phase 2 (January 2027) mandates full operational compliance, meaning that 2027 begins with the highest regulatory compliance bar Singapore fintech has faced.

For payments operators, the capital threshold distinction between Standard Payment Institutions (SPI, minimum SGD 100,000 base capital) and Major Payment Institutions (MPI, minimum SGD 250,000) is less consequential than the operational compliance stack: MPI licensees must now maintain safeguarding accounts, submit to annual external audits, and comply with enhanced anti-money-laundering (AML) transaction monitoring requirements. Operators that previously operated under transitional exemptions — approximately 60–80 firms by KIRA analyst count — faced hard exit or licensing deadlines in H2 2025 [Kira estimates based on MAS licensing register analysis].

BNPL: voluntary code, finite ceiling

Buy Now, Pay Later remains outside the PSA perimeter as of Q2 2026, governed instead by the industry Code of Conduct developed under MAS guidance. Since May 2024, all four accredited BNPL providers in Singapore (Atome, Grab PayLater, SPayLater/SeaMoney, and Singtel GOMO) are accredited as compliant [MAS Parliamentary Reply 2026]. The SGD 2,000 unsecured credit cap, mandatory bureau checks above that threshold, and prohibition on compound interest on outstanding amounts constrain the structural addressable market. MAS has indicated it will review the framework "as appropriate" — market participants should model a scenario in which a legislative framework (akin to the UK's approach) applies by 2028–2029, which would impose licensing requirements on current code-only operators.

Digital token service provider framework: hard licensing from June 2025

From 30 June 2025, Digital Token Service Providers (DTSPs) providing DPT services to customers outside Singapore were required to be licensed or cease operations [MAS 2025]. The threshold represents a decisive enforcement posture: MAS has stated it will "generally not issue a licence" to operators that cannot demonstrate institutional-grade compliance infrastructure, customer asset segregation, and risk governance. Established players — OSL, Coinhako, and three others holding Major Payment Institution licenses for DPT services — are in a structurally advantaged position versus late applicants or unlicensed operators.

Stablecoin framework: draft legislation expected 2026

MAS confirmed at the Singapore FinTech Festival (November 2025) that draft stablecoin legislation will be published in 2026. The framework mandates full reserve backing in high-quality liquid assets and reliable redemption mechanisms, and applies to single-currency stablecoins pegged to the SGD or any G10 currency issued in Singapore. StraitsX and Paxos Digital Singapore are positioned as likely first MAS-regulated stablecoin issuers [Kira estimates based on public MAS dialogue]. The BLOOM initiative (October 2025) supports integration trials of tokenized bank liabilities and regulated stablecoins into payment rails — an architectural move that, if successful, will embed regulated stablecoins into commercial settlement by 2027.

04

Digital payments infrastructure

Singapore's payment infrastructure is operating at a level of maturity that domestic volume growth alone cannot justify — the growth thesis is now explicitly regional. PayNow registrations surpassed 5 million by end-2024, with cumulative processed value exceeding SGD 50 billion [MAS 2025]. PayNow linkages to India (UPI), Malaysia (DuitNow), and Thailand (PromptPay) are already live; the Philippines and Indonesia connections form part of the Project Nexus Phase 3 blueprint, with live implementation targeted for 2025–2026 [Kira estimates].

Project Nexus — the five-country instant-payment corridor coordinated by the BIS Innovation Hub — was incorporated in Singapore in 2025. When fully live, it will connect SGD, MYR, THB, PHP, and INR instant-payment rails into a single interoperable network. For Singapore-based fintech operators, Nexus represents a structural opportunity: operators with MPI licenses and FX conversion capability will be able to serve SME cross-border payment flows that currently rely on correspondent banking intermediation at significantly lower cost and latency.

GrabPay and the super-app payment layer

GrabPay's wallet share position — estimated at 35.3% of the Singapore digital wallet market as of 2024 [Kira estimates] — is underpinned by its transactional ubiquity across ride-hailing, food delivery, and grocery verticals. Grab's 43.9 million monthly active users in Southeast Asia generated USD 253 million in financial-services revenue in 2024 [Grab 2024 filing]; Singapore remains the highest-ARPU market within that base given income levels and smartphone penetration. The risk for GrabPay is structural: as PayNow deepens bank-to-merchant direct-payment linkages, the marginal utility of a closed-loop wallet diminishes for merchants who prefer settlement directly to their bank account.

Competitive landscape

Singapore's fintech competitive map is best understood by segment, with dominant positions varying sharply across payments, wealthtech, BNPL, and digital banking. No single operator commands cross-segment scale comparable to a universal bank; instead, the market presents a series of segment-level duopolies or triopolies with fragmented challenger tails. Regulatory licensing asymmetries — particularly in digital assets and cross-border payments — are the primary driver of competitive position entering 2027, rewarding operators that invested in compliance infrastructure ahead of growth.

06

Embedded finance & open banking

Embedded finance represents the most structurally significant growth vector in Singapore fintech through 2027 — not because the absolute market size is largest, but because it is the mechanism by which financial services distribution shifts from direct-to-consumer fintech to B2B2C sector platforms. Singapore's embedded finance market reached USD 8.48 billion in 2025, with a projected 5.0% CAGR to USD 10.3 billion by 2030 [GlobeNewswire 2025]. The sub-segments with the highest near-term growth are embedded wealth (via super-apps and digital brokerage integrations) and embedded insurance (via e-commerce and travel platforms), both of which benefit from Singapore's comparatively high financial literacy and product uptake rates.

The structural driver is API-first infrastructure maturation. Singapore's banking sector — led by DBS, OCBC, and UOB — has invested heavily in open API suites since 2020; by 2025, all three majors expose read and write APIs for account data, payment initiation, and loan applications to certified third parties. This enables fintech operators to embed financial products into non-financial platforms (logistics, healthcare, e-commerce) without requiring users to leave the host application. GrabPay's 35.3% wallet share is partly a consequence of this model — Grab embeds lending, insurance, and investment products into a rideshare and delivery app — but the model is being replicated across a broader set of verticals including property, healthcare, and B2B procurement.

Project Nexus and cross-border embedded payments

Project Nexus — the BIS-coordinated five-country instant-payment corridor incorporating Singapore, Malaysia, Thailand, the Philippines, and India — transforms the addressable market for embedded payment products from domestic to regional. Once live, an e-commerce operator in Singapore can embed a PayNow-originating payment that settles in real time to a Philippine merchant's GCash or InstaPay account, without correspondent banking intermediation. The revenue pool for settlement, FX conversion, and liquidity management across this corridor is material: cross-border SME payment flows between ASEAN-5 and India exceeded USD 320 billion annually as of 2024 [Kira estimates based on BIS corridor data].

Wealthtech as embedded finance anchor

Syfe's USD 10 billion AUM milestone and Revolut's 2025 robo-advisory launch both reflect the same structural bet: that wealth management will increasingly be embedded in multi-product financial apps rather than distributed through standalone platforms. The competitive dynamic is compressing fees — Revolut entered at a 0.75% management fee versus StashAway's tiered model starting at 0.8% — and the volume advantage will accrue to operators that reach sufficient scale to absorb fee compression while maintaining product-development spend. Syfe's Q4 2025 profitability and USD 80 million Series C [Fintech Singapore 2026] position it as the operator best capitalized to sustain that competition.

Digital assets & stablecoins

Singapore's digital-asset sector entered 2027 through a regulatory funnel that has simultaneously clarified the operating framework and concentrated the competitive field. The June 2025 DTSP licensing deadline effectively removed unlicensed operators — MAS's stated posture of "generally not issuing a licence" to operators that cannot demonstrate institutional-grade compliance means the remaining licensed cohort faces structurally reduced direct competition [MAS 2025]. The competitive dynamic now favors institutional-grade operators over growth-first startups, a reversal of the 2021–2023 pattern.

Retail investor protections introduced in June 2025 — mandatory Risk Awareness Quiz prior to cryptocurrency trading on licensed platforms, customer asset segregation, enhanced AML monitoring — add compliance cost but also credentialing value for operators that complete implementation. Retail engagement with digital assets remains lower in Singapore than in the UAE or Hong Kong, constrained by the retail protection framework; the professional investor and institutional segment (family offices, asset managers, corporate treasuries) is the structural growth driver.

Stablecoin regulatory framework: timing and impact

Draft stablecoin legislation is expected in 2026, covering single-currency stablecoins pegged to SGD or G10 currencies and issued in Singapore. The mandatory full-reserve requirement and real-time redemption mechanism will distinguish MAS-regulated stablecoins from unregulated alternatives and create a credentialing moat for compliant issuers. StraitsX and Paxos Digital Singapore are the most advanced candidates for the first MAS-regulated stablecoin designation [Kira estimates based on MAS consultation engagement record]. Once regulated stablecoins are integrated into the BLOOM trial infrastructure — which tests settlement between financial institutions using tokenized bank liabilities and regulated stablecoins — Singapore will have the most complete regulated digital-asset settlement stack in ASEAN.

Tokenization: from pilot to infrastructure

Project Guardian (MAS, 2022–present) has progressed from proof-of-concept to live trials involving tokenized government bills, tokenized fund products, and FX settlement using wholesale CBDC. By 2026, approximately 15 financial institutions are participating in Guardian trials [MAS Project Guardian 2026]. The implication for 2027 is directional: tokenized fixed-income and fund products are likely to enter limited commercial distribution through licensed operators in Singapore ahead of any other ASEAN jurisdiction, creating a first-mover advantage for operators who have built the back-office infrastructure to custody and transfer tokenized securities.

Strategic implications for market participants

Sequence

PSA Phase 2 compliance ahead of Q4 2026

Operators with MPI licenses should prioritize PSA Phase 2 compliance infrastructure (safeguarding accounts, AML monitoring, audit readiness) before the January 2027 deadline. Compliance costs incurred pre-deadline are operating costs; non-compliance costs post-deadline are existential. Kira estimates

Build

Cross-border Nexus capability ahead of live implementation

Operators with FX conversion and cross-border settlement licenses should build Nexus-integration capability now, targeting the USD 320+ bn annual ASEAN-5 + India SME corridor that becomes addressable at meaningfully lower cost once Nexus is live. Kira estimates

Pivot

Wallet operators: embed financial products, exit closed-loop

Closed-loop wallet share is structurally under pressure from PayNow's bank-direct model. Wallet operators should accelerate embedded lending, insurance, and wealth product integration — Grab's USD 253 mn financial-services revenue in 2024 demonstrates the unit economics are defensible when transaction data anchors credit underwriting. Position

Digital-asset operators: license first, then scale

The post-June 2025 licensing landscape means that unlicensed digital-asset operators face mandatory exit while licensed operators face structurally reduced direct competition. Operators that completed MAS licensing before the deadline have a 12–18 month window to build customer base before any new licensed entrants reach scale. Kira estimates

Anticipate

Stablecoin legislation: prepare issuance infrastructure now

Operators positioned to be among the first MAS-regulated stablecoin issuers (StraitsX, Paxos, and potentially two to three others) should finalize reserve management and redemption infrastructure ahead of draft legislation publication, to minimize the compliance gap when the legal framework activates. Hedge

BNPL: model a legislative upgrade scenario

BNPL operators should stress-test their financial models against a scenario in which a credit-licensing requirement is imposed by 2028–2029. The SGD 2,000 cap and Code of Conduct protections may not be the endpoint of MAS's regulatory intent; operators that build credit-license-ready governance structures now avoid a compressed remediation cycle.
