The disputes franchise is the durable moat

Singapore's defensible position is its international dispute-resolution infrastructure. SIAC registered 625 new cases in 2024 with 91% international and USD 11.86 bn in total disputed value [SIAC 2024]. The Singapore International Commercial Court rose from 2 cases in 2015 to 28 in 2024 [SICC 2024]. This franchise pulls premium cross-border advisory work that is structurally insulated from commoditization — counsel-led, bespoke, and relationship-anchored.

The volume layer is being repriced

Below the premium tier, routine work — contract review, due diligence, eDiscovery, compliance — is the substitution frontier. APAC is the fastest-growing ALSP region at 9–11% CAGR through 2032 [RM 2025], and MinLaw's 2026 guidance notes up to 44% of legal tasks are AI-automatable [MinLaw 2026]. Practice economics that depend on leveraging junior hours against this layer face structural compression.

The disputes hub: steady caseload, widening international reach.
SIAC registered 625 new cases in 2024, with parties from 72 jurisdictions — a record breadth — and USD 11.86 bn in total disputed value [SIAC 2024]. Absolute filings dipped modestly from 663 in 2023, but the franchise's international depth and the SICC's complementary rise reinforce Singapore's position as the region's dispute-resolution anchor.

Caseload: breadth over raw volume

The 625 filings in 2024 [SIAC 2024] sit slightly below 2023's 663, but the international mix tells the structural story: 91% of cases were international and 72 jurisdictions were represented, up from 66 [SIAC 2024]. South Korean parties were the top users, signalling deepening reach into Northeast Asia. SIAC issued 167 awards and saw 143 expedited-procedure requests — up from 94 — pointing to demand for faster, lower-cost resolution.

The SICC complement

The Singapore International Commercial Court rose from 2 cases in 2015 to 28 in 2024, with originating filings (18) surpassing transfers (10) for the first time [SICC 2024]. As the recommended supervisory court for SG-seated arbitration, the SICC closes the loop on set-aside and enforcement, making the seat more attractive than rivals lacking an integrated commercial court.

Why the hub pulls advisory revenue

Maxwell Chambers — the world's first integrated dispute-resolution complex, tripled in size in 2019 — co-locates institutions, arbitrators, and counsel [Kira estimates]. The seat's gravity converts into upstream transactional and downstream enforcement mandates, anchoring the premium tier of the USD 5.05 bn market [Deep Market 2024].

Litigation leads on size; corporate leads on growth.
Within the USD 5.05 bn market [Deep Market 2024], litigation and dispute resolution hold the largest revenue share, while corporate and M&A advisory posts the fastest growth. The 8.7% lawyer-population increase between mid-2023 and end-2024 [Wikipedia LIS 2024] is adding capacity faster than the commoditizing volume lines can absorb at historic rates.

Where revenue concentrates

Litigation and arbitration is the leading service segment by size in 2024 [Deep Market 2024], pulled by the SIAC/SICC franchise. Corporate, banking, and M&A advisory together form the second pillar and the fastest-growing line, tracking regional deal flow routed through Singapore holding structures. These two pillars hold the bulk of partner-level margin; the remaining lines face the steepest substitution pressure.

The capacity-versus-rate squeeze

Aggregate lawyer headcount grew 8.7% from May 2023 to December 2024 [Wikipedia LIS 2024]. With the volume layer repricing under ALSP and AI substitution, that incremental capacity cannot all be billed at legacy associate rates. Firms that fail to redeploy junior capacity toward advisory and supervised-AI workflows will see realization rates erode through 2027 [Kira estimates].

The two-economy split

Practice economics are bifurcating. The premium economy — disputes, complex corporate — sustains hourly billing and partner leverage. The volume economy — review, diligence, compliance, routine drafting — moves to fixed-fee, managed-service, and platform delivery. Operating both under one cost base is the central strategic tension for 2026 [Kira estimates].

ALSP market entry

The substitution frontier: ALSPs enter an open, fast-growing market.
Asia-Pacific is the fastest-growing ALSP region, expanding at 9–11% CAGR through 2032 [RM 2025], and held roughly 19% of the global ALSP market in 2024 [Spherical 2024]. Singapore and Australia lead regional adoption, supported by regulatory environments that the Law Society has characterised as accommodating to non-traditional providers.

What ALSPs actually take

ALSP delivery concentrates on the volume layer: contract management, due diligence, eDiscovery, IP support, and regulatory compliance [RM 2025]. These are precisely the lines where in-house teams have become more sophisticated and cost-conscious. The opportunity is substitution of internal junior hours and traditional-firm leverage, not displacement of partner-led advisory.

Why Singapore is the entry beachhead

The Law Society's published position on ALSP regulation signals a permissive, innovation-friendly stance relative to peers [LawSoc 2025]. Combined with the disputes hub's deal-flow gravity and a deep bilingual talent pool, Singapore offers ALSPs a credible regional headquarters from which to serve ASEAN, Japanese, and Korean in-house demand [Kira estimates].

Competitive response from incumbents

The strategic question for Big Four and international firms is whether to build captive managed-services arms or cede the volume layer. Rajah & Tann and WongPartnership's early GenAI tooling adoption [LBO 2025] suggests incumbents intend to internalize substitution rather than concede it — narrowing the open window for pure-play ALSP entrants.
