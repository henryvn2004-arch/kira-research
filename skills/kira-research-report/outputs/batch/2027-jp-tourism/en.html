How we built this view

This view is built from two evidence streams: primary inputs — hotel front-desk and inbound-operator reads, regional DMO conversations, channel checks — and secondary anchors — statistical authorities, listed-operator filings, ministry releases. Every quantitative claim carries an explicit source tag.

Primary inputs

Property & channel floor checks

Operator & DMO conversations
Conversations with inbound DMCs, regional destination-management organisations and OTA category teams; cross-read against JNTO arrival flows and JTA spending surveys.
Analyst synthesis

Secondary anchors

Listed-operator & industry filings

Triangulated estimates

Five strategic implications for the next 24 months

Market participants should treat 2026-27 as the post-tailwind reset, not a pause before another FX-driven surge. The decisions that lock in yield through the decade are regional-capacity, high-value-channel and labour-productivity commitments made now, while the China lane is rebuilding.

IMPLICATION · 01
Underwrite to income-led demand, not the weak yen
The cheap-Japan FX push is judged largely spent by end-2025 [JTB 2026]. Plan 2027 occupancy on income-led natural demand and brand pull, and treat any renewed yen weakness as upside, not the budget base. Per-visitor spend held at JPY 229,000 in 2025 [JTA 2026].

IMPLICATION · 02
Treat the China lane as upside, hold the West as base
China arrivals fell 60.7% in January 2026 on a travel advisory [JNTO 2026] and are unlikely to recover quickly. Anchor budgets to Western and long-haul demand, which grew through the shock; China's high spend (JPY 2.0 trn, 21% of total) [JTA 2026] returns as a margin kicker, not the base case.

Section 04 · Macro Context
007 / 017

From an FX boom to an income-led plateau

Japan recorded 42.68m arrivals and JPY 9.46 trn in visitor spending in 2025 [JNTO 2025] [JTA 2026], both records. The macro pivot for 2027 is that the weak-yen push behind that boom is judged largely exhausted — leaving spend-per-visitor and a high-value mix, not FX, to carry the revenue line.

The yen did the heavy lifting in 2025

With the yen near ¥150/USD against ¥110 in 2019 [BOJ 2025], Japan felt cheap enough to pull both volume and trade-up: lodging spend rose 26.7% to JPY 3.46 trn, food and drink 18.8% to JPY 2.07 trn [JTA 2026]. The effect is real but non-repeating — analysts judge the FX-driven push roughly exhausted by end-2025 [JTB 2026].

2027 demand must be income-led

With the tailwind fading, growth shifts to the natural rise in outbound demand from a wealthier Asia-Pacific and West [JTB 2026]. The 2026 forecast already shows the inflection: 41.4m arrivals (-2.8%) but spending up to JPY 9.64 trn as longer-stay Western visitors lift the average [JTB 2026].

The plan banks on yield per visitor

The Tourism Nation plan targets lifting spend per visitor from JPY 229,000 in 2025 toward JPY 250,000, en route to JPY 15 trn on 60m visitors by 2030 [JTA 2026]. The macro bet for 2027 is explicit: value per head, not arrival count, carries the revenue target.

Section 05 · The China Demand Shock
009 / 017

China: from a 30% surge to a directed collapse

China arrivals reached 9.10m in 2025 (+30.3%), the second-largest source [JNTO 2025], before a November 2025 travel advisory cut them 45% in December and 60.7% in January 2026 [JNTO 2026]. The 2027 question is how fast a politically driven shock — not a demand-driven one — reverses.

What triggered the collapse

After the prime minister's remarks on a Taiwan-Strait contingency, Beijing advised citizens against travel to Japan and directed agencies to cut Japan-bound volume to 60% through March 2026 [Nikkei 2025]. Carriers offered free changes and refunds; group tours saw mass cancellations [JNTO 2026]. January 2026 China arrivals were just 385,300, down 60.7% [JNTO 2026].

Group collapses, FIT is the cushion

Group tourism took the hardest hit — regional ryokan that depend on Chinese coaches reported near-empty Lunar New Year periods [Wedge 2026]. But individual travellers are already over 80% of the Chinese flow [NRI 2025]; the FIT base, skewed younger and more independent, is the channel most likely to return first once confidence steadies.

Why the revenue hit is contained

China was Japan's top spender at JPY 2.0 trn, 21% of 2025 spending [JTA 2026], so the loss is real. But its share of arrivals had already fallen from 30% in 2019 to 21% in 2025 [JNTO 2025]; the diversified Western and East-Asian base absorbs much of the volume gap, if not the full spend.

Section 06 · Source-Market Mix
010 / 017

The diversification that de-risks the China shock

2025's top markets were South Korea 9.46m, China 9.10m, Taiwan 6.76m, the US 3.31m and Hong Kong 2.52m [JNTO 2025]. East Asia's combined share fell from 70% (2019) to 65%, while the West rose from 14% to 18% [JNTO 2025] — the structural reason the value strategy survives the China cut.

Korea and Taiwan: travel-to-Japan is now routine

Korea (9.46m, +7.3%) and Taiwan (6.76m, +11.9%) [JNTO 2025] have made repeat short-haul trips to Japan a habit — high-frequency, lower-spend, but resilient and weather-proof against the China shock. Together with Hong Kong they anchor the East-Asian volume base even as China swings [Auncon 2026].

The West is the yield engine

Per-capita spend is led by long-haul: Germany JPY 394,000, the UK JPY 390,000, Australia JPY 390,048 — roughly 1.7x the JPY 229,000 average [JTA 2026]. The US passed 3m for the first time (3.31m, +21.4%) and is now the #4 market [JNTO 2025]. These cohorts stay longer and spend more per trip — the explicit target of the high-value tilt.

Spend share still leans East — for now

By spending, China (21.2%), Taiwan (12.8%), the US (11.9%) and Korea (10.4%) lead [JTA 2026]. The 2027 shift is that the West and Gulf rise in the spend mix as China steps back — concentrating value in fewer, longer, higher-margin visits.

Section 07 · Regional Dispersal
012 / 017

Dispersal is now policy, with money behind it

The new Tourism Nation plan sets a 130m regional-overnight-stay target for 2030, 2.2x the 2025 base at ~17% CAGR [JTA 2026]. It is funded by tripling the departure tax to JPY 3,000 from July 2026, lifting the agency's budget to JPY 138.3 bn — 2.6x the prior year [JTA 2026]. Dispersal moves from aspiration to engineered demand.

The regional-stay target is the headline metric

Overnight stays concentrate in the three metro regions; the plan now targets 130m regional stays by 2030, matching the metro share, on ~17.2% annual growth [JTA 2026]. Repeat-visitor and rural-overnight sub-targets sit beneath it — a deliberate shift from a single arrivals headline to distribution metrics.

The departure tax funds the push

From July 2026 the International Tourist Tax rises from JPY 1,000 to JPY 3,000 per departure [MOF 2026], earmarked for overtourism measures, regional dispersal and outbound support. The FY2026 JTA budget jumps to JPY 138.3 bn from JPY 53.0 bn — a 2.6x increase [JTA 2026] that subsidises local governments directly.

Overtourism control is the other half

The plan expands overtourism measures from 47 regions in 2025 to 100 by 2030 [JTA 2026], with state subsidies for congestion easing and crackdowns on non-compliant private lodging. Dispersal and overtourism control are two sides of the same metric: move demand off Kyoto and Mt. Fuji and into the regions.

Section 09 · Regulatory & Policy
015 / 017

Tax becomes the steering instrument

From July 2026 the departure tax triples to JPY 3,000 [MOF 2026], while lodging taxes spread across 17-plus prefectures and cities and Kyoto introduces a top luxury-stay band of JPY 10,000 per night [Kyoto City 2026]. A second integrated-resort application round opens in 2027 [MLIT 2026], framing the next decade of managed, higher-value supply.

Departure tax: small per head, large in aggregate

At JPY 3,000 the departure tax is negligible against a JPY 229,000 trip [JTA 2026] yet raises the FY2026 tourism budget 2.6x to JPY 138.3 bn [JTA 2026]. It is a steering instrument, not a deterrent — the revenue is ring-fenced for dispersal, overtourism control and outbound support.

Lodging levies tier the destinations

Accommodation taxes now span Tokyo, Osaka, Hokkaido, Fukuoka and others; Kyoto's reform tops out at JPY 10,000 per night on luxury stays from March 2026 [Kyoto City 2026]. The effect is to price congestion in the honeypot cities while leaving regional stays comparatively cheap — reinforcing the dispersal aim.

The 2027 IR round opens the supply frontier

Japan set a second IR application window for May-November 2027 [MLIT 2026]; the JPY 1.51 trn MGM Osaka resort opens around 2030 with ~20m projected annual visitors, ~6m international [GGRAsia 2026]. IRs are positioned as stay-type, high-value anchors beyond the existing metro cluster.

Section 10 · AI Impact
016 / 017

AI in Japanese hospitality: the answer to the labour wall

With near 80% of operators short-staffed [Teikoku 2025], AI is less a luxury than a capacity tool. About 37% of Japanese hotel chains already use AI for revenue management [HostQ 2026], and a national subsidy funds up to 50% of labour-saving investment, capped at JPY 5m per facility [JTA 2025].

Yield management defends the rate

With supply capped, operators lean on AI dynamic pricing to lift ADR rather than chase volume — already the highest-ROI use case, with ~37% of chains deployed [HostQ 2026]. As the yen tailwind fades, rate optimisation becomes the main revenue lever [Kira estimates].

Multilingual service for a diversifying mix

A broader Western, Gulf and East-Asian mix raises the value of AI translation and concierge tools; real-time interpretation is being deployed at transport and front-desk touchpoints to keep service human despite thin staffing [Nucamp 2025].

Automation as the labour answer

Robotics and back-office automation target the exact friction points — check-in, housekeeping scheduling, predictive maintenance — that the labour gap exposes, backed by the up-to-50% state subsidy [JTA 2025].

Section 11 · Outlook & Endnote
017 / 017

5-year outlook: spending compounds even as volume wobbles

Our base case sees arrivals reaching ~52-55m by 2030 — short of the 60m target on capacity and the China drag — while spending tracks closer to plan as the high-value mix and dispersal lift per-head yield [Kira estimates].

BULL · 2030
~60m arrivals
China normalises to 9-10m by 2028 [Kira estimates]; capacity unlocks via AI productivity and the IR pipeline; spending hits the JPY 15 trn target [JTA 2026]. Requires a fast political thaw and resolved labour constraints.
BASE · 2030
~52-55m arrivals
China rebuilds slowly toward ~7-8m; the West and East-Asia base compound [JNTO 2025]. Capacity and labour cap volume short of 60m, but spending nears plan as per-head yield rises toward JPY 250,000 [JTA 2026].
BEAR · 2030
~45-48m arrivals
China stays depressed below 5m on prolonged tension; a stronger yen erodes the cost edge and labour shortages bind hard [JTB 2026]. Volume stalls, though the high-value mix cushions the spending line [Kira estimates].
