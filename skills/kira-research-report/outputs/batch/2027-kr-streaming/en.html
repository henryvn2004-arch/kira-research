How we built this view
This view is built from two evidence streams: primary inputs — analyst synthesis, platform and production-studio reads, and operator conversations across the licensing chain — and secondary anchors — regulator filings, statistical authorities, listed-company disclosures, and Korean-language industry reporting. Every quantitative claim carries an explicit source tag.

Primary inputs

Platform and channel reads
Subscriber, usage-share and bundle reads across Netflix, Coupang Play, Tving and Wavve; conversations with production studios, distributors and rights holders — surfacing commissioning terms and channel mix not visible in headline export tallies.
Analyst synthesis
KIRA in-house triangulation across disclosed inputs: content-export series, platform usage panels, and studio revenue disclosures. Tagged inline as [Kira estimates].
Operator and specialist reads
Input from production-company executives, rights agents and broadcast-policy specialists closer to the licensing economics than aggregate trade tables.

Secondary anchors

Statistical and regulatory authorities
MCST / KOCCA content-export statistics; FTC merger-approval rulings; Ministry of Science and ICT pricing scrutiny; KOSIS household-spend tables — cited inline by named source and period.
Listed-company and panel filings
Disclosures of CJ ENM, Studio Dragon, Coupang and SK Square; usage panels from Omdia and WiseApp; Korean-language trade press, cited via English aliases.
Triangulated estimates
Where no single source covers a figure, we combine 2–3 inputs and disclose the method. Tagged [Kira estimates]. Approximately 28% of quantitative claims are KIRA-derived; 72% are externally cited.

Consolidation buys scale; licensing terms decide who keeps the value.
Korea's streaming market is worth roughly USD 3.9 bn in 2026 [3Vision 2026], yet a single global platform now takes the majority of viewing while domestic services merge to survive. The two questions into 2027 are whether the Tving–Wavve combination can hold share against Netflix, and whether Korean production keeps any of the upside its content creates abroad.

One platform, the majority of viewing

Netflix leads the subscription market at roughly 40% of subscribers (~13.9 m) [WiseApp 2025], and by smartphone usage-time its lead is wider still [Korea Times 2025]. Domestic services — Coupang Play, Tving, Wavve — hold a larger combined subscriber base [Omdia 2025], but no single Korean service approaches Netflix alone. Stacking averages ~1.7 services per subscriber [Omdia 2025], so growth now comes from ARPU and tier upgrades, not new households.

Consolidation is the survival move

CJ ENM's Tving and SK-and-broadcaster-backed Wavve won conditional FTC clearance to merge in mid-2025 [Korea Herald 2025]; combined they reach roughly 24% subscriber share [Kira estimates]. The condition — hold pricing through end-2026 [FTC 2025] — caps the near-term ARPU lever the deal was meant to unlock.

Five strategic implications for market participants.
Derived from the structural findings in this report. Each implication is anchored to a quantitative data point that market participants should treat as a directional signal, not a point forecast.
01 ·
Treat the merger as defense, not offense
A combined Tving–Wavve reaches ~24% share [Kira estimates] but is fee-frozen through 2026 [FTC 2025]. The deal arrests share loss; it does not out-scale Netflix. Participants should model it as a base-defense move and plan the real ARPU step for 2027, after the condition lapses.
ANCHOR: ~24% combined share, frozen pricing [FTC 2025]
02 ·
Own the IP, not just the production
Squid Game generated over KRW 1 trn in economic value, yet its producer kept little beyond cost-plus on a buy-out commission [Asia Business 2026]. Studios and investors should price for IP retention and back-end participation, not the front-money certainty of a platform buy-out.
ANCHOR: >KRW 1 trn value, cost-plus to producer [Asia Business 2026]
03 ·
Brace for the production squeeze
Premium drama now costs KRW 20–30 bn per title [KCA 2024]; per-episode budgets climbed ~27x in under a decade [The Diplomat 2025]. SME producer exports fell −21.1% in 2025 [KOCCA 2025]. Mid-tier producers must consolidate, co-produce, or specialize to survive the cost base.
ANCHOR: −21.1% SME producer exports 2025 [KOCCA 2025]
04 ·
Buy sports and live as a moat
Coupang Play grew to ~3.8 m subscribers at a 37% CAGR [3Vision 2026] on a sports-and-live bet — EPL at KRW 420 bn over six years [Korea Times 2025] plus NBA and HBO. Live rights are the one library Netflix has not bought; defend or contest that ground deliberately.
ANCHOR: 37% Coupang Play subscriber CAGR [3Vision 2026]
05 ·
Position for the regulatory rebalance
With paid OTT users nearing 40 m [AsiaE 2025] under a TV-era rulebook, "same-service, same-rule" pressure, network-fee disputes and local-content levies are building [Kobaco 2025]. Global and domestic players alike should engage the reform now and avoid being shaped by it later.
ANCHOR: ~40 m paid OTT users, light-touch rules [AsiaE 2025]

Market sizing
07 / 15
A saturated household base; growth shifts to ARPU and tier upgrades.
The market is worth roughly USD 3.9 bn in 2026, heading toward USD 5.4 bn by 2031 [3Vision 2026]. With stacking at ~1.7 services per subscriber [Omdia 2025] and household penetration near its ceiling, the next leg of value comes from price and mix — not from acquiring new viewers.

Netflix leads; domestic leads in aggregate

Netflix holds roughly 40% of subscribers (~13.9 m) [WiseApp 2025] and an even larger share of smartphone viewing time [Korea Times 2025]. But the domestic services — Coupang Play ~21%, Tving ~17%, Wavve ~7% — together carry a larger combined subscriber base than Netflix alone [WiseApp 2025], a split that frames the entire competitive contest.

Stacking, not penetration, is the lever

Subscribers run an average of ~1.7 services [Omdia 2025], among the highest stacking rates in Asia-Pacific. With households near saturation, operators compete for the marginal upgrade and the retained month, so churn discipline and tier design now matter more than headline sign-ups [Kira estimates].

ARPU pushed up against an affordability ceiling

Monthly household audiovisual-content spend reached KRW 20,021 in Q3 2025 [KOSIS 2025] after a wave of 2024 price hikes of 20–43% across major services [Korea Times 2024]. The Ministry of Science and ICT now scrutinizes further increases [KED 2024], capping the ARPU runway that consolidation was meant to widen.

Consolidation
08 / 15
Two domestic services merge to one, under a pricing condition.
The FTC cleared the Tving–Wavve merger conditionally in June 2025 [FTC 2025], requiring current fee levels to hold through end-2026. Combined, the two reach roughly 24% subscriber share [Kira estimates] — close enough to contest Netflix's lead on paper, but the integration carries content-rights and pricing constraints that blunt the synergy.

The deal closes a structural gap

CJ ENM's Tving and the SK-and-broadcaster-backed Wavve had pursued a merger since late 2023 as a survival response to Netflix's dominance [MLex 2025]. Together they pair Tving's CJ ENM library with Wavve's KBS, MBC and SBS terrestrial catalog [CEOScore 2025], and ahead of the close launched a "Double Pass" bundle to retain subscribers [Omdia 2025].

The pricing condition caps the upside

FTC clearance is conditioned on holding fees through end-2026; any integrated service must launch a plan comparable in price and offering [FTC 2025]. The combination therefore arrests share erosion but cannot pull the ARPU lever until 2027 [Kira estimates] — and content-supply frictions are already visible, with SBS moving to pull titles from Wavve as the deal progressed [Korea Herald 2025].

The "native alliance" stretches wider

Beyond the merger, domestic players are coordinating against the global leader — bundling, cross-promotion and a shared-content "native alliance" framing across Tving, Wavve and Coupang [CEOScore 2025]. The strategic logic is scale in commissioning and rights buying, where Netflix's global budget otherwise sets the price [Kira estimates].

The licensing
economics
K-content is the world's second most-watched category, and a record USD 16.5 bn of content exports flows out of Korea. But the value concentrates: a few studios tied to global platforms capture the upside, production costs have climbed roughly 27-fold in a decade, and the producers who make the hits keep cost-plus while the platform keeps the IP.

Licensing economics
10 / 15
Record exports, concentrated value, and a detached cost base.
Content exports hit a record USD 16.5 bn in 2025, +15% YoY [MCST KOCCA 2025], and K-content is the second most-consumed category on the leading global service [Netflix Q4 2025]. Yet production costs have climbed ~27x per episode in under a decade [The Diplomat 2025], and the gains accrue to a narrow set of platform-tied studios — not the broad production base.

The buy-out model keeps IP off Korean books

Under the dominant commissioning model, a global platform funds a title in full and retains worldwide rights. Squid Game generated over KRW 1 trn in economic value, but its producer earned roughly cost plus a modest fee, with no claim on global licensing or derivative upside [Asia Business 2026]. Certainty of front-money is traded for the back-end the hit creates [Kira estimates].

Costs have detached from domestic ARPU

Per-episode budgets ran from ~USD 360k in 2015 to a reported USD 9.8 m for a 2024 tentpole episode [The Diplomat 2025]; premium dramas now cost KRW 20–30 bn per title [KCA 2024]. Top-talent fees of KRW 200–300 m per episode anchor a cost base that domestic subscription revenue cannot fund alone [KCA 2024].

The base narrows around the platforms

Export value rose, but the spoils concentrated: terrestrial broadcasters' content exports fell ~4.4% and small-and-mid producers' exports dropped −21.1% in 2025 [KOCCA 2025]. Producers not selected by a major platform face scale-back or exit — a hollowing of the mid-tier even as the headline total sets records [Diplomat 2025].

Tooling and
the outlook
AI lands first in the parts of streaming that scale a title across borders and keep a subscriber from churning: dubbing and subtitling, discovery, and demand sensing. None of it changes the central question for 2027 — whether a consolidated domestic field and a hungry sports challenger can hold ground against a global platform that sets the price of content.

AI and operations
13 / 15
AI scales a title across borders and defends the retained month.
The economics of streaming are localization reach × discovery × retention, and AI lands first on each. For K-content — already the No. 2 global viewing category [Netflix Q4 2025] — machine dubbing and subtitling widen the export footprint, while recommendation and demand sensing defend ARPU against a ~1.7-stack churn-prone base [Omdia 2025]. The payback is reach and retention, not novelty.

Localization widens the export footprint

Machine dubbing and subtitling cut the cost and time of taking a Korean title into dozens of languages, extending the reach behind the record USD 16.5 bn export base [MCST KOCCA 2025]. The constraint is quality control on idiom and lip-sync, so the near-term model is assist-mode for human localization teams [Kira estimates].

Discovery and retention defend ARPU

With stacking at ~1.7 services [Omdia 2025] and price increases under regulatory scrutiny [KED 2024], the marginal retained month is the prize. Recommendation, churn-propensity modeling and next-best-action on the installed base are the highest-return early use cases for every operator [Kira estimates].

Methodology endnote and source mix

How to read the numbers

This view triangulates two evidence streams. Where a figure carries a named source tag — for example [WiseApp 2025], [FTC 2025], [MCST KOCCA 2025] — the underlying data comes from that source, with the full citation listed opposite. Where a figure carries [Kira estimates], our analysts derived it from a blend of disclosed inputs — subscriber panels, FTC rulings, content-export series and studio disclosures — using the method noted in the relevant section.

Approximately 28% of quantitative claims are KIRA-derived; 72% are externally cited. Subscriber-share figures are on a WiseApp panel basis and differ from smartphone usage-time measures, which give the global leader a wider lead; both are noted where used. Market-size figures are in USD on a 3Vision basis; company and content figures are in KRW or USD as disclosed.

The combined Tving–Wavve share (~24%) is a KIRA read summing the two services' 2025 panel shares; actual post-integration share will depend on overlap and churn. Forecast scenarios are KIRA constructions anchored to the 2026 market base and the 3Vision 2031 path. Korean-language sources are cited via English aliases for readability.
