The pipeline is real; the wires are the question

By February 2025 the energy department had awarded more than 1,400 renewable service contracts representing roughly 154 GW of potential capacity [DOE 2025], of which 66 GW is offshore wind across 92 contracts [DOE 2024]. Set against ~7.1 GW of renewable capacity actually installed [GlobalData 2026], the gap between paper and steel is the whole story. Connection — not allocation — now gates the build.

Grid investment is rising, but behind the curve

The grid operator's Transmission Development Plan commits roughly PHP 485 bn and adds 23,325 MVA of transformation capacity by 2034 [NGCP 2025]. Yet about 30% of projects committed for 2025–2027 still lack a system impact study [NGCP 2025], and Competitive Renewable Energy Zones plus inter-island links are years from carrying offshore wind to load centres.

Section 05 · Demand
007 / 021

Demand is compounding into a structural supply gap

Peak demand more than quadruples to 2050, the reserve margin is already thin, and a key Luzon gas source runs dry in 2027. The demand case for renewables is no longer ideological — it is arithmetic.

A quadrupling demand curve

System peak demand runs from 16,596 MW in 2022 to a projected 68,483 MW by 2050 — a 5.2% average annual climb [DOE PDP 2024]. Even the near term is tight: Luzon, Visayas and Mindanao are flagged as adequate but thin through 2026, with reserves narrowing sharply in the dry-season peak [ICSC 2026].

A 2027 supply cliff in Luzon

The Malampaya gas field is estimated to run dry around 2027, with ~4 bcm of reserves left [ICSC 2026], affecting five Luzon plants. That removes firm capacity precisely as demand peaks — and turns the renewables build from a climate goal into a reliability necessity.

Why the gap favours RE

Solar and onshore wind deploy in 12–24 months; offshore wind and large transmission take five-plus years [Kira estimates]. The supply cliff is near-term, so fast RE fills the immediate gap while offshore wind anchors the 2028+ baseload-replacement story.

Section 05 · Market sizing
008 / 021

Renewable capacity on a path from 7 to 30 GW

Installed renewable capacity roughly quadruples by 2035 on the official trajectory, with solar leading the near-term adds and offshore wind the post-2028 step-change. The build rate, not the resource, is the variable.

From ~7 GW to ~30 GW

Renewable capacity is forecast to climb from about 7.1 GW in 2025 to ~30 GW by 2035 [GlobalData 2026]. Reaching it means sustaining an adds pace the country has only briefly hit — and the 2025 base year shows both the momentum and the gap.

Solar carries the near term

Solar PV reached ~3.6 GW by end-2025 after ~899 MW of additions in the year, largely utility-scale [pv magazine 2026]. Over 1 GW of total capacity was added in 2025 [DOE 2026]. Solar's short lead time makes it the immediate gap-filler before offshore wind arrives.

The auction underwrites the curve

The 10-year auction program offers 25 GW for delivery 2027–2035 [DOE 2026], converting policy targets into contracted, price-anchored volume. That is the mechanism that turns the 30 GW forecast from aspiration into a bankable pipeline.

Section 05 · Generation mix
009 / 021

RE is 22% of generation, with a mandate to reach 35%

Renewables supplied just over a fifth of power in 2025, hydro still leads the clean mix, and the target is 35% by 2030. Closing the 13-point gap in five years is the core build challenge — and offshore wind is the swing variable.

Where the mix stands

Of ~126,941 GWh generated to mid-2025, renewables supplied 28,193 GWh, or 22% [DOE 2025]. Hydropower remains the clean-mix anchor at ~41% of RE output [DOE 2025], with solar closing fast on the back of utility-scale adds.

The target gap

Policy sets 35% RE by 2030 and 50% by 2040 [PEP 2025]. Moving from 22% to 35% while total demand grows 5%+ a year means RE generation must roughly double in absolute terms this decade — a build rate that needs offshore wind volume to clear.

Why coal is still in the frame

Coal capacity is projected to rise from ~13.0 GW to ~15.4 GW by 2035 [GlobalData 2026], holding baseload while RE scales. The mix shift is therefore additive, not substitutive in the near term — which keeps grid integration, not generation, as the binding question.

Section 06 · Pipeline
011 / 021

From 66 GW of contracts to 3.5 GW that can actually build

The contracted pipeline is enormous, but only a thin first wave is grid-connected, permitted and port-served. GEA-5 and two anchor zones define the bankable near term.

The funnel from contracts to steel

Ninety-two offshore wind service contracts hold 66 GW of potential [DOE 2024]; the GEA-5 auction targets 3,300 MW of fixed-bottom for 2028–2030 delivery [DOE 2026]; and two anchor zones — San Miguel Bay and Guimaras Strait — carry a combined ~3.5 GW [World Bank 2024]. The funnel from licence to built capacity narrows by an order of magnitude at each gate.

The first zones

San Miguel Bay (Camarines Sur) and Guimaras Strait are positioned as the country's first large-scale zones, together capable of ~11 TWh a year [CleanTechnica 2026]. They lead because they pair strong wind with shallow water, short export distances and proximity to substations.

The 2027 milestone

GEA-5 certificates of award are slated for February 2027 [DOE 2026]. That makes 2027 the year offshore wind moves from speculative to contracted — but with delivery in 2028–2030, the megawatts arrive later.

Section 06 · Project economics
012 / 021

The auction price sets the bar offshore wind must clear

A reserve price near PHP 10.4/kWh, USD 3–4 bn of capex per gigawatt, and a still-forming supply chain frame the economics. First-of-a-kind risk is the cost — and the reason patience may pay.

The GEA-5 reserve price

The regulator set a GEA-5 reserve price built off a preliminary PHP 10.3859/kWh [ERC 2026], reflecting port rental, fisheries compensation, inflation and FX. That band is materially above solar and onshore wind — the premium the system pays for firm, large-scale offshore volume.

Capex and the cost curve

A 1 GW project needs USD 3–4 bn depending on depth, distance and port readiness [GWEC 2026]. Offshore wind LCOE sits well above solar today, but global trendlines suggest waiting could cut future LCOE 40–60% [GWEC 2026] — a real option value in sequencing.

Jobs and local value

Offshore wind generates roughly 17,500 job-years per GW over a farm's life, about half direct [GWEC 2026]. Upgraded ports anchor fabrication, O&M and shipbuilding — turning the build into a multi-decade coastal-industry case, not just a generation one.

Section 07 · Connection queue
015 / 021

The connection queue, not the resource, gates the build

Committed capacity outpaces studied capacity, renewables dominate the connection queue, and a third of near-term projects are not yet impact-assessed. Where the wires reach decides which megawatts count.

A queue heavy with renewables

About 17,249 MW of committed capacity is expected to connect, with over 53% from renewables — led by solar at 5,976 MW and wind at 2,125 MW [NGCP 2024]. The pipeline is clean; the constraint is throughput.

The study gap

Roughly 30% of projects committed for 2025–2027 still lack a system impact study [NGCP 2025], the technical gate before connection. Without it, timelines slip — turning paper capacity into delayed capacity regardless of financing or permits.

Why offshore wind is hardest

Offshore wind lands far from load centres and demands new high-voltage export and onshore reinforcement. The first zones were chosen partly for substation proximity [CIP 2025], but scaling beyond them needs the inter-island and CREZ build that is still years out.

Section 07 · Transmission investment
016 / 021

PHP 485 bn of wires, racing a faster pipeline

The grid operator's plan adds substantial transformation capacity and inter-island links — but the build cadence trails the contracted generation pipeline. The race between MVA and GW is the report's central tension.

The transmission plan

The Transmission Development Plan 2025–50 commits about PHP 485.2 bn, adding 2,148 circuit-km and 23,325 MVA of transformation capacity by 2034 [NGCP 2025]. The longer 2023–40 plan targets ~3,871 km of lines and 34,150 MVA across voltage levels [NGCP 2025].

CREZ and inter-island links

Competitive Renewable Energy Zones plus the Mindanao-Visayas interconnection and planned Visayas-Luzon reinforcement are how RE output reaches demand [NGCP 2025]. These backbone projects — not generation — are the gating items for offshore wind at scale.

The cadence gap

In 2025 the operator energised 14 substation upgrades [NGCP 2025], real progress against a pipeline measured in tens of gigawatts. The structural risk is timing: if wires lag generation, the result is curtailment and stranded capex — the failure mode to underwrite against.

Section 07 · Regulatory frame
017 / 021

Policy has done its job; execution is the open question

Full foreign ownership, a rising portfolio mandate, faster permitting, and an auction program have de-risked the policy layer. What remains is delivery — and curtailment rules that are not yet written.

The reforms that unlocked capital

Since November 2022, renewables allow 100% foreign ownership [DOE 2025], which preceded a roughly 20 GW surge in projects and 154 GW of contracted potential [DOE 2025]. The Renewable Portfolio Standard now requires utilities to raise RE sourcing 2.52% a year, up from 1% [DOE 2025] — a steady, mandated demand pull.

Permitting and the auction engine

EVOSS and the Green Lane shorten permitting, and the Green Energy Auction Program converts targets into price-anchored, contracted volume [DOE 2025]. Together they make the policy layer one of the most investor-friendly in the region.

The unwritten rule

There is no grid-wide curtailment framework yet [Chambers 2025]; congestion is managed through transmission planning alone. As variable RE scales, the absence of clear curtailment and compensation rules is the policy gap most likely to surprise investors.

Section 08 · Strategic implications
020 / 021

Five moves for market participants

The opportunity is large and the policy is settled; the edge now comes from sequencing around the grid constraint. Each move below is a way to own scarcity rather than chase capacity.

01
Buy queue position, not just licences
A grid-connection agreement is the scarce asset. Target projects that already hold one, or sites with substation proximity, over raw service contracts [NGCP 2024].
1.65 GW shows connections can be locked early
02
Stake a port before competitors do
Staging-and-assembly capacity gates construction. Early positions at Pambuhan or Pulupandan, or an O&M base, become structural moats [GWEC 2026].
USD 3–4 bn capex needs a working port
03
Price to the GEA-5 floor, hedge to the slip
Underwrite PPAs against the ~PHP 10.4/kWh reserve band [ERC 2026], but build schedule slippage and FX into the model — first-of-a-kind risk is real.
2028–30 delivery, not 2027
04
Partner for the local layer
Foreign equity is unconstrained, but grid, permitting and port access are local. The CIP-ACEN model is the template — pair capital with market access [ACEN 2025].
25% local stake as access, not just equity
05
Watch the curtailment rulebook
No grid-wide curtailment framework exists yet [Chambers 2025]. The first version will reprice every variable-RE asset — engage early in its design.
~30% of near-term projects lack SIS
