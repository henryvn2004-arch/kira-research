How we built this view
This view is built from two evidence streams: primary inputs — analyst synthesis, intermediary and fund channel checks, and practitioner reads across dealmakers and owner-operators — and secondary anchors — SME Agency and METI publications, the SME White Paper, credit-bureau succession surveys, and listed dealmaker filings. Every quantitative claim carries an explicit source tag.

Primary inputs

Dealmaker channel checks
Conversations with M&A intermediaries, buyout-fund partners, and succession-support advisers — surfaces real deal cadence, fee behaviour, and post-close integration friction not visible in headline transaction counts.
Analyst synthesis
KIRA in-house triangulation across disclosed inputs: SME Agency succession data, credit-bureau owner-age surveys, and listed dealmaker disclosures. Tagged inline as [Kira estimates].
Operator and specialist reads
Direct input from acquirers running serial-acquisition (rollup) platforms, searcher-led operators, and owner-sellers navigating third-party succession for the first time.

Secondary anchors

Government and policy sources
SME Agency (Chusho-cho) succession and M&A data, the 2025 SME White Paper, METI guideline revisions, the Succession & Handover Support Centers' deal records, and the Succession / M&A Subsidy framework — cited inline by named source and date.
Credit bureau & listed filings
Teikoku Databank successor-absence surveys; financial disclosures of Tokyo Stock Exchange-listed intermediaries and serial acquirers; published buyout-fund deal-value series.
Triangulated estimates
Where no single source covers a figure, we combine 2–3 inputs and disclose the method. Tagged [Kira estimates]. Approximately 30% of quantitative claims are KIRA-derived; 70% are externally cited.

The seller is demographic, not cyclical

The peak age of Japanese business owners has risen to roughly 66–67 years [SME White Paper 2025], and the SME Agency estimates that by 2025 about 1.27 million owners aged 70 or older — a third of all firms — lack a successor [SME Agency 2024]. Crucially, more than half of the SMEs that closed in 2023 were profitable [SME White Paper 2025]: this is a supply of viable assets created by demography, not distress, and it does not turn with the economic cycle.

Capital and acceptance have arrived together

Third-party succession via M&A — once stigmatised — is now normalised; succession deals already exceed 65% of buyout activity [Kira estimates]. Domestic and foreign buyout funds cleared JPY 3 trn in deal value for a fourth straight year [Bain Japan 2025], and listed serial acquirers raise public equity expressly to consolidate succession-ready SMEs at 2–5x EBITDA [Kira estimates]. The bottleneck is shifting from willingness to deal capacity.

Five strategic implications for market participants.
Derived from the structural findings in this report. Each implication is anchored to a quantitative data point that market participants should treat as a directional signal, not a point forecast.
01 ·
Sourcing, not capital, is the binding constraint
With buyout capital past JPY 3 trn for four years [Bain Japan 2025], the scarce resource is proprietary access to owner-sellers. Acquirers with originating channels — banks, support centers, searcher networks — win the assets; financial-engineering edge has compressed.
ANCHOR: 1,028 succession deals in 2025 [RECOF 2025]
02 ·
Entry multiples reward speed and trust
Pre-IPO serial acquirers buy cash-generative SMEs near 2–5x EBITDA [Kira estimates], cheap by Western standards. The discount is a premium on certainty and continuity for the seller — buyers who preserve the name, staff, and brand transact faster and cheaper.
ANCHOR: 2–5x EBITDA entry band [Kira estimates]
03 ·
The rollup is the dominant value architecture
Listed platforms in manufacturing, IT, food and services compound through serial acquisition, sharing back-office and procurement while leaving operations local. Integration discipline (PMI), not deal count, separates durable compounders from acquirers that stall.
ANCHOR: 20+ deals post-IPO for leading platforms [secondary]
04 ·
Government subsidy de-risks the transaction
The Succession / M&A Subsidy now funds four frames including PMI and re-challenge, with support up to JPY 20 mn [SME Agency 2026]. Certified-institution status routes buyers and sellers toward subsidised, lower-friction deals — a structural tailwind to volume.
ANCHOR: up to JPY 20 mn subsidy per deal [SME Agency 2026]

Conduct risk is the sector's reputational tail

Deal pipeline
07 / 15
The pipeline: third-party succession goes mainstream.
Succession-purpose deals hit 1,028 in 2025 — a record, +11.6% and 20.1% of all Japanese M&A [RECOF 2025]. The public Succession & Handover Support Centers alone brokered 2,023 third-party deals in FY2023, +20% YoY, with a cumulative 10,174 since launch [SME Agency 2024] — evidence the pipeline now runs through formal, repeatable channels.

From taboo to default option

Third-party succession was historically resisted; family or employee handover was the norm. That has inverted. Succession now drives more than 65% of buyout activity [Kira estimates], and intra-family succession is a minority path. The normalisation is self-reinforcing: each completed deal lowers the perceived stigma for the next owner-seller, widening the funnel.

Public channels scale the funnel

Government Succession & Handover Support Centers recorded 2,023 matched third-party deals in FY2023 [SME Agency 2024], with the successor talent bank hitting a record 106 matches. These public rails de-risk first-time sellers and feed private intermediaries and funds downstream, turning a fragmented search problem into a structured pipeline.

The flow is supply-led

Unlike corporate-carveout M&A, succession deal flow is governed by owner age, not deal appetite. With the owner-age peak at 66–67 [SME White Paper 2025] and rising, the supply of sellers builds mechanically each year — a pipeline that deepens through 2030 regardless of rate or equity-market conditions [Kira estimates].

Owner demographics
08 / 15
The cliff: aging owners, profitable closures, lost output.
The successor-absence rate eased to 50.1% in 2025 — down 2.0pt and a seventh straight annual decline [TDB 2025], yet the absolute stock of heirless firms remains vast. The SME Agency's "2025 problem" frames the stakes: 1.27 million owners aged 70+ without a successor, risking ~6.5 million jobs and JPY 22 trn of GDP [SME Agency 2024].

Owner age has shifted up a generation

The average SME owner aged from 47 in 1995 to 66 by 2015 [SME White Paper 2025], and the modal owner age now sits at 66–67. The effective retirement age of small-business managers has drifted from roughly 62 three decades ago to 70.5 today [secondary] — owners are staying longer precisely because no successor exists, deferring rather than resolving the transition.

The closures are "black-ink"

The defining feature of the cliff is kuroji haigyo — profitable closure. More than half of SMEs that shut in 2023 were profitable [SME White Paper 2025]; one widely cited read puts it at 56% of 2023 closures [secondary]. These are viable, cash-generative assets lost to the absence of a buyer, not to failure — the precise inventory M&A can rescue.

Improving rate, deepening stock

A falling absence rate reflects better matching and rising M&A acceptance, not a shrinking problem. The SME base itself contracts roughly 1% a year [secondary] as demography bites; the policy aim is to convert as many heirless-but-viable firms into transfers as possible before the owner exits [Kira estimates].

Buyout capital
10 / 15
Record capital, deepening domestic and foreign demand.
Japanese buyout deal value cleared JPY 3.1 trn in 2024 — a fourth straight year above JPY 3 trn [Bain Japan 2025], and 2025 ran far hotter: PE-backed buyouts reached USD 27.6 bn by late August, nearly triple the prior-year pace [PitchBook 2025]. Total Japan-involved M&A approached USD 350 bn for 2025 [Bloomberg 2025], with succession the structural floor under SME deal flow.

Both ends of the barbell are funded

At the large-cap end, take-privates set records — Q1 2025 alone logged USD 21.4 bn across 55 deals, nearly matching all of 2024 [PitchBook 2025]. At the SME end, domestic succession funds and searcher vehicles proliferate. The middle — once thin — is filling as funds extend down-market to chase the demographic supply [Kira estimates].

Domestic funds scale alongside foreign capital

Foreign sponsors remain prominent, but domestic platforms are scaling fast: one homegrown manager closed a JPY 250 bn vehicle in 2026 [secondary], and Advantage Partners — which raised Japan's first PE fund in 1997 — anchors a maturing domestic bench. Low relative funding costs and cheap entry multiples keep the IRR math attractive [Kira estimates].

The search-fund / ETA frontier

Entrepreneurship-through-acquisition is emerging as a distinct SME channel. A Search Fund Association of Japan now organises searcher-led succession, mirroring the US ETA model [secondary]. For the smallest viable firms — below institutional-fund minimums — searchers and individual operators are becoming the natural buyer of record [Kira estimates].

Intermediary structure
11 / 15
Matching infrastructure: consolidating leaders, a crowded tail.
The advisory layer is fragmented yet consolidating: roughly 2,900 certified M&A support institutions [SME Agency 2025] sit beneath a handful of scaled, listed intermediaries. Most certified players were founded after 2010 and run fewer than ten dedicated professionals [SME Agency 2025] — a structure that concentrates trust, brand, and deal-flow at the top.

Rollup playbook
12 / 15
The rollup playbook: six plays that compound succession supply.
Listed serial acquirers turn the succession supply into a compounding engine — buying cash-generative SMEs near 2–5x EBITDA [Kira estimates], financing cheaply, and sharing scale while leaving operations local. The discipline is repeatable; the edge is sourcing and integration, not leverage.
PLAY · 01
Buy cheap, certain, and cash-generative
Target stable, profitable SMEs near 2x EBITDA pre-IPO, up to ~5x at scale [Kira estimates]. The seller trades headline price for continuity and certainty; the acquirer underwrites durable cash flows, not turnarounds.
FRAME: certainty premium for the seller
PLAY · 02
Own proprietary sourcing
Deal flow comes from banks, support centers, accountants, and searcher networks — not auctions. Proprietary origination is the moat; platforms that rely on intermediated processes pay up and lose the relationship-sensitive seller.
MOAT: origination, not financial engineering
PLAY · 03
Finance on low-cost debt
Ultra-low domestic funding plus public equity lets serial acquirers compound: buy near 2x EBITDA with debt, lift consolidated multiple on the listed platform [Kira estimates]. The arbitrage is private-to-public re-rating, disciplined by rising rates.
ENGINE: multiple arbitrage on re-rating
PLAY · 04
Preserve name, brand, and people
Leading platforms keep the acquired firm's name, staff, and local autonomy [secondary] — decisive in a relationship-driven seller market. Continuity reassures employees and customers and is the explicit reason owners choose one acquirer over another.
SIGNAL: continuity wins the deal
PLAY · 05
Share scale, centralise back-office
Group procurement, finance, HR, and systems are pooled while front-line operations stay local. Food, IT, and manufacturing platforms have run 20–30+ acquisitions on this model [secondary]; shared infrastructure is where synergy actually accrues.
SYNERGY: shared back-office, local front line
PLAY · 06
Win on integration (PMI)
Post-merger integration — not deal count — separates compounders from stallers. Government now subsidises PMI directly via a dedicated frame [SME Agency 2026]; disciplined PMI converts a portfolio of small deals into a durable platform.
EDGE: PMI discipline, subsidised

Policy & guardrails
13 / 15
Policy: from encouragement to guardrails on conduct.
Policy has moved from promoting third-party succession to governing how it is done. The SME M&A Guidelines (2020), certified-institution registry (2021), and a four-frame Succession / M&A Subsidy now scaffold the market — while rising intermediary-misconduct cases push the next phase toward conduct standards and fee transparency [SME Agency 2026].

Methodology and sources

Methodology summary

This view triangulates two evidence streams. Where a figure carries a named source tag (e.g. [RECOF 2025], [TDB 2025], [SME Agency 2024], [Bain Japan 2025]), the underlying data comes from that source; the full citation is given in the sources list. Where a figure carries [Kira estimates], KIRA analysts derived the number from a blend of disclosed inputs — government publications, credit-bureau surveys, fund and intermediary disclosures, and channel checks — using methodology disclosed in the relevant section. Approximately 30% of quantitative claims in this report are KIRA-derived; 70% are externally cited. USD and JPY figures are stated as reported by each source and not re-based; deal counts and market values should be treated as directional bounds, not audited accounts. Forecast deal-flow paths assume the demographic owner-age trajectory holds and no major disruption to financing conditions.

Coverage notes

"SME M&A" here means third-party business transfers of small and medium enterprises driven by succession, plus the buyout and serial-acquisition activity that absorbs them. Deal counts use succession-purpose classification and may differ from total-M&A tallies. Buyout deal values are as published and mix domestic and foreign sponsors. Entry-multiple bands are KIRA triangulations of disclosed serial-acquirer economics, not per-deal figures.
