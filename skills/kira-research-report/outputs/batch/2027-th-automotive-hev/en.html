Chinese brands own the battery-electric headline, but in 2025 hybrids quietly outsold pure EVs. The 2026 CO2-based excise reset and a used-car residual shock hand Japanese incumbents a defensible mass-market position — even as their total-market share slips toward the high-60s.

Hybrids out-sold pure EVs in 2025

Hybrids reached 151,000 units at a ~25% share of the passenger market in 2025, outselling battery-electric vehicles by nearly 26,000 units [Proliance 2025]. Toyota and Honda lead the segment; HEVs bridge fuel efficiency with no range anxiety, the binding concern for upcountry travel [Proliance 2025]. xEV demand grew 43.8% in H1 2025 with HEVs at 50.6% of that mix [MReport 2025].

Chinese brands own BEVs, not the market

Chinese brands hold 70-80% of BEV volume with seven of the top-ten EV brands [Gasgoo 2025], but their total-market share is ~22% versus Japanese makers' 69.3% [Proliance 2025]. Chinese share rose from 4% in 2021 — a real handover in BEVs, a contest still open in the mass market.

Section 03 · Strategic Implications
005 / 026

Five strategic implications for the next 24 months

Market participants should treat the 2026 CO2-excise transition and the EV3.5 production deadline as the structural decision points — not the 2030 30@30 target. Powertrain bets, residual-value engineering and channel choices made now lock in cost and trust positions through the decade.

IMPLICATION · 01
Hybrids are the defensible centre, not a way-station
HEVs outsold BEVs in 2025 (151k vs 126k) [Proliance 2025] and hold value where BEVs collapse. Range-anxiety and residual trust keep a large fuel-backed segment alive past 2027 — the incumbents' moat is real, not nostalgic.

IMPLICATION · 02
The 2026 CO2 excise rewards low-emission powertrains
From Jan 2026 excise is CO2-based: HEVs at ≤100 g/km pay 6%, locked through 2032 for BOI participants [HLB 2026]. High-emission ICE gets dearer; the policy is a lever pulling buyers toward HEV/BEV — favouring whoever localises first.

IMPLICATION · 03
Win Thailand as an export base, not just a sales market
FTI plans 1.50m units in 2026 — 950k for export vs 500k domestic [Khaosod 2025]. BYD ships from Rayong to Europe [CleanTechnica 2025]; the right-hand-drive ASEAN hub outlasts the domestic price war for both camps.

IMPLICATION · 04
Engineer residual value, not just sticker price
A one-year-old BEV can shed ~30% on price cuts plus ~30% used [Krungsri 2025]; used HEVs hold far better. Battery-health certification is now the demand lever — the buyer's real cost is ownership minus residual.

EV3.5 forces 1:3 local output by 2027 [BOI 2025] into a soft market, and the price war is culling weaker Chinese entrants. Service depth and after-sales, not launch cadence, decide who survives the shake-out.

Section 04 · Macro Context

Macro indicators shaping auto demand

Thailand's economy grows slowly — 2.4% in 2025 and ~1.5% in 2026 [Krungsri 2025] — with household debt near 87% of GDP constraining new-car credit. The powertrain transition is supply- and policy-driven, layered onto a sector worth ~10-11% of GDP and 850,000 direct jobs, not onto strong consumer pull.

Growth pace

The economy grew 2.4% in 2025, slowing to ~1.5% in 2026 before a modest recovery to ~2.0% in 2027 [Krungsri 2025]. Auto demand is cyclically pressured — total vehicle sales remain below the 2018-19 peak even as electrified share climbs past 45% of the market [FTI 2026].

Household debt & credit

Household debt reached 86.7% of GDP (THB 16.44tn) in Q4 2025 [Nation 2026], keeping auto-loan approval tight; rejection rates on pickup and passenger-car financing rose through 2024-25 [Krungsri 2025]. Borrowing increasingly funds daily expenses, not assets — a structural drag on big-ticket purchases [Nation 2026].

Auto-hub depth

The sector contributes ~10-11% of GDP, employs ~850,000 directly and supports 1.5m indirect jobs [Wikipedia/BOI 2025]. That manufacturing base — not consumer demand — is why supply is racing ahead of the soft home market.

Section 04 · Macro Context
008 / 026

Policy ladder: from EV3 to the 2026 excise reset

Thailand's transition runs on an incentive ladder anchored by the 30@30 production target [BOI 2025], the EV3 / EV3.5 subsidy packages, and — newly — a CO2-based excise structure from January 2026 that adds a hybrid incentive alongside the all-BEV bet.

Anchor framework

The 30@30 policy targets zero-emission vehicles at 30% of total production by 2030 — about 725,000 cars [BOI 2025]. EV3 (2022-23) and EV3.5 (2024-27) layer purchase subsidies, excise cuts and import-duty relief on top [EY 2024].

The 2026 CO2 excise reset

From 1 January 2026 excise is calculated on CO2 emissions, safety standard, local content and ADAS [Thaiautonews 2026]. HEVs at ≤100 g/km pay 6%; 100-120 g/km pay 9% — locked through 2032 for BOI participants [HLB 2026]. High-emission ICE rises in cost.

The hybrid hedge

A BOI HEV incentive (≥THB 3bn investment over four years) [Legal500 2025] now formally backs the Japanese multi-pathway view. From 2026 HEV batteries must be produced in Thailand; other components from 2028 [HLB 2026].

Section 05 · Sector Sizing
010 / 026

Market size & growth: Thailand auto 2022-2030

Total vehicle sales reached ~621,000 units in 2025, up 8.5% [FTI 2026], with electrified models at 45% of the market. Hybrids led the electrified mix at 151,000 units, ahead of BEVs at 126,000 — a powertrain split that defines the competitive contest [Proliance 2025].

Sales & electrified share

Total sales of ~621,166 units in 2025 grew 8.5% [FTI 2026]; electrified models hit 45% of the market. Within that, HEVs (151k) outsold BEVs (126k) [Proliance 2025], while PHEVs added ~17k. xEV demand grew 43.8% in H1 2025, with HEVs at 50.6% of the xEV mix [MReport 2025].

The supply-demand gap

Domestic BEV output rose to 70,914 units in 2025, up 632% from 9,688 in 2024 [Proliance 2025] as EV3.5 mandates bit, while HEV production rose 12.3% to 214,317 units. ICE output fell 29.1% to 247,929 units — the powertrain pivot in one set of numbers.

Price convergence

Average BEV retail price fell to ~USD 35,000 — near ICE parity at ~USD 34,000 [Krungsri 2025]. With the entry-price advantage gone, the contest moves to financing access, after-sales and residual value, not headline cost — where hybrids and incumbents are strong.

Section 06 · Segment Economics
011 / 026

Segment economics: HEV vs BEV vs ICE

Each powertrain has a distinct economic profile. HEVs combine fuel savings, no charging dependency and strong residuals; BEVs win on running cost but bleed residual value; ICE is squeezed by the 2026 excise. The 2027 contest is decided where these profiles meet the household budget.

HEV — the defensible centre

Hybrids deliver 25-29 km/l (Yaris Ativ HEV at 29.4 km/l) [Paultan 2025] with no range anxiety or charging dependency, and hold residual value far better than BEVs. The 2026 excise of 6% at ≤100 g/km [HLB 2026] preserves their price competitiveness against ICE.

BEV — lowest running cost, weakest residual

BEVs reach near sticker parity with ICE [Krungsri 2025] and offer the lowest fuel cost, but a one-year-old unit can shed ~30% on new-price cuts plus ~30% as a used unit [Krungsri 2025]. The total-cost-of-ownership advantage erodes once residual loss is counted.

Export economics

Thailand's value as a right-hand-drive export base spans both camps. The EV Board expects EV exports of ~12,500 units in 2025 rising to ~52,000 by 2026 [Chinadaily 2025], with one export unit counting as 1.5 toward the local-production obligation [BOI 2025].

Section 08 · Demand & Channels
019 / 026

Credit, residuals and the route to market

Demand is gated by credit and residual trust. Each BEV new-car discount resets the residual of every BEV already on the road, pushing risk-averse buyers toward hybrids — while charging supply has scaled to ~11,600 chargers but the DC fast-charging gap persists.

The residual shock

A new BEV can shed ~30% on sticker cuts in year one, then lose roughly another ~30% as a used unit — versus single-digit annual loss for ICE and far better retention for hybrids [Krungsri 2025]. The used-car association warned in late 2025 the price war risks spreading damage market-wide [Autolife 2025].

Credit and the trade flow

With household debt at 86.7% of GDP [Nation 2026], finance houses tighten on BEV collateral whose value is hard to underwrite. The broader used-car market fell ~10% to 285,000 units in H1 2025 [Thansettakij 2025], and new-car discounting pulled buyers out of the resale channel.

Charging & channel shift

Public charging scaled to ~3,720 stations and ~11,600 chargers, but highway DC density remains the binding constraint outside Bangkok [Kira estimates]. Chinese brands lead with experience stores; incumbents lean on legacy dealer and after-sales depth — the trust layer hybrids exploit.

Section 09 · Regulatory Landscape
020 / 026

The 2026 excise reset rewires the contest

The January 2026 CO2-based excise is the decisive policy shift: it locks low-emission hybrids at 6% through 2032 [HLB 2026], makes high-emission ICE dearer, and pairs with a BOI HEV incentive that formally backs the Japanese multi-pathway view — while EV3.5 still forces BEV localisation.

The CO2 excise structure

From 1 Jan 2026, excise is set by CO2, safety standard, local content and ADAS [Thaiautonews 2026]. HEVs at ≤100 g/km pay 6% and 100-120 g/km pay 9% — held flat 2026-2032 for BOI participants, stepping up to 8%/10% otherwise [HLB 2026]. The lever pulls buyers toward low-CO2 powertrains.

The BOI hybrid hedge

The BOI's HEV incentive (≥THB 3bn over four years) [Legal500 2025] requires local battery production from 2026 and other components from 2028 [HLB 2026]. It legitimises the hybrid path Toyota and Honda were already taking.

EV3.5 still tightens

AI in Thailand's auto economics: where it moves the needle

AI's highest-value role here is not in the car — it is in certifying battery health and pricing residuals. Solving the used-BEV trust gap is worth more to the 2027 market than any in-cabin feature, because it unlocks the resale channel the price war broke.

The trust-gap thesis

Used-BEV prices are unstable largely because battery condition is opaque [Krungsri 2025]. AI-driven state-of-health (SoH) and remaining-useful-life (RUL) estimation can turn that opacity into a credible, certifiable number [MDPI 2025].

From passive to adaptive BMS

AI/ML battery management — using fleet data to estimate SoC, SoH and RUL in real time [Dialzara 2025] — supports predictive maintenance and longer battery life [MDPI 2025], directly lifting residual confidence. Ford's systems forecast battery faults up to 10 days ahead [Dialzara 2025].

Inventory discipline

Demand sensing across the dealer network can cut the stock overhang that fuels the price war [Kira estimates], aligning EV3.5-mandated output with where it can actually clear.
