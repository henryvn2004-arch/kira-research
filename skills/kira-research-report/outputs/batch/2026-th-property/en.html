How we built this view

This view draws on two evidence streams: primary inputs — land-office transfer records, listed-developer disclosure, and analyst synthesis — and secondary anchors — the central bank, the national housing data agency, and consultancy market datasets. Research spans English and Thai-language sources; every quantitative claim carries an explicit source tag.

Primary inputs

Transfer & ownership records
Land Department ownership-transfer data on foreign and domestic units — nationality split, unit counts, transfer value — reconciled by province and quarter to separate real demand from headline launches.
Listed-developer disclosure
Presales, transfers, launch pipelines and segment mix from SET-listed developers and active Phuket resort-residence builders — the operating reality behind the aggregate, including foreign-buyer exposure.
Analyst synthesis
KIRA in-house triangulation across disclosed inputs — unsold stock, absorption, price-per-square-metre by tier and province. Tagged inline as [Kira estimates].

Secondary anchors

Macro & policy authorities
Central-bank policy-rate, household-debt and loan-to-value data, the IMF growth path, and the cabinet stimulus package — transfer-fee cuts, mortgage-registration relief, and the LTV relaxation — cited inline by named source.
Housing-market datasets
The national real-estate information centre's Bangkok-region and provincial series and consultancy condominium/villa datasets, reconciled against transfer records and developer disclosure — cited by source and period.
Triangulated estimates
Where no single source covers a figure — mid-tier share of Bangkok unsold stock, Phuket buyer-nationality mix, the 2028 scenario fan — we combine 2–3 inputs and disclose the method. Tagged [Kira estimates].

Bangkok: demand-starved

The Bangkok-region overhang is a credit story, not a launch story. Unsold condominium stock is forecast near 97,900 units worth ~THB 488 bn by end-2025 [REIC 2025], while developers cut new launches to a 15-year low. Mortgage rejection ran ~40% under THB 3m, up to 70% at the entry tier [Terra 2025] — the glut sits precisely where credit is thinnest.

Phuket: capital-rich

Phuket runs the opposite dynamic. Foreign buyers take ~60% of prime villa and condominium deals [Colliers 2026]; villa sales rose ~13% in 2025 with prime-area prices up 15–18% [Colliers 2026]. Tourism — a record 17.4 m airport passengers in 2025 [AoT 2025] — and constrained land, not domestic credit, set this market's floor.

Five strategic implications for market participants

Five forces govern who captures value across Thailand's split property market through 2028. Each is anchored to documented data and KIRA synthesis.

01 · CREDIT
Domestic credit gates the Bangkok mass market
Mortgage rejection ran ~40% under THB 3m [Terra 2025] with household debt near 86.8% of GDP [BOT 2025]. Bangkok absorption is a lending problem; the ~98k unsold condo stock clears only as credit access widens.
Anchor: ~40% reject rate
02 · FOREIGN
Phuket runs on foreign, not local, capital
Foreign buyers take ~60% of prime Phuket deals [Colliers 2026]; Russians alone hold 40–60% of pool-villa sales [Reloc8 2025]. The island decouples from Thai credit — its risk is external, not domestic.
Anchor: ~60% foreign share
03 · STIMULUS
The fee window pulls Bangkok demand forward
Transfer and mortgage fees cut to 0.01% for homes ≤THB 7m, extended to mid-2027, plus 100% LTV [MOF 2026]. Real relief — but front-loaded, and it barely touches the foreign-driven Phuket segment.
Anchor: 0.01% fee to 2027

Both markets destock into 2026
Bangkok new condo supply falls below 40,000 units in 2026 [CBRE 2026]; Phuket new condo supply moderates to 6,000–8,000 units after a 25,000-unit surge [Colliers 2026]. Disciplined pipelines protect pricing on both sides.

SECTION 04

Macro & the fee window

A sub-2% growth economy with household debt near 87% of GDP cannot self-fund a domestic housing recovery. The 2026 lever is policy — transfer-fee cuts, 100% loan-to-value, and a lower policy rate — a window that reaches Bangkok far more than the foreign-funded islands.

Section 04 · Macro & Policy
007 / 018

Soft growth, heavy debt, and a policy push to pull demand forward

The macro backdrop is the constraint on domestic demand, and policy is the counterweight. Weak growth and high household debt cap Bangkok purchasing power; a fee-cut window and lower policy rate are engineered to convert latent demand before the 2027 deadline.

Growth & rates

The economy grew about 2.1% in 2025 and is seen near 1.6% in 2026 [IMF 2026], among the slowest in the region — some call it the weakest stretch in three decades [Nation 2026]. The central bank cut its policy rate to 1.25% by end-2025 and is expected near 1.0% in H1 2026 [BOT 2025], easing mortgage costs but not restarting demand alone.

Household debt

Household debt sits near 86.8% of GDP, the highest among ASEAN peers, down from 88.4% at end-2024 [BOT 2025]. Tight bank lending and weak confidence drove mortgage rejection to roughly 40% under THB 3m, up to 70% at the entry tier [Terra 2025] — the single biggest brake on the domestic mass market.

The stimulus window

The package cuts transfer and mortgage-registration fees to 0.01% for homes ≤THB 7m, now extended to mid-2027 [MOF 2026], while the central bank relaxed loan-to-value to 100% [BOT 2025]. Time-boxed relief targets Bangkok owner-occupiers; the foreign-quota reform stayed on the shelf.

Section 05 · Bangkok Overhang
009 / 018

Launches at a 15-year low, stock still building

Two clocks diverge. New launches have collapsed as developers protect balance sheets, while unsold stock keeps rising because absorption is slower than the destocking — the market is grinding down inventory, not re-accelerating.

Supply pullback

Only about 13,700 new condominium units launched in Bangkok in the first nine months of 2025, against a 2014–2024 average near 52,000 a year [REIC 2025]. CBRE sees new Bangkok condo supply falling below 40,000 units in 2026 and toward ~20,000 in 2027 [CBRE 2026] as the sector enters a deliberate defensive mode.

Rising unsold stock

Even with the launch freeze, BMR unsold condominium stock is forecast near 97,900 units worth ~THB 488 bn at end-2025, up from ~93,000 [REIC 2025]. New projects sold just ~32% of units within six months of launch in 2024, down from 45% in 2022 [REIC 2025] — supply discipline buys time but has not yet cleared the backlog.

The vacancy signal

Beyond unsold stock, completed but empty units weigh on the market: nationwide vacant housing is estimated near 1.64 million units [Thansettakij 2025]. In CBD Bangkok, condominium rental vacancy runs an elevated 18–22% [Colliers 2025], capping the investor case for new mid-tier supply.

Section 05 · Segment Economics
010 / 018

A two-speed city: resilient top, oversupplied middle

The Bangkok glut is concentrated, not general. Luxury and super-luxury clear at 80%+ sales rates; the THB 100k–150k/sqm mid-tier and sub-THB 3m mass market along suburban rail are where unsold inventory and mortgage rejection collide.

The resilient top

Downtown and branded stock is insulated by cash-rich domestic and returning-expat demand. Super-luxury and luxury towers post sales rates above 80%, with branded launches near a 93% take-up on existing supply and downtown asking prices seen up to +15% in 2026 on a thin, differentiated pipeline [CBRE 2026].

The oversupplied middle

The mid-tier — THB 100k–150k/sqm — is the pressure point. Suburban rail corridors (outer Sukhumvit, Bang Na, Rama 9, the Green and Purple line extensions) hold the bulk of unsold condominium stock, where absorption now runs years rather than quarters [Kira estimates].

The mass-market squeeze

Below THB 100k/sqm and under THB 3m per unit, demand exists but credit does not: mortgage rejection there reached ~40%, up to 70% at the entry tier [Terra 2025]. Sub-THB 3m condominiums are precisely the segment REIC flags as most oversupplied [REIC 2025] — where the fee window helps but cannot substitute for a mortgage approval.

Section 06 · Phuket Demand
012 / 018

Foreign capital and record tourism power the island

Phuket runs on demand Bangkok cannot access. Foreign buyers take ~60% of prime deals, tourism hit record volumes, and constrained land keeps prices climbing — the island's floor is external capital, not domestic credit.

Investment scale

Over 2021–2025 Phuket recorded 45,066 newly launched residential units worth ~THB 470 bn (~USD 13 bn) [TH-Property 2025]. In 2025 alone, 72+ projects added over 10,300 units worth ~THB 82 bn [Colliers 2026]. Prices are forecast up 8–10% through 2026, outpacing Bangkok's 5–7% [Colliers 2026].

The buyer mix

Foreign buyers dominate, taking ~60% of prime villa and condo deals [Colliers 2026]. Russians and CIS buyers are ~25–30% of off-plan sales and 40–60% of pool villas; Europeans ~25%; Thai and Southeast Asian ~20%; a fast-growing Middle Eastern segment ~10% [Reloc8 2025]. Chinese demand favours new condominiums.

The tourism engine

Phuket International handled a record ~17.4 million passengers in 2025, over its design capacity [AoT 2025], within Thailand's 33 million arrivals and a 39–40 million 2026 target [TAT 2026]. Phase II expansion (+44% by 2030) and the planned Andaman airport underwrite long-run demand [AoT 2025].

Section 06 · Phuket Segments
013 / 018

Villas lead, branded residences command the premium

Phuket's growth concentrates in the west-coast prime belt. Villas outperform on price and privacy; hotel-branded residences fetch the highest premiums; beach-proximate condominiums carry the freehold-friendly foreign volume — each on a distinct ownership structure.

Villas: the outperformer

Villa sales rose about 13% in 2025, with prime-area prices up 15–18% as affluent buyers favour space and privacy [Colliers 2026]. Bang Tao averages ~THB 284k/sqm and Layan ~THB 197k/sqm; Bang Tao land reaches ~THB 80m per rai, with Layan land appreciating 12–18% a year [Nation 2026].

Branded residences

Hotel-brand residences — Banyan Tree, Anantara, Marriott, Rosewood — anchor the ultra-prime tier with rental management and lifestyle facilities. Banyan Tree's Oceanus at Bang Tao offers units of 416–768 sqm [Banyan 2026]; Botanica's luxury villas booked ~THB 4.8 bn of 2025 sales, +30% across 13 new projects [Botanica 2025].

Ownership & yield

Condominiums remain the simplest freehold route (foreign quota ≤49% of a building); villas use leasehold or Thai-company structures, with nominee arrangements under tighter enforcement since 2024 [Siam Legal 2025]. Managed prime villas report net yields of ~6–8% [SKHAI 2026], supporting the investment case.

Section 10 · Technology in the Channel
016 / 018

Digital channels and AI tools reshape buyer acquisition

In a two-speed market, distribution efficiency matters differently on each side. Over 60% of property transactions now begin online [KenResearch 2025]; developers deploy AI-assisted tools to clear Bangkok mid-tier stock and to reach thinner, more international Phuket demand at lower cost.

The channel shift

Thailand's property-portal and proptech market is valued near USD 1.2 bn [KenResearch 2025], with over 60% of transactions starting online — a structural move favouring developers and agencies with digital reach into foreign buyer pools, especially for Phuket's Russian, European and Middle Eastern demand.

AI in the sales funnel

Adoption clusters around four uses: AI-driven buyer matching and lead scoring, automated multilingual enquiry handling, virtual tours and 3D walkthroughs for remote overseas inspection, and data-led pricing and absorption analytics. Each compresses acquisition cost where every qualified buyer is harder won.

Where it pays

The return is sharpest in two places: the oversupplied Bangkok mid-tier, where marketing efficiency decides absorption speed, and cross-border Phuket sales, where virtual tours and multilingual handling extend reach to diversifying foreign buyers. The technology does not create demand — it lowers the cost of finding and converting it.

Methodology & sources

How the numbers were derived, and the full citation for every named source used in this view.

Methodology

This view triangulates two evidence streams. Where a figure carries a named source tag (e.g. [REIC 2025], [Colliers 2026]), the underlying data comes from that source; the full citation appears at right. Where a figure carries [Kira estimates], KIRA analysts derived it from a blend of disclosed inputs — land-office transfer records, listed-developer disclosure, consultancy datasets — using the method disclosed in the relevant section.

The Bangkok mid-tier share of unsold stock, the Phuket buyer-nationality mix, area price premia, and the 2028 scenario fan are KIRA constructions reconciled against published transfer and survey data. Approximately 25% of quantitative claims are KIRA-derived; the remainder are externally cited. Research drew on both English and Thai-language sources; local sources are cited via English aliases for traceability.
