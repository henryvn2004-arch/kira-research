Revenue holds while volume erodes

Japan generated roughly USD 11 bn in mobile IAP revenue over the twelve months to July 2025 [Sensor Tower 2025], second only to China's iOS market in Asia, even as revenue slipped 2.2% and downloads fell 2.5% to 628 mn. The market is no longer growing on users; it is being held up by retention and monetization of a loyal, high-spending base — an economy of depth, not reach.

Regulation reprices the gacha engine

In mid-2025 the Consumer Affairs Agency finalized updated guidance mandating explicit probability display for all banner tiers, with sub-rarity breakdowns, across every storefront operating in Japan [CAA 2025]. Combined with JOGA/CESA self-regulation, transparency is now table stakes — and because operators rarely region-split, Japan's rules effectively set a global standard for gacha disclosure.

02 · Strategic Implications 05 / 15
Five strategic implications for market participants

Derived from the structural trends in this report — relevant to domestic publishers, overseas developers entering Japan, IP holders, and platform and payment partners operating in the market.

01 · DISCLOSURE
Build transparency into design, not as a patch
Treat probability display, pity ceilings, and rate-up clarity as product features, not compliance overhead. Operators that surfaced odds early carried it globally; late movers face rework across every storefront. Disclosure that reads as fairness can lift trust and retention in a market where 40%+ of players never spend.
Anchor: all banner tiers now require sub-rarity odds [CAA 2025]
02 · IP
Anchor launches to durable IP, not novelty
Anime- and manga-tethered titles deliver the most reliable, highest-value revenue in Japan. A 40-year-old franchise can anchor a new title to ~USD 300 mn in year one [Kira estimates]. Original IP without a media flywheel faces a structural acquisition-cost disadvantage; license or partner before building from zero.
Anchor: USD 2.5 bn+ lifetime, one franchise, JP alone [Kira estimates]
03 · DEPTH
Defend the high-ARPU spender, not the install
With downloads in structural decline, growth lives in spend per user. Japan's ARPU of ~USD 579 is roughly four times the APAC average [Statista 2025], and 55+ players are the most likely high spenders. Live-ops cadence, whale care, and event design matter more than user-acquisition volume.
Anchor: ~4x APAC average ARPU [Statista 2025]
04 · COMPETITION
Close the production-value gap or cede ground
Chinese and Korean studios reset player expectations with high-budget, anime-styled live-service titles. Domestic incumbents that under-invest in production risk slow share erosion even on home IP. Co-development or selective acquisition may beat organic catch-up on the 2026–2028 horizon.
Anchor: −2.2% domestic revenue, rising import pressure [Sensor Tower 2025]
05 · EXPORT
Export the IP, not just the build
The strongest domestic titles now travel: card-battler and franchise titles posted material overseas revenue in 2025. Japan still keeps ~70% of domestic IAP with home publishers [Sensor Tower 2025], but the upside lies in disciplined global rollouts of proven IP rather than defending a shrinking download base.
Anchor: ~70% domestic IAP retained by JP publishers [Sensor Tower 2025]

An economy of depth

Japan delivers roughly 9–11.5% of global mobile-game revenue from about 2% of the world's players [Sensor Tower 2025]. ARPU reached approximately USD 579 in 2025 [Statista 2025] — the highest of any major market, near four times the APAC average. The market is sustained by a minority of committed spenders, not by breadth of installs.

Volume is in slow structural decline. Annual downloads sit near 628 mn, down 2.5% YoY and below the 2020 peak [Sensor Tower 2025], while domestic IAP holds above ¥1.6 trn [4Gamer 2025]. iOS drives the majority of high-value spend; Google Play contributes steadier, lower-value volume.

The spender curve

Over 40% of mobile players spend nothing; paying users spend roughly ¥10,200 a month on average [Statista 2025]. Spend skews older — players aged 55+ are the most likely to be heavy spenders, with the highest share buying USD 500+ a year [Kira estimates based on spender surveys].

Demographic gravity

Japan's aging population reshapes the demand curve. The most reliable revenue increasingly comes from long-tenured players who age with their titles, not from youth acquisition. This rewards franchises with a decade of equity and penalizes titles dependent on a constant inflow of new, low-LTV installs.

The structural risk is concentration: a maturing base means revenue is anchored to a shrinking, aging cohort of high spenders. Operators face a long-horizon question of cohort replacement — whether younger players can be converted to comparable lifetime value, or whether per-user economics quietly compress as the base ages.

Outlook to 2030

Our analysts expect domestic revenue to hold broadly flat to modestly lower in real terms — a ~0 to 2% annual drift through 2030 absent a breakout title [Kira estimates], with reported-USD figures swinging on the yen. Growth, where it exists, will come from overseas IP exports rather than the domestic install base.

04 · Franchise Economics 08 / 15
IP-led franchise economics: the durable revenue moat

In Japan, anime- and manga-based titles generate the most reliable, highest-value revenue. IP loyalty is the moat — a proven franchise de-risks user acquisition and underwrites years of live-ops monetization that original IP rarely matches.

Why IP wins

Pokémon TCG Pocket reached the top revenue spot in Japan in 2025 and ~USD 990 mn in global IAP for the year [Kira estimates], crossing USD 1 bn faster than Pokémon GO. Japan was its single largest market. The lesson: a beloved IP collapses acquisition cost and re-engages lapsed fans on launch day.

Durability is the second edge. Uma Musume Pretty Derby has earned more than USD 2.5 bn since 2021 in Japan alone [Kira estimates], with the home market supplying ~90% of revenue. Fate/Grand Order, years past launch, still cleared ~USD 321 mn in 2025 [Kira estimates]. These are multi-year annuities, not one-off hits.

The competitive twist

The highest-grossing anime mobile titles of 2025 increasingly come from Chinese studios — Honkai: Star Rail led the anime category at ~USD 421 mn [Kira estimates], applying Japanese aesthetic conventions at higher production budgets. Domestic IP remains a moat, but execution quality is now contested.

06 · Title Lifecycle 11 / 15
Top titles: the lifecycle read behind the charts

New peaks, faster decay

The top of the 2025 chart mixed enduring franchises with new arrivals. Pokémon TCG Pocket took the #1 revenue position in Japan [Sensor Tower 2025] and drove a ~305% YoY surge in the card-battler genre, while strategy titles such as Last War: Survival and Whiteout Survival climbed the rankings — a foreign-developed cohort capturing Japanese wallets.

But novelty decays. Pokémon TCG Pocket's player count and revenue cooled materially through 2025 after its launch spike [Kira estimates], a reminder that even category-defining launches normalize. The durable revenue lives in titles that have already survived the decay curve.

The survivors

Monster Strike (Mixi), Fate/Grand Order, and Dragon Ball Z Dokkan Battle remain top-tier years after launch — long-tail annuities sustained by event cadence and IP affection rather than acquisition spend. Their persistence is the clearest evidence that retention, not reach, is the Japanese model.

Genre rotation

  • Card battlers surged on Pokémon TCG Pocket — fastest-growing category in 2025.
  • Gacha-RPG remains the structural core — Fate/Grand Order, Uma Musume, Monster Strike.
  • 4X / survival strategy rose sharply via foreign titles, broadening the spender base beyond traditional RPG fans.
  • Sports and franchise sims (eFootball, Dokkan) carry domestic IP into overseas markets.

What it means for 2026

The competitive question is no longer whether domestic IP can monetize — it demonstrably does — but whether incumbents can match the production cadence and global rollout discipline of Chinese and Korean studios. Titles that combine durable Japanese IP with global-standard production and transparent monetization are the 2026 winners.

For new entrants, the lifecycle read is unambiguous: budget for a launch spike followed by normalization, and engineer live-ops depth that earns the multi-year annuity rather than chasing the peak.

07 · Gacha Regulation 13 / 15
Gacha regulation: from the 2012 ban to mandatory disclosure

Japan governs gacha through the Act against Unjustifiable Premiums and Misleading Representations (Premiums Display Act), enforced by the Consumer Affairs Agency, plus industry self-regulation from JOGA and CESA. The 2025 disclosure update is the most consequential change since the 2012 "complete gacha" ban.

08 · Outlook 14 / 15
Five-year outlook: flat at home, growth through IP exports

A mature, high-ARPU market with declining volume and tightening disclosure. The base case is broadly flat domestic revenue; upside and downside hinge on import competition, regulatory ripple, and how well domestic IP travels.

Base case — flat depth

Domestic IAP holds near USD 11 bn through 2030 [Kira estimates]: a loyal, high-ARPU base offsets volume decline. Disclosure adds compliance cost but does not materially dent spend, since transparency in a trust-sensitive market can support retention.

Upside — IP travels

If domestic studios pair durable IP with global-standard production and disciplined overseas rollout, total franchise revenue can rise toward USD 13 bn-equivalent by 2030 [Kira estimates], driven by exports rather than the home install base.

Downside — ceded ground

If incumbents under-invest against Chinese and Korean production, and the yen weakens reported USD, domestic revenue could drift toward USD 8.5 bn by 2030 [Kira estimates] as import titles take share of the spender wallet.

Methodology endnote and source key

How to read our tags

This view triangulates two evidence streams. Where a figure carries a named source tag (e.g. [Sensor Tower 2025], [Statista 2025]), the underlying data comes from that source; the full citation appears in the source list. Where a figure carries [Kira estimates], KIRA analysts derived the number from a blend of disclosed inputs — store-grossing reads, operator filings, and regulatory publications — using the method disclosed in the relevant section.

Approximately 40% of quantitative claims are KIRA-derived; 60% are externally cited. Title-level revenue figures rely on app-intelligence reads, which are estimates of gross player spend and are treated as KIRA triangulation where no operator disclosure confirms them. All USD figures are period-average conversions; reported revenue in USD is sensitive to JPY movement.
