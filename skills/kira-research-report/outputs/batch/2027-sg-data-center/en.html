Singapore's data center market — a 2027 allocation test

A USD 4.2 bn market built on 700+ MW of live capacity now grows by permit, not by land. A green-energy gate, not demand, sets how much new hyperscale capacity actually lands — and pushes the remainder offshore.

The setup

Singapore's data center market was worth about USD 4.2 bn in 2024, on a path toward ~USD 5.6 bn by 2030 [Operator filings 2025], on a live base above 700 MW with the lowest colocation vacancy in the region, near 1.4% [Structure Research 2024]. After the 2019–2022 build pause, supply is now released in deliberate tranches rather than left to the market.

The gate

The constraint is policy, not land. The Green Data Centre Roadmap released at least 300 MW near-term plus 200 MW more for green deployments [IMDA GDCR 2024], and the DC-CFA2 call (Dec 2025) allocates 200+ MW under a 50% green-energy mandate and a 1.25 PUE bar [IMDA-EDB 2025]. Capacity that cannot clear the gate goes to Johor and Batam.

Five strategic implications for market participants

Five forces govern who captures Singapore's rationed build-out through 2027. Each is anchored to documented data and KIRA synthesis.

01 · POLICY
The green gate is the binding constraint
Capacity is released by call, not by demand. DC-CFA2 puts 200+ MW behind a 50% green-energy mandate and a 1.25 PUE bar [IMDA-EDB 2025]. Sustainability credentials, not capital, decide who lands. Allocation, not land, sets the 2027 ceiling.
Anchor: PUE 1.25 DC-CFA2 bar
02 · POWER
A 7% power ceiling caps onshore scale
Data centers already drew about 7% of national electricity [IMDA GDCR 2024]. Onshore growth depends on imported low-carbon power — a target of 6 GW by 2035 [EMA 2025]. Power, not building, throttles incremental MW.
Anchor: 6 GW 2035 import target
03 · SPILL-OVER
Johor absorbs what cannot land
Johor live supply grew ~145% a year, 2019–2024 [Structure Research 2024], with a 2,000+ MW pipeline. Singapore keeps the high-value core; bulk and training capacity migrates 30 km north. Treat the two as one metro.
Anchor: ~145% Johor CAGR
04 · CROSS-BORDER
JS-SEZ formalizes the twin-hub
The Johor–Singapore Special Economic Zone (Jan 2025) frames a single cross-border corridor [Reed Smith 2025]. Latency-sensitive nodes stay in Singapore; capacity-hungry workloads anchor in Sedenak and Nusajaya. A structural split, not a loss.
Anchor: Jan 2025 JS-SEZ signed
05 · DEMAND
AI and sovereignty are the demand floor
High-density AI racks and a trusted-hub positioning keep utilization near full; vacancy sits at ~1.4%, the lowest in APAC [Structure Research 2024]. Demand is structural; the question is purely how much supply policy will release. A scarcity premium holds.
Anchor: ~1.4% vacancy rate

Section 04 · Market Sizing
007 / 017

Revenue compounds while capacity is rationed

Two clocks run at different speeds. Installed capacity inches up under allocation; market value compounds faster as AI-grade racks, high density, and scarcity premiums lift revenue per MW.

Revenue curve

The market was worth about USD 4.2 bn in 2024, projected near USD 5.6 bn by 2030 (~5% CAGR) [Operator filings 2025]. Colocation, the largest segment, was around USD 2.2 bn in 2024 on a similar path [Operator filings 2025] — growth is value-led, not volume-led.

Capacity curve

Live capacity sits above 700 MW at full build [Structure Research 2024]. New supply arrives in policy tranches: ~500 MW released across the GDCR and the two calls [IMDA GDCR 2024]. [Kira estimates] put realized 2027 onshore additions well below latent demand.

Scarcity premium

Colocation vacancy is about 1.4%, the lowest in APAC [Structure Research 2024]. Tight supply holds rents and utilization near the ceiling; Singapore trades volume for value and a trusted-hub premium.

Section 04 · Demand Drivers
008 / 017

Three demand engines: AI compute, cloud anchoring, sovereignty

Demand is the structurally easy side. AI-grade compute, a deep cloud and finance base, and a trusted-hub positioning each pull capacity in — and reinforce each other against a fixed supply.

AI compute

DC-CFA2 explicitly ties allocation to Singapore's value as a trusted hub for AI, requiring applicants to show AI in operations [IMDA-EDB 2025]. AI racks draw far more power per cabinet, lifting demand for retrofit and high-density white space far faster than floor area — sharpening the power question on page 010.

Cloud anchoring

Singapore is the regional control plane for global cloud and finance. The top five operators hold roughly 65% of installed capacity [Structure Research 2024], anchoring availability zones that tenants will not casually relocate. Latency-sensitive and regulated workloads stay onshore.

Sovereignty & trust

A strong data-protection and connectivity regime makes Singapore the default node for compliance-sensitive workloads across the region. This converts trust into durable colocation demand that is independent of any single AI-investment cycle, holding vacancy near 1.4% [Structure Research 2024].

Section 05 · Power & Sustainability
010 / 017

A 7% power ceiling, a 1.3 PUE target, and imported clean power

The binding question is not floor space but where firm, low-carbon power comes from. The roadmap caps efficiency at the source and routes growth through imported renewables.

The ceiling

Data centers already consumed about 7% of national electricity [IMDA GDCR 2024]. The 2019–2022 moratorium existed precisely to contain that share; growth now must come from efficiency and clean supply, not unconstrained connection.

The efficiency gate

The Green Data Centre Roadmap targets PUE ≤ 1.3 at 100% IT load over a decade [IMDA GDCR 2024]; DC-CFA2 tightens that to 1.25 at full load, with Green Mark DC 2024 Platinum and SS 715:2025 equipment standards [IMDA-EDB 2025]. Efficiency is a precondition to allocation.

The clean-power route

Singapore targets up to 6 GW of low-carbon electricity imports by 2035, from 4 GW in 2021 [EMA 2025], having begun with a 100 MW Lao hydro pilot. Direct green procurement — not unbundled certificates — is what now satisfies the DC-CFA2 mandate.

Section 05 · Capacity Allocation
011 / 017

From a pilot to a power play: how the MW are rationed

Allocation has evolved from a small pilot to a deliberate instrument. Each round raises the green bar — the 80 MW pilot became a 200+ MW call gated on 50% green energy and a tighter PUE.

The pilot

After lifting the pause, the first DC-CFA awarded about 80 MW to four operators — Equinix, GDS, Microsoft, and an AirTrunk–ByteDance consortium [IMDA-EDB 2023]. It established selectivity: capacity is a privilege earned on efficiency and economic contribution.

The roadmap

The Green Data Centre Roadmap (May 2024) released at least 300 MW near-term, plus a further 200 MW for green-energy deployments [IMDA GDCR 2024]. It paired capacity with Green Mark DC 2024 and equipment-efficiency standards by 2025.

The call

DC-CFA2 (opened Dec 2025, closed Mar 2026) allocates 200+ MW under a 50% green-energy mandate, PUE 1.25, Platinum certification, and an AI-in-operations test [IMDA-EDB 2025]. Unbundled certificates do not qualify — only direct clean procurement.

Section 06 · The Spill-Over
013 / 017

Johor and Batam absorb the overflow; JS-SEZ formalizes it

Singapore's restraint is Johor's boom. Cheaper land and power, abundant grid, and a formal SEZ turn the constraint into a twin-hub corridor — the high-value core stays south; bulk capacity anchors north.

Johor's surge

Johor live supply grew about 145% a year, 2019–2024, the fastest in Southeast Asia [Structure Research 2024]. A pipeline above 2,000 MW sits across Sedenak and Nusajaya tech parks; Malaysia drew roughly USD 27 bn of approved DC investment, 2021–2023 [SCMP 2024].

The JS-SEZ corridor

The Johor–Singapore Special Economic Zone (signed Jan 2025) frames a single cross-border hub [Reed Smith 2025]. The 7,300-acre Ibrahim Technopolis and Sedenak anchor capacity-hungry workloads; Singapore keeps low-latency and regulated nodes.

The structural split

Treat Singapore and Johor as one metro with two roles. AI training and bulk storage migrate north for power and land; inference, finance, and sovereign workloads stay south. [Kira estimates] put most net-new regional MW landing in Johor through 2027.

Methodology

This view triangulates two evidence streams. Where a figure carries [Kira estimates], KIRA analysts derived it from a blend of disclosed inputs — allocation texts, operator disclosure, agency roadmaps — using the method disclosed in the relevant section.

Capacity and value figures reconcile published infrastructure datasets against operator disclosure; the released-versus-demand gap and the 2027 scenario fan are KIRA constructions. Approximately 25% of quantitative claims are KIRA-derived; the remainder are externally cited.
