Demand has cleared the tipping point.

BEV registrations in Malaysia grew from 274 units in 2021 to 44,800 in 2025 — a 163× volume expansion in four years [MAA 2026]. Q1 2026 data confirmed the trajectory is intact: 14,591 units registered in the first three months, a 113.7% year-on-year gain [JPJ 2026]. The market is no longer experimental; it is structural.

The policy architecture is pivoting to local assembly.

Full import and excise duty exemptions for CBU EVs expired on 31 December 2025. From January 2026, CBU imports face standard tariffs — 30% import duty plus 10% excise duty plus 10% sales tax [MITI 2025]. CKD models retain full exemption through 2027, creating a hard localisation incentive. Proton's Tanjung Malim EV plant and BYD's interim CKD licence are the first movers under this new regime.

Chinese OEMs lead on volume; premium players face margin pressure.

BYD and Chery ranked among the top-six makes in overall Malaysian car sales in 2025 for the first time [MAA 2026]. Five of the ten best-selling EVs in 2025 were BYD models. Tesla's 7,282 deliveries and BMW's 2,700+ electrified units confirm the premium tier, but CBU tariff exposure forces price increases that could compress 2026–27 volumes.

Five implications for market participants

The CKD pivot, Chinese OEM surge, and infrastructure shortfall each carry distinct strategic consequences. Below are the five reads our analysts consider most actionable through 2027.

01 ·
Localise or face structural price disadvantage.
CBU tariff exposure — ~50% combined duty — makes imported EV pricing untenable below RM200,000. Brands without a CKD path face margin compression or market exit. The window to secure Tanjung Malim-type assembly agreements closes as Proton and BYD claim production slots first.
Anchor: CBU duty = 30% import + 10% excise + 10% ST [MITI 2025]
02 ·
Position below RM150k — mass-market is where volume lives.
Over 85% of Malaysian households cannot absorb the typical RM180–280k CBU EV price point without policy support [Kira estimates]. CKD models priced at RM100–150k — BYD Atto 2, Proton eMas 5 — capture the growth stratum. Premium brands should plan volume realistically and defend margin, not chase units.
Anchor: EV share >6.95% at premium; mass CKD to drive penetration above 10% [JPJ 2026]
03 ·
Anticipate charging density as the binding constraint.
Malaysia had 5,360 public charge points at end-2025 against a 10,000-unit roadmap target — a 46% shortfall. Range anxiety remains the top stated adoption barrier. Operators with DC fast-charger strings along the NSE and in East Malaysia gain structural first-mover advantage as EV density rises.
Anchor: 5,360 vs 10,000 target [EC 2025]
04 ·
Hedge against Proton e.MAS volume normalisation.
Proton's e.MAS 7 outsold the second-ranked BYD Sealion 7 by nearly 2× in 2025 [paultan.org 2026], partly on national-brand premium and launch incentives. As launch excitement fades and BYD's CKD ramp enters volume phase, the competitive equation rebalances. Distributors should model inventory levels conservatively from Q3 2026.

Section 04 · Macro & automotive context
007 / 022

Automotive market context: a large, incumbency-heavy base.

Total industry volume (TIV) is projected at ~790,000 units in 2026, a modest contraction from the 799,000 recorded in 2025 [MAA 2026]. Proton and Perodua together account for roughly 60% of TIV — a national-brand dominance that sets the competitive frame for any EV challenger.

Automotive market structure

Malaysia's TIV is supported by household income growth and favourable auto-loan availability, but median household income of RM7,900/month [DOSM 2023] creates a structural ceiling on EV affordability without active incentives. The national car brands — Proton and Perodua — hold their position via sub-RM80k pricing and a Bumiputera policy framework that gives them structural distribution advantages.

The EV segment until 2025 was overwhelmingly concentrated in the RM130–300k CBU band — a price point accessible to roughly the top quintile of earners. The CKD transition aims to shift this band down to RM100–150k, which addresses the upper-middle income tier representing approximately 25–30% of households [Kira estimates].

Geographic concentration

EV charging density and early-adopter registrations are heavily Klang Valley-weighted. Selangor and KL together account for an estimated 65–70% of BEV registrations [Kira estimates based on JPJ data]. East Malaysia (Sabah, Sarawak) has minimal charging infrastructure and contributes negligible EV volume — a structural gap the TNB Electron Sabah expansion in 2026 begins to address.

xEV market segmentation 2025

The broader electrified vehicle market is a layered stack. Full BEVs (44,800 units) are now matched by a growing hybrid tier, as consumers not yet ready to commit to full electric adopt HEV and PHEV as stepping stones — particularly relevant in the mass-market segment where charging anxiety remains acute.

HEV and PHEV are tracking up as an affordability bridge. Total xEV share of TIV is estimated at 11–12% in 2025 [Kira estimates], suggesting broader electrification momentum beyond the BEV headline figure.

Section 06 · Government policy & incentives
010 / 022

From broad exemptions to targeted localisation support.

The policy architecture has executed a deliberate pivot: broad CBU duty holidays (2022–2025) seeded awareness; targeted CKD exemptions (2026–2027) are seeding local industry. The transition was anticipated but operationally disruptive for import-dependent brands.

EEV (energy-efficient vehicle) framework

Malaysia's National Automotive Policy designates full BEVs as the highest tier of the EEV framework. This status unlocks additional investment incentives for manufacturers — pioneer status, investment tax allowances, and preferential MIDA approval — beyond the consumer-facing duty waivers. Proton's Tanjung Malim plant qualified under this regime to receive industrial support for its EV assembly line [MIDA 2026].

Key risk: the 2028 excise duty cliff

The current CKD exemption expires 31 December 2027. Post-2028 excise duty on locally assembled EVs has not been gazetted. Market participants modelling 2028+ pricing face material uncertainty. An excise rate above 10% would reprice the sub-RM150k CKD band upward by RM15,000–25,000 — potentially suppressing mass-market adoption precisely as penetration approaches the critical 10% threshold [Kira estimates]. The lobby window is concentrated in 2026–early 2027.

Position and trajectory

BYD entered Malaysia in 2023 through Sime Darby Motors and reached the top of the EV volume table within two years. Five of the ten best-selling EVs in Malaysia in 2025 were BYD models — Sealion 7, Atto 3, Atto 2, M6 (MPV), and Seal 6 [paultan.org 2026]. The breadth of the model lineup, covering compact SUV, sedan, and MPV segments at distinct price points, is the brand's structural moat against single-model challengers.

BYD secured an interim manufacturing licence in September 2025, with production located at Tanjung Malim — the same industrial zone as Proton's main plant. The licence structure requires that 80% of CKD output be exported, with domestic sales capped at 10,000 units annually. This architecture protects Malaysia's duty advantage for the export allocation while creating a predictable domestic quota [BYD/MITI 2025].

2027 outlook

BYD's domestic ceiling of 10,000 CKD units creates a deliberate volume cap that Proton e.MAS is positioned to absorb. Whether BYD renegotiates this cap — or supplements with additional CBU allocations at duty-bearing prices — is the primary medium-term uncertainty for the brand's Malaysian market-share trajectory.

Model lineup and price positioning

  • BYD Atto 2 — compact SUV, est. RM139,000 (CKD). Entry-level volume driver for the mass-market tier.
  • BYD Atto 3 — mid-size SUV, est. RM149,000–165,000. Highest cumulative volume through 2024.
  • BYD Sealion 7 — performance SUV, ~RM180,000. #2 EV in Malaysia 2025 by units.
  • BYD Seal 6 — mid-size sedan, ~RM160,000. Targets the premium-affordable crossover segment.
  • BYD M6 — electric MPV. First Chinese EV MPV at volume in Malaysia; appeals to multi-passenger households.

Competitive risks

  • Domestic cap of 10,000 CKD units constrains upside absent renegotiation.
  • Proton e.MAS captures national-brand premium that BYD cannot replicate.
  • Chery CKD entry would compress the RM100–150k segment.
  • Residual value uncertainty across Chinese EV brands could suppress upgrade cycle demand by 2027.

The national EV play

Proton's EV strategy is structurally different from any other brand competing in Malaysia: it is the only player that can simultaneously claim CKD duty benefits, national-brand loyalty, and Geely's scale economics. The e.MAS 7 outsold the second-ranked BYD Sealion 7 by nearly 2× in 2025 [paultan.org 2026], a performance driven partly by launch euphoria but also by the genuine affordability gap that CKD pricing creates relative to comparable CBU imports.

The e.MAS 7 is built on Geely's GMA (Global Modular Architecture) platform with LFP (lithium iron phosphate) battery chemistry. Battery options are 49.52 kWh (345 km range) and 60.22 kWh (410 km range) [Proton 2024]. The eMas 5, launched October 2025, addresses the compact crossover segment with Standard Range (30.12 kWh) and Long Range (40.16 kWh) variants — broadening Proton's price ladder toward the RM120k entry point.

Risk: post-launch normalisation

Launch-phase demand curves compress over 12–18 months. As BYD's domestic CKD volume ramps and Chery's CKD path materialises, Proton's market-share advantage narrows. The brand must deliver on aftersales quality and charging network access to retain loyalty beyond initial buyers.

Platform architecture and Geely dependency

The GMA platform is shared with Geely's global lineup, giving Proton engineering scale it could not fund domestically. However, the arrangement means Malaysian EV design is substantively Chinese — a political dynamic that MITI and the national automotive press are increasingly noting. Proton's differentiation is brand equity, local pricing, and government alignment, not independent technology.

2026–27 product roadmap

  • e.MAS 5 full CKD ramp at Tanjung Malim through 2026 — addresses the RM100–130k price band most directly.
  • Third e.MAS model reportedly in development on GMA platform; possible 2027 reveal.
  • Tanjung Malim plant expansion to accommodate joint Proton/BYD CKD output — potential production bottleneck if both brands ramp simultaneously.
  • Fleet and government procurement — Proton is well-positioned for public-sector EV adoption mandates that Malaysia has signalled for 2027.

Geely partnership context

Geely's 49.9% stake (acquired 2017) deepened into an active engineering collaboration from 2023 onward. The partnership gives Proton access to Geely's global supply chain, battery procurement scale, and software-defined vehicle capabilities — the same architecture underpinning Volvo's EX lineup and Zeekr.

Key infrastructure metrics

Structural gaps

  • East Malaysia (Sabah, Sarawak) has minimal DC fast infrastructure — a functional barrier to EV adoption in Borneo.
  • NSE (North-South Expressway) DC density is insufficient for 400km+ range reassurance; Gentari's NSE rollout is the most material single intervention in progress.
  • Apartment and landed-terrace home charging penetration is low: 30.7% of consumers report uncertainty about home charger installation [Oppotus 2025].

Grid investment underpinning

TNB plans a RM35 billion grid upgrade investment 2025–2030 for energy transition, on top of RM54 billion in non-ET capital — a combined RM90 billion commitment to Malaysia's electricity infrastructure [TNB 2026]. This provides the physical grid capacity backstop for the 10,000-charger target and beyond.

Section 09 · Consumer adoption — barriers & enablers
018 / 022

Awareness is near-universal; conversion remains price-filtered.

EV awareness among Malaysian consumers reached 99% by Q1 2025 — up from 93% in Q4 2024 [Oppotus 2025]. Familiarity (hands-on engagement) jumped from 52% to 69% in the same period. The gap between awareness and purchase intention is driven by structural, not perceptual, barriers.

Adoption barriers

Affordability — the primary filter
The sub-RM100k segment is where Perodua sells 250,000+ units annually. No EV currently competes at this price point. Even with CKD exemptions, the lowest BEV sits at ~RM120–139k — a 30–50% premium above mass-market ICE alternatives. Price is structural, not perceptual [Oppotus 2025, Kira estimates].
Range anxiety and charging access
Public DC fast-charger density is 46% below target. Concerns about charging inconvenience and highway coverage remain the most-cited barrier after price. 30.7% of prospective buyers express uncertainty about home charger installation [Oppotus 2025].
Apartment and strata living constraints
Over 40% of urban Malaysian households live in apartments or condominiums where individual charging installation requires management-body approval — a process that remains cumbersome and inconsistently governed. Without standardised strata EV charging regulations, this segment cannot convert.
Residual value uncertainty
Used-car buyers and hire-purchase lenders apply conservative residual value assumptions to EVs — particularly Chinese-branded models with limited resale history in Malaysia. This suppresses effective purchasing power by inflating the all-in financing cost.

Adoption enablers

CKD price reduction — the key catalyst
Every RM10,000 reduction in CKD EV pricing expands the addressable buyer pool materially. The Proton eMas 5 at ~RM120k and potential Chery CKD entry below RM120k represent the next step-change. Sub-RM100k BEV is the conversion threshold for the mass-market tier [Kira estimates].
RM2,500 annual charger tax relief
Government income tax relief for home charger installation (through 2027) reduces the effective first-year ownership cost by RM2,500. Combined with RM3,000 rebate, the home charging setup is economically close to neutral for income-tax-paying buyers.
Fuel subsidy rationalisation
Malaysia's RON95 petrol subsidy is under review. Any reduction in petrol subsidies narrows the per-km running cost gap between ICE and EV — the most direct economic argument for EV conversion among the cost-conscious mass market.
Hybrid as an on-ramp
HEV and PHEV sales are growing as consumers hedge between ICE comfort and EV economics. Toyota, Honda, and Proton hybrids at sub-RM130k price points are familiarising buyers with electrification — creating a migration pathway to BEV in the 2027–2030 window.

Section 11 · Strategic implications for market participants
021 / 022

Strategic implications: five reads for 2027 planning.

Malaysia's EV transition is moving faster than the infrastructure and policy framework can cleanly accommodate. The five reads below reflect where our analysts see the asymmetric opportunities and risks through 2027.

For EV manufacturers and importers

The CKD window is narrow and competitive. Tanjung Malim has limited assembly capacity; Proton and BYD are first-movers. Brands that delay CKD negotiations beyond 2026 face either CBU pricing at a structural disadvantage or a multi-year wait for alternative assembly capacity. Chery's position — in active discussion but not yet contracted — illustrates the stakes. Every quarter of delay is equivalent to RM10,000–15,000 in effective competitive disadvantage at the consumer price point [Kira estimates].

Price the mass market, not the premium buyer. The 80th percentile of Malaysian car buyers purchases at RM60–90k. No EV reaches that price point yet. The brand that achieves sub-RM100k CKD pricing first — whether Chery, Perodua (working with Toyota or Geely), or a new entrant — captures the structural long position in Malaysia's EV decade.

For charging infrastructure operators

DC fast-charger density along the NSE and East-West corridors is the critical gap. Operators that secure highway concession sites now — before EV density creates competitive bidding — establish moats that are structurally difficult to displace. AC apartment-complex charging (with strata management partnerships) is the under-served urban opportunity: 40%+ of urban households live in strata properties with no current charging access.

Invest in reliability, not just deployment counts. As the EV fleet scales, charger uptime becomes a reputational variable. Operators deploying at speed without maintenance protocols risk reputation damage that concentrates with the incumbents who have the deepest networks — exactly the wrong dynamic.

For financial and mobility sector participants

Residual value models need recalibration. Hire-purchase lenders and fleet leasing companies currently apply conservative residual value haircuts to Chinese-branded EVs — typically 10–15% below comparable ICE models at 3 years. As CKD volume accumulates and secondary market liquidity develops, these haircuts compress. Lenders that move early on data-anchored EV RV models gain a competitive origination edge in the growing EV HP segment.

Fleet procurement is the 2027 catalyst. Malaysian government and GLCs have signalled intent to electrify public sector fleets. This procurement, if operationalised, adds 5,000–10,000 units of demand that is price-insensitive to the premium tier and reliably CKD-sourced. Proton e.MAS and BYD are the primary beneficiaries; fleet operators should position their service and maintenance networks ahead of award cycles.

Watch list: the 2028 excise cliff

The most consequential near-term decision for Malaysia's EV market is not made by any brand — it is made by MITI in determining the post-2028 excise duty rate for CKD EVs. A rate above 10% would represent a RM15,000–25,000 effective repricing of the mass-market CKD segment at the exact moment penetration approaches the critical 10% adoption threshold. Market participants in the EV supply chain should treat engagement with the duty consultation process as a strategic priority, not a compliance task [Kira estimates, MITI 2025].

Estimation methodology ([Kira estimates])

Figures labelled [Kira estimates] are derived using one or more of the following methods, disclosed here in aggregate:

  • Market-share triangulation: Brand-level EV share estimates derived by applying known unit totals (MAA/JPJ) against publicly reported model rankings (paultan.org, soyacincau.com) and partial quarterly disclosures. Not independently audited.
  • Penetration-rate forecasting: 2026F–2030F BEV penetration rates modelled from a base of confirmed Q1 2026 trajectory (JPJ) and CKD capacity disclosures (Proton, BYD). Three scenarios (base/bull/bear) use policy assumption sets as described in Section 10.
  • Price modelling: Post-CBU-tariff pricing estimates derived from pre-2026 CBU prices plus standard tariff rates (30% import + 10% excise + 10% ST) per MITI gazette. Actual retail prices may vary by brand discretion.
  • Geographic concentration: Klang Valley BEV share estimated from JPJ data cross-referenced against charging network density and household income distribution by state (DOSM 2022/2023).
  • Addressable-market sizing: Household income thresholds applied to 2023 DOSM household income distribution to estimate income-qualified buyer pool at CKD price points. Conservative method; does not adjust for hire-purchase accessibility.
