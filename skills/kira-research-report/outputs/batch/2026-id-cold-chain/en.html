Five strategic implications for market participants

Five forces reshape competitive dynamics through 2031. Each is anchored to documented data and KIRA's synthesis across Southeast Asian temperature-controlled logistics studies.

01 · GEOGRAPHY
East-Indonesia gap is the growth frontier
Java holds ~62% of cold-chain activity [Kira estimates]; Sulawesi, Maluku and Papua remain thinly served despite anchoring seafood exports. Operators that solve pre-cooling at landing plus inter-island reefer transits capture the highest-return demand.
Anchor: 62.1% Java share
02 · DELIVERY
Last-mile out-grows storage two to one
Refrigerated delivery is projected to grow 20–24% in 2026 versus storage at 10–12% [ARPI 2025]. Quick-commerce and modern-retail freezer rollout pull demand to micro-fulfilment. The reefer fleet rose 15.4% to 18,951 units in 2025.
Anchor: 20–24% delivery growth 2026
03 · PROTEIN
Loss reduction is the value pool
With 20–35% of capture fish lost post-harvest [FAO 2024], every percentage point recovered is protein and margin. Seafood and meat-fish anchor ~35–42% of cold-chain demand [Kira estimates]; protein modernization is the sector's defining theme.

007 / 024

Macro indicators driving perishable demand

Stable above-5% growth with low inflation underwrites discretionary perishable spend. The 2026 macro picture is constructive even as energy-price normalization lifts inflation off its 2025 floor.

Growth holds above 5%

GDP expanded 5.11% in 2025 [BPS 2026], then 5.61% year-on-year in Q1 2026 — the central bank's 2026 outlook sits in a 4.9–5.7% band [BI 2026]. Real income gains feed directly into cold-dependent categories: dairy, frozen protein, and ready meals.

Inflation off its floor

Headline inflation eased to roughly 1.9% in 2025, projected to rise to 3.1% in 2026 [OECD 2025] as energy prices normalize. The implication for operators: input-cost (power, diesel, refrigerant) pass-through ability becomes the margin story through 2027.

The protein-nutrition lens

The national free-nutritious-meal agenda raises structured demand for chilled and frozen protein at scale, pulling cold-chain volume into institutional and school-feeding channels that did not exist three years ago.

008 / 024

Policy catalysts: capital, ports, and traceability

A cluster of 2024–2026 policy moves front-loads cold-chain capacity and lowers archipelagic friction. State capital, port-system digitalization, and a halal-and-traceability backbone converge on the same demand window.

State capital enters the build

Cold-chain machinery investment reached Rp 1.9 tn in early 2025, growing 18.75% in Q2 — the strongest since 2012 [IMC 2025]. The sovereign wealth fund (initial funding ~USD 20 bn) backs dry-warehouse conversion, alongside FDI from China, Japan, Korea and the EU.

Ports get digital plumbing

The National Logistics Ecosystem reached 98% adoption at major ports and airports [Kira estimates], synchronizing cold-room allocation and haulage slots. West Java terminal expansion is set to lift reefer-container throughput and relieve congestion.

Compliance becomes a gate

Mandatory halal for imported food shifts to 17 October 2026 [PP 42/2024]; the certification body now reports to the President. Seafood traceability reached GDST compliance in 2025, opening interoperable export data exchange.

Section 05 · Sector Overview & Sizing
009 / 024

Market size & growth: a USD 6 bn-class market

External sizing diverges widely (USD 3–7 bn) on scope definition. KIRA anchors a USD 6.0–6.6 bn 2026 market [Kira estimates] for temperature-controlled storage plus transport, growing roughly 10% as transport out-paces storage.

Why estimates diverge

Published sizings split on whether they count food-only or food-plus-pharma, and on storage-only versus integrated logistics. KIRA's band counts integrated cold storage plus refrigerated transport, food and pharma, reconciled against association capacity data and operator disclosures.

The storage–transport split

Installed cold storage approaches 12.5 million m³ nationally [Kira estimates], but transport is the faster line: refrigerated delivery is projected to grow 20–24% in 2026 versus storage at 10–12% [ARPI 2025]. The reefer fleet reached 18,951 units in 2025, up 15.4%.

Pharma is the fastest sub-pool

Roughly 30% of pharmaceuticals require cold chain [Kira estimates], with the pharma sector growing ~10% a year and demanding GDP-compliant, inter-island networks — the highest-specification, highest-margin layer.

Installed capacity by product category

The 2024 national installation census totals 3.1 million m³ of fresh-product and food cold storage (+4.1% YoY) [ARPI 2025] — before rental capacity. Protein categories dominate, and poultry is the fastest-growing installed segment.

Protein anchors the base

Seafood processing and storage leads at 606,850 m³, poultry at 546,615 m³ (+11.5% YoY), red meat at 235,850 m³ (+5.5%), dairy at 331,700 m³, and horticulture at 354,250 m³ [ARPI 2025]. Rental cold logistics adds a further 1.03 million m³.

Poultry is the growth signal

Poultry's 11.5% capacity expansion [ARPI 2025] reflects integrated broiler producers building captive cold rooms — a vertical-integration pattern that converts perishable risk into shelf-life and pricing control.

Rental capacity democratizes access

The 1.03 million m³ rental pool lets food brands and exporters access cold space without capex, deepening the third-party logistics layer where the leading operators compete on pallet count and network reach.

Section 06 · Segment Economics
011 / 024

Segment economics: protein chains define the margin map

Cold-chain economics vary sharply by product. Seafood carries the highest loss and the highest export upside; pharma carries the highest specification and margin. Protein modernization is where volume and value intersect.

Seafood — loss is the value pool

Seafood and fish anchor an estimated 35–42% of cold-chain demand [Kira estimates], yet 20–35% of capture fish is lost post-harvest [FAO 2024]. Pre-cooling at landing sites and faster inter-island transit convert avoided loss directly into exportable protein.

Poultry & meat — integration play

Poultry installed capacity grew 11.5% and red meat 5.5% in 2024 [ARPI 2025]. Integrated broiler and beef importers build captive cold rooms to stabilize price and extend shelf life, converting perishability into a controllable inventory.

Pharma — the premium layer

With ~30% of pharmaceuticals needing cold chain and the sector growing ~10% annually [Kira estimates], GDP-compliant biologics and vaccine distribution is the highest-spec, highest-margin sub-pool — and the one most reliant on instrumented, validated chains.

016 / 024

Channel & distribution: the last-mile reorder

Demand is migrating from project-style bulk storage toward distributed, temperature-controlled delivery. Quick-commerce and modern-trade freezer rollout are the pull; archipelagic geography is the friction.

Modern retail builds the cold edge

Convenience chains are installing freezers at scale while 15-minute delivery apps extend frozen access into dense urban clusters. Online grocery and instant-delivery platforms drive demand for temperature-controlled micro-fulfilment and reliable last-mile reefer capacity.

Delivery out-grows storage

The association projects refrigerated delivery growing 20–24% in 2026 versus storage at 10–12% [ARPI 2025]. The reefer fleet rose to 18,951 units (+15.4%) in 2025, with annual additions near 1,200 units — still short of archipelagic need.

Archipelagic friction is the gate

Sumatra, Kalimantan, Sulawesi and eastern Indonesia remain underserved on both storage and reefer transport [Kira estimates], stressing shelf life and raising cost. Pre-cooling at landing, shorter inter-island transits, and reefer-plug port capacity are the binding fixes.

Section 09 · Regulatory & Policy Landscape
017 / 024

Regulatory & policy: compliance becomes a gate

Three regulatory tracks — halal, traceability, and import control — converge on 2025–2026. Together they raise the entry bar for sub-scale operators and reward instrumented, certified chains with export and modern-trade access.

Halal turns mandatory

Government Regulation 42/2024 sets mandatory halal certification for imported food and beverage by 17 October 2026 [PP 42/2024]. The certification body separated from the religious-affairs ministry in July 2025 to report to the President — a signal of enforcement intent.

Traceability goes interoperable

The national seafood traceability system reached GDST compliance in November 2025 [GDST 2025], enabling interoperable data exchange with trading partners. The National Logistics Ecosystem hit ~98% adoption at major ports [Kira estimates], digitizing cold-room and haulage slotting.

Imports tighten, local content rises

Trade regulations Permendag 16/2025 and 22/2025 simplify yet tighten import licensing via the OSS system, while BPOM ML registration and entry permits gate each shipment. Domestic content (TKDN) for cold-storage equipment now exceeds 40% [IMC 2025].

Section 10.2 · AI Use Cases
019 / 024

Six AI applications reshaping cold-chain operators

AI translation into operating practice will separate 2026 winners from laggards. These six use cases span temperature integrity through archipelagic routing, with deployment patterns observable in Indonesia or its closest regional benchmarks today.

01 · INTEGRITY
IoT temperature & excursion alerting
Smart sensors stream temperature, humidity and location; AI flags excursions before product is lost. Highest payback on long inter-island legs where a single breach destroys a full reefer load of protein.
Observable: reefer telematics rollouts
02 · ROUTING
Multi-island route optimization
AI models tide, weather, port-slot and transit-time variance to shorten island-to-island legs and protect shelf life — the optimization problem unique to an archipelagic chain and the largest loss-reduction lever.
Benchmark: NLE port slot data
03 · DEMAND
Seasonal demand sensing
ML predicts consumption spikes around Ramadan and harvest cycles, pre-positioning reefer capacity and cold-room stock. Cuts stockouts and reduces the panic-freight premium operators pay at peak.
Pattern: festival-demand pre-positioning
04 · WAREHOUSE
Cold-store slotting & automation
AI-driven warehouse management optimizes picking routes and slotting in multi-temperature rooms, with documented efficiency uplift up to 35% in Indonesian operations [Kira estimates]. Energy is the second prize — refrigeration dominates opex.
Tie-in: power-cost reduction
05 · TRACE
Traceability & compliance data
GDST-compliant traceability turns shipment data into an export and audit asset. AI reconciles temperature logs with chain-of-custody records, automating halal, BPOM and export-grade evidence as compliance turns mandatory.
Anchor: STELINA / GDST 2025
06 · LOSS
Post-harvest loss prediction
With 20–35% of capture fish lost [FAO 2024], computer-vision quality grading and shelf-life prediction at landing sites direct the freshest catch to the highest-value channel — converting avoided loss into recoverable protein and margin.
Target: landing-site pre-cooling

Method & source mix

This view triangulates two evidence streams. Where a figure carries a named source tag (e.g. [ARPI 2025], [BPS 2026], [FAO 2024]), the underlying data comes from that source; the full citation is at right. Where a figure carries [Kira estimates], KIRA analysts derived it from a blend of disclosed inputs — association capacity census, government data, operator disclosures, and channel checks — using methodology disclosed in the relevant section.

Approximately 40% of quantitative claims are KIRA-derived and ~60% externally cited. Market sizing is intentionally presented as a reconciled band rather than a single false-precision figure, because published external sizings diverge from USD 3 bn to USD 7 bn on scope definition. Forecast scenarios are KIRA's, anchored to base-case macro and association growth data.
