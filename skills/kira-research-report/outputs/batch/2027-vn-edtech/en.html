Demand is becoming structural

English-center spend is no longer purely discretionary. Decision 2371/QĐ-TTg targets English as a second language for 100% of K-12 students by 2035, compulsory from grade one by 2030 [Decision 2371 2025]. IELTS exemptions for the high-school exit exam rose from 28,600 in 2021 to 67,000 in 2024 [VNN 2025]. Tutoring already absorbs 43% of secondary-grade household education spend [BMI 2024].

Supply is consolidating

Circular 29/2024 bans primary academic tutoring, bars teachers from tutoring their own pupils, and mandates business registration [MoET Circular 29 2024]. Informal teacher-led supply contracts; licensed, branded center chains and online platforms absorb the redirected demand. The first profitable scaled operators have emerged [Galaxy Education 2025].

Section 04 · Macro context
006 / 018
Macro indicators driving education demand
Sustained growth, a deepening urban middle class, and an unusually high household education-spend share underpin a resilient demand base for 2027 and beyond.

Growth and incomes

Vietnam grew an estimated 8% in 2025, with growth moderating to 6.3–6.8% in 2026 and ~6.7–7.1% in 2027 — the strongest in East Asia & Pacific [World Bank 2026]. Urban per-capita monthly spend reached VND 3.8m (~USD 150), up 15.4% from 2022 [GSO 2024].

A spending middle class

56% of households now sit in the middle-and-above income bands [Vietnam-Briefing 2025]. In major cities, education absorbs ~47% of household spend [FiinGroup 2024] — among the highest shares globally, and structurally weighted toward English and exam preparation.

A large, captive cohort

The secondary-school-age population is ~24.5m and still growing modestly to 2030 [BMI 2024]. Demand is broad-based rather than concentrated in a thin affluent tier.

Section 05 · Market sizing
007 / 018
Market size & growth: tutoring and English training
The addressable opportunity spans a large informal tutoring pool plus a formalizing English-training market and a fast-growing digital layer. Digital is the fastest-compounding slice.

Three nested markets

We frame the opportunity as three nested pools: total household tutoring and English spend; the licensed center-and-platform market; and the online-delivered slice. The online-education slice alone reached ~USD 398m in 2025 and is forecast to USD 627m by 2029 at 12.1% [Statista 2025].

English-training compounding

The English-language training market grew at a CAGR above 10% across 2019–2024 and is now standardizing on quality and brand [EY-Parthenon 2025]. Digital English-learning specifically is a smaller but faster slice within it [Kira estimates].

Formalization expands the measured base

Much tutoring spend was previously informal and unmeasured. Circular 29 pushes it into registered providers, enlarging the addressable, trackable market even where total spend is flat [Kira estimates].

Section 06 · Segment economics
008 / 018
Segment economics: K-12 tutoring versus English training
The two segments have diverging economics under the new regime: tutoring is volume-driven and now licensing-gated; English training is brand-and-outcome driven with stronger pricing power.

K-12 academic tutoring

Tutoring is the larger pool by participation — it takes 43% of secondary-grade household education spend and 32% at primary [BMI 2024]. Post-Circular 29, primary academic tutoring is curtailed and the segment shifts toward licensed centers and exam-prep for final-year cohorts [MoET Circular 29 2024].

English-language training

ELT carries stronger unit economics: premium positioning, recurring multi-year enrollment, and visible outcomes. In the high-end center segment ILA holds ~42% and Wall Street English ~15% share [Vietdata 2022]. Test-prep demand is surging — IELTS exemptions hit 67,000 in 2024 [VNN 2025].

Why the split matters

Capital and brand flow toward English and test-prep, where willingness-to-pay is highest and policy is a tailwind. Pure academic tutoring becomes a compliance-heavy, lower-margin volume business [Kira estimates].

Section 07 · Regulatory landscape
009 / 018
The regulatory reset: Circular 29 and Decision 2371
A supply-side crackdown and a demand-side mandate land within months of each other, redrawing who can operate and how large the captive cohort becomes.

Circular 29 — supply discipline

Effective February 2025, Circular 29/2024 bans paid academic tutoring of primary pupils, bars teachers from charging students they teach in class, and requires every paid provider to register and publicly disclose subjects, schedules, teachers, and fees [MoET Circular 29 2024]. Enforcement has been visible, with provincial suspensions of non-compliant tutoring.

Decision 2371 — demand mandate

Issued October 2025, Decision 2371/QĐ-TTg targets English as a second language for all K-12 students by 2035, compulsory from grade one by 2030, reaching ~30m students across ~50,000 institutions [Decision 2371 2025]. It follows Resolution 71-NQ/TW (August 2025).

The teacher-supply bottleneck

The mandate requires at least 200,000 teachers trained to teach in English by 2035, with ~22,000 more needed for pre-school and primary alone [Decision 2371 2025]. Private centers and AI tooling are positioned to fill the gap.

Position

Formed in 2020 around the acquisition of HOCMAI, Galaxy Education runs three brands: HOCMAI (K-12 online), ICAN (early-years English and platform tech), and FUNiX (online vocational and degree) [Galaxy Education 2025].

Section 09 · AI impact
012 / 018
AI in Vietnam tutoring & English delivery: capacity, not novelty
AI adoption in Vietnamese edtech sits between pilot and integration. The binding constraint it relieves is teacher supply — the same bottleneck the ESL mandate creates.

Where adoption sits

Pronunciation and speech-assessment AI is already mainstream in Vietnamese English learning; one homegrown app reports 50m users globally [ELSA 2025]. Auto-grading and adaptive practice are moving from pilot to integration at scaled platforms [Galaxy Education 2025].

Why it matters now

Decision 2371 needs ~200,000 teachers trained by 2035 [Decision 2371 2025]. AI extends each teacher's reach and gives learners 24/7 practice — directly addressing the gap.

The risk

Parents still reject online-only delivery (72%) [EY-Parthenon 2025]. AI wins as augmentation inside blended models, not as a teacher replacement.
