The structural shift

HBM is the memory architecture that allows AI accelerators — NVIDIA H/B-series, AMD Instinct, Google TPU — to achieve the memory bandwidth AI inference and training workloads require. DRAM wafers converted to HBM generate 5–8× the revenue per wafer of standard DDR5 [Kira estimates]. This is the single largest ASP expansion event in DRAM history.

The 2026 contest

SK Hynix leads with HBM3E volume and early HBM4 samples delivered to NVIDIA and hyperscalers; Samsung is narrowing the yield gap and redirecting fab capacity. Micron participates but at ~10% share [Kira estimates]. The 2026 question is whether Samsung can close the yield deficit before SK Hynix locks in a second generation of design wins.

Where Korea's HBM producers and their supply-chain partners must act. Each implication is anchored to the section that develops the evidence.

CAPACITY
Pace HBM4 ramp to match hyperscaler commit cycles.

HBM allocation is driven by 12–18 month hyperscaler commit windows. Over-ramping ahead of qualification risks stranded capacity; under-ramping cedes design wins to competitors. The 2026 constraint is advanced packaging throughput, not DRAM wafer starts [Kira estimates].

PRICING
Defend the ASP premium through HBM4 differentiation.

The 5–8× ASP premium over standard DRAM is a function of process exclusivity, not just capacity tightness [Kira estimates]. As Micron scales and Samsung yields improve, maintaining the premium requires HBM4 differentiation — bandwidth density, power envelope, and thermal interface.

See AI memory premium dynamics · p.011

Monitor Samsung's yield recovery timeline closely.

Samsung's HBM3E yield gap is the single variable that most changes SK Hynix's 2026 pricing power. A Samsung recovery in H1 2026 would compress SK Hynix's premium; a delay extends the supercycle [secondary]. Supply-chain intelligence on Samsung's 8Hi and 12Hi stacking yields matters more than public guidance.

Track hyperscaler capex revisions as the primary demand signal.

HBM demand is a direct function of AI accelerator silicon orders, which are themselves a function of hyperscaler data centre capex [secondary]. Alphabet, Microsoft, Amazon, and Meta together indicate the 2026 trajectory; any capex deferral ripples into HBM allocation within two quarters.

Section 02 · Macro context
07 / 14

Korea macro and the semiconductor export machine

Semiconductors represent roughly 20% of Korean exports [KITA], making HBM's pricing cycle a direct macroeconomic variable — not a sectoral footnote. The 2026 backdrop combines a recovering chip cycle, export control friction, and domestic policy tailwinds for capacity expansion.

Semiconductor as export engine

Korea's semiconductor sector generated USD 135 bn in exports in 2024 [KITA], with memory (DRAM + NAND) accounting for the majority. HBM's 5–8× ASP premium means that each wafer conversion to HBM has an outsized impact on the trade balance relative to unit volumes [Kira estimates].

Export control friction and reorientation

US BIS restrictions on advanced memory exports to China [BIS 2023] have pushed both SK Hynix and Samsung to deprioritize China-bound NAND and DDR in favour of HBM, which is overwhelmingly destined for US hyperscalers and GPU manufacturers. This reorientation reinforces the AI memory premium [secondary].

Policy tailwinds for capacity

The Korean government's K-Chips Act (2023) provides tax credits up to 15% for semiconductor facility investment [MOTIE], reducing the effective cost of HBM capacity expansion and incentivising on-shore advanced packaging scale-up.

Section 03 · HBM market sizing
08 / 14

HBM market size: from niche to critical infrastructure.

HBM grew from a USD 4.5 bn market in 2023 to an estimated USD 34 bn by end-2026, driven entirely by AI accelerator adoption at hyperscalers and AI chip fabless firms. Korea supplies over 90% of this market [Kira estimates].

The AI-driven inflection

The HBM market tripled between 2023 and 2025 — a trajectory driven by NVIDIA GPU wafer shipments, not DRAM cycle recovery. Each NVIDIA H100 contains 80 GB of HBM3, and each H200/B200 contains 141 GB / 192 GB respectively [NVIDIA]. The memory attach per GPU is increasing, not plateauing [Kira estimates].

Korea's structural dominance

SK Hynix holds approximately 50–55% of global HBM supply; Samsung holds approximately 35–40%; Micron holds the remainder [Kira estimates]. In HBM3E specifically — the highest-ASP product shipping at volume in 2025–26 — SK Hynix leads with the majority of NVIDIA design wins [secondary].

Wafer economics: HBM vs standard DRAM

An HBM wafer set consumes 3–4× more DRAM die area than equivalent DDR5 capacity, with the cost recovery coming from the premium on the integrated product. The effective ASP per gigabyte for HBM3E is approximately 6–7× DDR5 [Kira estimates] — a margin structure unlike any prior DRAM cycle.

Section 04 · Capacity expansion

SK Hynix and Samsung: HBM capacity roadmaps

SK Hynix is ahead on HBM3E volume and HBM4 early qualification; Samsung is investing aggressively to close the yield gap and win allocations at Google, AMD, and domestic AI chipmakers. The capacity race is being fought in advanced packaging floors, not DRAM fabs.

SK HYNIX
The technology leader
SK Hynix was the sole qualified HBM3E supplier to NVIDIA through most of 2024–25 [secondary]. Its M15X fab in Cheongju and the new Yongin campus are the primary HBM production sites. HBM4 samples were delivered to NVIDIA in H2 2025 [SK Hynix IR]; volume ramp targets H2 2026.
· HBM3E 12Hi: volume production since Q1 2025
· HBM4: early qualification samples Q3 2025
· DRAM wafer capacity to HBM: ~40% by end-2026E
· Packaging capex: USD 3–4 bn per year [Kira estimates]
SAMSUNG ELECTRONICS
Closing the yield gap
Samsung's HBM3E yields improved through 2025 after NVIDIA qualification issues delayed initial ramp [secondary]. Samsung DS division targets HBM3E volume allocations at Google TPU v6 and AMD MI400 series in 2026 [Kira estimates]. HBM4 development runs in parallel at the Hwaseong campus.
· HBM3E 8Hi: volume qualification completed H2 2025
· HBM3E 12Hi: ramp targeting Q2 2026
· HBM4: development phase; volume H2 2026–H1 2027E
· HBM capex: USD 4–5 bn per year [Kira estimates]
Advanced packaging bottleneck
TSV (through-silicon via) drilling and mass reflow bonding are the yield-limiting steps for 12Hi stacks. Throughput at these steps, not raw DRAM wafer starts, gates 2026 HBM supply [secondary].
DRAM conversion economics
Converting one DRAM wafer start to HBM costs roughly 3–4× more to process but sells for 5–8× more per effective bit shipped [Kira estimates]. The economic case for conversion is structurally superior to standard DRAM while the AI premium holds.
Micron's limited participation
Micron holds ~10% HBM share and is scaling HBM3E at its Boise facility. Its capacity expansion is constrained by smaller DRAM wafer base and less packaging infrastructure [secondary]. Not a near-term threat to the Korea premium.

Section 05 · AI memory premium dynamics
11 / 14

AI memory premium: structural, not cyclical.

The HBM price premium over standard DRAM is not a function of typical DRAM supply-demand cycles. It reflects the architectural lock-in of HBM into AI accelerator design — a qualification barrier that takes 12–24 months to change, not 3–6 months like commodity DRAM pricing.

The premium anatomy

HBM3E (24 GB / 36 GB per stack) sells at approximately USD 18–24 per gigabyte in 2025 contract pricing, versus USD 3–4 per gigabyte for DDR5 [Kira estimates]. The differential reflects: (1) stacking and packaging cost, (2) yield-gated scarcity, and (3) the lack of viable substitutes in AI accelerator design specs.

Qualification lock-in

An AI accelerator SoC — NVIDIA Blackwell, AMD Instinct MI400, Google TPU v6 — is designed with specific HBM interposer and interface specs. Changing the HBM supplier requires a silicon respin, at minimum a new package qualification, and months of system validation [secondary]. The design-win stickiness is the premium's floor.

Margin structure for SK Hynix

SK Hynix's DRAM operating margin recovered from a trough of −25% in 2023 to an estimated +40%+ in 2024–25, driven almost entirely by HBM mix shift [SK Hynix IR]. HBM now represents the majority of DRAM revenue despite being a minority of bits shipped [Kira estimates].

Section 07 · Demand drivers
13 / 14

Demand architecture: hyperscalers, AI fabs, and the GPU memory attach.

HBM demand is derived demand — a function of AI accelerator silicon orders, which are themselves a function of hyperscaler capex commitments. The 2026 demand picture is constructive: aggregate hyperscaler AI infrastructure capex is accelerating, not moderating.

Hyperscaler capex as the primary signal

The four largest US hyperscalers (Alphabet, Amazon, Microsoft, Meta) guided total combined capex toward USD 300+ bn in 2025, with data centre and AI infrastructure representing the majority of incremental spend [secondary]. Each USD 1 bn of AI compute capex translates to approximately USD 60–80 mn of HBM demand [Kira estimates].

Rising memory attach per GPU

The HBM memory per accelerator has grown with each product generation: H100 (80 GB), H200 (141 GB), B200 (192 GB) [NVIDIA]. At equivalent AI cluster sizes, each successive GPU generation requires significantly more HBM supply — a structural tailwind independent of hyperscaler unit count [Kira estimates].

Emerging demand vectors

Beyond hyperscalers: (1) sovereign AI programs (EU, Japan, UAE, KSA) building domestic GPU clusters; (2) enterprise AI inference at edge nodes; (3) domestic Korean AI chip startups (Rebellions, Sapeon) qualifying HBM for custom accelerator designs [secondary]. These are margin-incremental, not volume-dominant, in 2026.

Section 08 · Strategic implications
14 / 14

Five strategic implications for market participants

The 2026 HBM inflection creates asymmetric positioning opportunities for investors, supply-chain participants, and hyperscaler procurement teams. The structural dynamics favour Korea's duopoly through at least 2027.

INVESTMENT
Position in the HBM supply chain, not just the memory producers.

The advanced packaging ecosystem — TSV equipment makers, thermal interface material suppliers, CoWoS substrate providers — captures a rising share of the HBM value chain and trades at lower multiples than SK Hynix. Semes (TSV), Nepes Arc (assembly), and Hanmi Semiconductor (bonder equipment) are direct beneficiaries [Kira estimates].

SUPPLY CHAIN
Build allocation visibility 18+ months ahead, not 6.

HBM allocation cycles are longer than standard DRAM. Hyperscaler procurement teams that lock in HBM3E allocations 12–18 months ahead gain both price visibility and supply security; those operating on spot face allocation shortfalls in tight-yield quarters [secondary].

RISK
Hedge against Samsung yield recovery compressing the premium.

A Samsung HBM3E 12Hi yield recovery in H1 2026 is the primary downside scenario for SK Hynix pricing power. At full Samsung qualification, effective HBM supply increases ~20–25% without new wafer starts — compressing the premium by an estimated 1.5–2.0× [Kira estimates].

TECHNOLOGY
Track HBM4 specification as the next qualification cycle anchor.

HBM4 doubles per-stack bandwidth to ~1.5 TB/s and introduces logic-on-base-die capabilities. The qualification cycle will begin in 2026 with NVIDIA and custom hyperscaler ASICs; whichever supplier locks in HBM4 design wins in H1 2026 sets the pricing baseline for 2027–28 [secondary].

GEOPOLITICS
Watch for US–Korea policy alignment tightening the China supply barrier further.

The current export control regime restricts Chinese access to advanced HBM equipment and know-how, preserving Korea's structural lead. A further tightening — covering HBM3E finished products shipped to China-based AI deployments — would accelerate demand reorientation toward US and European hyperscalers and reinforce the Korea premium through 2027+ [Kira estimates].
