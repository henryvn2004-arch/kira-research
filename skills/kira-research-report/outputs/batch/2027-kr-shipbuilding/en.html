The LNG order book is the revenue anchor

Korea's big three — HD Hyundai Heavy Industries (HD HHI), Hanwha Ocean, and Samsung Heavy Industries (SHI) — collectively hold a combined backlog estimated at USD ~64 bn, with LNG carriers accounting for 55-60% of units [Kira estimates]. New-build prices for 174,000 cbm LNG carriers hit USD 262 m in early 2026, up from USD 189 m at the 2021 trough, as global LNG demand from Europe and East Asia locks in long-term supply agreements [Clarkson 2026].

Margins recovering, not yet peak

The 2022-23 cycle was marked by fixed-price legacy contracts signed when steel costs surged. HD HHI's shipbuilding operating margin recovered to 7.4% in FY25 from near breakeven in FY23 [HD HHI FY25]. Hanwha Ocean and SHI are tracking a similar lag trajectory. All three have now repriced backlogs; the new contracts carry better pass-through provisions.

Five strategic implications for the next 24 months

Korea's shipbuilding franchise runs on two linked engines: a locked-in LNG carrier order book and an emerging offshore wind installation vessel segment. The moves below sequence where to apply capital and positioning over the next two years.

01
Lock steel and labor now, before the backlog peak
The 2026-2028 delivery window is the densest in a decade. Yards with pre-arranged steel supply agreements and long-term skilled-worker retention programs will defend margins; those repricing inputs mid-contract absorb the squeeze.
ANCHOR · USD 64 bn combined backlog
02
Price new LNG orders at cycle peak, resist volume pressure
At USD 262 m per vessel, new-build prices are at a multi-decade high. Booking incremental volume at diluted prices to fill berths past 2028 destroys the margin recovery. Selective order discipline preserves the pricing floor.
ANCHOR · USD 262 m per 174k cbm carrier
03
Invest in WTIV design now — the orders open 2027-2028
European offshore wind installation vessel procurement is accelerating; US Jones Act-compliant WTIVs are in active development. Korea's yards have the steel-fabrication scale and crane-capacity heritage to compete, but design investment must precede the RFP window.
ANCHOR · USD 8.4 bn WTIV market by 2030F
04
Accelerate ammonia and dual-fuel propulsion R&D
IMO's CII rules tighten annually; owners ordering now want 2030+ optionality on fuel flexibility. Yards that can offer ammonia-ready or methanol-dual-fuel LNG carriers at a credible delivery date command a premium and reduce the risk of order cancellations as regulation tightens.
ANCHOR · 2030 IMO carbon targets
05
Watch the China capacity build — respond with quality, not price
Chinese yards are capturing ~24% of new LNG carrier orders, double their 2020 share. Korea's response cannot be a price war; it must be technology leadership (cargo containment systems, propulsion efficiency, delivery precision) and customer relationship depth with major gas traders.
ANCHOR · ~24% China LNG order share

Section 01 · Order book & market
007 / 014

Order book & market size: the backlog is the business

The order backlog — measured in CGT and contract value — is the primary forward revenue indicator for yard operators. Korea's collective backlog is large enough to sustain full utilization into 2028, at which point new ordering decisions determine the post-cycle trajectory.

Korea holds the LNG technology moat

Korea's three yards built their LNG carrier capability over 30 years around proprietary cargo containment systems (GTT's Mark III and NO96 membranes, licensed and co-developed) and X-DF dual-fuel propulsion. In 2025, Korea took ~73% of global LNG carrier new orders by vessel count [KSA 2026]. China's yards, despite government-backed capacity expansion, trail on both delivery precision and cargo-containment certification.

Backlog extends to 2028-2029

Combined backlog across HD HHI, Hanwha Ocean, and SHI is estimated at USD ~64 bn [Kira estimates], with LNG carriers at roughly 55-60% by value. Delivery slots at the major yards are fully booked through 2027 and largely committed for 2028. The global orderbook for LNG carriers stood at 271 vessels in early 2026, of which Korea holds over 200 [Clarkson 2026].

Market size expanding with global LNG trade

Global LNG trade volumes rose to 412 mt in 2025 [IGU 2026], with Europe's pivot to non-Russian pipeline gas underpinning demand for 180+ new LNG carriers through 2030. The addressable market for Korean yards is USD 8-10 bn in annual LNG carrier contracts alone [Kira estimates].

Section 01 · Margin & pricing
008 / 014

Margin & pricing dynamics: recovery is structural, not windfall

All three yards are working through the legacy of low-priced, fixed-cost contracts signed in 2021-22 when steel surged post-pandemic. As those deliver out and new contracts carry improved pricing and pass-through provisions, operating margins are recovering on a predictable multi-year trajectory.

The 2022-23 margin hole was structural

Korean yards signed long backlogs at fixed prices when steel (POSCO HR coil) peaked near USD 1,080/t in H1 2022 [POSCO AR 2022]. Margin compression followed mechanically as costs rose against locked revenue. HD HHI's shipbuilding segment hit near-breakeven in FY23; Hanwha Ocean ran operating losses in 2022-23 through the restructuring period.

New contracts are better structured

Contracts signed from late 2023 onward incorporate steel escalation clauses and are priced at higher absolute levels — the 174,000 cbm LNG carrier now prices at USD 262 m vs USD 189 m at the 2021 floor [Clarkson 2026]. HD HHI's shipbuilding margin recovered to 7.4% in FY25 [HD HHI FY25]; the trajectory points to 9-11% at the next backlog cycle, if steel stays below USD 700/t [Kira estimates].

Currency is a two-way swing factor

Contracts are USD-denominated; costs are KRW-denominated. A 1,350 KRW/USD rate (approximate 2025 average) provides a meaningful operating tailwind vs. the 2019 rate near 1,165 [BOK 2026]. A sharp KRW appreciation — driven by geopolitical resolution or global dollar weakening — remains the chief margin headwind through 2027.

SECTION 02

Offshore wind installation vessels

The offshore wind installation vessel (WTIV) is the most capital-intensive, technically-demanding new-build type opening in the 2027-2032 window. Europe's offshore wind pipeline requires 30-40 new WTIVs; Korea's yards are the only non-European competitors with the jack-up heritage and steel-fabrication scale to build them on time.

Section 02 · WTIV opportunity
010 / 014

WTIV market opportunity: Europe's wind pipeline is the demand engine

Wind turbine installation vessels are complex, high-value assets purpose-built to lift and install next-generation offshore wind turbines (15-20 MW+). The global fleet is undersupplied relative to the installation pipeline through 2030, and orders must be placed by 2026-2027 to have vessels ready for the 2029-2032 construction window.

Global WTIV fleet is undersupplied

As of early 2026, the active WTIV fleet capable of handling 15 MW+ turbines numbers only 12-14 vessels globally, with a further 20-25 on order [WTIV Fleet 2026]. European offshore wind targets under REPowerEU and national plans require installing over 150 GW of offshore capacity by 2030 — a pace that requires 30-40 additional WTIVs [EWEA 2025].

Korea enters from a position of strength

HD HHI, Hanwha Ocean, and Daewoo Shipbuilding's successor entity (now Hanwha Ocean) have all undertaken WTIV concept design work. SHI delivered the first Korean-built WTIV, the Deme Orion, in 2020 — demonstrating the technical capability at scale. The crane capacity and jack-up heritage from semi-submersible production units translates directly [SHI WTIV 2021].

US Jones Act opens a separate lane

US offshore wind requires Jones Act-compliant WTIVs — built, owned, and operated in the US or by US-flagged vessels. Dominion Energy's Charybdis (built by Sapura in Texas) is the only Jones Act WTIV commissioned; the next wave requires US-content vessels where Korean yards can serve as technology partners or build at US facilities under licensing [BOEM 2025].

Section 03 · Regulatory & decarbonization
013 / 014

Regulatory & decarbonization: IMO's CII tightens, propulsion optionality commands a premium

IMO's Carbon Intensity Indicator (CII) framework ratchets annually through 2030, accelerating the retirement of older, less-efficient tonnage and raising the bar for new-build specifications. Korean yards are positioned to capture the premium for dual-fuel and alternative-propulsion vessels.

CII compliance accelerates fleet renewal

CII ratings range from A (best) to E (worst); vessels rated E for three consecutive years face operational restrictions. Roughly 30-35% of the global LNG carrier fleet is estimated to fall below C-rating by 2027 without retrofit or operational changes [DNV 2026]. This accelerates replacement ordering — structural demand for Korean yards.

X-DF propulsion is the current standard

WinGD's X-DF (dual-fuel, low-pressure LNG) and MAN's ME-GI (high-pressure) are the dominant propulsion choices for new LNG carriers. X-DF penetration on Korean-built LNG carriers exceeded 85% of 2024 deliveries [KSA 2026]. Both are designed for future ammonia and methanol co-firing — a hedge against the post-2030 propulsion transition.

Ammonia propulsion: 2028+ opportunity

MAN and WinGD both have ammonia-capable two-stroke engines in late development; commercial readiness is expected 2028-2030 [DNV 2026]. HD HHI and Hanwha Ocean are co-developing ammonia bunkering and fuel-handling systems. The first ammonia-ready LNG carrier new-build specifications are expected to enter Korean yards by 2027 [Kira estimates].

Section 04 · Forecast & sources
014 / 014

2025-2030 forecast: base / bull / bear

Korea's shipbuilding revenue trajectory over 2025-2030 is shaped by three variables: LNG carrier new-build pricing discipline, how quickly the WTIV segment scales, and whether the margin recovery holds against steel cost and FX headwinds. Scenarios anchor to 2025 combined big-three revenue near KRW 29 trn.

Base — KRW 40 trn by 2030. LNG carrier backlog delivers as scheduled; margins hold at 8-10%; WTIV orders begin in 2027-28. [Kira estimates]

Bull — KRW 50 trn. New-build LNG pricing holds above USD 270 m; WTIV segment contributes KRW 3+ trn by 2030; ammonia-ready premium captured. [Kira estimates]

Bear — KRW 31 trn. Steel cost spike erodes repriced margins; China captures >35% of LNG orders; WTIV investment returns delayed. [Kira estimates]
