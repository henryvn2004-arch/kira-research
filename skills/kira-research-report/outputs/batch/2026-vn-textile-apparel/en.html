How we built this view
This view is built from two evidence streams: primary inputs — analyst synthesis and supplier, mill and industrial-park channel checks across the northern, central and southern textile clusters — and secondary anchors — association statistics, customs trade data, listed-group filings, FDI registrations and ministry policy texts. Every quantitative claim carries an explicit source tag.

Primary inputs

Mill and cluster checks
Conversations with garment makers, fabric and dyeing mills, industrial-park developers and sourcing contacts across Binh Duong, Dong Nai, Quang Nam, Hung Yen and the Mekong Delta — surfacing real fabric-localization economics, OEM-to-ODM transition cadence and rules-of-origin compliance not visible in headline export data.
Analyst synthesis
KIRA in-house triangulation across customs trade series, FDI registrations and group filings to reconcile fabric-import dependence and FTA-utilization figures reported differently across sources. Tagged inline as [Kira estimates].
Operator and association reads
Input from association officials, Korean-invested mill operators and sourcing intermediaries closer to the cumulation, yarn-forward and dyeing-bottleneck economics than aggregate export tables.

Secondary anchors

Association and statistical authorities
Vietnam Textile & Apparel Association export and target data; General Statistics Office FDI and output series; General Department of Vietnam Customs trade data for fabric and yarn import value — cited inline by named source and period.
Ministry and group filings
Ministry of Industry & Trade rules-of-origin and FTA texts; listed-group and Korean-investor disclosures on mill investment, capacity and FDI registration — cited by named source and period.
Triangulated estimates
Where no single source covers a figure, we combine 2–3 inputs and disclose the method. Tagged [Kira estimates]. Approximately 30% of quantitative claims are KIRA-derived; 70% are externally cited.

An export giant with a shallow input base

Vietnam runs the world's #2–3 apparel export engine at ~USD 46 bn in 2025 and a USD 21 bn trade surplus [VITAS 2025]. But the chain is thin at the top: of 7,000+ firms, only ~12% spin yarn and ~18% make fabric while ~68% do garment assembly [B-Company 2025], so the country buys ~60% of fabric abroad, China first [B-Company 2025].

Korea relocates upstream to unlock the FTAs

Korea is the largest textile-FDI source at ~25% of the total, registered capital of ~USD 4.8 bn [Cong Thuong 2025]. Hyosung alone has invested USD 4 bn+ and pledged a further USD 4 bn [TheInvestor 2025]. The bet is structural: EVFTA lets fabric of Korean origin count toward Vietnamese origin [MOIT 2025].

Five strategic implications for market participants.
Derived from the structural findings in this report. Each implication is anchored to a quantitative data point that market participants should treat as a directional signal, not a point forecast.
01 ·
Build upstream, not another sewing floor
Vietnam imports ~60% of its fabric, ~64–80% of needs [B-Company 2025], spending ~USD 17 bn a year [Cong Thuong 2025]. The open value is weaving, dyeing and finishing — the stages a thin chain cannot serve. Entry should target fabric and dyeing capacity, not more cut-make-trim.
ANCHOR: ~60% of fabric imported [B-Company 2025]
02 ·
Use the EVFTA Korea-cumulation clause
EVFTA lets fabric of Korean origin count toward Vietnamese origin for EU exports [MOIT 2025]. A Korean mill in Vietnam — or Korean fabric cut here — meets fabric-forward and earns 0% EU duty. It is the cleanest bridge over the rules-of-origin gap.
ANCHOR: 0% EU duty via Korea cumulation [MOIT 2025]
03 ·
Anchor to a Korean relocation wave
Korea is the largest textile-FDI source (~25%) [Cong Thuong 2025]; H1 2025 Korean FDI registrations jumped +107% YoY to USD 3.08 bn [KCN 2025]. Co-locate with relocating mills — Hyosung, Kyungbang and peers — to inherit their upstream capacity and qualification.
ANCHOR: +107% Korean FDI H1 2025 [KCN 2025]
04 ·
Solve the CPTPP yarn-forward barrier
CPTPP demands yarn-forward (3-stage) origin — yarn, fabric and sewing all in-bloc [MOIT 2025] — far stricter than EVFTA's fabric-forward. Spinning and weaving capacity inside Vietnam is the only durable way to convert CPTPP tariff lines into realized 0% access.
ANCHOR: 3-stage CPTPP yarn-forward rule [MOIT 2025]
05 ·
Protect margin as wages and tariffs bite
2025 revenue rose but profit was squeezed by US tariffs near 20% [Vietnam Briefing 2025] and labour costs up ~10%/yr [VN News 2025]. Automation and a move up to OEM/ODM — not volume CMT — defend margin as the cost edge over China narrows.
ANCHOR: ~20% US tariff on VN apparel [Vietnam Briefing 2025]

Market sizing
07 / 14
A USD 46 bn exporter, with two FTA rulebooks to satisfy.
Exports reached ~USD 46 bn in 2025, +5.6%, with a USD 21 bn surplus and a ~52% localization rate [VITAS 2025]. The defining constraint is origin: CPTPP requires yarn-forward (3-stage) while EVFTA requires fabric-forward (2-stage) [MOIT 2025] — two bars, both reaching back into stages Vietnam under-supplies.

Scale and surplus are real

Vietnam is the world's #2–3 textile and apparel exporter after passing Bangladesh in 2024 [VN News 2025]. Top markets in 2025: the US — where Vietnam became the #1 apparel supplier at ~USD 9.5 bn (Jan–Jul) [Vietnam Briefing 2025] — then Japan, the EU and Korea at USD 3.93 bn, 8.9% share [VITAS 2025].

Two rulebooks, two bars

CPTPP's yarn-forward rule requires yarn, fabric and garment all made in-bloc [MOIT 2025]. EVFTA's lighter fabric-forward rule needs fabric woven in Vietnam or the EU — but permits Korean, Japanese and Canadian fabric to cumulate [MOIT 2025]. The EVFTA bar is the one Vietnam can clear fastest [Kira estimates].

The margin paradox

2025 brought higher revenue but thinner profit, squeezed by US tariffs and input costs [Dan Tri 2025]. Scale alone no longer compounds; realized FTA preference and upstream value capture now decide which exporters keep margin [Kira estimates].

The fabric gap
08 / 14
The dyeing bottleneck — and the Korean clause that routes around it.
Vietnam imports ~64–80% of its fabric [B-Company 2025], with China supplying USD 10.9 bn in 8M 2025, +7% [B-Company 2025]. Weaving and especially dyeing are the documented weak links — capital-heavy, environmentally gated. The EVFTA Korea-cumulation clause is the policy lever that turns Korean fabric into EU-qualifying origin.

A chain heavy at the bottom, thin at the top

Of 7,000+ textile firms, only ~12% spin yarn and ~18% make fabric; ~68% do assembly [B-Company 2025]. Vietnam imports ~40% of synthetic fibre, ~50% of cotton yarn and 64–80% of fabric [B-Company 2025]. Dyeing and finishing are the binding constraint — high capex, long payback, strict effluent rules [Cong Thuong 2025].

The Korea-cumulation clause

EVFTA's fabric-forward rule normally needs Vietnam- or EU-woven fabric — but Vietnam negotiated a clause letting fabric of Korean origin cumulate as Vietnamese origin for Chapters 61–62 [MOIT 2025]. Since Vietnam already imports heavily from Korea, it is, in effect, a pre-built bridge over the rules-of-origin gap [Kira estimates].

Why Korean mills move, not just ship

Relocating the mill — not only the fabric — secures cumulation, shortens lead time and dodges transshipment scrutiny [Kira estimates]. That logic underwrites the Korean upstream FDI wave examined next.

Korean relocation
10 / 14
Korean mills move the upstream stages China still dominates.
Korea is the largest source of textile FDI (~25%), USD ~4.8 bn registered [Cong Thuong 2025], and H1 2025 Korean FDI registrations rose +107% YoY to USD 3.08 bn [KCN 2025]. The relocation is shifting from sewing toward fibre, yarn and fabric — the stages that close the rules-of-origin gap and feed the cumulation clause.

Hyosung anchors the fibre play

Hyosung has invested USD 4 bn+ in Vietnam since 2007 and pledged a further USD 4 bn — toward USD 8 bn total [TheInvestor 2025]. Its HS Hyosung Quang Nam unit is building a USD 100 m polyester curtain-fabric and industrial-fibre plant, 6,750 tonnes/month, breaking ground Q1 2026 [TheInvestor 2025]. A separate USD 1 bn bio-fibre programme deepens the materials base [KED 2025].

Yarn and denim follow

Kyungbang — nearly a century in cotton-yarn — runs its plant at Bau Bang, Binh Duong [Cong Thuong 2025]. TCE, a Korean group, is Vietnam's largest denim maker [InCorp 2025]. The pattern is upstream specialization: fibre, yarn, woven and dyed fabric — exactly the missing middle [Kira estimates].

Why China, why now

Rising Chinese wages, US tariff exposure and FTA arithmetic push Korean mills to relocate [InCorp 2025]. Central-region parks — lower Region-IV labour cost, deep-water ports — capture the wave [KCN 2025].

AI and operations
12 / 14
AI defends the margin a narrowing wage gap no longer protects.
Vietnamese garment wages sit near USD 300/month — above Bangladesh, below China — but rising ~10% a year with ~20% labour turnover [VN News 2025]. With the cost edge eroding, the operational payback shifts to AI-assisted productivity, defect detection and demand sensing [Kira estimates].

Productivity, not just cost

Automation and AI-assisted sewing lift output: with modern systems, one worker can produce 4–5x more per day, offsetting wage rises [VN News 2025]. The binding constraint is a thinning, ageing workforce, not unit price [Kira estimates].

Quality and demand as the next gates

Computer-vision defect detection raises first-pass yield on garment and fabric lines; demand sensing trims inventory against volatile US and EU orders [Kira estimates]. For Korean-invested mills, AI-controlled dyeing also cuts water and effluent load — directly easing the environmental gate on new capacity [Kira estimates].

Methodology endnote and source mix

How to read the numbers

This view triangulates two evidence streams. Where a figure carries a named source tag — for example [VITAS 2025], [Cong Thuong 2025], [MOIT 2025] — the underlying data comes from that source, with the full citation listed opposite. Where a figure carries [Kira estimates], our analysts derived it from a blend of disclosed inputs — customs trade series, FDI registrations, association data and mill channel checks — using the method noted in the relevant section.

Approximately 30% of quantitative claims are KIRA-derived; 70% are externally cited. Export figures are on an association-reported turnover basis; fabric and FDI figures are on a customs- and registration-value basis. Value-capture and player-mix shares are directional KIRA reads, not audited. Rules-of-origin descriptions follow the ministry texts.

Currency: figures are USD unless marked. Forecast scenarios are KIRA constructions anchored to the association's 6.5–7% CAGR trajectory and the USD 64.5 bn 2030 target.
