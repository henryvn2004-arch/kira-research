How we built this view

This view draws on two evidence streams: primary inputs — land-office transfer records, listed-developer disclosure, and analyst synthesis — and secondary anchors — the central bank, the housing data agency, and consultancy market datasets. Every quantitative claim carries an explicit source tag.

Primary inputs

Transfer & ownership records
Land Department ownership-transfer data on foreign and domestic condominium units — nationality split, unit counts, and transfer value — reconciled by quarter to surface real demand, not headline launches.
Listed-developer disclosure
Presales, transfers, launch pipelines and segment mix from SET-listed developers — the operating reality behind the aggregate, including foreign-buyer exposure.
Analyst synthesis
KIRA in-house triangulation across multiple disclosed inputs — unsold stock, absorption, price-per-square-metre by tier. Tagged inline as [Kira estimates].

Secondary anchors

Macro & policy authorities
Central-bank policy-rate and household-debt data, the IMF growth path, and the cabinet stimulus package — transfer-fee cuts, mortgage-registration relief, and the loan-to-value relaxation — cited inline by named source.
Housing-market datasets
The national real-estate information centre's quarterly Bangkok-region series and consultancy condominium datasets, reconciled against transfer records and developer disclosure — cited by source and period.
Triangulated estimates
Where no single source covers a figure — e.g. mid-tier share of unsold stock, or the 2026 transfer path — we combine 2–3 inputs and disclose the method. Tagged [Kira estimates].

The setup

Two pressures meet in 2026. Bangkok ended 2025 with about 93,600 unsold condominium units worth ~THB 760 bn, condominiums making up nearly 38% of all unsold residential stock [REIC 2025]. At the same time, foreign condominium transfers reached 14,899 units in 2025 (+2.2%) but value fell 10.7% to THB 60.9 bn as Chinese buyers traded down to smaller units [REIC 2025].

The reset

The Chinese rebound is partial, not restored. China still leads foreign buyers but its share slid from roughly 60% to ~30%, pressured by a soft domestic property cycle and tighter capital controls [Nation 2026]. Q1 2026 foreign transfers fell 17.3% to 3,241 units [REIC 2026]. Russian and Indian demand partly fills the gap, but neither matches China's prior scale.

005 / 017

Five strategic implications for market participants

Five forces govern who captures Thailand's condominium recovery through 2028. Each is anchored to documented data and KIRA synthesis.

01 · DEMAND
Domestic credit, not China, sets the floor
Mortgage rejection ran ~40% under THB 3m and up to 70% at the entry tier in 2025 [Terra 2025]. With household debt near 89% of GDP easing toward 85% [BOT 2025], local purchasing power — not the partial Chinese rebound — gates absorption.
Anchor: ~40% reject rate
02 · OVERSUPPLY
The glut sits in the Bangkok mid-tier
Mid-tier stock at THB 100k–150k/sqm made up ~51% of Q1 2025 launches [Colliers 2025]; condos are ~38% of ~94k unsold units, concentrated in suburban rail corridors [REIC 2025]. Absorption there now runs years, not quarters.
Anchor: 93.6k unsold
03 · STIMULUS
The 2026 fee window pulls demand forward
Transfer and mortgage fees cut to 0.01% for homes ≤THB 7m, plus 100% LTV to Jun 2026 [MOF 2026]. Q1 2026 transfers rose 11.2% in volume [REIC 2026] — real, but front-loaded ahead of the deadline.
Anchor: +11.2% Q1 volume
04 · POLICY
The 75% quota reform stays on the shelf
A cabinet study floated raising the foreign quota from 49% to 75% and leases to 99 years [Bangkok Biz 2025], but it was shelved in Sep 2025 amid political resistance. The 49% cap holds — a real but unscheduled upside option.
Anchor: 49% quota holds
05 · STRUCTURE
Well-capitalised developers compound share
Large listed developers cut launches and lean into premium: Sansiri grew 2025 sales ~35% to THB 25 bn and targets THB 48 bn presales for 2026 [Sansiri 2026]. The recovery favours balance-sheet strength over volume.
Anchor: THB 48 bn target

Section 04 · Macro & Policy
007 / 017

Soft growth, heavy debt, and a policy push to pull demand forward

The macro backdrop is the constraint, and policy is the counterweight. Weak growth and high household debt cap purchasing power; a fee-cut window and a lower policy rate are engineered to convert latent demand before mid-2026.

Growth & rates

The economy grew about 2.0% in 2025 and is seen near 1.6% in 2026 [IMF 2026], among the slowest in the region. The central bank cut its policy rate to 1.25% in December 2025 [BOT 2025], gradually easing mortgage costs for buyers entering 2026 — but rate relief alone has not restarted demand.

Household debt

Household debt sits near 89% of GDP, among Asia's highest, and is projected to ease toward 85% in 2026 [BOT 2025]. Tighter bank lending and weak confidence drove mortgage rejection to roughly 40% under THB 3m, and up to 70% at the entry tier [Terra 2025] — the single biggest brake on the mass market.

The stimulus window

The 2026 package cuts transfer and mortgage-registration fees to 0.01% for homes priced ≤THB 7m, and the central bank relaxed loan-to-value to 100% through June 2026 [MOF 2026]. The package shelved the bigger foreign-quota reform in favour of immediate, time-boxed relief.

Section 05 · Foreign Demand
009 / 017

China retreats and trades down; the foreign pool diversifies

The “rebound” is real but qualified. Foreign units edged up while value fell — buyers are present but spending less — and the nationality mix is rebalancing away from a single dominant source.

The China retreat

Chinese transfer value fell ~30% in 2025, with buyers shifting to smaller units averaging ~THB 3.8m [Nation 2026]. In Q1 2025 China still led at 37.8% of foreign units and 37.3% of value [REIC 2025], but its share of the foreign pool has roughly halved from the 2022–24 peak near 60% [Nation 2026], pressured by China's property downturn and tighter outbound capital controls.

The rebalance

Total foreign transfers split: 14,899 units in 2025 (+2.2%) but value down 10.7% to THB 60.9 bn [REIC 2025]. Russians became the second-largest group in Q1 2026 (383 units, +33%; value +68.7%) [Nation 2026]; Indian buyers take larger, family-sized units for residence. The pool is wider but lower-value.

Visa & quota levers

The Long-Term Resident visa and a Longstay route for buyers of units ≥THB 3m keep a structural foreign channel open [BOI 2025]. A cabinet-studied move to lift the quota to 75% and leases to 99 years was shelved in Sep 2025 [Bangkok Biz 2025] — an upside option, not a 2026 catalyst.

Section 06 · Market Sizing
011 / 017

Launches at a 15-year low, transfers stabilising on stimulus

Two clocks again diverge. New launches have collapsed as developers protect balance sheets, while ownership transfers steady on the fee window — the market is destocking, not re-accelerating.

Supply pullback

New condominium launches contracted about 41% to THB 77.2 bn in 2025, roughly 16,400 units — the lowest in ~15 years [Colliers 2025]. New supply in Bangkok is set to fall below 40,000 units in 2026 and toward ~20,000 in 2027 [CBRE 2026] as developers deliberately starve the pipeline to let stock clear.

Transfer trajectory

The destocking is showing. Q1 2026 nationwide ownership transfers rose 11.2% in volume and 3.1% in value year-on-year, lifted by the stimulus [REIC 2026]. For full-year 2026, the housing data centre projects a slight contraction of −1.1% in volume and −2.3% in value [REIC 2026] — front-loaded demand fading after June.

Footprint

Condominiums remain the single largest unsold category at nearly 38% of Bangkok's ~94,000 unsold units [REIC 2025]. The average Bangkok price held near THB 150k/sqm in 2025, up ~5% as the mix tilted to resilient higher tiers [Colliers 2025].

Section 06 · Segment Economics
012 / 017

A two-speed market: resilient top, oversupplied middle

The glut is concentrated, not general. Luxury and super-luxury clear at 80%+ sales; the THB 100k–150k/sqm mid-tier along suburban rail is where unsold inventory and mortgage rejection collide.

The resilient top

CBD stock averages ~THB 236k/sqm and high-end towers above THB 320k/sqm post sales rates over 80% [Colliers 2025]. Branded and super-luxury launches hold a 93% sales rate on existing supply, and downtown asking prices could rise up to 15% in 2026 on a thin, differentiated pipeline [CBRE 2026]. Cash-rich domestic and returning-expat demand insulates this tier.

The oversupplied middle

The mid-tier — THB 100k–150k/sqm, ~51% of Q1 2025 launches [Colliers 2025] — is the pressure point. Suburban rail corridors (outer Sukhumvit, Bang Na, Rama 9, the Green and Purple line extensions) hold the bulk of unsold condominium stock, where rental vacancy runs 15–17% [Colliers 2025].

The mass-market squeeze

Below THB 100k/sqm and under THB 3m per unit, demand exists but credit does not: mortgage rejection there reached ~40%, up to 70% at the entry tier [Terra 2025]. Mass-market product made up ~60% of 2025 launches by unit [Colliers 2025] — precisely where absorption is slowest.

Section 10 · Technology in the Channel
015 / 017

Digital sales channels and AI tools reshape buyer acquisition

In a buyer's market, distribution efficiency matters. Over 60% of property transactions now begin online [KenResearch 2025], and developers are adopting AI-assisted tools to qualify thinner, more international demand at lower cost.

The channel shift

Thailand's property-portal and proptech market is valued near USD 1.2 bn [KenResearch 2025], and over 60% of transactions now start online — a structural move that favours developers and agencies with digital reach into foreign buyer pools. With Chinese demand thinner and more diversified, low-cost cross-border lead generation is a competitive lever, not a nicety.

AI in the sales funnel

Adoption clusters around four uses: AI-driven buyer matching and lead scoring, automated multilingual enquiry handling, virtual tours and 3D walkthroughs that let overseas buyers inspect units remotely, and data-led pricing and absorption analytics. These compress acquisition cost in a market where every qualified buyer is harder won.

Where it pays

The return is sharpest in the oversupplied mid-tier, where marketing efficiency decides absorption speed. Virtual tours and remote-transaction tools extend reach to diversifying foreign buyers; analytics help developers price stock realistically rather than carry it. The technology does not create demand — it lowers the cost of finding and converting it.

Methodology & sources

How the numbers were derived, and the full citation for every named source used in this view.

Methodology

This view triangulates two evidence streams. Where a figure carries a named source tag (e.g. [REIC 2025], [Colliers 2025]), the underlying data comes from that source; the full citation appears at right. Where a figure carries [Kira estimates], KIRA analysts derived it from a blend of disclosed inputs — land-office transfer records, listed-developer disclosure, consultancy datasets — using the method disclosed in the relevant section.

Segment-level shares, the nationality-mix split, and the 2028 scenario fan are KIRA constructions reconciled against published transfer and survey data. Approximately 25% of quantitative claims are KIRA-derived; the remainder are externally cited. Research drew on both English and Thai-language sources; local sources are cited via English aliases for traceability.
