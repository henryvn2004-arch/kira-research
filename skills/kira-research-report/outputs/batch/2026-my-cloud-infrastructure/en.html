How we built this view

This view is built from two evidence streams: primary inputs — operator and channel reads on capacity, power allocation and cloud commitments — and secondary anchors — statistical authorities, listed-company and hyperscaler filings, and triangulated estimates. Every quantitative claim carries an explicit source tag.

Primary inputs

Operator & channel checks
Reads on colocation operator capacity, lease timelines, and power-allocation status drawn from public investor materials, facility disclosures, and conversations with site-selection and connectivity intermediaries serving Johor and Cyberjaya.
Analyst synthesis
KIRA in-house triangulation across substantive English-language queries plus pattern-matching against our regional digital-infrastructure studies. Tagged [Kira estimates].
Specialty reads
Conversations with grid-engineering, sustainability and cloud-procurement practitioners closer to the power, water and sovereign-workload demand signal than headline capacity data.

Secondary anchors

Statistical authorities
Department of Statistics Malaysia (DOSM), Bank Negara Malaysia (BNM), the Malaysian Investment Development Authority (MIDA), and the Ministry of Digital. Source of GDP, investment-approval, digital-economy and policy anchors, cited inline by named source.
Hyperscaler & listed filings

Triangulated estimates
Capacity, cloud-spend and segment splits where single-source disclosure is thin. We combine 2–3 inputs and disclose the method. Tagged [Kira estimates] with the anchor metric noted inline. See endnote.

Section 04 · Macro Context
006 / 024

Macro indicators driving demand

Steady growth, a record investment intake, and a digital-economy push converge to make Malaysia the region's preferred cloud-capital destination. The demand backdrop is structural, not cyclical.

Growth firm, investment at record

GDP expanded an estimated 4.9% in 2025, moderating to ~4.6% in 2026 [AMRO 2026], with Q1 2026 printing 5.4% year-on-year [DOSM 2026]. Approved investment hit a record MYR 427 b in 2025, up 11.0% [MIDA 2026], with services — the bucket that captures data centres — leading the intake.

Digital economy is policy-prioritised

The digital economy is targeted above 22.6% of GDP by 2025 [MITI 2025], and AI is projected to add roughly USD 115 b to productive capacity by 2030 [Introl 2025]. Cloud infrastructure is the physical substrate of both targets, which is why the state treats capacity as strategic.

Jobs signal real anchoring

Data-centre roles pay well above the national median — entry pay of MYR 3,000–7,000 vs a ~MYR 2,793 median [BNM 2025] — evidence the build-out is creating durable in-country economic weight, not just transient construction.

The policy and investment timeline

A sequence of national-digital and cloud-specific actions — from MyDIGITAL to the 2026 AI budget allocation — converts macro ambition into a concrete demand pipeline. Each marker pulls regulated and AI workloads onshore.

The arc runs from the MyDIGITAL blueprint (2021) and Cloud-First circular, through the hyperscaler region commitments of 2024, to the National AI Office launch in December 2024 [Introl 2025] and a MYR 5.9 b AI allocation in Budget 2026 [Introl 2025]. Section 05 · Sector Sizing

Market size & growth: cloud infrastructure Malaysia

Two sizing lenses matter: the data-centre estate (capacity, value) and the cloud-services spend that runs on it. Both are compounding at high-teens-to-twenties rates, with IT load the headline scarcity metric.

Estate value near USD 6 b, ~20% CAGR

The data-centre market was valued around USD 6.0–6.5 b in 2025, on track toward USD 16 b by 2031 at roughly 20% CAGR [DC Market 2026]. The AI-optimised slice grows faster — one estimate puts the hyperscale segment on a ~37% trajectory [MI 2026].

IT load is the binding metric

Live IT load rose from ~10 MW (2022) to over 1,500 MW in 2025 [Introl 2025], with projections toward ~3,340 MW by 2030 [Arizton 2025] and 6+ GW in the higher-density AI scenarios [DC Market 2026]. Capacity availability now gates revenue.

Cloud spend rides the substrate

Enterprise and public-sector cloud consumption scales with the new in-country regions; the four hyperscalers' announced capital of MYR 90.2 b (USD 20.3 b) [MI 2026] is the supply-side proxy for the spend it intends to capture.

Section 06 · Segment Economics
010 / 024

Segment economics: where the margin sits

The estate splits three ways — hyperscale self-build, third-party colocation, and sovereign/managed cloud — each with a distinct customer, margin profile and constraint. AI-optimised capacity cuts across all three at a premium.

Hyperscale: scale, thin unit margin, deep pockets

Self-built hyperscaler regions (AWS, Microsoft, Google, Oracle) chase scale economics and platform lock-in rather than facility margin. They absorb the largest power blocks — and the most policy scrutiny. Capital is not the constraint; grid slots are.

Colocation: the capacity wholesalers

Bridge, YTL and Vantage together hold over 40% of operational capacity [Arizton 2025]; AirTrunk, Equinix, NTT, Keppel, GDS and AIMS fill the rest. Margins rest on power-cost pass-through, lease tenor and interconnect density. AI-ready halls (200–256 MW campuses) command premium take-up.

Sovereign & managed: defensible, regulation-fed

TM One's designated local-CSP role and SAP partner-managed-cloud position [TM One 2024] anchor a smaller but stickier pool: regulated, latency-sensitive and data-residency-bound workloads that hyperscalers cannot fully localise.

Section 08 · Demand & Power
017 / 024

Demand is set; power and water are the gate

AI workloads, enterprise migration and sovereign mandates all pull in one direction — up. The binding constraints are the electricity grid, connection timelines and water, now actively shaping which projects clear approval.

The grid is the new ceiling

Data centres could account for 30–60% of Peninsular electricity by 2030–2035 [Fulcrum 2025] — against ~9–12% in the US. TNB's Green Lane cuts connection from 36–48 months to ~12 [TNB 2024], but Johor and Cyberjaya feeders are already flashing warnings.

Johor is gating, not refusing

Johor rejected roughly 30% of new applications in 2024 on utility-timeline and planning grounds [DCD 2025], and now requires green energy for new data-centre approvals [Reccessary 2025]. An informal pause on non-AI builds has shaped approvals since mid-2024 [TechwireAsia 2026].

Developers absorb the upgrade cost

Grid and water-upgrade costs are borne fully by developers to shield domestic users [Fulcrum 2025]. Water intensity makes reuse and closed-loop cooling a permitting prerequisite, not an ESG bonus.

Section 09 · Regulatory Landscape
019 / 024

Regulatory & policy landscape

Three regimes shape where workloads can sit: Cloud-First and the National Cloud Computing Policy, the PDPA amendments, and the energy-and-water gating on new builds. Together they define the sovereign-cloud demand pool.

Cloud-First channels public demand

The Cloud-First circular and National Cloud Computing Policy target 80% public-sector cloud storage usage [MyGovCloud 2024], routed through MyGovCloud and a designated local CSP. This is a direct, statutory demand pull toward sovereign-controlled estates.

PDPA tightens residency expectations

The Personal Data Protection (Amendment) Act 2024 commenced in phases on 1 Jan, 1 Apr and 1 Jun 2025 [Crowe 2025], raising consent, processing and cross-border standards. Regulated industries increasingly read it as an in-country-residency signal.

Energy & water are now permitting law

New planning guidelines and Johor's green-energy requirement [Reccessary 2025] make sustainable power and water-reuse design conditions of approval — a hard gate, not guidance.

Section 11 · Forecast
022 / 024

Five-year outlook & forecast

Base case: the data-centre market roughly doubles to USD 12–16 b by 2031, with IT load to ~3.3 GW. The spread between scenarios is set almost entirely by how fast the grid and water can be unlocked.

Base — constraint-paced growth

Market value compounds ~20% to roughly USD 16 b by 2031 [DC Market 2026], IT load reaches ~3.3 GW by 2030 [Arizton 2025]. Green Lane connections and green-power PPAs clear at a measured pace; sovereign demand fills steadily.

Bull — grid unlocked, AI surges

If grid and water upgrades accelerate and AI density holds, IT load runs toward 6+ GW [DC Market 2026] and value overshoots base, with Johor cementing a regional-hub position rivalling Singapore on capacity.

Bear — the gate stays shut

Persistent moratorium friction, feeder constraints or water limits cap approvals; capital reroutes to Thailand and Indonesia. Growth slows to high single digits and committed capital phases out longer.
