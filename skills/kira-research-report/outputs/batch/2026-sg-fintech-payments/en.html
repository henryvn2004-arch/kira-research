This view is built from two evidence streams: primary inputs — regulator releases, operator disclosures, and analyst synthesis — and secondary anchors — central-bank statistics, scheme operator data, and triangulated estimates. Every quantitative claim carries an explicit source tag.

Scheme operator reads. Disclosures from NETS, the Association of Banks in Singapore, and the BIS Innovation Hub on PayNow, SGQR, and Project Nexus throughput and acceptance.

Analyst synthesis. KIRA in-house triangulation across multiple disclosed inputs where no single source covers a figure. Tagged inline as [Kira estimates].

Secondary anchors

Issuer & operator filings. Disclosures from named stablecoin issuers and payment platforms — cited by entity and period.

Triangulated estimates. Where coverage is partial, we combine two to three inputs and disclose the method. Tagged [Kira estimates]. Singapore's business press leads in English, so this view runs an English-language source base.

Section 04 · Market sizing
007 · SG-PAY-2026

A large, near-cashless pool growing into the high single digits

Singapore's payment value is sizeable and near-saturated on acceptance; the value migration is toward cross-border throughput and tokenised settlement rather than incremental domestic volume.

Mature core, compounding edges

The cards-and-payments value pool sits near USD 24 bn in 2025 [Kira estimates], compounding at roughly 9-10% toward the early 2030s. Digital payment volumes cleared USD 92 bn in 2025 [SG Payment Yearbook 2025]; the core is mature, so growth concentrates at the edges.

Wallets lead, A2A rises

Digital wallets now lead e-commerce, at 39% of online transactions [SG Payment Yearbook 2025]. Among Gen Z, 68% prefer PayNow account-to-account transfers [SG Payment Yearbook 2025] — the structural pull toward real-time, bank-rail settlement over card interchange.

Where value migrates

Domestic interchange and acceptance are flat-to-declining as a growth source. The rising lines are cross-border QR throughput and tokenised wholesale settlement — the two rails this report tracks [Kira estimates].

Section 07 · Cross-border QR
010 · SG-PAY-2026

Bilateral links proved demand; multilateral scaling is the unlock

Each bilateral corridor is a one-off integration. The structural problem is that linking N economies pairwise needs N-squared connections — which is precisely why a shared multilateral standard changes the economics.

The corridors live today

Singapore runs live QR linkages with Malaysia (NETS-DuitNow) and Indonesia (NETS QR-QRIS) [MAS 2025], letting travellers scan home-app QR codes at foreign merchants. Thailand's PromptPay and a wider ASEAN set follow the same account-to-account model.

The N-squared problem

Bilateral links do not compound. Connecting five economies pairwise needs ten integrations [Kira estimates]; each new corridor adds bespoke build, settlement, and FX arrangements. The model proves demand but caps scale.

The multilateral unlock

A shared rail collapses the count to one connection per economy [Kira estimates]. That is the Project Nexus thesis — a single standardised link replacing the corridor stack, with central-bank settlement behind it [BIS 2025].

What it is

Project Nexus is a BIS Innovation Hub design that connects domestic instant-payment systems through one standardised link per country instead of bilateral corridors [BIS 2025]. Nexus Global Payments, a non-profit incorporated in Singapore, operationalises and manages the rail [BIS 2025].

Who is in

The live-implementation phase covers the IPS operators of India, Malaysia, the Philippines, Singapore and Thailand, with Bank Indonesia as special observer [BIS 2025]. A live pilot ran ahead of full implementation targeted for 2026.

Why it matters for Singapore

  • PayNow becomes a regional node — Singapore's IPS plugs into a multi-economy rail through a single connection
  • Corridor stack collapses — replaces bespoke bilateral integrations with a shared standard
  • Settlement & FX standardised — central-bank-grade settlement behind retail QR
  • Hub positioning — NGP's Singapore incorporation anchors governance locally

The honest read

Go-live timelines for multi-central-bank infrastructure slip. The 2026 target is credible but contingent on each IPS operator's readiness and commercial model [Kira estimates] — the gating risk this report flags.

Section 10 · Stablecoin framework
013 · SG-PAY-2026

A regulated label that gates entry and rewards reserves

MAS's single-currency stablecoin framework applies to SGD- and G10-pegged coins issued in Singapore. The bar is deliberately high — and that bar is the moat for licensed issuers.

Scope & the MAS-regulated label

The framework covers single-currency stablecoins pegged to SGD or a G10 currency, issued in Singapore [MAS 2025]. Only compliant issuers may use the "MAS-regulated stablecoin" label; coins pegged to other assets stay under digital-payment-token rules [MAS 2025].

Reserves & redemption

Issuers must hold 100% reserves in the peg currency — cash, equivalents, or short government debt — with segregated accounts, published monthly attestations, and redemption at par within five business days [MAS 2025]. The rules take full effect in 2026.

Why the bar is the moat

High capital, custody, and disclosure requirements deter casual entrants. The framework converts compliance into credibility — the licensed label is a B2B trust signal that unlicensed coins cannot match [Kira estimates].

Section 12 · AI impact
015 · SG-PAY-2026

AI lands first in the cross-border risk and FX layer

For payments, the highest-ROI AI use cases sit in fraud and sanctions screening, FX routing, and compliance automation — exactly the friction points that multilateral and tokenised rails amplify.

Fraud & sanctions screening

Cross-border rails widen the attack surface; rule-based monitoring runs high false-positive rates. AI screening cuts cost per alert and tightens detection across multi-economy flows [Kira estimates].

FX & routing optimisation

Multilateral settlement multiplies currency pairs. AI-driven FX routing and liquidity optimisation lower the cost of each cross-border leg — a direct margin lever on Nexus-style volume [Kira estimates].

Compliance by design

Tokenised rails embed compliance into the infrastructure. AI document and transaction understanding automates KYC and reporting — material given Singapore's regulated-settlement posture [Kira estimates].

Singapore's business press leads in English, so this view runs an English-language source base; cross-border counterpart sources (Bank Indonesia, Bank Negara Malaysia, BIS) are cited via English aliases.
