Section 03 · Executive Summary

Philippine nickel — the raw-ore model meets its downstream reckoning

The Philippines is the world's second-largest mined-nickel producer, yet ships almost all of it as raw ore. The 2026 contest is whether policy can force value-add without breaking the cash engine. Three converging instruments — the SB 2826 export-ban bill, the RA 12253 margin-based fiscal regime live since February 2026, and a USD 12 bn HPAL pipeline — decide whether processing lands or capital simply waits out the cycle.

Volume leader, value laggard

The Philippines mined ~330 kt of contained nickel in 2024 [USGS 2025], second only to Indonesia, and supplies roughly 95% of the world's traded nickel ore [Benchmark 2025]. But almost all of it leaves as unprocessed laterite — only two HPAL plants operate, both shipping intermediate product to Japan for refining [Trade.gov 2025]. Raw ore averaged ~USD 26/wmt in 2025 [NAC FY2025]; battery-grade sulfate clears multiples higher. The value sits downstream, and the Philippines has not captured it.

Three policy levers now in play

The Senate passed SB 2826 in February 2025 to ban raw-ore exports five years after enactment [Senate PR 2025]; it sits in bicameral review. RA 12253's margin-based royalty and windfall tax took effect February 2026 [PwC PH 2026]. Both raise the cost of staying raw — but a multi-year nickel surplus and weak prices blunt the investment case for processing.

Five strategic implications

What market participants should do with the next 18 months — the window between the RA 12253 fiscal regime taking effect and the SB 2826 bill clearing or stalling in Congress.

01
Treat the export ban as a five-year clock, not a switch
SB 2826 imposes the ban five years after signing, not on day one [Senate PR 2025]. Capital should model a 2030-2031 effective date if the bill clears, and use the runway to lock feedstock-linked processing options rather than wait for certainty.
BAN LAG 5 yrs post-enactment
02
Margin-based royalty rewards efficient, integrated operators
RA 12253 ties royalty to profit margin (1-5%) plus a windfall tax above 30% margin [PwC PH 2026]. Low-cost, integrated producers keep more; marginal high-strip-ratio mines face the 0.1% floor but thin economics. The regime accelerates consolidation.
ROYALTY 1-5% margin-based
03
HPAL economics hinge on the price floor, not the policy
The ~USD 12 bn HPAL pipeline [BOI 2025] stalled once on Indonesian oversupply and a ~30% price fall [Mining.com 2025]. Processing FID needs a credible USD 17k+/t floor; below that, ore export remains the rational default regardless of mandate.

04
Permitting and FPIC are the binding constraint, not geology
EO 130 reopened new mineral agreements [PNA 2021], but LGU consent and Indigenous FPIC still gate every project [ICLG 2026]. Social licence, not reserves, sets timelines. Budget 3-5 years and local benefit-sharing for any greenfield.
RESERVES ~1 USD tn untapped
05
Diversify offtake away from a single Chinese buyer
China took ~66% of Philippine ore exports in 2024, down from 78% as regional rivals siphoned volume [Benchmark 2025]. Japan and Korea offer route diversity and downstream partnership; concentration risk is now a board-level metric.

Output up, value down — the price story of 2024

Ore tonnage slipped 6% to 33.4 M dmt in 2024 [MGB 2025], while the value of nickel production fell 15% to PHP 56.7 bn as the average nickel price dropped ~22% to USD 16,814/t [MGB 2025]. The sector's first-semester 2025 metallic output rebounded to PHP 140 bn (+22% YoY), but that lift was gold-led, not nickel-led [MGB 2025].

Big globally, small domestically

Mining contributed just 0.51% of GDP in 2024 on PHP 316 bn of gross production value [PSA 2025], despite the Philippines holding an estimated USD 1 tn in untapped copper, gold, nickel, zinc and silver [ICLG 2026]. Total mineral exports reached USD 7.38 bn [MGB 2025]. The value-capture gap is the structural opportunity the 2026 reforms target.

The value-add ladder: where the margin actually lives

Nickel laterite climbs from mine-mouth ore to battery-grade sulfate through several processing rungs. Each rung multiplies realised value — and the Philippines today sits almost entirely on the bottom two.

Two existing rungs, two missing ones

Saprolite feeds ferronickel and nickel pig iron; limonite feeds HPAL [Trade.gov 2025]. The Philippines runs only two HPAL plants — Coral Bay (Palawan) and Taganito (Surigao) — both shipping mixed sulfide to Japan for final refining [Trade.gov 2025].

Precursor, cathode and cell-grade sulfate are not made domestically. The captured margin stops at intermediate.

Ore export realisation~USD 26/wmt
Battery sulfate17-20k USD/t
Domestic HPAL plants2
Domestic refineries0

Why the ladder matters for policy

The economic logic behind SB 2826 and RA 12253 is to push capital up the ladder. But each rung needs scale, cheap power, sulfuric acid supply and a credible nickel price — none guaranteed in a surplus market [ING 2026]. A mandate cannot manufacture HPAL returns if the floor price sits below project economics.

The integration premium

Operators that own ore, HPAL and offtake capture margin at every rung and hedge price volatility internally. That integration premium — visible in Nickel Asia's JV structure [NAC FY2025] — is precisely what RA 12253's ring-fencing and margin-based royalty now reward over standalone raw exporters [PwC PH 2026].

Philippine nickel reaches end-markets through two distinct channels: bulk saprolite/limonite ore shipped mainly to Chinese NPI smelters, and HPAL intermediate routed to Japanese refiners. The mix — and its concentration — defines the sector's demand-side risk.

One buyer, falling share

China took ~66% of Philippine ore exports in 2024, down from 78% the prior year as regional neighbours siphoned ore for their own processing hubs [Benchmark 2025]. Concentration is easing but still acute.

Japan anchors the HPAL channel, taking mixed sulfide from Coral Bay and Taganito for refining [Trade.gov 2025] — a higher-value, stickier relationship than spot ore.

Demand pull: battery vs stainless

Battery-grade nickel demand is set to roughly triple by 2030 on mid- and high-performance EVs in Western markets [Benchmark 2025], but LFP chemistry's rise — especially in China — tempers the pace [ING 2026]. Stainless steel still anchors near-term ore demand, keeping NPI-grade saprolite the volume mainstay.

Why diversification is now strategic

A single dominant buyer leaves Philippine exporters exposed to Chinese demand cycles and policy. Japan and Korea offer not just route diversity but downstream-partnership pathways that align with the value-add agenda — the channel and the policy goal point the same way.

Section 09 · Political Risk
016 / 021

The permitting and social-licence chokepoint

Reserves are not the constraint. The binding limit on Philippine mining expansion is the layered consent regime — national permits, local-government approval, and Indigenous free, prior and informed consent — compounded by environmental opposition and the politics of who owns processing capacity.

Layered consent, long timelines

EO 130 reopened mineral agreements at the national level [PNA 2021], but the Local Government Code gives LGUs authority to evaluate and consent to each project in their jurisdiction [ICLG 2026]. On Indigenous ancestral domains, FPIC under IPRA adds a further gate. A single project can require national, provincial, municipal and tribal sign-off — any of which can stall it for years.

Environmental and community opposition

Critics argue lifting the moratorium exposed water, food supply, forests and Indigenous communities to mining pressure [Mongabay 2021]. Reporting on the nickel belt has documented environmental harm in producing provinces [Mongabay 2025]. Social licence, once lost, is slow to rebuild and shapes both timelines and reputational risk for offtakers downstream.

The ownership question

Much of the prospective processing capital is Chinese [Mining.com 2025]. Foreign ownership of strategic mineral processing sits at the intersection of investment policy and national-security debate — a political variable that can move independently of commercial merit.

Section 10 · AI Impact
018 / 021

AI in Philippine nickel mining: where it moves the needle

Adoption is early and operational. The economic case rests on cost-per-tonne reduction and compliance-cost avoidance — not new revenue — which fits a price-taking sector where margin defence is the whole game.

Why operational, not transformational

Philippine producers sell into global benchmark prices they cannot influence [ING 2026]. The realistic AI dividend is therefore cost and compliance: tighter grade control that lifts realised price per shipment, fuel and cycle-time savings on haulage, and automated ESG monitoring that protects social licence.

For the two HPAL plants and any future processing, predictive maintenance carries the largest single prize — unplanned downtime on an acid-leach line runs into six figures per day [Kira estimates].

Adoption reality

Deployment is concentrated among the listed leaders with the balance sheet to fund it; the long tail of exporters lags. The technology gap mirrors the consolidation story elsewhere in this report.

Base case — surplus caps the price to 2030

Consensus puts nickel near USD 15.5k in 2026 rising to ~USD 16k in 2027 [World Bank 2026], with the market in surplus until ~2030 on Indonesian and Chinese supply growth [Macquarie 2025]. BMI sees ~USD 16.7k in 2027 climbing to USD 19k by 2030 as the market tips toward deficit [BMI 2026]. Volume holds; value recovers only late in the window.

Bull — Indonesian supply discipline

If Indonesia curbs output via RKAB quotas, prices could stabilise at USD 19-20k in 2026 [AsiaToday 2026] and push higher — lifting the FID case for Philippine HPAL.

Bear — LFP and oversupply persist

Faster LFP substitution and a widening ~250 kt surplus by 2027 [ING 2026] keep prices in the mid-teens, stranding the processing pipeline and pressuring marginal mines under RA 12253.

Key sensitivities

Indonesian RKAB policy, EV chemistry mix, the SB 2826 timeline, and the peso/USD rate — most revenue is USD, most cost local — together set realised margin more than any domestic lever.

The policy-price interaction

The central tension of this report resolves on price. A downstream mandate only works if the price floor makes processing bankable. In the base and bull cases, RA 12253's integration incentive and a credible SB 2826 deadline can together pull capital up the value ladder. In the bear case, the same instruments risk mine closures and lost revenue without delivering the processing they intend [Fortune 2025].

Probability-weighted read

KIRA's base case is the most likely: a gradual recovery with partial downstream conversion led by the integrated incumbents, and a long tail that consolidates or exits under fiscal and ban pressure [Kira estimates].

Positioning by scenario

  • →Incumbent integrated producers: secure HPAL options now; the margin-based regime rewards you in every scenario.
  • →Ore-export-only operators: diversify offtake and prepare for consolidation; the ban clock and royalty squeeze are structural.
  • →Inbound processing capital: price the political and ownership risk explicitly; FID timing should track the price floor, not the mandate.
  • →Offtakers (Japan/Korea): the diversification window is open — partnership now buys supply security later.
