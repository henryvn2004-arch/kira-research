AUSTRALIA · EDUCATION EXPORT · 2027

Australia's international education export at a managed-growth inflection

The AUD 53.6 bn export engine enters 2027 inside a cap, not a boom: enrolment is now policy-rationed at 295,000 new starts, Chinese demand has stalled rather than collapsed, and the Southeast Asian pipeline has shifted from a diversification slogan to the explicit allocation lever. The question is no longer how fast the sector can grow — it is which providers and which source markets capture the rationed seats.

The export base

International education generated AUD 53.6 bn in export income in FY25 — AUD 23.5 bn in tuition and AUD 29.9 bn in goods and services spend [DoE 2025]. It is Australia's fourth-largest export and the largest services export, supporting roughly 240,000 jobs [UA 2025]. Five source countries supply 58% of students — China (24%), India (16%), Nepal (9%), Vietnam (5%) and Bangladesh (4%) [DoE 2025].

The 2027 shift

Growth is now administered. The National Planning Level rises to 295,000 new starts for 2026 [DoE 2025], with additional places awarded on two criteria — Southeast Asian engagement and student housing delivery. Chinese visa grants fell 25% in H2 2025 [THE 2025] yet China holds its 24% share; the slope, not the level, is the concern. The Southeast Asian pipeline is the policy-favoured replacement vector.

Section 04 · Macro & policy frame
07 / 20

The managed system and its allocation levers

Australia enters 2027 with international education explicitly administered: a planning-level cap, allocation criteria that privilege Southeast Asian engagement and housing, and a visa-charge regime that shapes who clears the funnel.

From boom to managed growth

The National Planning Level caps new commencements at 295,000 for 2026, up 9% from the 270,000 set for 2025 [DoE 2025]. No active provider receives a 2026 allocation below its 2025 level; public universities competed for an additional 17,500 growth places on top of the base [DoE 2025]. The VET sector, by contrast, is not capped — managed-growth arrangements give large providers ~5% growth and TAFEs priority visa processing [DEWR 2025].

Allocation rewards policy delivery, not ranking

Growth places were awarded on two demonstrated criteria: increased Southeast Asian engagement — consistent with Invested: Australia's Southeast Asia Economic Strategy to 2040 — and provision of student accommodation [DoE 2025]. For the first time, an institution's incremental international revenue depends on its housing pipeline and SEA partnerships rather than its brand pull [Kira estimates].

Visa settings as a throttle

The student visa application charge doubled to AUD 2,000 on 1 July 2025 [DHA 2025]. Combined with elevated refusal rates — 32.5% in February 2026 across the cohort [Academic Jobs 2026] — the visa regime now functions as a demand throttle independent of the headline cap. SEA markets carry comparatively low rejection rates (~4–15%) [ICEF 2025].

Section 05 · Market sizing
09 / 20

Export value, enrolments and the sector split

Australia's education export is large, concentrated, and increasingly bifurcated: degree-level coursework holds the value and the resilience, while pathway and language segments absorb the policy shocks first.

AUD 53.6 bn — value sits in spend, not just tuition

FY25 export income split AUD 23.5 bn tuition / AUD 29.9 bn goods-and-services spend [DoE 2025]. International students generate more than 80% of the tertiary sector's total revenue on some measures [AIIA 2025], and the RBA has flagged the cohort as a material driver of GDP and net migration [RBA 2025]. The goods-and-services half — rent, retail, transport — is why the housing-allocation lever matters economically, not just politically.

Enrolments plateaued on policy, not demand

Enrolments reached ~817,000 in the year to May 2025 — +1% on 2024 and +18% on 2019 [DoE 2025]. But YTD February 2026 commencements fell 7.3% year-on-year [DoE 2026], and student visas granted fell 36% from the FY23 peak to 372,000 in FY25 [DoE 2025]. The level holds; the leading edge is contracting under the cap and the visa charge.

Source concentration: 58% from five markets

China (24%), India (16%), Nepal (9%), Vietnam (5%) and Bangladesh (4%) together supply 58% of students [DoE 2025]. VET Indian enrolments collapsed from 85,844 in 2022–23 to ~41,700 by mid-2025 [DEWR 2025] — the sharpest single shift, and a warning on how fast a sub-segment can unwind.

Chinese demand: stall, not collapse

China still supplies 24% of Australia's international students and 86% of its new Chinese arrivals concentrate in the Group of Eight. Section 06 · Chinese demand
11 / 20

China: the recovery is a defence of yield

The headline "Chinese student recovery" narrative overstates the trajectory. China remains the largest and highest-yielding source market, but the leading indicators — applications, grants, demographics — all point to a plateau, with the value concentrated in Go8 postgraduate coursework.

The level holds — share steady at 24%

China held a steady 24% of international students into early 2026 [DoE 2026], and Chinese enrolments at the Group of Eight sit ~31% above their 2019 base [THE 2025]. By enrolled stock, China is not in retreat. The yield per Chinese enrolment — concentrated in higher-cost postgraduate coursework — is the highest in the source-market book [Kira estimates].

The leading edge is contracting

Chinese higher-education visa grants fell 25% in H2 2025 versus H2 2024, and applications fell 26% [THE 2025]. Commencements ran down ~9% over January–September 2025 [DoE 2026]. The plateau is structural: declining household income, a shrinking youth cohort, and a "tremendous improvement" in the perceived quality of Chinese universities have all compressed outbound demand [THE 2025].

Concentration is the exposure

86% of new Chinese students enrolled in the Go8 at the start of 2024 [DoE 2024]. That concentration means the China plateau lands disproportionately on a handful of high-value institutions — the same providers most exposed to any further bilateral or visa-policy shock. Diversification is a risk-management imperative, not a growth aspiration.

Vietnam leads; Indonesia and the Philippines follow

The Southeast Asian pipeline is the one source-market story where policy, demography, and recruitment economics point the same way. Three markets — Vietnam, Indonesia, the Philippines — carry the next wave, with Vietnam the highest-conviction near-term vector.

VECTOR 01 · HIGH CONVICTION
Vietnam

Already 5% of enrolments and among the fastest-growing source markets [DoE 2025]. Top scholarship-offer destination in 2025, low visa-rejection band, and a young, aspirational cohort. Australia has emerged as a top Asian recruitment market for Vietnam [ICEF 2025].

The demographic prize: the largest SEA population and the explicit focus of the Invested 2040 strategy [DFAT 2024]. Australian universities are building TNE branch presence in-country to capture demand that the onshore cap cannot absorb [Kira estimates].

The Philippines

Section 09 · Transnational education
17 / 20

Offshore delivery: the revenue hedge under the cap

Transnational education — Australian degrees delivered outside Australia via branch campuses, offshore partnerships, twinning, or online models — is the one growth surface the onshore cap does not touch. It is lower-margin per head but uncapped, and it sits squarely inside the Southeast Asia strategy.

Why TNE now: the cap makes it strategic

With onshore commencements rationed at 295,000 and the visa charge throttling the funnel, offshore delivery is the only uncapped volume lever [Kira estimates]. Universities are investing in branch campuses and hybrid models to stabilise revenue under capped onshore numbers, with TNE enrolments projected to grow materially over the next several years [Kira estimates].

Where the offshore build is happening

Indonesia and India are the headline TNE expansion markets, with Vietnam close behind [Kira estimates]. Indonesia is the explicit demographic core of the Invested 2040 strategy [DFAT 2024]; an in-country campus captures demand the onshore cap cannot, while satisfying the SEA-engagement allocation criterion simultaneously — a rare two-for-one.

The trade-off: margin for resilience

TNE yields less per student than an onshore enrolment — no goods-and-services spend accrues to Australia, and fee levels track local affordability [Kira estimates]. But it diversifies revenue away from a single-country, single-policy choke point. For boards weighing onshore China concentration against offshore SEA breadth, TNE is the resilience trade.

Five-year outlook: forecast to 2030

The base case sees export income holding around AUD 50–55 bn through 2027 and recovering toward AUD 58–62 bn by 2030 as the cap lifts gradually and value concentrates upward. China's share drifts from 24% toward ~20% as Southeast Asia's combined share rises past 18%. Transnational education becomes the fastest-growing revenue line. The tail risk is a further visa-policy tightening or a bilateral shock concentrated on the China-weighted Go8.

Section 10 · Five-year outlook
19 / 20

Forecast to 2030: managed recovery, reweighted mix

The 2027–2030 trajectory is one of administered recovery: value holds and then firms as the cap lifts gradually, but the source-market mix reweights decisively toward Southeast Asia and offshore delivery, with China defending rather than expanding its high-yield base.

Value: holds near AUD 50–55 bn, then firms

Export income held at AUD 53.6 bn in FY25 [DoE 2025]; KIRA's base case sees it soft to ~AUD 51 bn in FY26 as commencement declines feed through, then recover toward AUD 58–62 bn by 2030 as the cap lifts and value concentrates in degree-level coursework [Kira estimates]. The recovery is value-led, not volume-led — the cap structurally limits the volume path.

Mix: China defends, SEA expands

China's share drifts from 24% toward ~20% by 2030 as outbound demand plateaus [Kira estimates]; combined Southeast Asian share rises past 18% on the back of Vietnam, Indonesia and the Philippines [Kira estimates]. The reweighting is policy-engineered — the allocation criteria are explicitly designed to produce it.

Risks: visa policy and bilateral concentration

The principal downside is a further visa-charge or refusal-rate tightening that deepens the funnel compression already visible in the 32.5% February 2026 refusal rate [Academic Jobs 2026]. The tail risk is a bilateral or policy shock concentrated on the China-weighted Go8 — the segment least able to absorb it. TNE growth is the hedge against both.
