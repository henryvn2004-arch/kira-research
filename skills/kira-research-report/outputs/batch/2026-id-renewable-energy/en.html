The ambition is real, but the build run-rate is not

RUPTL 2025-2034 lifts planned renewable capacity from 20.9 GW to 52.9 GW including storage, with 42.6 GW of generation: solar 17.1 GW, hydro 11.7 GW, wind 7.2 GW, geothermal 5.2 GW [RUPTL 2025]. Yet the 2025 geothermal add was just 112.7 MW [ThinkGeo 2026] — roughly a fifth of the 500-600 MW/year the pathway implies. Demand is structural: MEMR forecasts 4.8-5.2% annual electricity-demand growth [MEMR 2025].

Procurement reform is the swing variable

MEMR Reg 5/2025 replaces the PPA provisions of MEMR 10/2017, adds a 30-year build-own-operate structure, sharpens risk allocation, and caps the procurement clock at 180 days for direct selection, 90 days for direct appointment [MEMR Reg 5/2025]. The reform attacks the binding constraint — not resource, but the time and bankability gap between award and financial close.

Five strategic implications for market participants

Where capital, project effort, and policy engagement should concentrate over the next 18-24 months — ranked by the asymmetry of return given the procurement reset and the geothermal exploration-risk wall.

01
Position for the 90/180-day procurement clock
MEMR Reg 5/2025 caps direct appointment at 90 days and direct selection at 180 days [MEMR Reg 5/2025]. Developers with shovel-ready feasibility, secured land, and pre-arranged debt convert the faster clock into first-mover COD slots. Slow-prep bidders lose the window.

Underwrite the exploration-risk wall, not just the plant

Treat the IPP mandate as the entry door

Section 04 · Macro context

Demand growth is the structural tailwind

Indonesia grew 5.61% YoY in Q1 2026 [BPS 2026], its fastest since 2021, with gross fixed capital formation up 5.96%. Electricity demand tracks growth at 4.8-5.2% a year [MEMR 2025] and is set to roughly quadruple by 2050 — the demand case for new clean capacity is not in question.

Growth holding above 5%

Q1 2026 GDP reached IDR 6,187.2 trn at current prices, up 5.61% YoY [BPS 2026]. Processing industry, trade, and construction led on the production side; gross fixed capital formation grew 5.96%, signalling the investment appetite the power build needs. Full-year 2026 is tracking around 5% [BPS 2026].

Electricity demand compounds

MEMR projects 4.8-5.2% annual electricity-demand growth and a roughly fourfold rise by 2050 [MEMR 2025]. Industrialisation, data centres, EV charging, and the captive-power pull (smelters, downstream metals) are the marginal drivers — each lifts baseload, where geothermal fits.

The transition is capital-hungry

Meeting the net-zero-2060 path needs ~USD 16 bn/year of renewable investment [WRI 2024] against the USD 3 bn/year that flowed in 2015-2021 — a 5x step-up. The capital gap, not the demand, is the binding macro constraint.

Coal still anchors the grid — that is the policy gap

The renewable share reached only ~14% in late 2024 against a 23%-by-2025 target [Ember 2025], and coal supplies roughly two-thirds of generation. The revised National Energy Policy is expected to cut the 2030 renewable target to 19-21% — an honest reset that reframes geothermal as a scarce firm-clean option.

The mix is fossil-heavy

Coal supplied about 62% of generation in 2023 and near 68% in more recent reads [Ember 2025]. The renewable share hit only ~13.9% by December 2024 [Ember 2025] — far short of the 23%-by-2025 National Energy Policy goal set in 2014.

Targets are being reset down

The revised National Energy Policy is expected to lower the renewable target to 19-21% by 2030 [Ember 2025], a realistic recalibration after years of slow additions. RUPTL 2025-2034 still front-loads thermal: 27.9 GW lands in 2025-2029, with most renewables and storage back-loaded to 2030-2034 [RUPTL 2025].

JETP frames the finance

The Just Energy Transition Partnership pledges USD 20 bn of public and private finance [WRI 2024]; a meaningful slice is expected to back geothermal. Disbursement pace and bankable-project supply — not the headline pledge — will decide its impact.

Section 05 · Sector overview & sizing
010 / 029

A second-largest fleet on an eighth of the resource

Installed geothermal capacity reached 2.74 GW in 2025 [ThinkGeo 2026], up from 2.64 GW at end-2024 — second worldwide. Against a national resource of ~23.7 GW across 362 identified sites [MEMR EBTKE 2025], utilisation sits near 12%. The constraint is delivery cadence, not geology.

Resource is vast, utilisation thin

MEMR has identified ~23.7 GW of geothermal potential across 362 sites [MEMR EBTKE 2025], holding roughly 40% of global reserves. Installed 2.74 GW means ~12% utilisation [ThinkGeo 2026]. Sixty-two geothermal work areas plus twelve survey-and-exploration assignment zones are staged for tender via the Genesis platform [MEMR EBTKE 2025].

The run-rate is the problem

Over 2017-2023 the fleet added only ~789 MW — about 113 MW/year [IDX Channel 2025]. The 2025 add was 112.7 MW [ThinkGeo 2026], versus the 500-600 MW/year the 5.2 GW pathway implies. The gap between target and run-rate is the central sizing tension of this report.

RUPTL sets the 2034 mark

RUPTL 2025-2034 allocates 5.2 GW of new geothermal with a 0.9 GW interim mark by 2029 [RUPTL 2025]. Total renewables-plus-storage reaches 52.9 GW; geothermal is the smallest-MW but highest-capacity-factor block.

011 / 029

The renewable build is a 42.6 GW generation programme

Beyond geothermal, RUPTL 2025-2034 commits 42.6 GW of renewable generation plus 10.3 GW of storage [RUPTL 2025]. Solar leads on nameplate at 17.1 GW; geothermal is small in MW but carries the firmest delivery profile. Reading the programme by technology shows where capital and procurement effort concentrate.

Solar leads nameplate, geothermal leads firmness

The 2025-2034 renewable add splits as solar 17.1 GW, hydro 11.7 GW, wind 7.2 GW, geothermal 5.2 GW, bioenergy 0.9 GW, plus 0.5 GW nuclear [RUPTL 2025]. Solar dominates MW, but its ~15-20% capacity factor means delivered energy ranks differently — geothermal and hydro punch above their nameplate.

Storage is now an explicit line

RUPTL adds 10.3 GW of storage (battery + pumped hydro) [RUPTL 2025] to firm the intermittent build — the first plan to treat storage as a sized capacity block rather than an afterthought. It changes the economics of solar and wind bids.

Back-loaded by design

Of the 69.5 GW total capacity add, 27.9 GW lands in 2025-2029 (mostly thermal) and 41.6 GW in 2030-2034 (mostly renewables and storage) [RUPTL 2025]. The renewable surge is a second-half programme — entry timing should respect that.

Section 06 · Segment economics
012 / 029

Geothermal: high capital, high risk, high firmness

Geothermal carries the heaviest upfront capital of the renewable set — ~USD 4-5 m/MW with drilling at 40% [ThinkGeo 2026] — but delivers an 85-90% capacity factor no intermittent source matches. The segment economics explain both why it is strategic for baseload and why so little gets built.

Capital intensity is the entry barrier

Indonesian geothermal runs ~USD 4-5 m/MW, above the USD 3-4 m global norm [ThinkGeo 2026]. Drilling is ~40% of capex; a single field's wells cost USD 1.8-11 m each, and an exploration programme runs ~USD 30 m before resource is confirmed [ThinkGeo 2026]. That dry-hole exposure keeps generalist capital away.

But the energy is firm and dispatchable

At an 85-90% capacity factor [Kira estimates], a geothermal MW delivers ~4-5x the annual MWh of a solar MW. On a delivered-energy basis it competes even at the USD 9.41-9.76 c/kWh ceiling [PerPres 112/2022] — and provides the baseload the grid needs as coal is wound down.

Solar wins speed, loses firmness

Solar at ~USD 0.8-1.0 m/MW [Kira estimates] builds in months, not years, with no resource risk — hence its 17.1 GW RUPTL lead. But its ~15-20% capacity factor and need for the 10.3 GW storage block change the true system cost. The two segments are complements, not substitutes.

013 / 029

Where the geothermal value chain earns its margin

The chain runs from work-area award through exploration drilling, field development, and 30-year generation. Margin concentrates in proven operating fields; the exploration phase destroys capital for the unlucky. De-risking instruments and cogeneration are the two levers shifting the economics.

The exploration phase is the value gate

Once a field is proven and producing, geothermal is an annuity: 30-year build-own-operate contracts under MEMR Reg 5/2025 [MEMR Reg 5/2025], firm offtake, low marginal cost. The destroyed value sits upstream — a failed ~USD 30 m exploration programme [ThinkGeo 2026] with no recoverable asset. That bimodal payoff is why de-risking matters more than tariff.

De-risking reshapes the bet

The GREM facility provides USD 150 m of PISP public-window funds plus USD 122.5 m of donor reimbursable grants [World Bank 2019], sharing dry-hole loss so developers do not fully bear exploration failure. It converts a binary bet into an underwritable one — the single most important economic lever in the segment.

Cogeneration extends the revenue line

MEMR Reg 5/2025 lets surplus geothermal and brine energy be sold to PLN, and operators are layering cogeneration (~230 MW in PGE's pipeline) and green-hydrogen pilots onto producing fields [PGE 2025]. Each adds revenue without new exploration risk — the highest-return incremental capital in the chain.

Position

PGE is Indonesia's first and most active geothermal operator — builder of the country's first plant (Kamojang, 1978) and now running 727.5 MW across six operating areas [PGE 2025]. Its 2023 IPO raised ~USD 594 m, earmarked to fund the build toward 1 GW [PGE 2025].

Revenue reached USD 318.9 m for the nine months to September 2025, up 4.2% YoY and ahead of its own target [PGE 2025]. The state-backed balance sheet gives PGE the lowest cost of capital in the field — the structural lever incumbents elsewhere lack.

Pipeline

Lumut Balai Unit 2 (+55 MW) came online June 2025 [PGE 2025]; Hululais Units 1 & 2 (110 MW), ~230 MW of cogeneration, and Gunung Tiga exploration (55 MW potential) sit ahead.

Strategic read

  • Anchor developer for the RUPTL geothermal block — the natural counterparty for the 5.2 GW target and the firmest deliverer of the 0.9 GW-by-2029 interim mark.
  • Cogeneration and green-hydrogen optionality layer incremental revenue onto proven fields without new exploration risk — the highest-return capital available to it.
  • Execution, not access, is the constraint — reaching 1 GW in 2-3 years and 1.8 GW by 2033 requires roughly doubling its historical delivery cadence.
  • Partnership magnet — for foreign drilling-service and turbine OEMs, PGE is the largest single counterparty inside the TKDN-compliant envelope.

Position

Star Energy runs the largest operated geothermal fleet at 875 MW gross across three West Java fields [Barito 2025]. Ownership consolidated under PT Barito Pacific after Green Era acquired the 33.33% BCPG stake for USD 440 m in 2022, leaving Barito with 66.67% [Barito 2025].

Pipeline

Strategic read

  • Reserve quality is the moat — three producing fields insulate Star Energy from the dry-hole risk that gates new entrants.
  • Barito group integration links it to a broader renewables platform (Barito Renewables) and domestic capital access via IDX.
  • Brownfield expansion is the low-risk growth path — Unit-3 and binary additions extend output without re-running exploration.
  • A natural consolidator — with proven operating capability, Star Energy is positioned to absorb work areas others cannot finance through exploration.

Position

Sarulla Operations Ltd runs a 330 MW field — one of the largest single-contract geothermal projects globally [Supreme/INAGA 2025]. The consortium pools Medco Power Indonesia, INPEX, Ormat Technologies, Itochu, and Kyushu Electric Power, blending Indonesian, Japanese, and US capital and technology.

The structure matters more than the single asset: Sarulla is the working template for foreign-capital entry into Indonesian geothermal — risk-shared across a multinational consortium, with offtake to PLN under a long-dated PPA.

Relevance

For foreign developers eyeing the 73% IPP mandate, Sarulla demonstrates how to combine local-content compliance, international financing, and Japanese / US turbine and drilling technology inside one bankable vehicle.

Strategic read

  • The JV is the entry archetype — multinational risk-sharing is the realistic route past the exploration wall and the TKDN gate for non-Indonesian capital.
  • Japanese strategic alignment (INPEX, Itochu, Kyushu) maps directly onto KIRA's JP enterprise-buyer thesis — the consortium model travels.
  • Single-field concentration caps growth; Sarulla is a structure to replicate, not a scale platform to ride.
  • Offtake certainty via long-dated PLN PPA is the bankability anchor the new MEMR Reg 5/2025 framework aims to standardise.

Supreme Energy operates three power stations across two Sumatra work areas for 130 MW total [Supreme/INAGA 2025] — principally Muara Laboh and Rantau Dedap. It is the clearest example of a domestic independent that has cleared the exploration-risk wall on greenfield resource.

Its scale is modest beside PGE and Star Energy, but its track record of taking fields from exploration to commercial operation gives it credibility for the next round of work-area awards under the Genesis tender platform.

Trajectory

Supreme sits at the expansion stage — proving incremental capacity on its existing concessions and positioned to bid for fresh work areas as MEMR Reg 5/2025 shortens the procurement clock.

Strategic read

  • Proof an independent can clear exploration risk — the de-risking facilities and the new procurement clock matter most to developers in exactly this band.
  • A natural JV or acquisition target — proven operating capability plus concession footprint is what scale incumbents and foreign entrants need.
  • Genesis-tender beneficiary — the faster 90/180-day clock disproportionately helps mid-tier developers with shovel-ready pipelines.
  • Sumatra concentration aligns with the resource geography but exposes it to single-region grid and permitting risk.

Section 08 · Demand drivers & offtake
021 / 029

One buyer, one plan, one bottleneck

Generation reaches ratepayers through a single offtaker — PLN. The RUPTL it controls hands 73% of the new build to IPPs [RUPTL 2025] but keeps PLN as the only counterparty, so PPA terms and grid build pace set the real ceiling on how fast clean capacity can land.

The monopsony shapes every bid

PLN buys all utility-scale generation. RUPTL 2025-2034 lifts the renewable plan from 20.9 GW to 52.9 GW including storage, with IPPs developing 73% [RUPTL 2025] — PLN cannot finance the gap alone. But single-buyer PPA terms, payment certainty, and curtailment risk are what developers underwrite, and that is precisely what MEMR Reg 5/2025 sets out to standardise.

The grid has to reach the resource

Geothermal sits in volcanic uplands far from load centres; solar and wind cluster where the sun and wind are, not where demand is. Shared-use transmission and grid expansion are the physical gate [IEEFA 2025]. Without firm interconnection, awarded MW cannot reach ratepayers — transmission is the silent constraint on the whole build.

Captive and industrial demand is the wildcard

Smelter, downstream-metals, and data-centre load — some captive, off the PLN grid — lifts marginal electricity demand fastest. Channelling that pull toward clean baseload rather than captive coal is the policy contest that decides the renewable share.

022 / 029

The finance is pledged; disbursement is the test

Meeting net-zero 2060 needs ~USD 16 bn a year of renewable investment against the USD 3 bn that flowed in 2015-2021 [WRI 2024]. JETP pledges USD 20 bn and every USD 1 bn invested is modelled to return USD 1.41 bn [WRI 2024]. The economics work — the binding question is bankable-project supply.

The investment gap is 5x

Renewable finance must rise from ~USD 3 bn/year to ~USD 16 bn/year to track the net-zero-2060 path [WRI 2024]. That gap, not demand, is the constraint — and it is why the RUPTL leans on IPP capital and why procurement bankability matters so much.

JETP frames the public finance

The Just Energy Transition Partnership pledges USD 20 bn of concessional and private finance [WRI 2024]. A meaningful slice is expected to back geothermal given its baseload value. Disbursement has lagged the headline — the constraint is the pipeline of financeable, COD-credible projects, which the procurement reform is meant to widen.

Returns justify the capital

WRI models USD 1.41 bn of economic return per USD 1 bn invested in renewables [WRI 2024], through clean-energy installation, O&M, and downstream value chains. For market participants the read is clear: the gating issue is deal supply and bankability, not whether the underlying economics clear.

Section 09 · Regulatory & procurement
023 / 029

The procurement reset is the most important policy move

Four regimes shape every awarded megawatt: MEMR Reg 5/2025 rebuilds the PPA and procurement clock, PerPres 112/2022 sets the geothermal tariff ceiling, the 40% TKDN rule gates local content, and the de-risking facilities share exploration loss. Together they decide whether the IPP mandate converts into steel in the ground.

PROCUREMENT REFORM · 2025
MEMR Regulation 5/2025 — renewable PPA guidelines
Replaces the PPA provisions of MEMR 10/2017. Adds a 30-year build-own-operate scheme, structured risk allocation assigning each risk to the party best able to manage it, and pre-COD share-transfer for financing [MEMR Reg 5/2025]. Crucially it caps the procurement clock at 180 days for direct selection and 90 days for direct appointment, and lets surplus geothermal energy be sold to PLN.
SOURCE: MEMR REGULATION NO. 5 OF 2025 · JDIH ESDM · ASHURST / ORRICK ANALYSIS 2025
TARIFF CEILING · ACTIVE
PerPres 112/2022 geothermal tariff structure
Sets the highest benchmark price referenced in any geothermal work-area tender: 9.76 c/kWh × location factor for ≤10 MW and 9.41 c/kWh for 10-50 MW in years 1-10, stepping to 8.00-8.30 c/kWh for years 11-30 [PerPres 112/2022]. Geothermal uniquely allows tariff escalation — a concession other renewables do not get, reflecting its capital intensity.
SOURCE: PRESIDENTIAL REGULATION 112/2022 · AHP / LEXOLOGY ANALYSIS
LOCAL CONTENT · BINDING GATE
40% TKDN local-content requirement
Developers have struggled to meet the 40% TKDN threshold under the existing pricing scheme, and geothermal construction must be done by domestic construction firms [Hogan Lovells 2025]. The rule protects local industry but raises cost for foreign-technology-dependent projects — recent relaxations target exactly this friction. MEMR Reg 5/2025 also addresses TKDN treatment.
SOURCE: HOGAN LOVELLS TKDN RELAXATION NOTE 2025 · ARMA LAW TKDN FRAMEWORK
DE-RISKING · ACTIVE
GREM resource-risk-mitigation facility
The Geothermal Resource Risk Mitigation programme provides USD 150 m of PISP public-window funds plus USD 122.5 m of donor reimbursable grants [World Bank 2019], sharing exploration-failure cost so developers do not fully bear dry-hole risk. It is the instrument that converts geothermal's binary payoff into an underwritable one — the practical key to mobilising private capital.
SOURCE: WORLD BANK GREM PROJECT 2019 · PT SMI · GI HUB CASE STUDY

Section 10 · AI impact
025 / 029

AI is pulled into geothermal by the cost of subsurface error

Adoption sits between pilot and integration: resource-prediction and drilling-optimisation models are moving into field use because the payoff is concrete. With drilling at 40% of a USD 4-5 m/MW project [ThinkGeo 2026], a few points of exploration-success or rate-of-penetration gain reshape returns.

Where the maturity sits

Deep learning on geological, geophysical, and geochemical data predicts reservoir location and quality, and interprets seismic and thermal-anomaly imagery to de-risk siting [ScienceDirect 2025]. These models are moving from research into developer workflows — the de-risking value is too large to leave on the bench.

Why the economics finally clear

The full-lifecycle review puts AI applications at varied technology-readiness levels, with over USD 9 m invested in geothermal ML initiatives to date [Stanford SGW 2024]. For Indonesian operators facing the steepest drilling costs globally, the ROI on resource prediction and rate-of-penetration optimisation is the clearest in the renewable set.

027 / 029

2034 outlook: the build gap is the story

The 5.2 GW geothermal target needs roughly 5x the 2017-2023 run-rate [IDX Channel 2025]. Our base case clears ~3.8-4.3 GW by 2034 — real progress, real shortfall. The forecast hinges on whether procurement reform and de-risking lift delivery cadence, or whether execution stays the binding wall.

Base case: progress, not target

We model the geothermal fleet reaching ~3.8-4.3 GW by 2034 [Kira estimates] — above today's 2.74 GW but short of the 7.9 GW cumulative the RUPTL implies. The reform shortens the clock and de-risking mobilises capital, but the physical build cadence cannot 5x overnight. The 0.9 GW-by-2029 interim mark is itself ambitious against history.

The swing variables

Three levers decide the path: procurement-clock execution (does the 90/180-day cap actually compress timelines?), TKDN friction (does local-content cost stall foreign-tech projects?), and transmission build (can the grid reach the resource?). Each can move the 2034 number by 0.5-1.5 GW.

Geothermal vs the wider build

Solar will overshoot geothermal on nameplate, but on firm delivered energy geothermal remains scarce and strategic. The renewable share likely lands near the revised 19-21% by 2030 [Ember 2025] — below the original 23% goal, honestly reset.

028 / 029

Three paths to 2034 for the geothermal fleet

Base case sees the fleet reach ~4.0 GW by 2034 on faster-but-still-constrained delivery. Bull and bear cases hinge on procurement-clock execution, TKDN friction, and transmission build. The RUPTL's own 7.9 GW cumulative target sits above even the bull case — a deliberate stretch, not a forecast.

Bear · clock stalls
~3.3 GW2034
Procurement reform under-delivers, TKDN friction stalls foreign-tech projects, and transmission lags the resource. Run-rate stays near the historical ~113 MW/year. The fleet adds barely 0.6 GW over the decade — the renewable share misses even the revised 19-21% mark [Ember 2025].
Base · reform partial
~4.0 GW2034
The 90/180-day clock compresses some timelines, GREM de-risking mobilises mid-tier developers, and PGE plus Star Energy deliver brownfield expansions. Run-rate roughly doubles to ~250 MW/year [Kira estimates] — real progress, but the 5.2 GW target slips past 2034.
Bull · reform lands
~5.5 GW2034
Procurement reform converts the IPP mandate to financial close at pace, TKDN is relaxed for specialist drilling tech, JETP capital flows, and transmission keeps up. Run-rate clears 350-400 MW/year. Still short of the 7.9 GW RUPTL path, but the closest realistic approach to it.

What to watch through 2026-2027

First the procurement signal: do the early MEMR Reg 5/2025 tenders actually close inside 90/180 days, and do PPAs reach bankability? That single read will discriminate bull from base faster than any capacity number.

Then the TKDN read: whether the local-content relaxations let foreign drilling and turbine technology in without cost penalty determines the foreign-IPP entry pace.

The strategic bottom line

Geothermal remains structurally scarce firm-clean baseload in a grid still two-thirds coal. The targets will likely be missed; the asset class will likely still be the most valuable clean-baseload position in Indonesia. Sequence capital to the 2030-2034 window, partner with reserve-holding incumbents, and underwrite the exploration wall through GREM — not around it.
