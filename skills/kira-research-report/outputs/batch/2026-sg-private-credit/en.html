Section 05 · Market sizing
007 / 018

From a USD 3.5 trn global pool to Singapore's wedge

Private credit has crossed USD 3.5 trn globally, with APAC the fastest-growing leg. Singapore does not hold the largest balance sheet, but it intermediates a rising share of regional origination, structuring and fund domicile — the lever that turns hub flow into booked AUM.

Global: scale, then stress-testing

Global private credit AUM reached USD 3.5 trn in the latest industry count [ACC 2026], from a sub-USD 1 trn base a decade earlier. Corporate lending is ~60% of the pool; asset-backed, real estate and infrastructure debt make up the other 40% [ACC 2026]. Deployment hit USD 593 bn in 2024, up 78% year on year [ACC 2026].

APAC: the convergence leg

APAC tracks from USD 59 bn (2024) to USD 92 bn by 2027 [AIMA 2025] — ~16% a year, above the global average. The under-penetration is the opportunity: banks still hold 80%+ of regional credit [AIMA 2025], a gap the US and Europe closed years ago.

Singapore: the intermediation wedge

We estimate Singapore-domiciled or Singapore-managed vehicles intermediate USD 18-24 bn of APAC private credit AUM in 2025 [Kira estimates], anchored by the Temasek platform and a fast-growing VCC fund base — a share rising faster than the regional pool.

Section 06 · Demand drivers
008 / 018

Bank retrenchment meets an unserved middle

Two forces, both structural. Basel-driven bank retreat narrows where regulated lenders will deploy, while an APAC MSME finance gap of USD 2.5 trn leaves cash-flow borrowers unserved. Private credit sits precisely in the space banks are vacating.

Why banks are stepping back

Under Basel III, APAC banks carry heavier equity charges against real-estate and mid-market exposures, narrowing appetite for private-credit-like risk [WB 2019]. The pullback is selective, not blanket: banks concentrate on investment-grade names and collateral-rich corporates with clear cash-flow visibility, leaving the cash-flow-lending middle to non-bank capital [Chambers SG 2026].

The structural starting point amplifies the shift. Banks held over 80% of APAC credit at end-2023 [AIMA 2025] — against roughly half in the US and Europe. Each percentage point that migrates from bank to non-bank balance sheets is a multi-year tailwind for fund managers.

Singapore's own SMEs illustrate the gap: only about a quarter of local SMEs access bank financing [WEF 2025]. The residual is exactly the borrower set private credit underwrites — revenue-generating, often asset-light, poorly served by collateral-led bank models.

Why borrowers choose non-bank capital

Mature companies increasingly seek non-dilutive, flexible financing — bespoke structures that align repayment with cash flows, faster execution, and certainty of close [AIMA 2025]. Private credit trades a higher coupon for speed, flexibility and a single-counterparty relationship.

  • Speed & certainty — direct origination closes faster than syndicated bank processes; decisive in competitive M&A.
  • Structural flexibility — covenant design, PIK toggles and amortisation tailored to the borrower's cash-flow shape.
  • Non-dilutive growth capital — founders preserve equity versus a growth-equity round.
  • Through-cycle availability — private lenders stepped in to fill liquidity gaps amid 2025 volatility [Chambers SG 2026].

The demand wedge is durable. Infrastructure, real estate and the SME "missing middle" all face capital shortages as bank retrenchment persists [CCLA 2025].

Section 11 · Regulatory & fund structure
013 / 018

The VCC, the retail door, and MAS supervision

Singapore's regulatory product is a flexible onshore fund wrapper plus a credible supervisor. The Variable Capital Company houses ring-fenced private credit sub-funds; a proposed retail framework would widen the investor base; and MAS supervision keeps the franchise investable.

The Variable Capital Company

Introduced by MAS and ACRA in January 2020, the VCC lets multiple open- or closed-end funds sit under one corporate umbrella while staying ring-fenced from each other [Asia Asset 2025]. Managers house private credit strategies in separate sub-funds — each with its own mandate, tenure and risk parameters — ideal for share financing, pre-IPO funding and private placements.

Adoption is migrating capital onshore. A re-domiciliation scheme that closed in January 2025 moved an estimated 220-250 funds — 16-18% of all VCC entities — predominantly from Cayman, Delaware and Guernsey [Sidley 2025]. ASEAN passporting under the Collective Investment Scheme framework is targeted for 2027 [Sidley 2025].

MAS supervision as the moat

A June 2025 MAS circular on VCC governance set out supervisory expectations from a 2024 thematic review [Sidley 2025]. The signal: open architecture, supervised credibly. Insurer capital treatment for structured products and infrastructure also resets, with new rules from 31 March 2026 [Sidley 2025].

Opening the retail door

On 27 March 2025, MAS issued a consultation on a retail Long-Term Investment Fund (LIF) framework — a route for retail investors to access private equity, private credit, real estate and infrastructure through MAS-authorised, Singapore-constituted vehicles [MAS 2025].

  • Addressable base widens — from accredited and institutional to mass-affluent retail.
  • Guardrails matter — liquidity terms and disclosure are the design crux, given private credit's illiquidity.
  • Distribution leads — reporting cadence and investor education must precede launch.

State capital alongside the rules

Policy and capital move together: a SGD 1 bn Growth Fund and a USD 154 m Long-Term Investment Fund were unveiled in 2025 to deepen the market [MTI 2025], plus up to USD 500 m in concessional capital for transition finance via FAST-P [MAS 2024]. The state both sets the rules and seeds the pool.

Section 12 · AUM growth dynamics
014 / 018

Where the next leg of AUM actually comes from

Singapore's AUM growth runs on three engines: state-seeded anchor capital, the wealth channel, and onshore fund migration. Each is live in 2026 — but the wealth channel carries both the upside and the most-watched risk after global retail vehicles wobbled.

Engine one: anchor capital

State and sovereign seed capital de-risks the market for everyone behind it — the SGD 1 bn Growth Fund [MTI 2025] and a ~USD 10 bn Temasek platform [Temasek 2024]. This is patient capital that draws co-investment rather than competing for it.

Engine two: the wealth channel

SeaTown's Fund III reached USD 900 m at second close, over 70% deployed, with the largest tranche from private wealth in Singapore and Hong Kong [Bloomberg 2025]. Family offices and mass-affluent flows — soon retail via the LIF — are the fastest-growing source of new commitments.

Engine three: onshore migration

VCC re-domiciliation moved 220-250 funds onshore [Sidley 2025], converting offshore AUM into Singapore-booked AUM. This is share captured from Cayman and Delaware, not new money — durable, regulation-anchored growth.

Section 13 · AI in private credit operations
015 / 018

From weeks to days, without ceding the credit call

Private credit runs on non-standardised documents — Excel, PDFs, servicer reports, bespoke borrower templates. AI's first win is operational: extracting and structuring that data, then monitoring it continuously. The technology enhances speed and consistency; it does not make the investment decision.

Operating leverage, not headcount cuts

AI-powered spreading, real-time portfolio monitoring and dynamic risk pricing compress decision timelines from weeks to days without sacrificing analytical rigour [Decimal Point 2025]. In APAC's less standardised, document-heavy deals, that data-handling edge matters more than in syndicated markets.

Monitoring is the higher-value front

Continuous covenant tracking lets managers detect potential breaches early, reducing default risk [Moody's 2025] — decisive as covenant breaches rose through 2025. A single platform can score risk, flag fraud indicators and watch compliance in real time.

Section 14 · Forecast & scenarios
017 / 018

Singapore's AUM wedge through 2031

The regional pull is structural; the variable is how much AUM Singapore books rather than merely routes. Our scenarios turn on three swing factors: the pace of bank retrenchment, the success of the retail-LIF launch, and whether the cycle delivers an orderly or disorderly test.

Base case
USD 38-44bn
SG-booked AUM by 2031 [Kira estimates]. APAC tracks to ~USD 92 bn by 2027 [AIMA 2025] and compounds; VCC migration and wealth flows lift Singapore's share. An orderly cycle, modest spread compression.
Downside
USD 26-30bn
A disorderly cycle test — defaults breach the ~1.5% 2025 base [KBRA 2025], retail vehicles see redemption stress, LIF launch slips. Growth continues but the wealth engine stalls and risk premia widen.
Upside
USD 50-58bn
Accelerated bank retreat, a clean retail-LIF launch, and faster onshore migration. Singapore captures a structural step-up in share of regional origination booked locally [Kira estimates].

What moves the number

  • Bank retrenchment pace — each point of APAC's 80%+ bank share that migrates is multi-year fuel [AIMA 2025].
  • Retail-LIF execution — a credible launch widens the funding base; a stumble (per global BDC stress) defers it.
  • Cycle outcome — orderly defaults validate the model; disorderly ones invite supervisory tightening [FSB 2026].

The directional read

The asset class is past its silent-expansion phase and into its first real test [WithIntel 2026]. For Singapore, the test is constructive: a credibility-led hub gains share precisely when capital becomes selective about where it books risk. Our base case assumes the city-state converts hub flow into AUM faster than the regional pool grows — the through-line of this report.

Methodology & sources

How this view was built, how to read the source tags, and the full citation list for every named source used across the report.

Methodology

This view triangulates two evidence streams. Where a figure carries a named source tag (e.g. [AIMA 2025], [MAS 2025]), the underlying data comes from that source; the full citation appears at right. Where a figure carries [Kira estimates], KIRA analysts derived it from a blend of disclosed inputs — industry-body sizing, manager fund-close disclosures, regulator data and regional pattern-matching — with the anchor metric disclosed inline.

Singapore-specific AUM figures are estimates: no single authority publishes a Singapore-only private credit AUM series, so we triangulate from the APAC pool, named-platform commitments and VCC fund-migration data. Approximately 25-30% of quantitative claims are KIRA-derived; the remainder are externally cited.

Research ran on an English-language baseline. Singapore's business and regulatory press leads in English, so no local-language query pass was required.
